RINGS OF DUST

Chapter Twelve

The reactor bay was the quietest part of the ship at the end of the day cycle.
The hum there was lower, more foundational — not a sound so much as a frequency
you felt in the soles of your feet. Sam sat against the cold metal wall with her
legs stretched out in front of her, watching the space where Ricardo paced.

He had been pacing for about four minutes. He was working something out.

"You ever wonder," Sam said, "what the actual shape of this is. Not what we were
told the shape was. The actual thing."

Ricardo stopped. "What do you mean."

"I mean we were handed a mission brief and a set of objectives and a timeline,
and those things were assembled by people who had their own objectives and their
own timelines, and we are on the inside of something we only partly see." She
was not anxious about it. She was observational. "That's not unusual. Most
people are on the inside of something they only partly see. I'm just wondering
what we're in the middle of, specifically."

Ricardo frowned. The question had lived in the back of his head for a while and
he had managed not to look at it directly. "You think we've been lied to."

"I think we've been given a specific portion of the truth. Which is different
from a lie. But it produces similar navigational problems." She looked at her
hands. "What would you do, if the mission turned out to be something other than
what we signed on for?"

Sam reached into her pocket. The matte-gray device came out small and flat in
her hand. She pressed the lower-left corner. A single red pulse — then steady.

"iRIS can't hear us," she said. She looked at Ricardo. "That's the other thing
it does."

Ricardo looked at the device. He looked at her.

"How long have you—"

"A while." She set it on the floor between them, indicator facing up. "I'll
explain more when there's time. Right now I want to know what you'd do."

Ricardo considered it, genuinely, without deflection. He sat down across from
her. "Make sure everyone gets home," he said. "The objective, the brief — I
don't care what those turn out to be. That's what I signed on for." He paused.
He looked at her. "That includes you. By the way."

Sam looked at him. The look had precision to it — not surprised, registering.
The kind of look you give something you've been waiting to confirm.

"I know," she said.

He held the look a beat longer than the sentence required.

"Good."

They sat with that for a moment.

"Cole's not here," Ricardo said.

"No."

"He's been in the forward crew quarters since second shift." Ricardo's jaw
tightened. "Something happened today."

"I know," Sam said. She picked up the device and stood. "I need to find him."

---

Cole was in the forward crew quarters standing at the narrow porthole with his
back to the room when Sam knocked on the frame.

He didn't turn around. "Come in."

She closed the door behind her. She took the device from her pocket and pressed
the lower-left corner. The indicator pulsed once and held steady.

"iRIS can't hear this room," she said. She set it on the shelf beside her,
face-up. "I wanted you to know that."

Cole's hands did not move from the porthole frame. He looked out at the black.

"It's not a malfunction," he said. It was not a question.

"No." Sam crossed the room and stood beside him — not at the porthole, a step
back, where he could feel her presence without having to look at it. Her voice
was quieter than usual. "Cole. Come sit down."

Something in it was different from her standard register. He heard it. He
turned around and looked at her face.

He sat.

Sam sat across from him. She gave it a moment — not building to something, just
making sure the ground was solid before she put weight on it. She leaned forward
with her forearms on her knees.

"I picked up a message about an hour ago," she said. "Same routing I've been
tracking. I've been sitting with it for a while because I wanted to be sure
before I came to you." She paused. "It was about Sharon."

Cole held her eyes.

"She was hurt in San Francisco," Sam said. "In the parking garage. It was
bad, Cole." She kept her voice steady but she didn't empty it — there was
something in it that acknowledged what she was saying and what it cost to say
it. "The message said she didn't make it."

The room was very quiet.

"Emily survived. That's confirmed. She was released and she's clear." Sam
stayed with him in the silence for a moment. "Emily is okay."

Cole said nothing for a long time.

The ship hummed. Outside the porthole, the black ran in every direction without
a visible edge. He thought about the copper-wire ring — the hummingbird feeder
wire bent into shape, the small smooth pebble at the top. He had pressed it
into her hand at the cabin driveway and she had closed her fingers around it.
He did not know where it was now.

Then, very quietly: "How."

"I don't know the details. The message was brief."

He stood and went back to the porthole. He looked at the black. Whatever was
happening on his face he had turned away from her, and she let him.

"Thank you," he said. His voice was steady. She could see from the set of his
shoulders that the steadiness was costing him something, and she did not
remark on it.

She stayed in the chair for a moment. She didn't try to fill it.

"Take whatever time you need," she said. "I'll be in the bay." She stood and
put her hand on his arm briefly — not a consolation, just an acknowledgment.
Present. Real.

She left.

Cole stood in the room for a long time. He looked at his hands. He thought about
the ring made of copper wire that he had pressed into her hand in the driveway
at the cabin and she had closed her fingers around it and said *I will*. He
thought about the way she said his full name — *Cole Nova* — not as a joke
exactly, but with the weight of someone who believed the name meant something.
He thought about sitting on the porch, watching the headlights come up the dirt
road, not knowing yet that she was on her way.

He had a list. He had learned, in the weeks after his father died, to keep a
list of everything about a person that you did not want to forget. He had
started the list for Sharon in his head already, had not known he was doing it
until now.

He pressed his fist against the porthole glass. The cold of it came through
immediately.

Then he pushed off the wall and went to find Taylor.

---

The bridge was running on minimal light at this hour. Taylor sat at the
starboard console with a holoscreen open in front of him, and he had the posture
of a man who is working but was also listening for the sound of a specific door.
When Cole came through the entryway, Taylor's hands did not stop moving and he
did not turn around immediately. He gave it a beat.

"Captain." He said it without looking. The word landed like a test.

Cole moved to the center console and stood. He was not close enough to be
threatening. He was close enough to be clear.

He was aware, with precision, of the distance between himself and Taylor. Of
the console between them. Of how long it would take to cross it. His body had
already filed its assessment without being asked, and he noted it the way he
noted a calculation — not because he intended to act on it, but because he
needed to know what it was saying.

He had learned, when his father died — the slow specific kind of death that
teaches you more about grief than you ever wanted to know — that the first
hours of it could arrive wearing the texture of rage. The two were almost
indistinguishable if you weren't paying attention. He had learned to pay
attention. To build the list. First: what do I know. Second: what do I need.
Third: in what order. The list was the lock. The lock was what kept the door
closed.

He had a list. He was building it.

"I have a question," Cole said.

Taylor turned his chair. His face was composed — the careful composition of a
man who had decided in advance what kind of conversation this was going to be.
"Go ahead."

"Did you know she was in danger."

Taylor's gaze held steady. He did not blink. He did not recalibrate. He had
already decided what his face was going to do and it was doing that. "We're all
in danger. That's the nature of—"

"Sharon. Did you know."

A pause. Taylor clasped his hands in his lap. "What I know," he said, slowly,
like he was choosing the weight of each word, "is that this mission has more
stakeholders than the crew manifest reflects. And that people who get caught
between those stakeholders — people who are carrying information that more than
one party wants — those people are at risk. Sharon was carrying information."

Something moved through Cole that was faster than thought. He felt his weight
shift onto his front foot. He felt the distance across the console compress, in
his body's own arithmetic, into something achievable.

He did not move.

He pressed it back — the same way he had pressed it back in the weeks after his
father died, when the grief kept arriving in the wrong shape and wearing the
wrong face, and you had to put it somewhere it couldn't reach the rest of you.
Into the compartment. Close the door. The list. What do I know. What do I need.
In what order. He held the list in front of it like a wall.

"That's not an answer."

"It's the truest thing I can say to you right now." He met Cole's eyes without
flinching. "Sharon knew things. About the delivery, about the crystals, about
the people she was working with. People who know too much become liabilities in
a war. That isn't a Collective truth or an Alpha truth. It's just what
happens."

Cole watched his face. He was watching for what had already happened — for what
Taylor had known before Cole walked in here, for the shape of the gap between
what Taylor said and what Taylor knew. He did not find it. Taylor was too
careful, had too much practice, had already made the calculation that ambiguity
served him better than a position.

"I'm going to find out exactly what happened," Cole said. "I want you to know
that."

Taylor nodded. "I would expect nothing less." He said it without irony, and that
was the most unsettling version of it.

Cole turned to the communications panel. He reached for the relay controls — not
because he expected them to work, but because he wanted to know. The screen
blinked and then went dark in the particular way of something that had been
configured not to cooperate.

He stood there for a moment. He looked at the dark screen.

"iRIS," he said.

"Working," iRIS responded. The reply came back a fraction of a second slower
than usual.

"Open a relay channel to Federation HQ."

"Communications are experiencing an alignment issue at the current approach
vector. Signal clarity is insufficient for—"

"Log the attempt."

"Logged."

Cole looked at Taylor. Taylor looked at Cole. Neither of them said what both of
them were thinking.

Cole walked out.

---

Eleven hundred miles south of Federation airspace, Emily Nova was in the back of
a taxi with her head against the window and her eyes closed. The cab smelled
like pine air freshener and old coffee. She had been awake for thirty-one hours
and the road under the tires was a rhythm she could almost follow but not quite,
and the almost-following of it kept dragging her toward the edge.

She jerked awake when her head slid on the glass.

The city lights were behind them. She could see the highway and the dark on
either side and the yellow dividing lines running past in a steady pulse. She
had seen this before — the yellow lines and the dark — and she pressed her palm
against the window to wake herself up further.

In her other hand she was holding the number Rachel had given her in the parking
garage. A handwritten sequence on the back of a Verguld Media parking receipt —
not Rachel's number. Rachel had been very clear about that. *Someone will
answer. Tell them where you are and where you're going.*

She dialed it.

It rang twice.

"Go ahead." A woman's voice. Not Rachel's. Measured, unhurried, with the
particular quality of someone accustomed to receiving information at odd hours.

"This is Emily Nova," Emily said. Her voice came out rougher than she expected.
"I'm in a taxi. I'm going to Texas. To Federation headquarters."

A brief pause. "We know who you are. Are you clear of Verguld?"

"Yes."

"All right." Another pause — not uncertainty, the pause of someone consulting
something. "When you arrive, don't go to the main entrance. There's a secondary
receiving entrance on the building's east face, flagged on Federation maps as
maintenance access. Someone will be there."

"Who?"

"Someone who knows what you're carrying." The voice shifted slightly — not
warmer exactly, but more direct. "Are you all right, Ms. Nova?"

Emily looked out the window. The yellow lines kept coming. The dark kept
holding its position on either side.

She thought about the parking garage stairwell. The look on Sharon's face when
she had said *go* — the specific economy of it, the way it meant every version
of *go* at once. She thought about the sound she had not been able to un-hear
in the hours since. She thought about her brother somewhere past Saturn's rings,
out of reach, carrying a mission that someone had designed to detonate, and she
was the only person moving toward him from this side and she was in the back of
a taxi she had paid for with a traceable card and she had been awake for
thirty-one hours and she had said the wrong thing in Marcus's office and she
did not know yet what it had cost.

She thought about what the question actually was. Not protocol. The voice had
shifted — the particular register of someone who had heard something in Emily's
voice that Emily had not put there on purpose. Something that had come through
in the gaps of being awake too long and too alone.

*Are you all right.*

She was not. Nothing was. Sharon was dead and Cole didn't know it yet and Marcus
knew which direction she was running and she was alone in the specific way that
is not solitude — not quiet, not private, but the kind of alone that arrives
when you run out of people to call and the ones you had are gone or unreachable
or both.

She thought about what she had said to Marcus in his office — the thing she had
said without thinking, the thing she had known the moment it left her mouth was
a mistake. *Cole's heart is gonna break when he hears about Sharon.*

"I don't know yet," she said.

The line closed.

Emily leaned her head back against the seat. She watched the highway. She kept
her eyes open this time, watching the yellow lines come and go in the dark, and
did not let herself drift.
