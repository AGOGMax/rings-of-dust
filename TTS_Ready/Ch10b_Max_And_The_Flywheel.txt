RINGS OF DUST

Chapter Ten (B)

Ray ran his dry fingertips along the rough edges of his weathered oak table,
feeling the familiar grit beneath his skin. The surface, scarred from years of
restless nights and unanswered questions, was as uneven as his thoughts. A
single light hung overhead, casting sharp, unforgiving shadows that made the
room feel even smaller than it was. The faint hum of the lamp above him was the
only sound — its flicker a reminder that nothing around him was as stable as it
seemed.

In front of him, the stolen fuel cell gleamed dully under the light's hesitant
glow. It was untouched since the day he'd left Alpha — abandoned, like
everything else. Ray stared at it, half-expecting it to give him answers, though
he knew better by now. The thought of selling it tugged at him, a practical
solution for the bills piling up on the kitchen counter. The black market would
pay a good price. But the longer he looked at it, the more something twisted in
his gut — a strange cocktail of anger and, worse, hope.

He hadn't realized how small his apartment had become until the two people
stepped inside.

Squirrel crossed the threshold first, his heavy boots thudding against the worn
hardwood floor. Red followed, her gaze sweeping across the space with a quiet
intensity that made Ray's skin prickle. They were not the kind of people who
fidgeted. They moved through the room and stopped and waited, and the quality of
their waiting was very different from the waiting of civilians.

They didn't sit. They didn't move toward the couch half-covered in old blankets.
Instead, they positioned themselves near the table — not threatening, exactly,
but settled in the way of people who intend to conclude this conversation before
leaving.

Squirrel didn't waste words. "We have a window of about seventy-two hours before
Alpha sends someone who isn't going to knock. You're already on a list, Ray.
You've been on it since the night you told your department head."

Ray looked at them. "You people have been watching me."

"We've been keeping you alive," Red said. "There's a difference."

"Not from where I'm sitting."

"I understand that." She pulled out a chair and sat down across from him —
unhurried, like she was prepared to be here a while. "What you found in that
locker didn't stop being important because they took it from you. Alpha knows
the discovery exists. They know you made it. And they have built a significant
amount of their current business model on the fact that the rest of the world
does not know what you know."

Ray's hand moved toward the blanket on the table, then stopped. He looked at
Squirrel. "What do you actually want from me?"

"We want you alive and working," Squirrel said. "We have a facility. Equipment.
Other researchers. What we don't have is someone who has held a charged cell in
his hands and watched it crystallize in the wrong container and understood what
he was looking at."

"It was an accident."

"Most of the important ones are."

Ray was quiet. Outside the window, a car passed and did not slow down and its
headlights swept the ceiling of his apartment. He had been watching headlights
for three weeks, logging which ones slowed down.

"You understand what the cell actually did," Red said. It was not a question.
"Alpha thinks they understand it. They're harvesting the output — the crystals,
the yield, the energy the conversion cycle produces. They built their whole
operation around the output." She leaned forward. "They never figured out what
they're actually dealing with. They only ever saw it as a container."

"They saw what they expected to see," Ray said, in spite of himself.

"Yes."

He looked at the cell under the blanket. "I packed the crystals in there because
it was convenient. The negative pressure, the nitrogen atmosphere — I thought it
was just a stable environment. I wasn't trying to trigger a reaction. I was
trying to avoid paperwork."

"And what you got," Squirrel said, "was a reaction that their entire R&D division
has been unable to reproduce reliably in two years of trying. Because they
started from the output side. They're backward-engineering a process they don't
have the right mental model for."

Ray pulled the blanket away from the fuel cell. He hadn't meant to do it — his
hands made the decision before his head caught up. The cell sat on the table,
dull and inert in the lamplight.

"I know how the cells work at eleven days," he said. "That's the window Alpha
operates on. They found the yield threshold and they've been farming it ever
since — harvest, reset, repeat." He reached into the drawer and pulled out a
battered notebook. Several pages were covered in diagrams, heat maps, rough
models of accumulation behavior. "What I don't know is what happens if you
don't reset."

Red looked at the notebook. "You think longer cycles change the math."

"I think they change everything." Ray's voice had changed. He was not frightened
anymore. He was precise, and precision was, for him, a kind of territory. "I've
run the calculations a dozen times. I keep getting results that don't make sense
under any model I can test here. What I need—" he stopped. He had spent three
weeks trying to say this clearly and still couldn't quite get there. "I need
proper instruments. A controlled environment. I need to test this under
conditions I cannot replicate in an apartment."

He tapped the notebook. "Alpha built their entire operation on eleven days
because that's where they found the yield window. They never asked what would
happen at thirty days. Or sixty. Or longer." He looked at the fuel cell. "They
saw a harvest. I think they missed the actual discovery."

Squirrel looked at Red. Something passed between them — not surprise. Recognition.

"What kind of facility?" Ray said.

"Off-site. Secure. Equipment and two researchers who understand the chemistry."
Red pulled a card from her pocket and set it on the table — plain, no logo, a
handwritten address. "Tonight."

"Tonight."

"The window closes tomorrow morning."

Ray picked up the card. He set it back down. He picked it up again and held it.

"There's something else," he said. "The data from the Saturn mission parameters —
the yield curves in the public-domain research. There's a distribution pattern
that doesn't match what I'm seeing at ground level. I think the cell behaves
differently in low gravity." He looked at his own numbers. "I think if I could
test this in the right conditions — off-planet, sustained duration — the results
would look completely different from anything Alpha has on record."

Squirrel and Red looked at each other.

"That's a longer conversation," Red said.

"I'm going to need more than a safe house and two colleagues," Ray said.
"Eventually."

"We know," Squirrel said. "One problem at a time."

Ray looked at the fuel cell for the last time in his apartment. He put his
notebook on top of it, carefully, like he was tucking something in.

"Seventy-two hours," he said.

"Less, now."

He stood up. He went to his closet and pulled out a bag from the top shelf and
stood holding it for a moment. Then he started packing. He was not fast about it
and he was not slow. He was precise. He took the notebook. He took the fuel
cell, wrapped in the blanket. He left the loan statements on the counter.

At the door, he turned back and looked at the apartment once — the lamp still
burning, the oak table still scarred, the lamp's flicker still going. Then he
followed them out into the dark.
