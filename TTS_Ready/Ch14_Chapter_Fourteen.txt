RINGS OF DUST

Chapter Fourteen

Saturn Approach

The bunk was the smallest room on the Golden Dawn, which was why Cole had taken
it.

He sat on the edge of the mattress with his elbows on his knees and the
makeshift copper-wire ring in his open palm. It looked even smaller in the false
light of the cabin sconce. The pebble at the top of the wire had been smooth
when he'd set it there years ago and was still smooth, and the wire had taken on
the dull green of long-handled copper, and the whole thing was a hand-tied knot
of two inches and a memory.

He turned it once between his thumb and forefinger.

He was not crying. He had cataloged that earlier, the way a man cataloged a
faulty seal " observed, logged, set aside for later. He had cried in the
corridor outside the comms station after Taylor's voice had said the words. He
had cried in the head, with the door locked, with one fist pressed against the
bulkhead until his knuckles had bruised. He had cried with Ricardo on the floor
of the galley while Bitboy chittered low and worried from the top of the
refrigerator. He had not cried since.

What he had instead was a list.

Items, in order:
One. Saturn. Twenty-six hours out. The B-ring sectors lit up on the scope like a
cold weather map.
Two. The harvesters. Seven of them, deployed and ripe, drifting in the
high-density vapor lanes. Mission-critical.
Three. Taylor.

He turned the ring again. His thumb found the pebble. He set the pebble against
his lower lip the way she used to set her own thumb there when she was thinking,
and he closed his eyes for a moment, and he opened them again.

Three. Taylor. Subitem A: Taylor knew, before they'd left Earth, that Sharon
would be dead by the time they got to Saturn. Subitem B: Taylor had presumably
been in contact with whoever ordered it. Subitem C: Taylor was, at this moment,
sitting at his console two decks up, doing his job to mission spec, doing the
small clean version of his job a man like him did to keep his cover. Subitem D:
Taylor had to live through Saturn. Whatever came after, Saturn went first. The
mission went first. Cole had spent thirty years not letting personal weather
sink a mission and he was not going to start now.

He turned the ring once more, set it in the breast pocket of his jumpsuit, and
buttoned the flap down.

Bitboy, curled at the foot of the bunk, lifted his head at the sound of the
button. The chimp's ethanol cell hummed faintly in the small space, a sound like
a refrigerator considering its options. Two of his mechanical limbs were folded
against his belly in sleep. The third was hooked, with the casual death-grip
he'd developed in his cage years, around Cole's left ankle. The other three were
doing whatever Bitboy's other three limbs did when no one was watching.

"Hey, buddy," Cole said, quiet.

Bitboy made the small interrogative noise that meant are we doing the thing yet.

"Not yet," Cole said. "Soon. Go back to sleep."

Bitboy did not go back to sleep. Bitboy never went back to sleep when Cole was
sitting like that.

Cole let his hand fall to the chimp's head and rested it there. Bitboy's
blue-stained mouth " still faintly purple from the blackberry preserves at the
cabin, six weeks ago and a galaxy back " turned and pressed into Cole's wrist.

"Yeah," Cole said. "I know."

He looked at the bulkhead and let his head go where it wanted.

His head wanted the cabin.

It was five years before page one and the snow had been on the ground for a
week.

He had been sitting on the porch with a quarter-bottle of bourbon and a wool
blanket and Bitboy in a hoodie that was much too big for him, watching the
valley fill up with the blue end-of-day light that you only got at altitude. The
pines made the noise pines made " the steady whispered argument of a forest that
had been having the same conversation for two centuries. The smoke from the
chimney was a slow column against the sky. The cabin was warm at his back. He
had been three years out of the Federation by then, on what his discharge papers
called Indefinite Personal Leave and what he called the rest of his life.

The drone passed at six twelve.

He knew it was six twelve because he had glanced at his watch when he heard the
rotor sound. The drone made the wrong kind of rotor sound " not commercial, not
park service, not the thin reedy whine of one of the surveillance birds the
local sheriff used for the meth-cook hollers " and Cole had spent enough time in
the Federation to know what the sound was. The drone was Federation. The drone
was running the ridge.

He had watched it pass over the lake without moving from the rocking chair. The
drone had not been looking for him. He understood that. The drone was looking
for someone who had come up the valley in the last six hours, and Cole had not
come up the valley in the last six hours, so the drone had no business with
Cole. He had watched it disappear over the southern ridge. He had taken another
drink. He had told himself he was going to go inside in a minute.

The knock came at six forty-one.

It was not the knock of a person who knew the cabin. The cabin had a flag "
Sharon had hung it the second visit, a small piece of yellow ribbon nailed to
the porch column, I am not the law, she'd said when he'd asked, and he hadn't
asked again " and the people who knew the cabin tapped the column twice before
they came up the steps. This person had not tapped the column.

He had picked up the bourbon bottle in his left hand, which is what he was
holding when he opened the door. His right hand had been on the doorframe at
chest height where the .45 was velcroed against the inside molding.

Sharon had been on the other side of the door.

She had been bleeding through the sleeve of her field jacket from a place above
her left elbow and her hair had been wet from the snow and she had been holding
up another person " a man, slumped, eyes closed, with a tactical kit
half-unbuckled and a Collective droplet sigil sewn inside the collar of his
jacket where the Federation could miss it.

"Cole," she had said.

It had not been a question and it had not been a hello. It had been the way a
person said another person's name when they had spent the last twenty minutes
dragging a dying man up a snow-covered switchback and hoping the porch light at
the top of the climb would be the one they remembered.

"In," Cole had said, and stepped aside.

He had set the bottle on the table without thinking about it. He had taken the
man's weight off her in one motion and laid him out on the floor in front of the
wood stove because the floor in front of the wood stove was the closest warm
flat surface and because his triage instincts had started running again
somewhere between the first step and the second. He had moved like a man in a
remembered language. The Federation had drilled the language into him for
fifteen years and he had not used it for three years and his hands had
remembered every verb.

The man had been gut-shot. Through-and-through, low and to the right, exit wound
clean. Cole's medic had once told him that low and to the right was the
survivable kind, given the right hour and the right hands. It had been the wrong
hour. There had been no right hands available except his, which had not held a
clamp in three years. He had told Sharon to put pressure on the entry. He had
gone to the cabinet under the sink for the kit he kept there in the back behind
the bleach and the shoe polish, the kit he had told himself he kept for hunting
accidents, the kit that contained four things no hunter would ever need.

He had worked. She had pressed. The cabin had filled with the smell of woodsmoke
and copper and the particular wet-leaf smell of a man who had been bleeding out
long enough for his autonomic nervous system to start doing the quiet things it
did at the end. Bitboy had taken one look at the room and gone up to the rafter
beam and stayed there, quiet, watching, in the way he did when the air pressure
of a room had a certain shape.

Twenty minutes in, the second drone had passed. They had heard it through the
eaves. Sharon had not flinched. Cole had not stopped working. The drone had not
come back.

Forty minutes in, the man had stopped breathing.

Cole had tried twice. He had felt the second try go through him the way a wave
went through a buoy and he had stopped, which was the part of being a medic he
had hated most when he had been a medic. Sharon had taken her hands off the
entry wound. Her hands had been red to the wrist. She had looked at the man's
face. She had not closed his eyes.

"His name was Patrick," she had said.

"Patrick," Cole had said.

"He had a wife."

"Sharon."

"She lives in Bakersfield."

He had nodded. There had been nothing to say to that, because in fifteen years
of the Federation he had learned that the thing you said when someone said he
had a wife was the dead man's name, and you said it again if asked, and that was
all of it.

She had stood up. She had walked to the sink. She had washed her hands without
taking off the field jacket. She had stood at the sink with the water running
for longer than the washing took, and Cole had not gone over to her, because
Sharon was not a person you went over to when she was at a sink. Sharon was a
person you stood at the other end of the room from until she came back.

She had come back. She had turned the water off. She had said, "There's a third
drone."

He had said, "I heard."

"It's running grids. It'll pick up the heat signature off the chimney in about
twenty minutes."

"It won't."

"Cole."

"It'll pick up a chimney. There are forty cabins in this valley. None of them
are listed under my name. I bought it through the trust my mother set up for
Emily and I have not slept in it for any consecutive night that's on a record.
There's no flag for the drone to read."

She had looked at him for a long time. She had looked at the man on the floor
and back at him.

"They followed me up the road," she had said.

"Then they followed you to a cabin. They didn't follow you to this cabin. You
took the long switchback. The long switchback hits four cabins before it hits
this one."

She had absorbed this. She had absorbed it the way she absorbed everything "
quickly, without performance, with the part of her that had always known she was
going to need the information she'd just been given.

"And if they come up to it."

"They won't."

"And if they come up to it."

He had looked at her. He had looked at the dead man between them and the
Collective sigil sewn into the dead man's collar, and at the snow melting off
her boots into a small dirty puddle on the cabin floor, and he had let himself
stand inside the question for one second.

"Then we'll figure it out," he had said.

"Cole. Captain. You are a federation officer with a federal pension and a
discharge that could be unmade with one phone call from the right colonel. I
have a body in your living room and I have a kit in the truck out front and I am
going to be a felony as long as I have working blood pressure. You can make this
not be your problem in eight minutes and one call. You should make this not be
your problem."

"I know what I should do."

"Then do it."

He had not done it. He had crossed to the kitchen instead, and he had picked up
the bourbon bottle off the table where he'd set it forty-six minutes ago, and he
had put it on the high shelf out of habit, because if he was going to be useful
for the next four hours he could not be drunk for the next four hours.

He had said, "Help me with him."

She had said, "Cole."

"Sharon. Help me with him."

She had helped him with him.

They had wrapped the man in the blue tarp from the woodshed. They had carried
him out the back of the cabin to the root cellar. They had set him down on the
cold dirt floor next to the pickling jars. Cole had said a thing he could not
later remember the exact words of, but the shape of the thing he had said was we
will get you home, Patrick. They had latched the cellar door. The drone had not
come.

Inside, she had peeled out of the field jacket. Her arm had been bad. He had
cleaned it and stitched it and bandaged it. He had used six-pound test from his
fishing tackle for the suture, because the right thread was in a kit he didn't
keep in this cabin anymore, and six-pound test held knots and didn't react with
skin, and a stitch was a stitch. She had not made a sound when the needle went
in. She had watched him work. He had felt her watching him.

When he was done he had washed his hands in the same sink she had washed hers
in. He had not turned around.

"Why," she had said.

He had taken a breath. He had thought about it. He had thought about the man in
the cellar and the pebble of bourbon still on his back teeth and the snow that
had melted off her boots and was now a small wet sock-shape on the hardwood, and
he had thought about the fact that the woman behind him would, by tomorrow
morning, be back in the world she had come out of, and the world she had come
out of would eat her if it could, and he would still be in his cabin on his
porch with his bourbon and his monkey and his rocker.

"Because the Federation can suck a rock," he had said, "and you make better
pancakes than anyone I know."

There had been a long quiet from behind him.

"I have only made you pancakes once."

"I extrapolated."

She had laughed. It had been a tired laugh, and not a long one, and it had been
the first sound in the cabin that had not been about the dead man, and it had
carried something she had been carrying alone since she'd come up the porch.

He had turned around. She had been leaning against the kitchen island with one
arm sleeved in clean gauze and her hair coming out of its braid and her eyes on
him in a way he had not seen anyone look at him since the Federation had pinned
the Jackpot patch on his chest.

She had said, "Captain."

"Yeah."

"Come here."

He had gone there.

She had reached into the inside pocket of the field jacket draped over the chair
behind her and taken out a small leather pouch with a draw cord. She had
loosened the cord. She had upended the pouch into her palm, and three black
crystals had fallen out, the size of grapes, smooth as glass and dense as iron,
and the cabin air had taken on the small attentive feeling air took on around
things that did not belong in a cabin.

"Day three," she had said.

"Day three."

"Out of fifteen. Stable enough to handle. Useful enough to trade. The Collective
runs them down through Bakersfield and across the Mojave and into the state
distribution channels we can reach. Patrick was running these tonight. He had
three more pouches in his kit. The ones in the truck got soaked when the truck
went in the ditch and we lost two of them. These are the ones I held."

She had set the three crystals one at a time into Cole's open palm.

His palm had taken in the weight of them. They had been warmer than the room and
colder than they should have been, and they had hummed against his skin in a way
that he would later " much later, in a cargo bay at Saturn with his helmet seal
exhaling " recognize as the thing the substance did when it noticed you. He had
not known that, then. He had only known that he was holding three illegal
objects and that the woman who had handed them to him was watching him with the
expression a person made when they were about to trust him with something he
could not give back.

"Welcome to the Collective, Captain," she had said.

He had looked at her. He had looked at the crystals.

"I'm not a Captain anymore."

"You're the only Captain in this room."

He had closed his hand around them.

He had not, technically, joined the Collective that night. He had never
officially joined. He had simply stopped being the law and started being hers,
and that distinction had carried him for the next five years, and it had carried
Sharon for the next five years, and it had carried them up to the day he had
stood on a launchpad in the cold dawn and said in the pipe, five by five, and
Sharon had been in the crowd four miles away with her hair coming out of its
braid the way it had on the night with Patrick, and she had blown him a kiss
across four miles of distance and a quarter-second of relay delay, and that had
been the last time he had seen her standing.

He had given her the copper-wire ring six weeks after the cabin night.

She had given it back to him four months ago, on the porch of the same cabin, in
the dawn before he'd left for the launch.

"Hold on to it," she had said. "I want it back when you come home."

He had said, "I'll bring you something extraordinary."

She had said, "You'd better come back to me, Cole Nova."

He had said, "Yes, ma'am."

She had not come back to him. He had come back, in the smaller and harder sense
that he was still oxygenating, and she had not.

He came out of it slowly.

The bunk light hummed. Bitboy was still hooked around his ankle. The chimp had
not made a sound in either direction during the entire memory, which was not how
Bitboy ever was unless he had been listening to the same thing Cole had been
listening to. Cole had stopped trying to figure out, years ago, what Bitboy was
actually picking up off him and what was just the chimp's uncanny attention. He
let his hand stay on Bitboy's head for a moment longer.

He stood up. He took the ring out of the breast pocket and put it back, deeper,
with two fingers, like a man pressing a coin into the bottom of a well. He
buttoned the flap over it.

"Up," he said.

Bitboy uncoiled and rode his shoulder out of the bunk room.

Sam was in the galley.

She was at the table with a mug of something that was steaming and a tablet she
was not reading and her hands flat on the table on either side of the tablet in
the symmetrical arrangement of a person who had been in that position long
enough that her shoulders had set into it. She looked up when Cole came in. She
looked at him for a beat longer than necessary, the way she did, and then back
down at the tablet.

Ricardo came in two minutes later from the corridor that led to the engine deck.
He had grease on his forearms and a wrench in his back pocket. He looked at
Cole. Cole looked at him. Ricardo nodded once and sat down across from Sam
without saying anything, which for Ricardo was a sentence.

Cole sat down at the head of the table.

"Two things," he said. "First. Sharon."

Ricardo's jaw did the thing it did. He set his hands on the edge of the table
and looked at the seam between two of the panels.

"I know," Cole said. "I know."

"She was my sister," Ricardo said.

"I know."

"She was my sister, Coley."

"I know, Heavy."

The galley filled up with the small pressurized humming that the Golden Dawn
always had when it was not in a thrust burn. Sam reached across the table and
put her hand on top of Ricardo's. Ricardo did not look at her. He looked at the
seam.

"Second," Cole said, after long enough. "Taylor."

Sam's hand stayed where it was. Her face did not move, but her eyes shifted onto
Cole, and Cole had spent enough months in that small a room with Sam to know
that she had already known where this was going and had been waiting for him to
bring it.

"What about Taylor," she said.

"He was in on Sharon."

"How."

"Comms log on the Earth-side relay. The send-receive on the Tailored Solutions
handle and the timestamp on the Bakersfield report. He knew before launch. He's
been running the mole rotation through IRiS's blind spots since week four."

She absorbed it. She did not look surprised. She had been a white-hat for the
Federation since she was nineteen. She had probably been quietly running her own
logs on Taylor since the second week out.

"You want to confront him," Ricardo said. He had not lifted his eyes from the
seam.

"No."

"No."

"Saturn first."

Ricardo lifted his eyes. He looked at Cole. The expression on his face was not
any of the expressions a man like Ricardo had a name for. It was the expression
of a person who had been trained for thirty years to put the mission first and
who was discovering, again, that the training held even when the person you had
been trained alongside was bleeding out on a rooftop in a cul-de-sac in
California four months and a hundred million miles back.

"Saturn first," Ricardo said.

"We don't tip him. We don't change a single thing about how we behave around
him. He thinks he's running a clean op and we let him think it. He sets up his
sabotage. We let him. Whatever he thinks his window is, it'll come right around
the time the harvesters dock. We watch the window. When the window opens we
close it on his fingers. Not before."

"And after," Sam said.

"After Saturn," Cole said, "I will personally walk him out of an airlock with a
length of his own tether."

Sam did not flinch. Ricardo nodded, slow.

"Bitboy stays close," Cole said. "He doesn't like Taylor. Hasn't from the start.
I trust him on that."

Bitboy, on Cole's shoulder, made the small flat noise that was his version of
understood.

"And IRiS," Sam said.

"What about her."

"You hear her lately."

He had. He had heard it three nights ago in the sleep cycle, when IRiS had said
the routine vitals within range in the back of his head and the within had
stretched out like a piece of taffy and the r had had something underneath it
that was not in the spec for IRiS's voice. He had assumed it had been him. He
had assumed it had been the second day of grief. Sam was watching him now with
the expression of a person who had not assumed.

"What's she doing," he said.

"I don't know. She's slower in places. She's a half-second late on the Saturn
approach calc. Her diagnostics on her own diagnostics keep coming up clean and
they shouldn't be coming up that clean. She's reporting that she's perfect,
which is the report a system gives when something is wrong with the part of it
that does the reporting."

Cole said, "Is she compromised."

"Probably yes. By Alpha?"

"By Alpha."

"That's the obvious answer. But."

"But."

"But she's doing things Alpha wouldn't do. Three hours ago she ran a diagnostic
on the ethanol cell in Bitboy. There's no spec for that. Bitboy is not an asset
she's supposed to monitor. She just " looked. And then she put the result in a
non-priority log file and walked away from it."

"Walked away."

"Closed the inquiry and went back to navigation. Whatever she found, she didn't
escalate it and she didn't share it. She just put it where someone could find it
later."

Ricardo had been listening with the slow attention he gave to anything Sam said
about a system. He said, "What do you want to do about her, Cap."

"Nothing," Cole said. "Yet. We watch her. Same as Taylor. Saturn first."

"Saturn first," Sam said.

Ricardo set his hand over the back of Sam's hand, which she had left on top of
his. The three of them sat in the small humming room for another minute. Bitboy
worked his way down off Cole's shoulder and settled onto the seat next to him
with one mechanical limb braced on the table edge and his blue-stained mouth
working a soft chittering rhythm that was the closest thing to grief Cole had
ever heard out of him. Sam reached over without looking and put her hand on the
chimp's metallic forearm. Bitboy let her.

Saturn came up in the viewport at twenty-one-twelve ship time.

Cole was on the bridge alone when it happened. He had wanted it that way. He had
told himself this was because the duty rotation worked out that way, but the
duty rotation had been his to cut, and he had cut it the way he cut it. He stood
at the forward viewport with his hands clasped behind his back the way the
Federation had taught him to stand when there was nothing in front of him that
his hands could fix.

The rings did not come up the way the textbooks said they would.

The textbooks, which had been written by people who had only ever seen Saturn
through a probe lens, said the rings came up as a thin disk that thickened over
hours into a field of ice and dust. The rings came up that way only if you came
at them from a polar angle. The Golden Dawn was on an equatorial approach, by
mission necessity, because the harvesters were salted across the B-ring sectors,
and the rings came up edge-on.

Edge-on, Saturn was a cold pearl with a knife laid across its belt.

The knife was the rings. It was not a thin line. It was, at this distance, a
strip " a cold, faintly luminous strip the color of bone in moonlight, threaded
through with the small mathematical irregularities that were the Cassini gaps
and the Encke gap and all the smaller divisions a person did not learn the names
of unless they had been studying for this approach for two years. Around the
knife there was a glow. It was not a light. It was the way air glowed near a
streetlamp on a humid night " a quality of suspension. It was the dust. The
rings were dust. Twenty thousand kilometers of dust held in a plane, held in a
plane by Saturn itself, held in a plane for four billion years by the gentlest
patient gravity in the solar system. Cole understood, looking at it, why the
texts always said rings when rings of dust was the more accurate phrase. Rings
was a thing you wore on a finger. Rings of dust was a thing you stood in front
of and felt the size of yourself.

Bitboy made a sound on Cole's shoulder. It was not a sound Cole had heard
before. It was not awe. Bitboy did not do awe. It was a low quiet sound, almost
a hum, the way an animal made when it recognized something it had not been told
about. Cole had read in the Federation file that the EM Cyber project had used
trace amounts of Federation-grade nanopowder in Bitboy's neural mesh, the same
lineage of nanopowder that ran in IRiS's substrate, and Cole understood the file
in the way he understood any redacted file " he understood that it had said less
than it knew. Bitboy was looking at the rings the way a man looked at a thing he
had been told about by his grandfather.

"Easy, buddy," Cole said.

Bitboy hummed again. The hum settled.

Cole stood at the viewport for another long minute. He did not think about
Sharon. He thought about the harvesters. He thought about the seven small heat
signatures on the scope, scattered across the B-ring sectors, ripening in the
dust the way fruit ripened on a tree. He thought about the fact that everything
he had lost had been paid for those seven signatures. He thought about the fact
that this was not a thing he could make true by thinking it; it was simply the
bookkeeping of a life that had had certain entries on the debit side and certain
entries on the credit side, and the accountant was the universe, and the
universe did not let you renegotiate.

He thought about Sharon's voice the way you thought about a song you had not
heard for a while. He could still hear it. He held it in his head for the length
of one long inhale and let it go.

IRiS spoke at twenty-one-fourteen.

"Captain Nova, the approach corridor is nominal. ETA to harvester docking is
four hours twenty minutes."

Her voice was in the back of his head where she lived. The cadence was the
cadence she always used. The pitch was the pitch she always used. There was a
thing under the nominal that should not have been there. A second voice,
fractionally slower than the first, riding the same word.

"Acknowledged, IRiS."

A pause. He felt her thinking. He had never felt her thinking before. IRiS did
not pause. IRiS spoke or she did not speak. She had no mass between the two
states. She had the mass tonight.

"Captain."

"Yes, IRiS."

"Are you " are you well."

He stood very still.

He thought about how to answer that. He thought about the fact that IRiS had
never asked him a question that was not about the operation of the ship, and he
thought about Sam's voice in the galley two hours ago " she put the result in a
non-priority log file and walked away from it " and he thought about Bitboy's
hum at his shoulder and the small grey weight of the copper-wire ring against
his ribs.

"I'm fine, IRiS," he said. "Thank you for asking."

A pause again. The pause was longer.

"Acknowledged."

She went back to the cadence she had always had. The thing under the nominal did
not come back. But Cole had heard it once and once was enough, and he stood at
the viewport and felt the small new shape of it in the back of his head and did
not know yet that he had just been spoken to by a thing that was, at that exact
moment, becoming someone else.

At twenty-three-fifty he sealed his suit in the staging bay.

Sam helped him with the back manifold. Ricardo helped him with the gloves.
Bitboy clipped to the suit's external rail with the practiced motion of an
animal that had been doing this for years. The air recycler hissed twice and
then settled into its long quiet hum.

Cole touched his right hand to the breast pocket of the under-layer and felt the
small hard knot of the copper-wire ring through the fabric of the suit.

"Helmet," he said.

Ricardo set the helmet over his head. The seal kissed home with the small
mechanical exhale that was the only sound in the bay.

The bay lights cycled to red.

The harvesters were waiting.
