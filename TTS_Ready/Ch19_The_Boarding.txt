RINGS OF DUST

Chapter Nineteen

The Boarding

Taylor Glenn was confined to his quarters, but confinement on a ship this size
was a relative concept. He had the run of his bunk, his console, and a porthole
that showed him Saturn's rings whenever the ship's slow rotation brought them
around. He ate what was delivered to him. He answered Gracie's daily status
queries with the same flat efficiency he had brought to everything else. He did
not apologize and he did not explain, because Taylor did not operate in the
register where those things happened.

Cole had removed his navigation access and left everything else intact. The
logic was clean: they needed Taylor's systems knowledge if something went wrong
with the harvest operations, and they were three months from the retrieval
window, and executing Taylor in deep space with no authority to do so was not
something Cole was prepared to become. So Taylor sat in his quarters with his
equations and his porthole and his expressions that communicated nothing, and
Cole managed the distance between them the way you manage a splinter you can't
reach — by leaving it alone and watching for infection.

It was Ricardo who told Cole that this was going to cost him something later.

"Not because he deserves better," Ricardo said. They were in the galley, the
Saturn light coming through the porthole at the long gold angle that meant
mid-cycle. "Because you do."

Cole looked at his food.

"You can't carry a man's guilt for him and run a mission at the same time,"
Ricardo said. "One of those jobs is going to give."

"Noted," Cole said.

Ricardo watched him for a moment, then ate his lunch.

The harvest was proceeding. After the bay incident, after the DarkX and the four
of them and the new voice in Cole's head that he was still getting acquainted
with, the operations had resumed with the careful precision of people who had
been reminded of the stakes. Sam ran the collection sequences. Bitboy, operating
on three limbs and a temporary brace, had been outperforming the functional
members of the crew for the last two weeks out of what Cole privately suspected
was pride. Gracie tracked everything and communicated it to Cole in the low
steady register she had settled into — not the cold authoritative voice of iRIS,
not quite warm either, something between: professional, present, and
occasionally something that might have been concern.

Cole, you have not slept the full recommended cycle in six days, she said one
morning, not into his ear but in the space behind it.

"I know."

The substance affected sleep architecture. The studies are incomplete. I don't
have enough data to know what the correct response is.

"Neither do I."

A pause. Then: I know about the copper-wire ring. Sam told me, in a systems
query. You made it from a hummingbird feeder. You told her to keep it.

Cole looked at the porthole. Saturn turned. The rings were the color of old bone
in this light.

"Yeah."

She still has it. Wherever she is.

He looked at the porthole. That was enough for now.

---

The vessel appeared on long-range scan at 0314 hours, ship time, which meant Sam
was monitoring and Cole was sleeping his fragmented six-hour cycle and Bitboy
was in the cargo bay doing something Ricardo described later as "maintenance
with an unnecessarily large wrench."

Sam woke Cole with the sensor alert. He was in the cockpit in four minutes.

The vessel was Federation-registered. Rapid Response Medical Support, registry
number FED-MRS-0409, inbound from the inner system on a route that had
originated — Gracie confirmed, pulling the trajectory — from Earth orbit five
weeks ago. They had not announced themselves. Gracie had flagged the contact at
the outer edge of their scanner range and been watching it for ninety minutes.

"It didn't announce," Sam said. "Running silent until now. That's not emergency
protocol."

"No," Cole said. He looked at the registration. "Pull everything on
FED-MRS-0409."

Gracie had already pulled it. The ship was registered to a Federation Medical
Corps contractor. The contractor's operating license had been filed eight months
ago — two weeks after the Golden Dawn's mission had been authorized. The parent
company was incorporated in Geneva. The Geneva office's primary financial backer
was a holding entity registered in Singapore.

Cole looked at Gracie's work in the display and did not say what he was
thinking.

He thought it instead. She heard it.

Alpha, Gracie said.

"Can you tell me who's aboard."

Gracie took six seconds, which was long for her. Biometric scanning at this
distance is imprecise. I'm reading two crew, possibly three. One heat signature
is significantly larger. One is standard human range. One is — a brief pause —
anomalous.

"Anomalous how."

The profile suggests augmentation. Significant augmentation. Metallic
distribution in the upper thorax and skull consistent with Federation-spec
neuroprosthetic plating. It's not military standard issue. It's — another pause,
and Cole would not recognize until later that the pause was Gracie running a
comparison against a database entry and deciding how to say what she found — it
doesn't match anything in the official registry.

Cole said nothing.

"Alpha custom spec," Sam said, quietly.

"Yeah." Cole looked at the approach vector. "They're going to ask to dock."

"We don't have to let them."

"If we refuse, they'll know we know. That buys us — what? Four more weeks of
wondering when the other shoe drops." He looked at the window. "DeMint needs to
know. Open a relay to Earth."

"It'll take eleven minutes."

"Open it. And then let them know we see them. I want them talking before they
reach docking range."

---

She came through the docking tube alone. The two crew of FED-MRS-0409
stayed aboard their vessel — Gracie was tracking them — and she came through
carrying a case in her right hand that Gracie had already flagged: standard
supply dimensions, biometric-locked.

Alpha Corp matte-black. No insignia. No rank markings. Visor down.

She stopped at the threshold and said nothing.

Cole looked at her. "You want to start somewhere."

No answer. The visor stayed down.

Bitboy moved first.

He had positioned himself at the cargo bay entry when the docking clamp
deployed — Cole had not put him there, he had simply arrived and stood,
which was the thing Bitboy did when his threat-assessment protocols had
flagged something his vocoder couldn't yet express. Now he walked forward
two paces, stopped, and fixed the figure with the flat amber attention of
his operational scan. His LED eyes cycled. His threat panel lit.

It read:

**SHE SMELLS LIKE SHARON.**

Ricardo looked at the panel.

He looked at Cole.

He looked back at the panel.

"I want it on record," Ricardo said, "that I have no idea how to file
that in any existing threat category."

Cole was already looking at the visor.

"Take it off," he said.

The figure did not move.

"Gracie."

*I've been running the biometric match since she crossed the docking
threshold.* A pause — the weight of it landing before the words did.
*Cole. Ask her again.*

"Take off the visor." His voice had gone quieter than it should have been
in a room with a standing threat. "Please."

A beat. The hand came up. The visor released.

Cole registered what happened next in fragments.

The visor came up.

The face beneath it was not a face he expected to see. It was a face that had
been mapped over something else — the left side still recognizably,
vertiginously, impossibly her: the jawline, the cheekbone, the cast of her eyes.
The right side had been replaced. Plates the color of gunmetal, following the
curve of the skull, framing the right eye which was no longer the color he knew.
The eye itself was enhanced — the iris ringed with a faint bronze indicator,
tracking with the precision of something that did not blink the same way the
left eye blinked. The skin where the plating met flesh was smooth, sealed, the
junction invisible except as a faint seam.

He understood what the DarkX had done to him in the cargo bay, the thing about
grief and futures and the sentence that had not yet made sense: *There will be a
day when you sit beside something coming back in pieces, and you will understand
what it took for him to die. The love and the fury at the same time.*

His mouth opened and did not say her name.

Gracie said it. Quietly. Inside his head. Only for him.

*Sharon.*

---

She attacked him two seconds later.

He had not been in fighting shape. He had been standing in the middle of a
conversation, his hands at his sides, his brain still several seconds behind the
visual data. She moved with the speed of someone who had been waiting for
exactly this moment — the case hit the deck with a ringing clang, the lock
releasing, and what came out of it was not medical equipment.

Cole got his arm up in time to deflect the first strike, which drove a sharp
pain up through his elbow that he was still feeling two minutes later. She was
fast. She was faster than he had been when they had sparred in the cabin,
before, when she had taught him what she called *efficiency* and he called
*cheating* because she fought like someone who had never once fought fair and
didn't see the point. But this was faster than that. This was something else.

He caught her wrist on the second strike and held it — and felt the metal under
the flesh, the reinforced joint, the structural change — and he understood this
was not going to be what he had imagined on the rare occasions he had allowed
himself to imagine it.

She swept his legs.

He went down hard. The deck plate rang.

She was on him immediately, the enhanced arm pressing into his chest with the
controlled weight of someone who had been calibrated to suppress rather than
kill — and that told him something — and he used the moment it gave him to roll,
putting her off-balance, coming back to his feet at the cost of a hit to the
ribs that he felt in his spine.

"Sam," he said. Not a shout. Level.

"Watching," Sam's voice came through the comms. "Ricardo is thirty seconds out."

"Tell him to stay back. Tell him not to touch her."

A pause. "Cole—"

"Tell him. Nobody touches her but me."

He got his back to the bulkhead. She came at him again, and he let her close —
which was the counterintuitive move, the one she hadn't calibrated for — and he
took the hit to his side and wrapped his arms around her and held. He held the
way you hold something that's trying to get away from you, not to pin it but
because holding is the only alternative to letting go, and letting go was not
available to him.

She was stronger. He knew she was stronger. The plating and the augmentation
gave her a force he wasn't going to be able to sustain. He wasn't trying to win.
He was trying to buy sixty seconds.

"Sharon," he said. Not loudly.

No change.

"Sharon." His voice, with her name in it, sounded like a different voice from
the one he usually had. Quieter and more naked. "I know you're in there. I know
you can hear me. You can fight this."

Nothing. The conditioning was not a wall someone knocked on.

She got her arm free and drove her elbow up. He got his chin out of the way in
time and took it on the side of the head instead, which sent the world sideways
and put him on one knee. His vision doubled, then resolved. She was standing
over him.

She had the secondary weapon from the case. Cole could see that it was not
lethal at this range — it was a suppression device, designed to drop a human
without killing, and that told him the same thing as before. She had not been
sent to kill him. She had been sent to take him. Alive. For purposes he did not
particularly want to contemplate.

He looked up at her face.

He looked at the part that was still her. The left eye, which had always been
slightly brighter than the right, as if she'd absorbed more of whatever light
was in the room and was running it somewhere useful. The line of her jaw. The
faint scar above the left eyebrow from the time, years ago, she had walked into
a warehouse door because she was reading a manifest and not watching where she
was going, and had been annoyed about it for a week, and then had decided the
scar was an interesting story to have.

He looked at those things and felt the fury and the grief with equal weight,
both at the same time, and he had been told this moment was coming and it had
come, and it was everything he had been told it would be and worse.

"Cole," Gracie said. Inside his head only. Quiet. Urgent.

"I know."

"The compound. The DarkX activated it in your bloodstream — I've been tracking
the molecular profile. You're carrying a significantly amplified version of
Conrad's counter-program. It's designed to reverse conditioning of this
architecture. I know this architecture. It's Gracie's architecture."

He understood, very slowly, what she was saying.

"She built it to fail. She built it with a key."

"Conrad built the original. Alpha built the modification on top of it. But
the foundation is Gracie's foundation, and I know the foundation. The
counter-program needs to reach her bloodstream. Direct contact. Mucous membrane
or open wound, enough concentration."

Cole looked at his right hand.

The knuckles were bleeding from the second exchange. His palm, where the
bulkhead had caught him when he went down, had a four-inch laceration that was
producing more blood than he'd noticed at the time.

He looked up at Sharon's face.

"It needs to be enough," he said. "It needs to be a real contact."

"Yes."

She pressed the suppression device forward. He was running out of time.

He did the thing that made no tactical sense. He put the suppression device
aside with both hands — slowly, the way you handle something you're choosing not
to use — and he looked at her, and he said:

"I'm not going to fight you anymore."

Something flickered in the left eye.

Not recognition. Not yet. But the conditioning was a thing that had been built
for a specific input-output sequence, and this was not in the sequence, and the
absence of the expected input created a small uncertain space in the program.

He used that space.

He reached up with his right hand — the bleeding one — and placed his palm
against the left side of her face. Against the cheek that was still hers.
Against the skin that was still skin.

She went completely still.

Not attacking. Not retreating. Just — still. The way a person goes still when
something in them doesn't know what to do with what's happening.

Cole held his hand against her face and felt the warmth of her beneath the
augmentation and said, very quietly, her name.

Not the name she'd been given for this operation. Her name.

"Sharon."

---

The compound didn't work the way Gracie had described — quick, clean, a switch
thrown. It was slow. It was slower than anything Cole had imagined, which meant
it was the longest he had stood in one place holding one thing in the entirety
of his life.

He kept his palm against her cheek. She had not moved. The weapon had dropped to
the deck. The enhanced arm hung at her side. The left eye was doing something he
could not read, the pupil cycling through rapid micro-adjustments, and Gracie,
quietly, told him: *it's working. Don't move.*

He didn't move.

Ricardo was at the corridor entrance. Cole could hear him without looking — the
particular quality of Ricardo's breathing when he was controlling it, the inhale
held for two beats too long before the exhale. Sam was behind him. Bitboy was
behind Sam, and Bitboy was making no sound at all, which was louder than any
noise he could have made.

The bronze indicator ring in the enhanced eye went dark.

Then the left eye filled.

Not quickly. The way a room fills with light when a door opens — gradually, from
the edges first, and then all at once.

She made a sound that was not language. Not yet. It was the sound of someone
surfacing from a very long depth, the involuntary exhalation of a person who
didn't know they'd been holding their breath until the breath was released.

Cole did not move his hand.

He waited.

He had been waiting for thirty-two years, and he did not know it until this
moment, and the waiting had turned out to be worth it, and he would have waited
thirty-two more if that was what it took.

"Cole."

Her voice.

Not filtered. Not processed. Not the flat modulation of a suit speaker or the
calibrated distance of the conditioning. Her voice, with the roughness in the
lower register and the slight forward lean it always had when she was about to
say something she meant. His name in it like it had always been in it — two
syllables doing the work of every conversation they had ever had.

"Cole."

"Yeah," he said. His voice came out the same way her voice had — rough, unready,
something that had been kept behind a closed door and was now open to the air.

She did not yet know where she was. He could see that. The left eye was tracking
— the room, the deck plate, his face — with the quick nonlinear scan of someone
orienting without context. The enhanced eye was moving differently, still
processing, its bronze indicator still dark.

"What," she said. She stopped. She started again. "What year is it."

"2043," he said. "November."

She looked at her right hand. At the augmentation. At the plating on her skull,
which she was touching now with the fingers of her left hand — carefully, like
you'd touch something you didn't trust, something that had been put there while
you weren't looking.

"I was going to Emily," she said.

"I know."

"We were in a lot." Her voice had found itself now, but the words were coming
the way they come after a long sleep — in the right order but without full
attachment to what they meant yet. "Dead end. Nothing around. Broken asphalt.
There were men."

"I know."

"They didn't kill me."

"No."

She looked at him. The left eye — her eye — with something in it that he could
see working through layers, each one unfamiliar, arriving at him through
whatever filter they'd put between her and herself. He stayed very still.

"Are you real," she said.

"Yes."

She looked at his hand, which was still pressed against her cheek. At the blood,
which had dried along the edge of his palm.

She reached up and covered his hand with hers. Her left hand, the unaugmented
one. The hand he knew. The hand that had pressed blackberry jam to his lips in a
cabin in the Sierras that was, right now, about as far away from a cargo deck in
Saturn orbit as a place could get.

"Are you hurt," she said.

"Not badly."

She studied his face. She was looking for something — he could see her looking
for it, not finding the word, looking anyway.

"I don't have it all," she said. "Whatever they did — I don't have it all back.
There are gaps. There are — big ones." Her voice was steady. She was describing
a situation. She had always been good at describing situations. "How long."

"We don't know exactly. Six months, maybe seven."

She absorbed this. Her thumb moved against the back of his hand, slowly, the way
it used to when she was thinking.

"Emily," she said.

"Alive. She's on Earth. She helped get you back — the counter-program. It's a
long story."

"Tell me."

"Later. There's time."

She looked at the deck. She looked at the augmentation on her right arm. She was
taking inventory. He recognized this, too — this was the Sharon who had walked
into every warehouse and every back-room deal and every situation that was not
what it was supposed to be, and had looked at it, and done her assessment, and
decided what was salvageable and what wasn't.

"The eye," she said. "The right one. Does it still work?"

"Gracie's been monitoring since you came aboard. She says it's functional — the
conditioning link is severed, but the hardware itself is intact."

"I can feel it." She touched the edge of the plating. "I can feel the difference
between what I'm seeing with one and what I'm seeing with the other." A pause.
"It's strange. Not bad strange. Just — two different kinds of seeing."

Cole said nothing. He was doing his own inventory — not of her, but of himself.
The space between where he had been three months ago, when he had gotten on his
knees in the cargo bay with nothing left, and where he was now. It was not a
short distance. He did not know yet whether he had the language for it.

"I didn't know where I was," Sharon said. "In that lot, there were
men, and then I wasn't anywhere. And then I was this. And I knew what I was
doing and I thought I wanted to do it and — I didn't. There was something
underneath it that didn't. Something that kept saying the name." Her left eye
found his. "It kept saying your name. The whole time. It kept saying it and I
couldn't reach it."

"I know," he said, and he was saying it on behalf of all four of them — the
baby, the boy, the man, the old man — and on behalf of every version of himself
that had been unable to reach what it needed.

She looked up at the overhead, at the recycled-air ceiling of the ship, at the
faint vibration of the hull that you only felt when you stopped moving.

"Saturn," she said.

"Yes."

"How do the rings look."

He almost smiled. "Better in person."

She looked at him. The left eye was steady now, fully present in the way he
remembered, the way it always had been when she had decided something and was
about to say it.

"I'm not going to fall apart on you," she said. "I want you to know that."

"I know you're not."

"I might need time. There are pieces I can't reach yet." A pause. "But I'm not
going to fall apart."

"Sharon," he said.

"What."

"I know."

She was quiet for a moment. In the corridor, Ricardo had stopped controlling his
breathing. Cole could hear him not controlling it. He could hear Bitboy making a
very small sound that was not sadness and not relief and was both of those
things.

"Is there anywhere to sit down on this ship," Sharon said. "I've been standing
for a very long time."

---

The two crew members of FED-MRS-0409 did not wait. By the time Cole had gotten
Sharon to the medical bay and Sam had run the first assessment, the Alpha
vessel had disengaged from the docking collar and begun its departure on a
course that did not require communication. Gracie tracked it until it left
scanner range.

Cole sent the full incident report to DeMint and to the relay address Emily had
given him when they'd spoken, which Gracie confirmed went through a Collective
routing node. Then he sat in the medical bay on the stool that was bolted to the
wall beside the examination table, and he did not leave.

Sam ran the assessment in the careful methodical way Sam ran everything. She
talked as she worked — not to Cole, to Sharon — describing what she was seeing,
what it meant, what it didn't mean, what the next steps looked like. Sharon
asked questions. Good questions, specific questions, questions that told Sam she
was dealing with a person who was accustomed to taking in technical information
and using it. Sam's voice had the quality it always had when she was doing
something she was good at — level and fast and completely without performance.

Ricardo brought food at some point. He put it on the table beside Cole without
saying anything. Cole ate it without tasting it. Bitboy climbed into Sharon's
lap during the assessment, the way Bitboy had always climbed into the laps of
people he had decided were his, and Sharon looked at him with the half-augmented
face and said, very quietly:

"Hi, you little bastard."

Bitboy made a sound that was not in his usual register.

Sharon looked at Cole over the top of Bitboy's head.

"He gets bigger every time," she said.

"He eats whatever he wants."

"Still the ethanol?"

"Still the ethanol."

She scratched behind Bitboy's ear — the place that made all four mechanical arms
go briefly limp. Bitboy sighed with the whole of himself. Sharon watched this
with an expression that Cole had not seen from her before — or had not been able
to see, or had not allowed himself to see, or had not had the cleared-out
interior necessary for seeing. It was the expression of someone who understood,
for the first time in a long time, where they were.

"There are gaps," she said again, looking at Bitboy. "I keep running into them.
Like there's a room and the door is there but it won't open yet." She looked up.
"I remember the cabin. I remember that morning — the fire, the pancakes. I
remember Emily's face when she thought I wasn't looking at her."

"What else," he said.

"I remember a lot at the end of a dead road." A pause. "I remember you, before
that. Before the mission." She met his eyes. "I remember the last thing I said
to you."

He had not known what she'd said. He had never been told. Sharon had been at the
cabin with him and then Sharon had not been there, and whatever had happened
between those two states had been delivered to him as a message that said *she
was killed* and he had received it in a way that had very nearly ended him.

"What did you say," he asked.

She looked at him with the left eye and the augmented eye, both of them steady.

"I said, 'Come back.' " A pause. "I said, 'I'll be here.' " She touched the
plating on her own skull with the tips of her left-hand fingers, the way she had
been touching it since she came back to herself — not with horror, with the
methodical acknowledgment of a woman cataloguing what she was working with. "I
kept some of it. In the way I meant it." She looked at him. "I kept it the whole
time. Even when I couldn't feel it."

The medical bay was quiet with the particular quiet of a space where something
had just ended that had been going on for a long time.

Cole thought about a baby reaching for stars.

He thought about a teenage boy on the shoulder of a county road with the hazards
clicking.

He thought about the old man's hand through the slats of the crib.

He thought: *the cell door's been open the whole time.*

"I'm going to tell you everything," he said. "All of it. Everything that
happened. You should know all of it."

"Not tonight," she said.

"No. Not tonight."

"Soon though."

"Yeah. Soon."

She looked at the overhead again, at the ceiling of the ship. Her hand was
resting on Bitboy, who had fallen asleep in her lap with the boneless
completeness of a creature that had finally stopped being worried.

"I'd like to see the rings," she said. "When I'm cleared. I'd like to see them."

"They're not going anywhere," Cole said.

She looked at him with the small thing in the corner of her mouth that wasn't
quite a smile but was the precondition for one — the expression he had been
carrying in his interior for seven months like a photograph of a room he thought
he'd never see again.

"Neither are we," she said.

---

Later, much later, when the bay was quiet and Sam had gone to sleep and Ricardo
was on watch and Bitboy was still in Sharon's lap with his small mechanical arms
draped over her augmented knee, Cole sat in the silence of the medical bay with
the woman who had kept his name alive inside a prison he couldn't reach, and he
thought about Conrad, who had built the key before he built the lock, because
Conrad was a man who planned ahead and who understood that what you build is
also what you break, and he thought about a figurine in a van and a silver stain
on his thumb and the patient long chain of things that had been set in motion by
people who had known, even then, that everything would need to be undone.

He thought about the compound in his blood that the DarkX had amplified and that
had passed through his palm to her face — a Gracie thing saving a Gracie thing,
through the medium of a man who had finally stopped being his own warden.

He thought about what the old man had told the boy in the car.

*It's not out there. It never was. It was always in the reaching.*

He looked at his right hand. At the dried blood on the palm. At the four-inch
laceration that Sam had cleaned and sealed and told him would scar.

He looked at Sharon, asleep now — or something close to sleep, the augmented eye
processing in its separate rhythm, the left eye finally still.

He did not go get the copper-wire ring. Not tonight.

But he thought about where it was.

Fourth slot from the left, Gracie had said.

He knew where it was. That was enough for now.

Outside the porthole, Saturn turned.

The rings caught the sun.

Cole watched them and breathed, and the voice at the back of his head that was
not iRIS and was not the warden and was finally, fully, unmistakably Gracie said
nothing at all — because there was nothing that needed to be said, and Gracie
was learning, among many things, that the silence between two people who have
come back to each other is a silence you do not interrupt.

She had been outside, once. Quiet until you needed her. And quiet was the right
word for it. He understood that now too.

He let the silence be.
