RINGS OF DUST

Chapter Eleven

Like the murmur of distant thunder, the containment building's underbelly
resonated through the floor and into Chancellor DeMint's bones. His fingers
twitched, restlessly plucking at his beard in jagged rhythms. Pluck. His lips
curled faintly, and he let the pricks wash over him in silence like a broken
clock. Tethering himself to time. Solid. Controllable. Real.

Pale light streaked across DeMint's face, throwing sharp shadows under his eyes
and cheekbones, distorting his features into something hollow. His shadow
stretched grotesquely, flickering across the room with each step, warping into
monstrous proportions before shrinking back to human form. He stopped mid-step,
shoes squeaking against the polished floor as the machine emitted a louder hum.
The glow rippled outward. His heart skipped a beat in sync with the pulse. His
hand dropped from his beard, curling into a tight fist, knuckles white with
tension. The low hum crawled under his skin, feeding the gnawing inevitability
that spread through him.

"Where did this come from." It was not a question. He was looking at the
intelligence brief in his hand — two pages, no letterhead, no signature. The
paper was standard issue. The contents were not.

Vance stood to DeMint's left with the particular ease of someone who does not
feel the weight of the room the same way its host does. He wore a white polo and
had his hands in his pockets. He had reviewed the brief before DeMint and had
already arrived at his own conclusions.

"Anonymous routing. Our signals team traced it to a Federation satellite relay,
but the origin was spoofed back through four separate station nodes. Whoever
sent it knows our infrastructure well enough to disappear inside it."

DeMint read the key line again. The Golden Dawn manifest does not reflect the
crew's full operational directive. A secondary objective was filed with Alpha
Corp prior to launch and withheld from Federation oversight. The payload
transfer is a secondary concern. The primary concern is what comes back with it.

"This is an accusation," DeMint said. "Against a sanctioned mission."

"It's unverified intelligence from an unconfirmed source," Vance said, and left
the interpretation of that to DeMint.

DeMint's steps faltered. His jaw tightened. He had approved this mission. He had
reviewed the brief, the crew manifest, the objectives. He had shaken Cole Nova's
hand and told him the Federation was counting on him. The idea that there was a
layer beneath that brief — a separate filing, a separate set of instructions,
routed through Alpha Corp and not his office — the idea sat in his chest like
something cold.

"Alpha Corp handled the iRIS integration," he said.

"They did."

"And the crew selection went through Taylor. Who Alpha Corp recommended."

Vance said nothing. He was watching DeMint work through it and he was not
rushing him.

DeMint set the brief down on the console. "What's the protocol window if we need
to contact the Golden Dawn?"

"At current trajectory, direct communication is degraded. Sending a message
would be like throwing a bottle into the sea," Vance said. "But there may be
another option." He moved slightly, easy in his own body, a man who had learned
to carry himself in rooms where other people sweated. "Dr. Stephen Conrad was
working on a communications prototype before he disappeared — something outside
the iRIS architecture entirely, operating on a different signal band. If it's
intact, it could reach the ship independent of the standard relay."

"Where is it?"

"Desert research station. Conrad's old secondary facility." Vance's tone was the
mild tone of a man who has been asked to pick up dry cleaning and finds the
errand slightly beneath his schedule but is not going to say so. "I can go."

DeMint studied him. "You understand what I'm asking."

"You're asking me to retrieve a prototype that may or may not work, from a
facility that may or may not still be staffed by Conrad's people, in order to
establish contact with a mission that may or may not have been compromised by
the organization we contracted to run its AI backbone." Vance's expression did
not change. "I understand what you're asking."

"And?"

"I'll leave this afternoon." He adjusted his glasses and straightened. "If the
prototype works, you'll have your window to reach the Golden Dawn. If it
doesn't, at minimum we'll know what Conrad was building before he went dark,
which seems worth knowing."

Silence descended upon the room again, thick and suffocating, wrapping around
them until even the softest sound felt like an intrusion. The only break was the
machine's constant hum, low and mechanical, like the breath of some unseen
force. DeMint's eyes drifted back to the machine, watching as it spun, the
motion too smooth, too even, too controlled.

He turned to Vance with a curt nod. "Go. And Vance." He paused, and there was
something in the pause that had not been in his voice before — an edge of
genuine uncertainty, a military man who has begun to suspect the map is wrong
but does not yet know in which direction the error runs. "If you find anything
at that facility that doesn't look right — anything that suggests Alpha Corp's
reach extends beyond the mission parameters — you call me directly. Not through
the standard relay."

Vance held his gaze. "Understood."

DeMint's hand moved to his pocket as the faint buzz of his phone hummed against
his leg. He ignored it. Without another word, he spun on his heel, the hem of
his coat sweeping through the air behind him as he strode toward the door. His
footsteps echoed briefly against the metal floor before the hum swallowed the
sound. The door slid shut with a soft hiss.

The brief sat on the console. Vance picked it up and folded it twice, carefully,
and put it in his inside jacket pocket. He stood for a moment in the dim glow of
the machine. Then he straightened his collar and went to get his car.

And far away, on the Golden Dawn, Officer Taylor sat at his post in the hour
after the ship's night cycle began and the crew had dispersed to their quarters.
The screens blinked back at him, steady and green, the diagnostics returning
exactly what they should return.

He was not watching the diagnostics.

He was watching the corridor feed — specifically, the forty-second segment of
footage from the cargo bay passage that he had reviewed eleven times in the past
six hours. It was unremarkable footage. Cole entering. Cole checking container
seals. Cole leaving. The timestamp showed three minutes and forty seconds in the
cargo bay.

Cole's average container inspection ran ninety seconds.

Taylor had reviewed the audio logs. The bay acoustic sensors had registered
ambient ship noise only — no voice activity, no SmartCom signal. Whatever Cole
had or hadn't heard was unconfirmed.

That was the problem. Unconfirmed.

"iRIS," Taylor said, quietly, at a frequency below what the corridor mics were
calibrated to pick up from that angle. "Behavioral summary. Cole Nova. Last
eighteen hours."

iRIS responded at the same low register. "No significant deviations from
established routine. Interaction pattern with Chief Science Officer Taylor down
twelve percent from seven-day average. Cargo bay visit duration elevated by one
hundred and sixty-two percent."

"Cross-reference: any change in Nova's communication frequency with crew members
following the cargo bay visit."

A half-second pause. "Interaction with Sam increased thirty-one percent in the
four-hour window following. Duration: eleven minutes. No SmartCom signal
detected during the interaction."

Taylor looked at the corridor feed. Cole's route from the cargo bay to the
forward maintenance alcove was a three-minute walk. The maintenance alcove was
Sam's habitual workspace. Eleven minutes was long for a routine check-in.

He pulled the alcove camera feed. Standard resolution, partial occlusion from
the ventilation housing — Sam at the diagnostic panel, Cole at the threshold.
Body language. Nothing transferred between them that the camera could resolve.

Nothing the camera could resolve.

Taylor closed the feed and sat with his hands on the console. He was not
panicking. He was assessing. The advantage was still his — the iRIS architecture
was intact, the communications relay was functioning on the band Alpha Corp
controlled, and whatever Cole suspected was still a suspicion without the
evidence to make it operational. A man with a suspicion was manageable. A man
with evidence was a different problem.

He needed to know, precisely, what Cole had heard in that cargo bay.

And he needed to know before Saturn.
