RINGS OF DUST

Chapter Three-F

The Pull


The twenty-third day.

The message from the routing broker had been sitting in her queue for twelve days. She had been reading it every morning and not answering it for twelve days. It was a contract notice — a cargo run, Titan to Callisto, the kind of job she took four or five times a year, standard pay, nothing remarkable about it except that the pickup window was closing in forty-eight hours and if she missed it she lost the contract and the broker would note the miss and the next contract would be harder to get.

She read it again. She closed the queue. She went to find Tyler.

---

He was at the east end of the tunnel, near the membrane. He was often there in the mornings — not doing anything, just standing near it. She had stopped finding this strange somewhere around day twelve.

"I'm leaving tomorrow," she said.

He looked at her. "Okay."

"The contract window closes in two days. If I miss it I lose the booking."

"I understand."

She had expected him to argue. He didn't argue. He had never once, in twenty-three days, tried to keep her. She understood now that this was part of what made him difficult to dismiss — he was not recruiting. He was simply here, and he was not surprised when people left, and he was not surprised when they came back.

"The gold," she said.

"Yes."

"I can't explain it."

"No," he said. "I know."

"Petra's explanation is physics. Your explanation is — something else."

"Both explanations produce the same prediction," he said. "The asteroid arrives on the eleventh day. It's always gold. The more we send, the more comes back." He was quiet. "The question of why has two possible answers. The physics answer says: the void redirects inbound asteroid traffic along a specific vector and gold-bearing asteroids happen to cluster in that approach corridor. The other answer says: the void receives what we give it and sends back something of equivalent value in a different form." He looked at the membrane. "Elsa spent the last years of her life trying to determine which answer was true. She died before she decided."

"What do you think?"

He was quiet for a long time.

"I think the physics answer is correct," he said. "I think the gold is exactly what Elsa's orbital calculations predict. I think there is a mechanism and it is real and it does not require anything supernatural to function." He paused. "I also think that mechanism is not an accident. I think the void has the specific gravitational signature it has because something put it there. I think the cells interact with it the way they interact with it because the same something built that interaction into them." He looked at her. "I think there is a difference between understanding the mechanism and understanding the intention. Physics explains the mechanism. The other answer explains the intention."

"You don't know that."

"No," he said. "I believe it."

She looked at the membrane.

"Elsa left notebooks for a granddaughter who hasn't come," she said.

"Yet," Tyler said.

"You think she's coming."

"Elsa thought so." He was quiet. "I think Elsa was right about most things."

---

She left the next morning.

She ran the departure checklist the way she always ran it — methodical, unhurried, the way you do things when you've been doing them alone for long enough that the ritual of the list is its own kind of company. She filed the departure notice with the colony's docking registry. She disengaged the clamp.

Tyler was not at the docking bay. She had not expected him to be.

Yun was there. She stood at the bay entrance with her hands in her pockets and watched Jill run the pre-launch sequence.

"Will you come back," Yun said.

"I don't know," Jill said. Which was honest. She had said this to herself a hundred times in the last week and the answer was always the same: I don't know. Not yes. Not no.

She launched.

---

For the first two hours she ran the Hound's Tooth on autopilot and sat in the back with the lights low and thought about nothing in particular.

Then she thought about the gold crater on the face of the asteroid. The spectrometric reading. The way Petra had said *Jackpot* in the specific tone of someone who has stopped being surprised by a thing without stopping being troubled by it.

She thought about Elsa Klinge's notebooks, sealed in a case in a supply room in an asteroid she was currently moving away from at eleven kilometers per second.

She thought about what Tyler had said: *I think there is a difference between understanding the mechanism and understanding the intention.*

She thought about the woman buried in the asteroid wall who had come out here with eleven people and fourteen years of research and a belief she had carried from her family through her work to the furthest place she could reach. Who had written, three days before she died, about energy moving forward through lines.

She did not know — could not have known — that the belief Elsa Klinge had carried out here was the same belief that had moved through a different line, in a different direction, and become something else entirely. That somewhere on Earth, in an office on the thirty-second floor of a building in Las Vegas, a man named Marcus Verguld had been raised by a father who had been raised by a grandfather who had received the same belief from a woman in the eastern lowlands and turned it into a system for accumulation rather than release. That the grandmother and the grandfather had drunk from the same well and carried what they found there in opposite hands.

She did not know that the shortage she had been flying around for four years — the managed scarcity, the eleven-day harvest windows, the controlled drip of an energy supply that could have been abundant — was the same belief expressed as policy.

She did not know any of this. She only knew that on the eighteenth day a gold asteroid had arrived from the same impossible vector it always arrived from, and on its face there was a crater that nobody could explain, and she had left anyway because the routing broker was waiting and the contract window was closing and she had a ship and a life and a trade circuit to run.

She would think about the gold crater for a long time.

She would not be able to stop.
