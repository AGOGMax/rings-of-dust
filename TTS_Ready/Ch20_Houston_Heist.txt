RINGS OF DUST

Chapter Twenty

Houston Heist

Emily was at the east entrance at 6:22.

She had been awake since four. Not because the alarm woke her — she had been
awake for two hours before it, lying in the motel dark with the road atlas on
her chest and the particular quality of silence that precedes something you've
been moving toward for two thousand miles. She had gotten up, done the stretches
the neurologist had recommended for mornings after long drives, taken her
medication, eaten the second granola bar from the bag's zippered pocket, and sat
at the edge of the bed for a moment with her hands on her knees.

She thought about Sharon checking the car before getting in. She thought about
Orin's shoes, and how he'd said your body is running on fumes without making it
sound like concern, which was somehow more useful than concern. She thought
about the three hours of sleep she'd gotten in a gas station parking lot east of
Indio, and the three-hour alarm that had gone off into the desert quiet.

She thought: I've already done the hard part twice. Once more.

Orin was at the entrance when she arrived, leaning against the maintenance
access door with the canvas jacket and the posture of a man who had been waiting
in visible spaces for a long time without being noticed. He did not comment on
her being eight minutes early.

"Our contact's already inside," he said. "Name's Pilar. She's a federation
systems technician — legitimate clearance, twelve-year employee,
Collective-adjacent since the patent wars. She's going to walk you in. I'll be
at the loading dock." He looked at the door. "The approach to Vance is yours.
Pilar can get you close, but she can't be in the room."

"Why not."

"Because she's been a ghost for twelve years and doesn't want to stop being
one." He looked at her. "And because Rachel thought you should do it."

"That's not a reason."

"It's the reason Rachel gave." A pause. "In my experience, when Rachel gives a
reason like that, she's seen something in a person that the person hasn't seen
yet."

Emily looked at the maintenance door.

"Vance Black," she said. "Tell me what you know."

"Operations, Federation Council Command. Fifteen years of impeccable service,
all of it inside the machine without knowing who was running it. Spent two weeks
at a Hall of Records facility in Nevada looking for evidence against Alpha Corp.
Found an empty vault. Found Rachel Vayes's authorization on the removal orders,
which means he now knows the situation is more complicated than he thought." A
pause. "He was shot. Marcus Verguld, concealed revolver, at a penthouse meeting.
He drove himself to a clinic in Venice Beach and told them he'd been mugged. He
discharged himself against medical advice the next morning."

Emily looked at him. "He survived."

"He drove himself to a clinic."

"What does he want."

"I think he wants to know whether anything he spent fifteen years building was
real." Orin looked at the door. "That's usually where people like Vance end up.
At the point where the ledger stops adding up and they have to figure out what
they actually believe." A pause. "He's in Building C, east wing, third-floor
conference room. He has a Federation briefing at nine. You have a window."

Pilar was inside the maintenance corridor — small, quick-eyed, dressed in
Federation Systems gray with an ID badge that Collective technical had made five
years ago and updated annually. She shook Emily's hand once, briefly, and turned
to walk. Emily followed. They moved through two service corridors, a badge
reader that Pilar handled without breaking stride, and a freight elevator that
deposited them in the third-floor mechanical access passage.

Pilar stopped at a ventilation access panel near a restroom door.

"Conference room is through there, past the restrooms, first door on the left.
He's alone in there until eight-thirty — that's his assistant's estimated
arrival window." She handed Emily a small device — flat, the size of a deck of
cards, with a single indicator light. "That's the copier. He'll have the case.
You open the case, place this on the prototype surface, hold for twenty seconds.
The light goes green. The prototype is intact in the case, the duplicate is in
the device." She looked at Emily. "He has to open the case. We can't do it from
outside — it's biometric."

"So either he opens it, or I steal it."

"Yes."

Emily looked at the device.

"Rachel said voluntarily," she said.

Pilar looked at her with the expression of someone who had been a ghost for
twelve years and had learned to keep her opinions out of her face. "Pilar says
you're going in there with twenty minutes and no backup and a man who doesn't
know you're coming. What it comes out as is up to you." She stepped back. "I'll
be here when you come out."

The conference room had the standardized quality of every Federation facility
Emily had ever seen — blue-gray walls, the council logo embossed on the display
screen, a table that was too long for one person. Vance Black was at the far end
of it, his briefcase closed on the table in front of him, a cup of coffee at his
elbow, reading something on a tablet with the focused attention of a man who was
using work to avoid thinking.

He looked up.

He was younger in person than she had expected. The white polo was the same as
her brother had described from the launch briefing, but there were dark circles
under his eyes that suggested the clinic in Venice Beach had not been the last
difficult night he'd had. His right shoulder sat slightly lower than his left in
the way shoulders do when there's scar tissue pulling at something underneath.

Emily closed the door behind her.

"Mr. Black," she said.

He did not move for his phone. He did not move at all. He watched her with the
particular stillness of someone who had been startled before and had learned to
wait before reacting.

"You're not my assistant," he said.

"No."

"And you're not Federation Systems." He looked at her ID badge — she had none —
and at her clothes — civilian — and at the device in her hand. His eyes moved
from her face to the device and back. "Emily Nova," he said.

She had not given her name. She kept her face even.

"Your brother is on the Golden Dawn," Vance said. He said it as an
acknowledgment, not an accusation. "You're the sister. The Collective's sister."
A pause. "Rachel sent you."

"I'm here because there's a counter-program in that case that can reach my
brother before someone kills him."

Vance looked at the briefcase. He set his tablet face-down on the table.

"And how, specifically," he said, "do you propose to get it."

"I was hoping you'd open the case."

He was quiet for a moment. He looked at the far wall, at the council logo, at
the long empty table. He picked up his coffee and drank from it. He set it back
down.

"I found an empty vault," he said. He was not talking to her. He was talking to
the wall, or to the coffee cup, or to the fifteen years that had deposited him
in this room with a man's bullet in his shoulder and a briefcase full of
evidence that nobody had told him how to use. "Drove six hours into the Nevada
desert because I thought there was something there. The transfers were already
done. The trucks were already gone. Rachel Vayes's clearance on the removal
orders. Rachel Vayes — she's on the news. She's on the Federation board." A
pause. "I called Marcus to tell him what I'd found. I thought — I don't know
what I thought. I thought Marcus would want to know." He looked at his right
shoulder. "He knew. He had already known for a very long time."

Emily did not say anything.

"I drove myself to a clinic," Vance said. "I sat in that car for twenty minutes
first, because I was trying to figure out whether any of it was worth
continuing." He looked at her finally. His eyes were the eyes of a man who had
done that calculation and arrived somewhere specific. "I don't know what Rachel
Vayes is. I don't know what she's building. I don't know what the Collective is,
exactly, or whether it's the solution or just another thing that thinks it is."
He paused. "But I know what Alpha Corp is. I know what Marcus is. And I know
what this case contains, and I know what it can do if it gets into the right
hands."

He put his palm on the briefcase's biometric reader.

The lock disengaged.

The case opened.

Inside was a device Emily had not seen before — slim, matte gray, the size of a
hardcover book, with a port array along one edge and a status light that read
steady green. Conrad's design was recognizable in the architecture the way a
handshake is recognizable — the same proportions, the same logic. She could see
what it was built to do.

She placed the copier flat against the prototype surface. The indicator light
blinked yellow. She counted.

At twenty, it went green.

She removed the copier and put it in her jacket. The prototype remained in the
case. She looked at Vance.

"You should close it," she said.

He closed it. His hand rested on the lid.

"What happens now," he said.

"The counter-program reaches the Golden Dawn. My brother comes home." She
paused. "What happens to you depends on what you decide to do next."

"I have a briefing in two hours."

"I know."

He looked at the case. He looked at the window — the early Houston light coming
through at the low angle of morning, the Federation complex spreading out below
in its organized grid.

"The briefing is with DeMint," he said. "He doesn't know about any of this. He
sent me here in good faith." A pause. "I think he's one of the ones who doesn't
know."

"He is," Emily said. She thought of Rachel at the pier, the water, the
Federation is not our ally in this, they think they are, but Marcus has spent
fifteen years making sure they aren't. "He's in a box, same as you were. He just
hasn't found the empty vault yet."

Vance nodded very slowly. He looked like a man who had been handed more
information than he could process in one sitting and had decided to be
systematic about it.

"Tell me what to say," he said.

She thought about Orin's shoes. She thought about Rachel: someone will be there.
She thought about Sharon saying I'll be here to Cole in a parking garage four
months ago, and staying there in the only way she'd been able to.

"Tell him the truth," Emily said. "Tell him everything you found. Tell him about
the vault, the trucks, the shoulder." She paused. "He's going to need to know."

Vance looked at her.

"Are you trained for this?" he said. "Any of it?"

"No."

He absorbed this. He seemed to find it neither reassuring nor alarming — more
like information about the category of person he was dealing with.

"You drove here alone," he said.

"From Arizona. With a Collective Operator who made himself visible at a gas
station in New Mexico to see if I'd notice."

Something shifted at the corner of Vance's mouth. Not quite a smile. The
precondition for one.

"Rachel," he said.

"Rachel," Emily confirmed.

She moved to the door. At the threshold she stopped and looked back at him — the
man in the white polo with the coffee and the briefcase and the fifteen years he
was in the process of deciding what to do with.

"Mr. Black."

He looked up.

"You drove yourself to the clinic," she said. "I don't know what that took. But
it meant something, that you kept going."

He held her eyes for a moment. Then he looked at the table.

"Good luck," he said.

Pilar was where she had left her.

They moved through the maintenance corridor and the freight elevator and the
badge reader and the service passage and out through the east entrance door into
the morning light. Orin was at the loading dock, sitting on a concrete block
with his pack between his feet, watching the parking lot with the unhurried
patience of a man who had learned that things either went right or they didn't,
and waiting was the job until you found out which.

Emily put the copier in his hand.

He looked at the indicator light — green, steady. He looked at her.

"He opened it voluntarily," she said.

Orin turned the device over. He looked at it for a moment with the expression of
someone who had done enough of these that he knew when one had gone the right
way, and it was still something.

"Rachel's going to want to hear from you directly," he said.

"I know."

"She's going to say—"

"I know what she's going to say." Emily looked at the building she had just come
out of. The complex in the morning light, functional, enormous, the particular
architecture of an institution that believed in its own permanence. She thought
about the east entrance, the maintenance corridor, a man with a briefcase
deciding who he was.

"Orin."

"Yeah."

"How do I get a message to the Golden Dawn."

He was quiet for a moment. He reached into his jacket and produced a different
device — smaller, with a single dial and an antenna port. "Collective comms
relay. It routes to a Falcon-class relay station that's been parked in Saturn's
outer approach for eight months." He looked at it. "It'll take about ninety
minutes for the signal to get there, depending on orbital alignment."

"And back."

"Another ninety. Give or take."

Emily took the device. She held it in her palm and looked at the antenna and
thought about the ninety minutes of space between Houston and Saturn and a ship
she had never been on, with a crew she had never met, and a brother she had not
spoken to in the better part of a year.

She turned the dial. The relay clicked on.

She said: "Cole. It's Emily. I'm in Houston. I have the counter-program. If you
can hear this — I know about Sharon. I know what happened to her. I need to know
you're both alive. Respond when you can."

She clicked it off. She held it.

"What do we do for ninety minutes," she said.

"I know a place that serves breakfast," Orin said.

They were on their second cup of coffee when the relay clicked.

The voice came through the speaker with the particular distortion of deep-space
comms — the slight compression, the warmth stripped out by the distance — but it
was unmistakably Cole. She recognized the cadence of him before she recognized
the specific words, the way you recognize a person's walk before you see their
face.

"Emily."

She set down the cup.

"I know about Sharon." He paused. Not the pause of hesitation. The pause of a
man who had something he needed to say carefully. "She's here. She came back.
She's — she's not all the way back yet, but she's here. She's safe." Another
pause. "She says she kept your name. While she was under. She kept both of us.
She wants me to tell you that."

Emily pressed her thumb against the edge of the cup. The diner around her
continued its morning business: plates, the coffee machine, a man at the counter
reading a newspaper. The ordinary world, conducting itself.

"Are you all right?" Cole's voice said. "Emily. Are you alive?"

She picked up the relay device.

"I'm here," she said. "I'm coming home."

She clicked it off and set it on the table and looked at the window, at the
Houston morning, at the light on the parking lot pavement.

Orin said nothing. He drank his coffee. Outside, a truck went past on the access
road. A sparrow landed on the window ledge and looked in and left.

Emily thought about Sharon saying I'll be here. She thought about the parking
garage in San Francisco, and a message that had said she was killed, and the
four days she had spent believing it, and what it had done to her, and what it
was doing now to know it wasn't true.

She thought about how much of the last two months had been built on a fact that
wasn't a fact. How much of the grief, and the driving, and the gas station east
of Indio, and the motel in Surprise, and the eleven hundred miles.

She thought: it still counts. Every mile of it still counts. You drove it
anyway.

She thought: Sharon drove herself here, too. She kept both of us. That's the
kind of person she is.

"I need to book a flight," she said.

"The Collective has a safe transit route," Orin said. "No flag, no trace. It'll
take a day longer."

"That's fine," Emily said. "I've got time."

She picked up the coffee cup. The coffee was still warm. She drank it and looked
at the window and thought about her brother's voice coming across ninety minutes
of space, distorted by distance and still unmistakably him, and she thought
about the thing he had said: she kept both of us.

She had not known, until he said it, how much she needed to hear that.

She set the cup down.

She said, quietly, to the window and the sparrow and the ordinary morning
proceeding around her: I know. I know you did.

She was, she realized, going to cry. She was going to cry in a diner in Houston
with a Collective Operator eating a breakfast burrito across from her, and she
was going to do it without apologizing.

She did.

Orin handed her a napkin without looking up from his plate.

"Good mission," he said.

"Yeah," she said. Her voice was wet. She pressed the napkin to her face and
breathed through it. "Yeah. It was."
