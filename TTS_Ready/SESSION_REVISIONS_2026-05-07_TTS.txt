REVISION 25
Chapter Nine — Sam and the Copper Ring
------------------------------------------------------------------------

What changed: Sam claimed she physically watched Cole make the copper
wire ring before the mission. This is a continuity error. Sam was a
Collective plant who first appeared at Fleet Command during crew
selection. She was not in Cole's personal life before the mission.

The fix: Sam's knowledge of the ring now routes through Ricardo, which
is consistent with her role.

CONTEXT BEFORE:
Cole leaned against the alcove frame. Outside the small porthole at the
far end of the corridor, the black ran in all directions without a
visible edge.

OLD PROSE:
"The ring," he said. "The copper wire. Before launch." He had not said
it to anyone. He was not entirely sure why he was saying it now. "You
saw it."

"I saw you make it," Sam said. She tightened a housing bolt. "You gave
her something to hold onto before you left."

"Ricardo told you about her."

NEW PROSE:
"The ring," he said. "The copper wire. Before launch." He had not said
it to anyone. He was not entirely sure why he was saying it now.

Sam set down the calibration tool. "Ricardo told me," she said. "You
gave her something to hold onto before you left."

"He told you about her."

CONTEXT AFTER:
"Yes." The flatness of it was its own information. She set down the tool
and looked at him steadily. "I'm sorry, Cole."

REVISION 26
Chapter Nine — Cole and Sam, End of the Alcove Scene
------------------------------------------------------------------------

What changed: The exchange ending the alcove scene was broken. Sam asked
"What did you call her?" and Cole answered with a deflection about
Sharon insisting on the name Nova. The question was ambiguous — it
could mean the ring or Sharon — and Cole's answer didn't address it.
The user noted it made no sense.

The fix: The exchange is replaced with a forward-looking beat. Sam says
she can't wait to meet Sharon when they get back. Cole says he can't
wait to introduce them. This is warmer, character-consistent, and
carries dramatic irony — neither of them knows Sharon is already on the
Alpha vessel shadowing their course.

OLD PROSE:
"What did you call her?" Sam asked.

He almost didn't answer that either. Then: "She insisted on Nova. She
said the other name was already taken."

Sam looked at him. Something passed across her face that was not pity,
something older and more useful than pity. She picked up her tool and
went back to work.

NEW PROSE:
"I can't wait to meet her when we get back," Sam said. "I bet you two
are an adorable couple."

Cole looked at her. The corner of his mouth moved, not quite a smile,
but close enough. "I can't wait to introduce you," he said.

Sam picked up her tool and went back to work.

CONTEXT AFTER:
Cole pushed off the frame and headed back toward the bridge. At the
corridor junction, he stopped.

"Sam."

She looked up.

"When the source comes through," he said, "don't wait."

She held his eyes for a moment. "I won't."

REVISION 27
Chapter Ten — Emily and Marcus, Full Rewrite
------------------------------------------------------------------------

What changed: The chapter had several structural problems. Emily did not
recognize Marcus until late in the scene, after the display screen
reveal. Her opening line was transactional rather than explosive. Marcus
ran a blackmail angle that was wrong for his character. He referenced
receiving the Golden Dawn payload, when in fact he wants the Saturn
crystal batch. And there was no tracker scene after Emily and Rachel
left the room.

The fix: Emily recognizes Marcus the moment he says his name. She comes
out swinging. Marcus wants Conrad's information from Vegas, not
blackmail leverage. He reveals the Saturn situation and Cole's danger,
which is the moment Emily's calculation shifts from offense to exit.
After they leave, Marcus orders a tracker placed on Emily. The chapter
ends with Emily dropping the card at the elevator plant.

------------------------------------------------------------------------

BEFORE — Emily's opening line (wrong register):
"How much are you going to pay me to stay quiet about your goons?"

BEFORE — Recognition comes too late (after the display screen):
Emily looked at the man standing at the desk. She knew that face. Not
from files or briefings. From a specific afternoon when Cole had a
bloody lip and this boy's knee on his chest...

BEFORE — Blackmail angle (wrong):
"Now, Emily. Look, business is just business. I won't let you blackmail
me because that simply does not work."

BEFORE — Marcus receiving crystals (wrong, he wants Saturn batch):
"We expected a drive with this info to be delivered along with the
crystals you dropped off," Marcus said.

------------------------------------------------------------------------

AFTER — FULL CHAPTER:

Rachel walked Emily into the building without speaking. Through the
lobby, into an elevator, three floors up, then into a long dark hallway
that ran in both directions. A cheerful fat Buddha statue sat underlit
at each end. Small, warm lights shone sharply downward from the ceiling
in focused beams, leaving the walls in dark. Two men in dark suits
peeled off the wall as they entered, one ahead, one behind. They did not
announce themselves. They did not look at Emily. That was the point. At
the end of the hall, a large set of double wooden doors stood decorated
with brass hardware and long polished bar handles. Rachel gripped one,
breathed, and pushed forward.

The guards took up positions outside the doors. Emily noted the
placement without turning: flanking the exit, not the entrance.

The room was wide and dim, the kind of dim that came from choosing not
to turn on half the lights. Floor-to-ceiling windows pulled cloud and
sky into the far wall. Along both sides, a dozen televisions ran news
feeds and sports scores at ambient volume. On one screen, a news anchor
sat composed at her desk. Emily looked at her. Then at Rachel. Rachel's
color shifted slightly and Emily filed that away.

The man was kicked back in a swivel chair with his feet on the wide
mahogany desk, the posture of someone who had designed this impression.
A glass of orange juice sat at the desk's corner. Four ice cubes. Emily
counted them without meaning to.

He got to his feet.

"Emily Nova." He moved toward her with the wide, unhurried stride of a
man who had never once been made to hurry. "I'm Marcus Verguld."

She knew that face.

Not from files. From a specific afternoon: Cole with a bloody lip and
this boy's knee on his chest, and her grabbing the collar and putting
him on the ground in front of his friends. He had looked up from the
dirt like he genuinely could not believe what had just happened to him.
She had not given him time to decide what to do about it.

He had grown up. The dirt was better carpeted now.

"I've kicked your ass before," Emily said. "Be aware that I will do it
again."

Something moved behind Marcus's eyes, not embarrassment. The quiet
recalibration of someone whose assessment had just been revised upward
by a measurable amount.

"You have a good memory," he said. He settled back onto the edge of the
desk and looked at her with the unhurried attention of a man who had
nowhere else to be. "Sit down."

"I'd rather stand."

He studied her a moment. Then he looked at Rachel, who stood near the
doors with her face arranged carefully toward the middle distance.

"You were in Las Vegas," Marcus said. "You and Sharon."

Sharon's name in his mouth with the same flat weight as Las Vegas and
Conrad, as if Sharon were a coordinate and not a person, not someone who
had been in a parking garage in San Francisco three days ago while Emily
was three states away and not there. Emily did not move.

"You met with Steven Conrad. He gave you something." He turned toward her
fully. "I want to know what it was."

"I don't know what you're talking about."

"The keychain," Marcus said. "Small plastic figurine, the Enterprise. You
held it. You drove it to Cole's cabin and put it in his hands." His voice
had the quality of a man reading from a list he had memorized a long time
ago. "What's inside it."

She thought about the hollow base. Conrad's specific instruction, calm
and level: it has to reach Cole's hands. He must hold it. She had thought
that was sentiment. She had thought Conrad was being theatrical about a
trinket.

She genuinely did not know.

"He farts in his sleep," Emily said, "and he's two months late on the
phone bill, and he has an unhealthy relationship with a four-armed monkey
that drinks hand sanitizer. I don't know anything about a keychain."

Marcus looked at her with the expression of a man who was neither amused
nor annoyed, simply cataloguing.

He turned to the desk. A few finger taps on the glass surface inlaid in
it. The banks of screens on the walls changed.

Emily recognized it immediately.

The encrypted drive. The research archive she and Sharon had been handed
in Vegas, the file structure, the visual signature of the export format,
the specific documentation of things that were not supposed to exist. She
recognized all of it.

She had put this in the hands of the people who needed it. It was already
where it was supposed to be.

Marcus was showing it to her like a card.

"We expected this through certain channels," he said. "It found other
channels. Contingency plans were made." He glanced at her. "You recognize
it."

"I've never seen that in my life."

"No." And she understood he did not mean I don't believe you. He meant
that's the right answer, and I haven't decided yet if it's the true one.
He was filing it separately from what he thought she knew.

He moved to the window. The clouds moved behind him.

"The Golden Dawn launched twelve days ago," he said. "Harvest sequence
underway. The last major raw crystal batch coming off Saturn's rings, the
one that changes the market permanently, if it arrives intact." He paused.
"There is a vessel considerably larger than the Golden Dawn that has been
pacing Cole's course since he crossed the inner belt. Cole doesn't know
it's there."

The room changed.

Not the room. Her position in it. The calculation restructured itself
around a single fixed point.

"What do you want," she said.

"What Conrad gave you. What he told you and Sharon in that hotel room
that you haven't told anyone." He came away from the window. "That is the
piece I don't have. And until I have it, I can't tell you what happens to
that mission when the larger vessel makes its move."

The room was very quiet.

Emily thought about Cole at nine, looking up at her from the dirt. She
thought about Sharon's hand across a table in Vegas. She thought about
Sharon in a parking garage in San Francisco and the message that came
afterward and the specific quality of a grief that hadn't been allowed to
land yet because there had been too many miles to drive, too many things
to do, and now she was standing in this room and the grief was still
waiting and she was still not done.

She thought: the only way to help him is to get out of this room.

"I don't know what was in the keychain," she said. "I handed it to Cole
and I drove away. Conrad didn't explain it to me."

Marcus watched her for a long moment.

"I believe the keychain part," he said. "The rest of it, I'm reserving
judgment on." He moved back to the desk. "You're more useful to me as a
question mark than as a problem. So you're going to walk out of here
tonight."

He held out a small card. Plain. No logo. A number printed on one side.

"If anything comes back to you, about what Conrad gave you, about what
he built, about any of it. There's a number."

Emily looked at it.

She took the card.

"Ms. Vayes will see you out."

---

After the doors had closed and the elevator had engaged and the sound of
footsteps on tile had faded from the corridor:

"Put one on her before she reaches the street," Marcus said. "The small
one. She won't feel it."

The man standing in the far corner of the room, not the guards, the other
one, the one who had been there before Emily arrived, nodded once and
moved toward the secondary exit.

Marcus sat back down. He picked up the orange juice. He looked at the
screens, still lit with the contents of the drive, and he thought about
Conrad. A man he had underestimated twice and did not intend to
underestimate a third time. He thought about what a careful man builds
into a children's toy when he knows someone might come looking.

He drank the juice.

The ice was already melting.

---

In the elevator, Rachel held her face toward the floor numbers. Her eyes
moved once toward the security camera mounted in the upper corner.

"Careful," she said.

Emily took the hint.

The parking garage was cold. Emily slipped the card behind the potted
plant on the landing without breaking stride. She understood what it was.
He did not believe she knew anything, believed it enough to let her walk,
but he wanted a thread.

She was not going to be the thread.

She turned to Rachel. A taxi was pulling into the garage entrance.

"What is Marcus going to do to Cole?"

Rachel's face held nothing. "Nothing happened," she said quietly. "For
both of us. Nothing happened."

Emily felt something that was not quite disgust, more the particular
exhaustion of having been in a room with a man who understood the price
of everything and had very deliberately not paid it.

"Nothing happened," she said.

She got in the taxi.

REVISION 28
Chapter Nineteen — The Boarding, Specialist Crane Removed
------------------------------------------------------------------------

What changed: Sharon no longer has a cover identity called Specialist
Crane. She is not embedded in the Golden Dawn crew. She is stowed aboard
Alpha's vessel as a deployed asset. When Alpha makes their move after
the Saturn harvest, Sharon comes through the boarding tube alone, no
cover story, no name. Bitboy's threat assessment triggers before anyone
can see her face. Ricardo gets the funny line. Then Cole asks for the
visor. Then the reveal.

The scene from the face reveal onward is unchanged. This revision
replaces only the approach and setup.

OLD PROSE — lines 142 through 181:

The operative introduced herself as Specialist Crane.

She came through the docking tube alone. The other two crew members of
FED-MRS-0409 stayed aboard their vessel. Gracie tracked them the entire
time. And Specialist Crane came through the tube with her helmet in the
crook of her arm and a case in her right hand that Gracie identified as
standard medical supply, biometric-locked, which was interesting because
a medical supply case did not require a biometric lock.

She was suited in a standard Federation gray, face obscured by the
transit visor she had not yet raised. Her movements were economy itself,
no wasted effort, no hesitation at the threshold, the particular
controlled locomotion of a person who had been trained in confined
spaces. The visor was still down.

"Captain Nova," she said. Her voice came through the suit's external
speaker with the flat modulation of filtered audio. "I'm authorized to
conduct a systems assessment and medical review of your crew under
Emergency Health Protocol Seven."

"There's no emergency health protocol active on this ship," Cole said.

"It was filed in response to an incident report filed from your
transponder six weeks ago."

"Our transponder reports are routine."

"The incident report wasn't routine. It flagged elevated atmospheric
contamination in your primary cargo bay." She still hadn't raised the
visor. "I'm here to assess ongoing crew exposure and clear the mission
to proceed."

Cole looked at her. He looked at the case. He looked at the visor.

"You can raise that," he said. "We're on a pressurized deck."

She did not raise it.

"Specialist Crane," Gracie said, in the ship's overhead speakers. "Our
environmental systems show deck pressure at standard. If you're
experiencing suit malfunction, I can assist."

A beat. Then the specialist's hand moved to the visor release.

NEW PROSE — same position, same ending point:

She came through the docking tube alone. The two crew of FED-MRS-0409
stayed aboard their vessel. Gracie was tracking them. She came through
carrying a case in her right hand that Gracie had already flagged:
standard supply dimensions, biometric-locked.

Alpha Corp matte-black. No insignia. No rank markings. Visor down.

She stopped at the threshold and said nothing.

Cole looked at her. "You want to start somewhere."

No answer. The visor stayed down.

Bitboy moved first.

He had positioned himself at the cargo bay entry when the docking clamp
deployed. Cole had not put him there. He had simply arrived and stood,
which was the thing Bitboy did when his threat-assessment protocols had
flagged something his vocoder couldn't yet express. Now he walked forward
two paces, stopped, and fixed the figure with the flat amber attention of
his operational scan. His LED eyes cycled. His threat panel lit.

It read:

SHE SMELLS LIKE SHARON.

Ricardo looked at the panel.

He looked at Cole.

He looked back at the panel.

"I want it on record," Ricardo said, "that I have no idea how to file
that in any existing threat category."

Cole was already looking at the visor.

"Take it off," he said.

The figure did not move.

"Gracie."

I have been running the biometric match since she crossed the docking
threshold. A pause. The weight of it landing before the words did. Cole.
Ask her again.

"Take off the visor." His voice had gone quieter than it should have been
in a room with a standing threat. "Please."

A beat.

The hand came up. The visor released.

Cole registered what happened next in fragments.

The visor came up.
