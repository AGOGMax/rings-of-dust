Chapter 15 — Surprise, Arizona
Rachel's phone call (Pilar replaces Vance Black)

What this replaced: A scene in which Rachel called to say that Vance Black,
a Federation Operations officer, was already en route to Houston with a
biometric case. Emily's role was to intercept that handoff. Vance was
described as wearing a white polo and designer glasses.

Why it changed: Vance Black is dead. He was shot by Marcus in Chapter 11.
The scene was rebuilt around Pilar — a Collective plant inside Federation
Systems, twelve-year employee, vault credentials, three years waiting.

CONTEXT BEFORE:
The road atlas was on the nightstand where she had left it. Sharon had kept
one in the glove box of every car she had ever owned. Emily had bought this
one at the Indio gas station. She had used it to plot last night's stretch.
The pen marks she had made on the Texas page were already starting to feel
like a conversation she needed to finish.

The burner buzzed.

She answered on the second ring.

NEW PROSE:
"It's in Houston." Rachel's voice was the same as the pier: below the wind,
precise, no wasted syllables. "Conrad's prototype. Federation secure storage,
sub-basement level, east wing of Space Command. It's been there for eight
months."

Emily sat on the edge of the bed.

"We have someone inside," Rachel said. "Twelve-year Federation Systems
employee. She's been positioned for this for three years — she has vault
credentials, she knows the access protocol, she's been waiting for someone to
hand it to." A pause. "Her name is Pilar. She'll walk you in, she'll open the
vault, and she'll walk you out. What she can't do is seen carrying the
device. That's your part."

"What does my part actually involve."

"A copier device, twenty seconds, a green light." Rachel's tone was level.
"You place it on the prototype surface. You hold it. You remove it. The
duplicate goes in your jacket. The prototype stays in the vault. Pilar locks
the door and neither of you was there."

Emily looked at the road atlas. Surprise, Arizona to Houston, Texas. She ran
her thumb along the spine of the page.

"That's eleven hundred miles."

"Yes."

"Rachel." She kept her voice even. "I'm not trained for this."

There was a silence that was not unkind. "I know what you are and aren't.
Pilar handles everything technical. What I need you to do is be present and
mobile and not stop before you get there." Another pause. "You've been doing
that part since the parking garage."

The line clicked.

Emily sat with the phone in her hand. Outside, a truck reversed in the
parking lot, beeping. The light through the curtains was the flat gray of
early dawn, not yet gold, the sky still deciding.

She thought about the east entrance. Maintenance access. A woman named Pilar
she had never met — twelve years inside the Federation, three years waiting
for this exact moment. She thought about what she knew how to do.

CONTEXT AFTER:
She had never fired a gun. She had once driven a delivery van for Sharon
through a rainstorm at four in the morning and gotten four cases of prohibited
optical components from a dock warehouse to a client's garage without a
single form on file. She had extracted herself from a parking garage in Los
Angeles while the man who had had Sharon shot was still in the building. She
had driven here from a gas station in the dark, alone, on three hours of
sleep, on a road she'd never been on.

None of that was training. All of it was experience.

Chapter 16 — Behind the Curtain
Long-cycle data discovery (EXTENDED_HORIZON_ANALYSIS)

What this added: The flywheel principle — the full discovery of what happens
to DarkX fuel cells past the eleven-day harvest window. New canon: 33 days
is non-linear yield. 60 days is efficiency inversion. 180 days in zero
gravity is exponential. This is Max's discovery, the one the Collective will
call the antigravity project.

CONTEXT BEFORE:
The energy crisis, as a technical problem, had been solvable for at least a
decade.

Max read the sentence twice. He made a small mark in the margin. Kept reading.

NEW PROSE:
The long-cycle data was buried three directories deep under a project
designation he did not recognize: EXTENDED_HORIZON_ANALYSIS. He found it
because he was methodical and because the indexing structure had a gap between
Q2 and Q4 that suggested Q3 had been archived rather than deleted — and
archived data, in his experience, was where the things you weren't supposed
to find actually lived.

Inside were 47 cell-monitoring records from cells that had not been
terminated at the eleven-day window. Some were accidents — containment
failures, scheduling errors, batch records that had fallen through the log.
Some were deliberate: an internal research project, nine months long, that
had been run in a sealed sub-lab and then classified at a level that should
have prevented its appearance in a pull-access terminal.

Someone had made an indexing error. Or someone had wanted it findable. Max
did not know which and did not have time to determine it.

He read the yield curves.

At thirty-three days, the output was non-linear. Not by a small margin. The
cells that had been allowed to run past the harvest window were not depleting
— they were amplifying. The dark matter crystalline structure, when left
undisturbed in the nitrogen environment past the eleven-day threshold, did
not plateau. It continued. The energy return per gram of feedstock at
thirty-three days was roughly four times what the standard eleven-day harvest
produced.

At sixty days, the data showed something else. The efficiency curve inverted.
The cell was producing more than the feedstock cost to run it. Not marginally.
Not within rounding error. Substantially more.

At the bottom of the file was a theoretical projection: 180 days in zero
gravity. The calculation was careful and conservative and it arrived at a
yield figure that Max read three times before he accepted that the zeros were
not a typo.

He sat with this for a moment.

Alpha had known about this for at least nine months. They had run the data,
understood what it meant, and classified it.

He scrolled to the end of the file. The last entry was a single line, a
recommendation from the lead researcher: Proceed to Saturn field test.
Recommend full gravity-isolation protocol.

The entry had no response logged. No approval. No denial. Just the
recommendation, sitting there, with a timestamp from eleven months ago, never
acted on.

The anomaly records were easier to find once he knew what to look for.

The batch logs used a status field he had initially skimmed past. When he
filtered for it, he got one entry for approximately every seven hundred and
forty standard batches.

Each of these entries documented a cell that had, at the standard eleven-day
harvest, presented with significant external crystal growth. One researcher,
in a handwritten annotation attached to a 2036 batch record, had used a
different word.

Jackpot.

The yield from a single jackpot event was equivalent to forty to sixty
standard harvests. Alpha tracked these separately. They did not appear in the
public supply figures.

Max cross-referenced the anomaly records against the grant filings. Alpha had
filed, over the previous seven years, nine separate government research grants
for the development of what their applications described as next-generation
fuel-cell stabilization technology. Combined, the grants totaled several
billion dollars, drawn from energy crisis relief funds allocated by the
Federation Council.

The technology they were describing in the grant applications was Max's
process. His recipe. His parameters. Renamed. Reformatted. Resubmitted with
incremental variations small enough to constitute new filings and large enough
to maintain the appearance of ongoing research.

Alpha had been paid billions of dollars to develop technology that already
existed.

CONTEXT AFTER:
He noted this the way he noted everything: as a data point. He added it to
the notebook.

It took him two and a half shifts to find the batch destruction logs.

Chapter 16 — Behind the Curtain
Extraction cafe and the naming of the Antigravity Project

What this added: The scene at the extraction cafe where Max gives the drive
to Red and Squirrel and explains what is on it. The moment Red says they need
a name for the zero-gravity discovery. Max says: antigravity project. This
is the origin of the code name used by the Collective throughout the rest of
the book and the name of the real-world project (agproject.io).

CONTEXT BEFORE:
The extraction point was a cafe awning two blocks ahead. He could see
Squirrel's jacket through the crowd.

He kept walking.

NEW PROSE:
At the cafe, Squirrel did not look at him when he sat down. Red had a coffee
in front of her and was watching the street. Max put the drive on the table
between them, face down, the way you set down something that has weight.

Squirrel's hand moved over it without a gesture. It was in his jacket before
anyone at the adjacent tables would have had reason to notice an exchange.

"What's on it," Red said. She was still watching the street.

"The long-cycle data," Max said. "What happens if you don't reset at eleven
days. Thirty-three. Sixty. A hundred and eighty." He paused. "In zero
gravity, the yield is exponential. They've known for nine months. It's buried
under an access tier that shouldn't have been reachable from my clearance
level."

Red looked at him for the first time.

"There's also documentation of the government grants," Max said. "Seven years
of applications. Billions drawn from the crisis relief funds. They described
my process as their own ongoing research." He looked at the table. "And the
destruction logs. They've been disposing of eleven to thirteen percent of
production per batch for eleven years to maintain the supply gap."

The table was quiet for a moment. Outside, a motorcycle passed on the street.

Red looked at Squirrel. Something moved between them — not surprise. The
particular stillness of people who have been building toward something for a
long time and have just been handed the piece that makes it load-bearing.

"The zero-gravity yield," Squirrel said. "That's the piece they're most
afraid of."

"Yes," Max said. "You can't manufacture a shortage of something that
compounds in everyone's hands."

Squirrel turned the coffee cup once. "We're going to need a name for this.
When we move it through the network, when the parallel release goes out." He
looked at Max. "Something that says what it is."

Max thought about the yield curve at a hundred and eighty days. About a fuel
cell in zero gravity giving back more than physics should allow — compounding,
escalating, refusing to stop.

"Antigravity project," he said.

CONTEXT AFTER:
Red looked at him. "That works," she said. She went back to watching the
street. "That's what we'll call it."

He was aware, with precision, of the distance between himself and Taylor. Of

closed.

NEW PROSE (second addition — after "Sharon was carrying information."):

shift onto his front foot. He felt the distance across the console compress,
in his body's own arithmetic, into something achievable.

his father died, when the grief kept arriving in the wrong shape and wearing
the wrong face, and you had to put it somewhere it couldn't reach the rest of

Chapter 11 — Chapter Eleven
Taylor queries iRIS for Cole/Sam audio — rain and thunder

What this added: After viewing the alcove camera feed, Taylor pulls the
acoustic log of the Cole/Sam interaction. The jammer has filled it with
rain and thunder. Taylor realizes he is dealing with a professional.

CONTEXT BEFORE:
He pulled the alcove camera feed. Standard resolution, partial occlusion
from the ventilation housing — Sam at the diagnostic panel, Cole at the
threshold. Body language. Nothing transferred between them that the camera
could resolve.

Nothing the camera could resolve.

NEW PROSE:
He pulled the acoustic log for the same window.

"iRIS. Acoustic record. Maintenance alcove, forward section. Eleven-minute
window. Sam's interaction."

iRIS played it back.

What came back was rain.

The sound of it was specific and complete — not static, not compression
artifacts, not the digital smear of a corrupted file. Rain. The kind that
arrives in sheets and fills a room with its own frequency. And beneath it,
low and certain, something that might have been thunder.

Taylor sat very still.

"iRIS. Confirm log source."

"Maintenance alcove acoustic sensor feed. Forward section. Log
authenticated."

He looked at the screen. The timestamp matched the camera footage. The log
was legitimate. What was in it was not alcove ambient.

The device did not jam the audio. It replaced it — filled eleven minutes of
recording with something that would read, on a first pass, as interference.
Not active suppression. Designed to look like a corrupted file. Designed to
not trigger an anomaly flag.

CONTEXT AFTER:
Taylor closed the feed and sat with his hands on the console. He was not
panicking. He was assessing. The iRIS architecture was intact. The
communications relay was functioning on the band Alpha controlled. But the
man with a suspicion now had a professional running interference for him.

A man with a suspicion was manageable. A man with a professional was a
different problem.

The sweep was a method she had developed herself: a pattern of rotational
frequency shifts that moved through the Alpha-controlled relay bands the way

gaps between transmissions more informative than the transmissions
themselves.

What she found at 04:23 ship time was consistent with what she had expected.

The Golden Dawn's primary relay was silent in the direction of Earth. The

The Alpha management relay, however, was active. Brief, encrypted, routed

logged the transmission time and moved on.

She composed her own report. Seven sentences. Coded in the Collective's
rotating structure, which meant that without the current key, the message
looked like a routine mineral survey update. She routed it through two of
the same Federation nodes Alpha was using.

The report said, in the structure that was not a mineral survey:

Sharon Teez — Collective-adjacent, confirmed. Killed in transit. The loss
is real. Grieve it.

Emily Nova — untrained, currently mobile, moving toward Houston under
