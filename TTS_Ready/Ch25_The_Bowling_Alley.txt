RINGS OF DUST

Chapter Twenty-Five

The Bowling Alley

The place was called Cosmic Lanes.

This was Sam's contribution. She had been asked by no one in particular, and by
the implicit consensus of the group, to find a bowling alley that could handle a
party of six — seven, counting Bitboy — and that did not have a policy against
service animals. Cosmic Lanes had fog machines and black lights and a carpet
pattern that appeared, under black light, to contain constellations that did not
match any real star chart. Sam described this detail to Cole by text, and Cole's
response was *perfect,* and Sam had made the reservation for Saturday.

They arrived in two vehicles. Ricardo's rental was first, which meant Ricardo
was first, which meant Ricardo had been the most excited, which was consistent
with Ricardo. He jumped out of the driver's seat while Sam was still gathering
her things, the trunk open before she had finished unbuckling her seatbelt.

Cole's car was second. He had driven the four of them — himself, Sharon, Emily,
Bitboy — in the Collective transit vehicle that he had been borrowing since he'd
landed, which was technically for operational transit and which Sommi had not
asked him to return yet. Sharon had shotgun. Bitboy had the back window and was
tracking every tree on the highway with concentrated interest. Emily was reading
the road atlas.

"We're going bowling," Cole said.

"I know," Emily said. "I'm looking at the route."

"To the bowling alley."

"There's an alternate."

"There is no alternate. It's a bowling alley."

Emily turned a page.

Sharon did not say anything. She was watching the exit signs with the expression
she had developed over the past three weeks of outpatient transition —
calibrated, present, taking in information at a rate that was comfortable rather
than obligatory. Dr. Hexo called it *deliberate orientation,* which was
clinical language for Sharon learning to be in the world again at the pace she
needed rather than the pace the world generally assumed.

She looked good. She looked like herself, in the way that a ship that has been
in space for two years looks like itself — some wear on the surface, the shape
of it changed by experience, but the fundamental structure intact and the lines
clean.

Cole had been learning not to stare.

He stared anyway, sometimes. She had not told him to stop. He thought she
probably noticed and didn't mind.

---

Cosmic Lanes was exactly what Sam had described.

The carpet was extraordinary. The lanes stretched out under the black lights in
their parallel lines, the pins at the far end glowing white, the fog from the
machines drifting at ankle level around the ball returns. A playlist was running
that Cole could not categorize — it was not quite classic rock, not quite
something you'd hear in a space between categories that someone had apparently
decided was the right sound for a bowling alley themed after the cosmos.

Ricardo was already at the shoe rental desk.

He was wearing a button-down shirt that he had pressed himself. Cole noticed
this because Ricardo had never, in twenty years of acquaintance, pressed a shirt
under any circumstances Cole was aware of. He had the expression of a man who
had made a decision and was carrying out the pre-launch sequence.

Sam was at his side. She was compact and precise and she laughed at Ricardo in
the specific way that only people who truly loved someone could manage — not
laughing *at* him, laughing *with* the version of him she saw that no one else
did. Cole had watched whatever this was develop over a hundred and forty days in
a metal tube between Earth and Saturn. He had liked her immediately, from the
first morning she had been clear-eyed when everyone else was still recalibrating
around iRIS. He had kept that observation to himself. Ricardo had not needed
any help.

She looked at Cole and Sharon as they came in and said: "Oh thank God, I was
afraid he was going to do the whole thing while we were waiting for you."

Ricardo turned around. "I was waiting," he said. He looked at Cole. "I was
waiting."

"I know," Cole said.

"Do you want to do it now or after?" Sam asked. She was already at the shoe
rack.

"The shoes are part of it," Ricardo said. "Everything is part of it."

---

They put on their shoes. Ricardo had arranged for two lanes side by side — one
for the people who would bowl with some measure of competence, and one, as he
described it, "for the experience." He did not specify which was which.

Sam went first on lane seven. She bowled with the ease of someone who had done
it enough times to be comfortable and not enough times to be competitive. Seven
pins on the first ball, spare on the second. She turned back to the group with a
small pleased expression.

Emily went second. She knocked over three pins, frowned at the remaining seven
as though they had said something personal, and declined the second ball.

"It's the approach," Sam said.

"It's the pins," Emily said.

Sam bowled a strike and did not comment on this.

Cole bowled. He had not bowled in years. His body remembered the weight of the
ball and the arc of the release before his mind did, which was consistent with
most things his body remembered from before the mission. Seven pins. Second
ball: two.

He sat back down beside Sharon, who had been watching the lanes with the
expression she used when she was processing something.

"How does it work," she said.

"The scoring?"

"The release. You're letting it go at that specific point — what makes that the
right point?"

Cole thought about it. "Momentum carries the ball past where your arm would
normally stop. You let the weight do the work."

She looked at the lane. She looked at her right hand — the augmented one.

"Hm," she said.

Ricardo walked up to the line with his ball.

The proposal happened between frames.

He had not planned it between frames, exactly — he had planned it after the
third frame on the theory that three frames was enough time to have bowling
context and not so much that the rest of the evening was consumed by the
anticipation. But the third frame had involved Sam's second strike and a brief
argument about the trajectory of her follow-through that had taken several
minutes, and by the time Ricardo had his ball and was standing at the line, the
timing had adjusted itself.

He set the ball in the return.

He turned around.

He reached into the pocket of the pressed shirt.

Sam had been watching him the entire time. The expression on her face was the
expression of a person who had been watching the pre-launch sequence and knew
what was coming and had decided to let it come exactly the way it wanted to
come.

He went down on one knee. On the cosmic carpet with the constellations that
didn't match any real star chart, with the fog drifting around him and the black
lights making the white of his shirt glow.

"Sam," he said.

"Ricardo," she said.

"This is a moissanite ring," he said. He opened the box. The ring was small and
precise and it caught the black light in the way that moissanite did —
differently from diamond, a slightly more colorful refraction, more blue and
green in the prism. "I want you to know that moissanite is harder than sapphire,
almost as hard as diamond, more brilliant in certain light conditions, and is
not ethically associated with the practices that have plagued the diamond
industry for the last seventy years. I want you to know all of this so that when
I tell you that I bought the best ring I could find for the woman I want to
spend my life with, you understand that this is that ring. Not the second-best
ring. The best ring."

Sam was laughing. She was crying. Both at once, in the way that sometimes only
both is the right amount.

"Also it was forty percent less expensive than a comparable diamond, which means
I have already begun being useful to you financially," Ricardo said.

"Ricardo," she said.

"Yes?"

"Yes," she said.

He stood up. He put the ring on her finger. She put her arms around him and he
put his around her and they held each other beside the ball return on lane seven
while Sam and Emily and Cole clapped and Bitboy, who had been monitoring the
proceedings from a seat on the floor with four limbs crossed, made a sound of
deep primal approval.

Sharon said, very quietly, to Cole: "That was perfect."

"Yeah," he said.

"The ring explanation was my favorite part."

"He's been workshopping that for months."

She looked at him sideways, with the small thing at the corner of her mouth.

---

Sharon's first bowling ball went into the gutter at a velocity that the physics
of the lane had not been designed to accommodate.

The sound it made was the sound of an object traveling faster than the surface
beneath it was prepared for — a short sharp concussive bang that arrived at the
moment of contact and then echoed in the lane's plywood-and-fiberglass
construction for longer than it should have. The ball did not reach the pins.
The ball reached the end of the lane at an angle that deposited it against the
side wall of the gutter, where it continued briefly before the bumper mechanism
stopped it with a second smaller bang.

The music stopped. The fog machines continued.

Sharon looked at her right arm. She flexed the augmented hand once, twice.

"Calibrating," she said.

Everyone was looking at the lane.

"Was that the speed of sound?" Emily said.

"A bit over," Sharon said. She turned to Cole. "What's the correct velocity for
a bowling lane?"

"Not that," Cole said.

"I'm getting it." She held out her hand for the second ball.

Ricardo held it back.

"Sharon," he said. "With respect. I am going to ask you to use the other arm."

"The other arm is the one that's been out of practice."

"The other arm is the one that hasn't previously endangered the structural
integrity of a federally regulated recreational facility."

Sharon looked at the gutter. She looked at her right arm. She looked at Ricardo
with the expression she used for people who were making reasonable points that
she was going to agree with.

"Fine," she said.

She used the left arm. Six pins. Second ball, four more.

"Calibrating," she said again, with satisfaction.

Cole watched her and felt the love and the fury and the release and everything
the substance had shown him, all in the same breath, sitting in a bowling alley
in California with fog machines and a carpet that was not astronomically
accurate. He felt all of it and it was not too much. It was exactly the right
amount.

She came back and sat down beside him.

"Tell me about the ring," she said.

He looked at her.

"The copper one," she said. "I remember you made it. I don't have the sequence
right — I know you made it before the mission. I remember holding it." She
looked at her left hand. "I don't know where it is."

"Hexo had it," Cole said. "He took it off before the augmentation. He gave it
to me when I got there."

She was quiet for a moment.

"He kept it," she said.

"He kept it."

She looked at him. "You knew I'd want it back."

"Gracie told me you'd want it back."

"Gracie is thoughtful."

"She's been developing opinions."

Sharon looked at the lane, where Emily was attempting a technique Sam was
demonstrating that neither of them had agreed on. She looked at Ricardo and
Sam, who were seated in the plastic chairs with the ring between them and a
conversation that had the quality of one that was going to be going on for
years.

She looked at Cole.

"I'm going to tell you something," she said.

"Okay."

"I'm not going to marry you on a delay." She held his eyes. "When you're ready
to ask, I'll be ready to answer. But I'm not going to accept a proposal that
comes with a qualifier. Not 'when you're better.' Not 'when the gaps close.' Not
'when everything is sorted.' When you want to ask, ask. I'll be here."

Cole looked at her.

"I already have a ring I'm going to replace," he said.

"I know." She held his eyes. "Ask me when you're ready."

He thought about what he had said to her when he made it: *for the first time,
I want to give you something to hold onto. Keep it safe for me.*

She had kept it on her the whole way to Alpha. She had kept it until the last
possible moment.

"Sharon," he said.

She looked at him.

He reached into his jacket pocket. He had been carrying the copper-wire ring
since Hexo had pressed it into his palm outside the recovery facility — and had
been waiting, since then, for the right moment.

He placed it on the table between them. The small pebble still secured at the
top. The wire still holding the shape of a ring.

"I kept my end of the promise," he said. "I brought back something
extraordinary. But I don't think I need to replace it. I think this is the one."

She looked at the ring on the table. She picked it up with her left hand and
held it between her thumb and forefinger in the particular light of the bowling
alley, and the black lights caught the copper wire and made it glow.

She put it on.

She looked at him.

"Yes," she said.

He looked at her.

"That wasn't a question," he said.

"I know," she said. "It still applies."

Something in the vicinity of Cole's chest performed a structural event that he
had no current word for.

He would find one later.

His phone lit up in his jacket pocket. He took it out.

Gracie was standing on the surface of it — holographic,
thumb-high, watching. She looked at Sharon's left hand. At
the ring. Then at Cole.

"She never took it off," Gracie said. "Not once. Not until
she couldn't."

Cole looked at Sharon.

"I know," he said.

Gracie looked at Sharon for one more moment — the way someone
looks at a thing they have been watching over from a long
distance and can finally see clearly. Then the projection
closed.

---

Bitboy found the vending machine at approximately the forty-minute mark.

The machine was in the alcove beside the restrooms and contained, in addition to
the standard soft drinks and snack items, a row of energy drinks that were, in
terms of their sugar and caffeine content, within the range of what Bitboy had
trained himself to detect through plexiglass at distances of up to fifteen feet.

The approach was methodical. The exploitation was efficient. The extraction
phase, in which Bitboy had successfully accessed the machine's internal
mechanism through a ventilation panel and was methodically consuming the
contents, lasted approximately four minutes before Sam noticed the alcove was
unattended.

Sam's voice came over from lane seven at a volume that was above her normal
register.

"Bitboy."

A pause.

Then a sound that was Bitboy attempting, through the consumption of seven energy
drinks, to engage the full extent of his mechanical augmentation, and the
subsequent sound of the vending machine arriving at its conclusion as a
structural entity.

"EVERYBODY OUT," Ricardo said. He was already moving. "RUN."

What followed was not a panic. It was the coordinated exodus of a group of
people who had been through worse than this and who understood, from previous
experience, that the window between *Bitboy has had an inadvisable amount of
ethanol-equivalent stimulant* and *Bitboy is in the parking lot* was exactly as
long as it took Bitboy to get outside, and that the best possible outcome was
reached by being outside first.

Emily grabbed the road atlas. Sam grabbed her bowling ball, for reasons she
could not fully explain later but that she stood behind. Ricardo picked up
Sam's jacket and his own. Cole caught Sharon's hand.

They were in the parking lot before the fog machines had noticed anything had
changed.

Behind them, the door to Cosmic Lanes swung open and shut twice in rapid
succession, which meant Bitboy was doing laps.

They stood in the parking lot in the California night, catching their breath.

"Is that covered by insurance?" Emily said.

"For us or for the bowling alley?" Ricardo said.

"Either."

"I'm going to say yes to both and we can figure out the details later."

Sam was laughing. She had been laughing since the vending machine sound. She
was leaning against Ricardo with the ring on her finger and the jacket over her
shoulders and the look of a woman who had been told who she was signing up for
and had decided yes, all of this, yes.

She put her hand in the jacket pocket.

Her fingers found a small square of folded cloth. She opened it. She already
knew what it was before she saw it — a compass face, the needle pointing north,
stitched in dark thread on a black background. She held it in her closed fist.

Ricardo said something. She laughed and held the ring up to the California sky
and let him have the moment, and she did not explain the thing in her other
hand. She would explain it to him later, when she could find the words for what
it meant that someone, somewhere, had been watching closely enough to know she'd
earned it.

Sam was looking at her bowling ball.

Sharon was looking at the sky.

Cole looked at her looking at the sky. She had her head tilted back slightly,
the augmented eye and the left eye both tracking the same point above them, the
particular quality of attention of someone who had been in orbit and was now on
the ground and had not yet finished reconciling the two.

He looked up.

The sky was good. California sky, the coastal marine layer still on the horizon
but the zenith clear, the stars visible and not competing with anything.

He thought about a mobile over a crib. He thought about a baby reaching up.

He thought: *it was always in the reaching.*

"Cole," Sharon said.

"Yeah."

"Thank you for coming to get me."

He looked at her.

He thought about the cargo bay. He thought about the compound in his blood and
Gracie's voice and the choice to stop fighting and reach instead.

He thought: *that's what you do. For the people you choose.*

"Always," he said.

The door to Cosmic Lanes opened one more time.

Bitboy bounded into the parking lot, all six limbs deployed, eyes at full
luminescence, vibrating at a frequency that suggested the seven energy drinks
had achieved complete integration with his augmentation system.

He looked at them.

They looked at him.

"Everybody in the cars," Ricardo said, with the calm of a man who has been in
worse situations and knows this one has a good ending.

They got in the cars.

They drove away into the California night, and behind them the lights of Cosmic
Lanes receded, and the fog came in from the coast in its patient way, and in the
car Bitboy slowly, across the next forty-five minutes, descended from his peak
and settled into the quiet satisfied state of an animal who had done something
inadvisable and found it worth it.

Nobody said this out loud.

Everybody was thinking it.

EPILOGUE

Marcus Verguld had an apartment in Dubai that he had purchased under a shell
company in 2019 and had not, until now, found a reason to use.

The apartment was on the forty-second floor and faced the Gulf, which was the
color of oxidized copper in the evening and black at night and the particular
hammered gold of a sunrise that had no fog in it. He had been here for six
weeks. He had been here under a name that was not his name, which was a
condition he was adapting to with the patience of a man who had always
understood that patience was the most important strategic asset and that
circumstances were, in most cases, temporary.

He had not expected the woman to wake up.

He sat with his orange juice — four near-perfect ice cubes, the glass cold
against his palm — and looked at the Gulf and thought about this.

Sharon Teez had been, in his assessment, a Collective asset of moderate
strategic value. Her access to Cole Nova through personal relationship, her
existing grey market network, her operational history — these were catalogued
values. Her conditioning was correctly executed, her deployment was correctly
timed, and the expected outcome of her deployment had been the retrieval of Nova
from the Golden Dawn and the subsequent delay of Conrad's counter-program
through a period that Marcus had calculated as sufficient.

He had not calculated for the compound.

He had not known about the compound because Conrad had told no one about the
compound except, apparently, a ship captain four years ago during a cargo hold
fire near Callisto, and a memory stick delivered in a figurine through an
unremarkable transaction in a cabin in the Sierra Nevadas that Marcus had
flagged as low-priority.

He set down the glass.

He had not known about Rachel.

This was the thing he turned over most often. In fifteen years, he had not
known. She had been on the Federation board. She had been on his advisory list
for three separate operations. She had been in rooms with him — not frequently,
but often enough that he had assessed her, the way he assessed everyone, and had
arrived at the conclusion that she was what she appeared to be.

He had been wrong about Rachel.

He had been wrong about Emily Nova.

He had been wrong about Sharon Teez.

He picked up the glass again.

He was forty-eight years old and in exile and under a name that was not his, and
the machine he had spent forty years building had been taken apart in a
Federation hearing by a scientist with a notebook, a news anchor who ran a
resistance network, a woman who had driven across the desert with a road atlas,
and a ship captain who had gone to Saturn and come back different.

He looked at the Gulf.

He had not, in forty years, ever seriously considered the possibility that he
was wrong about the fundamental thing. Not about tactics — he had been wrong
about tactics many times, and had corrected course, and that was ordinary. About
the fundamental thing. The premise. The necessity of the machine.

He considered it now.

The considering took a long time.

When it was finished, he was not sure what he had decided. He was sure that he
had decided something, and that it was the first decision in a long time that
had come from somewhere other than the ledger.

He looked at the Gulf for a while longer.

He set the glass down. The sound it made was slightly too loud in the quiet
of the apartment.

From across the table, a second glass — orange juice, four ice cubes, identical
— was lifted by a hand that had been still long enough to have learned the
silence of the room.

A voice said: "You built the cover."

Marcus did not look up immediately. He had known the voice for sixty-three years
and it had always had the same quality — the particular register of a man who did
not speak until he had finished thinking, so that when he finally spoke the
sentence had already been complete for some time.

He looked up.

Klaus Verguld was sixty-eight years old and looked, as he had always looked,
like the origin of something. Not the source — the origin. The point before the
source. On the table in front of him sat a glass of orange juice. Four near-
perfect ice cubes. He had not touched it. He had been in the apartment for three
days.

"Excuse me," Marcus said.

"The energy market. The Federation dependency. The managed recovery." Klaus
turned the glass without lifting it — one slow rotation, the way a man turns a
thing he has held so long its weight is no longer interesting. "You spent fifteen
years building the cover and began to believe in it. The ledger you were keeping
was never the ledger." A pause. "Your grandfather did not find those drives in an
unindexed room so that his grandson could administer a power grid."

Marcus was still.

"The signal is still there," Klaus said. "It was there before Henning's mine. It
was there before the Saturn surveys. It will be there after this hearing and
every hearing that follows. The Federation cannot legislate the existence of
something it does not know it found." He looked at the water and did not drink
it. "You built a sophisticated machine for the wrong purpose. That is a
correctable error. We will not repeat it."

Marcus held this the way he held everything: without rushing, without reacting,
letting it exist until he understood what it meant. Then he understood what it
meant. He had spent fifteen years believing himself to be the architect. He had
been the cover.

"The structure," he said.

"The structure continues," Klaus said. "The cover changes. That is the sum total
of what happened today."

Marcus got up. He rinsed the glass and set it in the rack. He went to bed.

Outside, the city ran its lights across the water in long amber lines, and the
sky above the Gulf was full of the particular quality of dark that came from a
sky with no clouds in it, and the stars above the city were faint but visible,
and they had been there for a very long time, and they would be there long after
the two men on the forty-second floor had worked out what they were going to do
with the rest of their lives.

---

The town was in the high desert of eastern Nevada. It had the look of a place
that had been something once — a main street with the bones of commerce
visible in the empty storefronts, a water tower with the original name bleached
to illegibility, a hospital that had been operational until fourteen months ago.

The hospital had been operational until the eastern basin grid failed.
Alpha Corp's supply management — characterized in the Federal investigation
as a load balancing optimization — had prioritized urban premium customers
during the shortage. Rural grid nodes were deprioritized. The hospital had
run on backup power for six days. Then it hadn't. Eleven people in the hospital
had died in that window. Their families were still in the town because they
had nowhere else to go.

The Collective had known this. They had not chosen the town by accident.
What they had not known was who told them where to look.

Taylor had been there eleven days when the first person recognized him.

He had a new name. A new license. He had been careful about where he went
and when. What he had not accounted for was the parallel network. The Panama
files had been running for four months. The specific documents naming Alpha
Corp's field operatives — the people who had worked adjacent to the machine,
who had moved through the rooms where decisions were made — had been in two
hundred thousand hands since the night they were released.

Someone in the town had read them. Someone had talked to someone else. The
name on the document had been connected, by a process that took eleven days
and the resources of people who had nothing left to lose, to the man who had
arrived at the bus station on a Thursday with the wrong face and the wrong
quiet.

Three weeks before Klaus arrived in Dubai, Marcus had learned where Taylor
was. He had passed the information to the Collective through a routing with
two extra nodes — the kind of routing that erased the sender and preserved
the message. The Collective had acted on it the way the Collective acted on
things: efficiently, without asking who had provided the source. They had
moved Taylor. They had chosen the town.

They had not known it came from Marcus.

Marcus had done this for one reason.

Forest Service Road 22. The black SUV. The men in sports coats. The attempt
to run Cole Nova and Ricardo Corazon off a mountain road at night, before the
mission launched, before Cole could become the problem Taylor had already
decided he would become.

Taylor had ordered that attack without authorization. Not from Marcus. He had
made the call on his own assessment, using Marcus's network and Marcus's
assets and Marcus's people, and he had not told Marcus that he had done it.
Marcus had learned about it from a deposition summary. He had read
*Davis, Brandon T.* and understood immediately what Taylor had ordered, and
with whose resources, and without whose permission.

He had not forgotten it.

What happened in the town after that was the town's calculation to make.
Marcus had given one name to one routing. The rest was not his operation.
He was forty-eight years old and in exile under a name that was not his.
He was not in the business of regret. He was still, however, in the business
of accounts.

This one was settled.

---

Sharon sat on the porch of the cabin in Sequoia.

It was late October. The Sierras had their first dusting of snow on the high
peaks, visible in the moonlight as a faint whitening of the granite. The pines
moved in the way they always moved in the evening, their slow agreeing sway. The
air had the particular clarity of high altitude in autumn — the kind that made
the stars look closer than they were, the kind that made the distance to things
feel like the right distance.

She had been out here for an hour. The others were inside. Cole was asleep in
the way he slept now — fully, without the intervals, the particular stillness of
someone whose body had relearned the thing that had been taken from it. She had
watched him sleep for a while before coming outside. She did that sometimes. She
was working on not finding it alarming that she found it necessary.

She looked at the stars.

The augmented eye tracked them with its distinct cadence — the precise
calibrated optics doing what optics did, measuring and mapping, processing the
photonic data of four-billion-year-old light arriving in a steady stream. The
left eye received them the way the left eye received everything — with the
particular warm wetness of a human eye that had grown up looking at things and
learning to feel something about what it saw.

She had two ways of seeing now. She was learning to use both.

She thought about that lot at the end of a dead road. She thought about the months she could not
reach. She thought about Bitboy's face in the first moment, and Emily at the
door, and the road atlas on the table, and a copper-wire ring on the fourth
shelf waiting.

She thought about the compound in Cole's bloodstream. About Gracie recognizing
the architecture of herself inside the thing that had been built on top of it.
About a scientist who had built the key before the lock, and a ship captain who
had carried it without knowing, and the particular long chain of small decisions
that had ended with her sitting on a porch in the Sierras in the first dusting
of October snow.

She thought: *what a strange way to come home.*

She thought: *yes. But here.*

On the wall of the cabin's exterior, near the base of the porch railing, someone
had made a small marking. She had not noticed it when they arrived. She had
found it this afternoon, in the low slant of autumn light that caught the
surface at the right angle to show texture.

It was a water droplet. Not sprayed or slapped — careful, intentional, the
stenciled outline of a single droplet mid-fall. The kind of image that had been
showing up in cities for the last two months, since the parallel network
released the Panama files and the Federation hearing ended and the phrase *the
flywheel begins* had entered the language of a hundred thousand people who had
been sitting in the dark waiting for things to change. The marking that the
Collective's street circuit had started using to signal that a node was active,
that the work was ongoing, that someone was here.

She looked at it for a moment.

A single drop. The beginning of something that would compound. The smallest unit
of a thing that, given enough time and the right conditions, would not stop.

She put her hand over it.

She felt the texture of the stencil under her palm.

She looked up at the stars.

The Sierras were quiet in the way the Sierras were quiet — enormous, patient,
indifferent in the best possible sense, containing everything without comment.
The pines moved. The snow on the high peaks caught the moonlight.

She breathed.

She was home.

She was here.

She was going to be more than fine.

She pressed her palm flat against the porch railing and looked at the stars for
a long time, and the stars looked back at her with the particular patience of
things that have been here longer than anything else and will still be here
after, and in the cabin behind her the people she had kept alive in the dark
were warm and asleep and real.

The night held.

Sharon held.

In the morning, the light would come through the east-facing window of the cabin
and fall across the kitchen table and Cole would make coffee in the particular
unhurried way he made it now, and Emily would argue about the route on the road
atlas, and Bitboy would be somewhere he wasn't supposed to be, and everything
