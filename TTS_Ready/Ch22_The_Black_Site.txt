RINGS OF DUST

Chapter Twenty-Two

The Black Site

The building was in Los Angeles.

This was not where Emily had expected it to be. She had expected something
offshore, something in a country without convenient jurisdiction, something that
matched the scale of what she had been dismantling for the last two months. But
Rachel, who had been precise about everything else, had said: Los Angeles. A
converted office building in the tech corridor west of downtown. Alpha's
primary domestic operations center, which ran under a subsidiary name that
appeared in no public registry. The kind of building that looked, from the
street, like any other glass-and-steel tenant floor.

The kind of building that a fifteen-year relationship between Alpha and the
Federation Council had kept invisible.

The DeMint task force had been in position since four in the morning. Six agents
in civilian clothes, two in Federation uniform, Red coordinating with
DeMint's team by comms from a vehicle a block east. Rachel had arranged her
own people on the perimeter — Emily did not know how many, and Sommi had stopped
explaining things she didn't need to know for operational reasons she trusted
implicitly now.

Emily was in the lobby.

She had been in the lobby for eight minutes. The lobby had a security desk and a
building directory and a coffee cart that had not opened yet. The directory
listed tenants by floor number and suite letter but not by name. Emily had
counted fourteen floors. She knew which floor she was going to. She had the
access code that the Collective's technical team had pulled from Marcus's
assistant's phone two days ago, when the assistant had been quietly contacted
and had, after a difficult conversation that Sommi described in three sentences
and would not elaborate on, chosen to cooperate.

The elevator opened.

She got in.

---

The fourteenth floor was not what it looked like from the street.

The corridor was long and low-lit, the carpet the particular shade of gray that
said *we chose this color to be invisible.* The air had the quality of filtered
recycled air that had been filtered and recycled for years — not unpleasant,
just neutral, the smell of a place that had been designed to have no smell. The
suite at the far end of the corridor had no signage.

Marcus was inside.

She knew this because Sommi had confirmed it twelve minutes ago from the
vehicle-mounted thermal scanner that the Collective team had repositioned at
three-thirty, and because the building's security logs, which DeMint's task force
had been monitoring since midnight, showed Marcus's biometric entry at 11:47 PM.
He had been here all night.

This was not unusual. The task force had flagged it as evidence that Marcus was
aware something was moving — he had retreated to a secure location, which meant
he had information he was evaluating and had decided to evaluate it here rather
than anywhere else. He had not tried to run.

Emily thought about this as she walked the corridor. Marcus did not run. Marcus
had been the most powerful civilian in the Federation's unofficial hierarchy for
fifteen years, and powerful men of a certain kind did not run. They assessed.
They adapted. They made calls. They had contingencies for contingencies.

He had not, Emily thought, made a contingency for her.

She used the access code.

The door opened.

---

The suite was empty.

She stood in the doorway for three seconds. The desk was there. A tablet,
face-down, on the surface. A chair pushed back the precise distance of a man who
had stood up without rushing. The window faced east, and the early morning light
came through it in the hard angular way of a sun that hadn't fully risen, turning
the empty chair precise against the glass.

He had been here. The air still had the quality of a room a person had recently
been in.

Emily looked at the tablet. She did not touch it.

She took out her radio.

"He's gone," she said. "Fourteenth floor, east suite. He was here. He's not here
now."

A pause. Then Sommi's voice: "Secondary exit flagged on the east side, sub-level.
Red is on it."

Emily stood in the empty room. She looked at the chair. She looked at the window.
The streets below were beginning to animate with the first deliveries, the lights
of buildings coming on one by one as the sun came up.

She said: "He's not running."

Sommi said: "I know."

"He's making a call."

"I know."

---

She was in the lobby when DeMint's coordinator's phone rang.

The coordinator — a man named Yuen, who had the quality of someone who had been
in coordination rooms for twenty years and had stopped being surprised by
anything — looked at the screen. He looked at Emily.

"It's for you," he said.

He put it on speaker and set it on the lobby desk.

The line was clear. No static, no compression — close, or using something very
good.

"Emily," Marcus said.

She looked at the phone. Sommi was three feet away, watching the street. Yuen
had stepped back.

"Marcus," she said.

"I know DeMint's task force has been in position since four." His voice had the
particular quality it always had — controlled, unhurried. "I know the proof from
Panama went into the parallel network forty-seven hours ago. It's been in about
two hundred thousand people's hands since yesterday morning." A pause. "Your
scientist is thorough."

"Where are you."

"Somewhere quiet." A pause. "I'll be in a position to be found when I'm ready to
be found. Which will be before the preliminary hearing. I'm not going to make
DeMint chase me around the planet. I'm just not going to let him dictate the
terms of my first conversation with his counsel."

"You're going to cooperate."

"I'm going to give a complete account." He said it the way he said most things —
without warmth, but without evasion. "Not because I've had a change of heart. I
want to be clear about that. I'm not contrite. I'm realistic. The machine is
done, and a complete account is the only leverage I have left. The officers who
took specific directives — I can name them. The procurement lines — I can
document them. The Alpha side of the flywheel arrangement — I have records going
back eight years. DeMint will want those. Rachel will want the ones that go
further."

"You understand she'll use them."

"Of course she will. That's what Rachel does. That's what I always did. The
difference between Rachel and me is that the thing she's building has a better
design than the thing I built." A pause. "I should have seen that sooner."

He said it the way a person says something they have already arrived at privately
— the sound of a conclusion being stated aloud for the first time. He had been
there for a while.

"Your brother," Marcus said. "Is he alive?"

"Yes."

"The woman. Sharon Teez."

"Yes."

The pause that followed was brief. The kind that closes an account rather than
opens one.

"Chen," he said. "My assistant. She'll be protected?"

"Yes."

"Good." He was quiet for a moment. "When your brother gets back — tell him he
was the right choice. Not for the reasons I chose him. For the reasons it turned
out he was."

The line ended.

---

Yuen had already moved to the other side of the lobby, speaking quietly into his
own phone. The building was being worked, floor by floor — evidence teams going
through rooms he had left prepared for them. He had known they were coming. He
had left everything they would need.

"He'll surface before the hearing," Emily said.

"Yes," Sommi said.

"He was never going to run."

"No."

She thought about the proof from Panama, in two hundred thousand people's hands.
She thought about Rachel spending fifteen years building something inside the
machine's walls, using its blind spots, placing people in its joints. She thought
about Ray in a park next to a fountain with a concrete heron. About Sommi in a
canvas jacket — a small wrench emblem stitched in dark thread above the left
breast pocket, nearly invisible unless you knew to look.

She thought about all the people who had been choosing, one at a time, to build a
different thing.

She thought about Sharon somewhere above the atmosphere, on a ship she hadn't
seen, looking at the same planet from the other side of it. *There you are. — S.*
Four words routed through the Meridian, ninety minutes old by the time they
arrived. The most precise thing anyone had said to her in months.

"What do we do now," Emily said.

"There's a flight to San Diego at eleven." Sommi looked at her. "Your brother
lands in six weeks, give or take the retrieval window."

Emily looked at the street.

Six weeks felt, at this particular moment, like the right amount of time. Long
enough to breathe. Long enough to stop moving and let the moving catch up with
her. Long enough to sit somewhere quiet and think about all of it — the pier,
the desert, the motel in Surprise, the eleven hundred miles, the burner phone,
the road atlas, the concrete heron listing east in a park in Panama City.

Emily stood in the lobby of a building that was, floor by floor, being taken
apart — quietly, properly, with the full weight of the evidence that had been
building since Conrad disappeared from a keynote in 2041.

She thought: *you're stronger than you think.*

She thought: *I know. I know I am.*

She had not believed it, yet, when she had thought it in a gas station east of
Indio. She had been working toward believing it.

She believed it now.

She picked up her bag.

She walked through the lobby doors into the morning.
