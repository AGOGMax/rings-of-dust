Max in Panama


The flight was three hours and forty minutes. Max slept through one of them, not by choice but because his body had been making decisions without him since the apartment.

He woke to descent. The seatbelt light. The PA announcing something in Spanish, then English, then Spanish again, as if the English had been a courtesy they'd already moved on from.

He did not have luggage. He had a backpack Red had handed him in the car — already packed, his name not on it, things inside he did not remember owning. A change of clothes. A toothbrush, still in its packaging. A paperback in a language he couldn't read. He hadn't opened it.

He came through arrivals into the main hall of Tocumen International Airport and stood still long enough that the people behind him had to move around him, which they did without comment, having seen that before.

The heat hit him outside. Not gradual — immediate, physical, the air swapping itself for something thicker. It was nine in the morning and already hotter than he'd lived in. He was wearing a jacket.

He took the jacket off.

He stood with the jacket over his arm and looked at the street in front of the terminal. Taxis, vans, a man with a handcart full of something green, someone's grandmother in a lawn chair conducting business of some kind. At the end of the pickup zone, a line of pedicabs: three-wheeled, fringed awnings, drivers who had the particular stillness of people who know how to wait and have been doing it.

He had an address on a piece of paper. Red had written it in block letters: CALLE 50, CERCA DE LA CRESTA. Below it: ASK FOR LUCIA. Below that: DON'T TAKE A CAB.

A pedicab driver made eye contact.

Max held the eye contact too long, which was apparently sufficient.

"You go?" the driver said.

Max showed him the paper.

The driver glanced at it, nodded once, gestured at the seat with the efficiency of someone who had done this thousands of times and found no reason to expand it. Max sat. The driver mounted the front and started pedaling.

They entered traffic.

Traffic in Panama City operated on principles Max did not have access to. There were lanes, but the lanes were advisory. Signals existed but were in negotiation with everything else. There was a sound system — horn, counter-horn, acknowledgment-horn, horn of general principle — that carried specific meaning to everyone who spoke it. He did not speak it.

The driver did not seem concerned. He cut between a bus and a delivery truck at a gap Max would not have called a gap, banked hard left on a street that appeared to be going right, threaded a roundabout by logic Max couldn't identify, and came out onto a boulevard lined with glass towers that threw the morning back at everything beneath them.

The city was big. This was the thing he hadn't expected — that it would be this operational, this indifferent to his arrival. He had not thought about Panama as a place. It had been a word on a piece of paper for two days. Now it was a city going about its morning in heat he hadn't lived in and languages he didn't speak and at a pace the pedicab was matching without effort.

A bus pulled alongside and blew its horn for reasons that weren't apparent.

He grabbed the side rail.

The driver looked back. "It's okay," he said. "Bus always does."

They turned off the boulevard into streets that were narrower and lower, the buildings closer together, the sound differently textured — generators, music through open windows, a saw running somewhere up a side street. A woman leaned from a second-floor window and called something down to someone Max couldn't see. A dog crossed the road with the authority of something that had done it many times and trusted cars to have figured it out by now.

The pedicab slowed on a block of storefronts — a pharmacy, a tienda, something selling fabric, something selling something he couldn't identify — and stopped in front of a door painted green. Not recently green. The green of something that had been green long enough to mean it.

"Calle Cincuenta," the driver said. "Near La Cresta. This one."

Max got out. He gave the driver the money Red had put in his jacket pocket — he didn't know how much, he just gave him all of it. The driver looked at it, gave some back, and pedaled away.

Max stood in front of the green door.

He was going to knock on it and ask for Lucia. He was going to do that in a moment. He was standing here first because this was the first time since the apartment that he'd been somewhere without someone telling him where to be, and he needed to use it.

He thought about the fuel cell on his table. About the number. About Alpha's face when he'd shown them — not surprise, exactly, but the specific expression of people confronting something they already knew and had decided not to. He thought about Red and Squirrel in his apartment doorway at six in the morning, with the car already running.

He was in Panama.

He knocked on the green door.
