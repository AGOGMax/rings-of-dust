MERIDIAN VI — THE CONFIRMATION
Slot: after Chapter Eleven

What Kate had not put in her seven-sentence relay was what
happened after she hit send.

She sat for eleven minutes.

The ship-day lighting ran at the same level regardless of hour
— the Meridian didn't track a planetary cycle. The station at
0300 looked identical to the station at 1400. Kate had stopped
noticing this. She had started noticing it again after San
Francisco.

Dayo found her there when he came on shift. He read the
confirmation on her screen and stood for a moment — not rushing,
not looking away, locating the feeling precisely, putting it
somewhere it could be carried.

"Her droplet's on the wall," Kate said.

"I know."

"I put it there when we left San Francisco. I didn't ask anyone."

"You didn't need to ask." He sat in the secondary comms chair.
"Petyr asleep?"

"Yes."

"Good." He paused. "A droplet means you're already carrying it
without knowing what it was. What Sharon had — before the patent
wars, before anyone put a patch on her arm — was bigger than a
droplet. She was already running her own version of the network
before the network found her."

Kate said nothing.

"A scalpel goes to someone who operates without flinching. A
wrench to someone who builds what others walk across. A sunglasses
to someone who sees and carries it without breaking. A compass to
someone who navigates when the map is wrong. A star to someone
who has held the weight of the whole structure for long enough
that it becomes part of them." He paused. "And then there is one
more. I keep it in a locked box in my quarters. It is a star with
olive leaf wreaths on each side — one left, one right. The House.
I have never given it out because the person who receives it has
to have demonstrated something that cannot be assessed in advance."

Kate was quiet.

"Sharon had it," Dayo said. "I was going to send it when the
mission was done. When she was safe."

Kate thought about what the Grand Admiral patch looked like in
that box. She had never opened it, but she knew it was there.

"We're going to get the data out," she said. Not a comfort.
A statement of intent.

"Yes," Dayo said. "We are."

Outside the viewport, Saturn's rings caught refracted sunlight
and held it — a thin, bright edge in the dark. The ship at this
hour made no sound you could name. Only the particular absence
of it: the held-breath quiet that had been held so long it had
become the normal texture of the air. Kate had stopped noticing
it. She noticed it now.

"The network needs people who choose it," Kate said.

"Sharon chose it before she knew there was a name for it," Dayo
said. "That's the thing about people like her. The network
doesn't recruit them. They arrive."
