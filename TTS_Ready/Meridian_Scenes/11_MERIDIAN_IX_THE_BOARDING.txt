MERIDIAN IX — THE BOARDING
Slot: after Chapter Nineteen

There was no good way to watch the thermal signature data and
know what it meant.

Kate had been tracking FED-MRS-0409 for nine days. The biometric
data she'd pulled from relay intercepts had confirmed what she'd
already suspected and confirmed it in a way she wished it hadn't.

"Boarding in progress," she said. Her voice came out steady.
She had decided it would.

No one spoke.

The augmentation signature was in the upper thorax and skull.
Significant modification. The signature was Sharon Teez and it
was also not Sharon Teez, and the two facts occupied the same
space without resolving.

Petyr said, "Is that—"

"Yes," Kate said.

Dayo was standing. He'd been standing for twenty minutes.

What the relay data could not confirm was whether the counter-
program had completed its transfer through Cole's system. The
compound variables were outside her modeling range. She had run
the math twice and stopped the second time, because the math
didn't account for what she actually believed, and she had learned
— slowly, over three years — that there were things the math
didn't account for that were nonetheless real.

"The bronze indicator on the augmentation's central power conduit,"
Loa said, from memory. "If Conrad's counter-program transferred—"

"It goes dark," Kate said.

"Can we read that from here."

"No."

Silence.

"I've been on this ship for three years," Petyr said suddenly.
Nobody told him to stop. "I got my droplet because someone in
Osaka read a paper I wrote about grid distribution inequities
and decided I was already carrying it. I have never been in the
field. I have never run an operation. I watch things happen from
very far away." He paused. "Does it get easier."

"No," Loa said.

"Does it matter," he said, "that we're watching."

The silence this time was different.

"Yes," Dayo said. "That's the answer. The watching is part of
how it holds together. Someone has to be here for the people
who are there."

He did not elaborate. He didn't need to.

Kate went back to her panel.

At 04:49 ship time, the augmentation signature on FED-MRS-0409
changed. The bronze indicator — the central power conduit,
detectable even at this range as a low-level electromagnetic
output — went dark.

Kate stared at her readout for four seconds.

Then she said: "Clear."

It wasn't standard terminology. It wasn't in any Collective
communication protocol she'd ever been issued. But Loa looked
up, and Dayo turned, and Petyr exhaled like he'd been holding
it for the better part of a month.

"Clear," Loa confirmed, reading the same data.

Dayo said nothing. He sat down and put his hands flat on the
console and looked at them for a long moment.

On the wall: the droplet. Gunmetal on gray. Sharon's.
