RINGS OF DUST

Chapter Twenty-Two-B

The Broadcast


The transport burn was the same as the first time.

Rachel had done this route four months earlier, for the prep visit — the first interview, the data review, the two days she had spent on the station learning the layout of what she had helped build from the outside for eleven years. She had braced correctly for the burn that time. She braced correctly this time. The two and a half g hit her like a hand pressed flat against her sternum and held her there, and she counted, and it released.

She opened her eyes on the curve of the Earth.

The window was small and scratched. The Earth didn't care. It was lit the same way it was always lit — white and blue and indifferent to being observed from ninety minutes of altitude — and she looked at it for the count of five because she had learned the first time that this was what she needed to do before she could think clearly about anything else.

Five.

She looked at her notes.

---

The Collective communications team had been on the station for ten days. Four people: two engineers, one director named Osei who had worked in broadcast infrastructure for twelve years before the network he worked for was quietly acquired by an Alpha subsidiary and he had left rather than stay, and one woman named Priya whose specific role Rachel had never been entirely clear on but who had, in the four months since the prep visit, assembled from components shipped through six different Collective supply lines a broadcast studio that looked, on camera, like it had always been there.

The studio was in the science module, which Dae-jung had vacated and rearranged with the particular cooperative spirit of someone who understood that the experiment running in this room was larger than the one he was being temporarily displaced from. He had moved his equipment to a corner and covered it with a cloth that was, Rachel noticed, the exact shade of the background Priya had specified.

"You didn't have to match it," Rachel said.

Dae-jung looked at the cloth. "I had cloth," he said.

The Federation insignia behind the interview position was subdued — not the centered, dominant version used for official proceedings, but the version used in the context of journalism, smaller, positioned at the upper right of the frame. Accurate enough that it read as legitimate. Not so large that it read as a claim.

Priya had thought about this for a long time.

"When they see it," Rachel said, looking at the setup, "they'll understand immediately what this is."

"That's the point," Priya said. "Give them one second to understand it, and they'll believe everything after."

Rachel had spent eleven years understanding this principle from the inside. She had never heard it stated so cleanly.

---

Ray was at the lab bench when she came through. He had the 180-day data on the display — not because he needed to review it, he had reviewed it a hundred times, but because having it visible was a way of being sure it was real. She had noticed this about him on the first visit: he processed certainty through verification, the same data looked at again, the number confirmed not because it might have changed but because confirmation was its own kind of thing.

"How are you," she said.

"Ready," he said. He was wearing the clean shirt Yael had insisted on. "You?"

"Yes."

She looked at the data on the display. The 33-day curve. The plateau. The 180-day break upward, the interior rate jumping to a hundred times, every cell beginning its eleven-day exterior cycle. Three hundred and sixty nine test runs. Three hundred and sixty nine identical results.

There was a second chart on the display beside it: the unlock data.

"Walk me through the unlock experiments," she said.

Ray looked at the chart. "I unlocked cells at different points — some at day 40, some at day 90, some after the 180-day threshold. Different cells, different stages." He paused. "Same result every time. The moment you break the lock, everything stops. The hundred-times interior growth drops back to baseline within hours. The exterior deposits stop forming. The mini-jackpot cycle ends. The one-time Jackpot window — gone. It reverts to an ordinary fuel cell."

She looked at the unlock curve — the sharp drop, the flatline after.

"So you can't harvest it and keep it running."

"You can harvest the exterior deposits. But the moment the lock breaks, the process ends. It's either locked and producing, or it's unlocked and it's finished." He looked at the display. "Alpha harvested the eleven-day output and reset. They never locked long enough to find out what was underneath it."

"I've been thinking about how to open," she said.

"I thought you had the opening."

"I have an opening. I've been thinking about whether it's the right one." She looked at the curve. "I want to ask you about the day you showed Alpha."

Ray was quiet for a moment. "That's not in the prep material."

"No."

He looked at the display. "What do you want to know."

"I want to know what their faces looked like," she said. "When you showed them the numbers and they understood what they were looking at."

He thought about it. The way he always thought — fully, without hurrying.

"They went very still," he said. "There was a man from the executive division. I didn't get his name. He went still in a specific way — not the way someone goes still when they're surprised. The way someone goes still when they've just confirmed something." He paused. "That's when I knew they'd seen it before. They weren't learning what the number meant. They were confirming that I had."

Rachel looked at him.

"That's the opening," she said.

---

The Grand Hall session began at nine in the morning, Federation Standard. The broadcast was scheduled to go live at ten — one hour into the proceedings, after the formal record had been entered but before the first testimony break, at the precise moment when anyone following the hearing online would be looking for context, for explanation, for someone to tell them what they were watching.

Osei had the feed queued. The Collective relay network had been routing to the station's transmitter for six days, tested three times, tested again that morning. The signal would go through the Meridian satellite array — which the Collective had maintained a secondary access agreement with for four years, buried in a service contract that nobody at Alpha had ever looked at closely enough to question — and out to every licensed receiver on Earth simultaneously.

At nine forty-seven, Osei said: "Thirteen minutes."

Rachel was in the chair. Ray was across from her. The Federation insignia was in the upper right of the frame. The light on the lab equipment behind them was exactly the color Priya had specified.

Rachel looked at her notes one more time. Then she set them down.

She had not used notes in an interview for six years. She had used them on the prep visit, for the data review, because the data was specific and she needed to be precise. Today she did not need them. She had been building toward this interview since before she knew what it was building toward — through eleven years of Alpha Corp press briefings and energy shortage updates and managed narratives delivered in the measured register of someone credible explaining why things were complicated. She had made herself useful to the people she was working against for eleven years because the moment you were useful was the moment they stopped watching you.

They had stopped watching her a long time ago.

"Five minutes," Osei said.

Outside the module window, the Earth rotated. She could see the Pacific — the size of it, the specific indifference of that much water at that much distance. She had grown up watching broadcasts from spaces like this — astronauts doing segments, scientists explaining things from orbit, the particular authority of height. She had understood, even as a child, that height was a kind of argument.

Today she was making it.

"Two minutes."

Ray straightened in his chair. He had the specific posture of someone who has rehearsed being relaxed.

"You don't have to look at the camera," she said. "Look at me."

"I know," he said. "I've done interviews."

"Before Alpha fired you?"

"One," he said. "For a materials journal. Nobody watched it."

"People are going to watch this one," she said.

He looked at her. He had the expression of someone recalibrating for an audience size he couldn't picture.

"How many," he said.

"Enough," she said.

"One minute," Osei said.

The light on the transmitter panel went from amber to green.

Rachel Vayes looked at the camera. She had the face she had always had on camera — level, unhurried, the specific quality of someone who has earned your trust in forty-seven previous appearances and is giving you no reason to question it now. She had built this face carefully, over eleven years, for exactly this use.

"Thirty seconds."

She thought about DeMint in the Grand Hall, at the committee table, reading from the record. She thought about what he was saying right now — the names, the procurement chain, the eight years of documentation. She thought about the people watching the hearing feed who were following along but not quite understanding the scale of what they were hearing, who needed someone to stand outside it and say: here is what this means, here is the number, here is what the number does to the world.

That was her job.

It had always been her job. It had just taken eleven years to do it.

"Ten."

The Pacific turned below them.

"Five. Four. Three."

Osei held up two fingers. One.

The light went live.

"Good morning," Rachel Vayes said. "I'm Rachel Vayes. I'm speaking to you from a research station in low Earth orbit, where I'm joined by Dr. Ray Vision — known to his colleagues as Ray — a materials scientist who worked for three years in Alpha Corporation's Dark Matter Stabilization division before being terminated for his findings." She paused — one beat, the beat she had learned meant the audience was ready to receive something. "I want to tell you what he found. And I want to tell you what Alpha did when he showed them."

She looked across at Ray.

"Ray," she said. "Tell me about the day you walked into that room."

Ray looked at her. Not at the camera. At her.

And he told them.

---

The broadcast ran for forty-one minutes.

Osei cut the feed at 10:48, two minutes before the Grand Hall broke for testimony. The Collective relay logged 340 million individual receiver pings in the first hour — more than any single broadcast in the network's fourteen-year operational history. By the time the Grand Hall reconvened, every major independent outlet on Earth had run a segment. By the time DeMint entered the second testimony period, the number in the broadcast — thirty-three days, a factor of ten, unlimited new fuel cells from existing units — was in every feed, every conversation, every government terminal that had been refreshed in the last ninety minutes.

Rachel sat in the chair after Osei cut the feed and looked at the blank camera for a moment.

She thought about the first press briefing she had ever conducted for Alpha Corporation. A quarterly energy update. Projected supply curves. The controlled language of a managed shortage delivered in the confident register of someone who trusted the people she worked for. She had been twenty-nine. She had believed, at the time, in the importance of her own credibility — the idea that being trusted was the same as being useful.

She understood now that they were the same thing, but not in the direction she had believed.

Ray was still in his chair across from her.

"Is that it?" he said.

"That's the broadcast," she said. "What happens next is the Grand Hall."

He was quiet for a moment.

"And after the Grand Hall?"

She looked at the window. The Earth was past the Pacific now — she could see the edge of the North American continent, the light on the coast.

"After the Grand Hall," she said, "the shortage ends. Not because anyone decided to end it. Because thirty-three days from now, every fuel cell unit that was already running will be charging at ten times the rate it was this morning. And nobody will be able to explain why it wasn't always doing that, without explaining everything."

Ray looked at the window.

"Conrad figured that out," he said. "Before he built the cell. He knew the thirty-three-day cycle would be the proof. Once it was in enough units, you couldn't hide it."

"He built the proof into the technology," Rachel said.

"He built the world he wanted into the technology," Ray said. "And waited for it to catch up."

Outside, the terminator line moved across the continent. On the other side of it, in a Federation building in a city she had been to many times and was not in today, DeMint was reading from a record that eleven people had spent eleven years assembling. The formal account. The names and the dates and the chain of decisions.

The broadcast was the other half of it. The part that made the record legible to everyone who hadn't spent eleven years following the evidence.

Rachel thought: we needed both. The record and the broadcast. The Grand Hall and the station. The formal proof and the person who could explain what the proof meant in a language that didn't require eleven years to understand.

She thought: that's why it was me.

She stood up.

"Good interview," Osei said, from across the room.

"Yes," she said. "It was."

She looked at Ray.

"Thank you," she said. Not for the interview. For the eleven times he had run the experiment in a back room in Panama. For the thirty grams in a bag between his lunch box and his headphones. For walking home with his box and being in his apartment when Red and Squirrel knocked.

Ray understood what she meant.

"The bus always does," he said.

She laughed. It was the first time she had laughed in a week, and it was a real one, and she let it be real.

Outside the window, the Earth kept turning.
