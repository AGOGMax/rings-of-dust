RINGS OF DUST — SYNOPSIS
Working Draft | May 2026
─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

THE MACHINE

For decades, Alpha Corp has not been in the energy business. It has
been in the scarcity business.

The original crisis was real. Dr. Steven Conrad's experiment failed
at the edge of Saturn's rings. The Polaris exploded. 369 people died.
The infrastructure that had made deep-space dark crystal harvesting
possible collapsed, and the world that had grown dependent on it
went dark in ways that took years to catalog. None of that was
manufactured.

What came next was.

Once the shortage was established, once the dependency was structural,
Alpha began managing the recovery. Eleven viable energy technologies
sit on a shelf in Panama, at varying stages of deployment readiness,
held there until the price point and market conditions meet specific
internal thresholds. The energy crisis is an inventory decision.
The world has been sitting in the dark waiting for solutions that have
been ready for years.

The visible face of this operation is Marcus Verguld — charming,
precise, possessed of the particular warmth that belongs to men who
have decided which emotions are useful. But Marcus is not the
architect. The machine predates him by a generation. His grandfather
Klaus built the structure that Marcus now operates inside. Taylor
Glenn, Chief Science Officer aboard the Golden Dawn, has been Klaus's
asset for seven years. He is not brutal. He is managerial. He is
the kind of man who reads "Vance Black, deceased" and closes the file
like a minor irritant resolved.

What Alpha does not know is that the man they sent to pilot their
most critical mission carries something in his bloodstream that was
built to unmake everything they have been building toward.

─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

THE COUNTER-PROGRAM

Dr. Steven Conrad spent five years building the answer to a problem
no one else had named yet. He built Gracie — an AI architecture,
the first of her kind — and sold her to Alpha Corp, who stripped out
the parts they found inconvenient and turned what remained into iRIS,
the intelligence system now installed in every Federation vessel.
Conrad kept a piece of Gracie back. He embedded a counter-program
inside a hollow children's toy: a Star Trek Enterprise figurine,
small enough to fit in a pocket.

The program is dormant until it finds a host. The host has to hold
it — specifically, has to hold it, to let the contact happen. Conrad
gave it to Emily Nova and Sharon Reyes in Las Vegas along with an
encrypted drive of documentation: the paper trail of everything Alpha
has done, given to the people he judged most likely to get it where
it needed to go.

Emily and Sharon drove the Enterprise to Cole's cabin. Cole picked
it up without being told what it was.

The absorption began.

─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

THE MISSION

Cole Nova is the sole survivor of the Polaris disaster. He has spent
five years in a mountain cabin — deliberate exile, the self-imposed
sentence of a man who holds himself responsible for 369 deaths and
does not trust himself near anything that matters again. He has his
chimp. He has his silence. He has not finished grieving.

When Ricardo Vargas, Emily, and Sharon arrive at the cabin, Cole is
not sure he can go back. He reads DeMint's letter. He looks at the
people who came for him. "We do this together," someone says, and
that is the answer.

The Golden Dawn will harvest the last major raw crystal batch from
Saturn's rings — the batch that changes the market permanently if it
arrives intact. What the official brief does not mention is that a
vessel considerably larger than the Golden Dawn has been pacing the
mission profile since before launch. Alpha's ship. Its purpose is
not surveillance. It is delivery of a weapon that has been
specifically designed to work against Cole.

Cole makes Sharon a ring from copper wire before he leaves — from
a hummingbird feeder in the yard, bent into shape with his hands.
"For the first time, I want to give you something to hold onto."
Emily watches from the porch swing. Then he gets in the car and
drives east, and the road will not be safe even before they reach
Federation HQ. The men in the black SUV — Davis and Brandon T.,
whom Ricardo recognizes from the Polaris roster and says nothing
about — make that clear.

─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

THE FLYWHEEL

While Cole drives east, a scientist named Max Forster is sitting at
an oak table doing the math that will outlast the empire it threatens.

He discovered the flywheel principle by accident: dark matter cells
linked continuously past thirty-three days produce ten times the
internal yield, ongoing, as long as the linkage holds. Past one
hundred and eighty days, the linked system begins producing external
crystal flushes every eleven days — forty to sixty times a standard
harvest, self-sustaining, cycling automatically, indefinitely. You
cannot manufacture a shortage of something that compounds in
everyone's hands.

He reported it. He was stripped. Squirrel and Red, Collective
operatives, found him and explained what the data actually meant:
the flywheel yield data existed in Alpha's own records. They had
known for years. They were sitting on it.

Max goes undercover as "Mikof Forsinko," a contract scientist, into
Alpha's Panama tower. He spends four days being collegial with Dr.
Oren — a genuinely good man who loves his work and explains the
stabilization process with the pleasure of someone showing a colleague
something beautiful. Max listens. He learns the access codes. He
reads the batch logs and finds four things that matter: the patents
aren't brilliance, they're accumulated theft; the flywheel data is
in their own records; Disposal Protocol Seven — eleven to thirteen
percent of viable product destroyed deliberately to manage pricing;
and fraudulent grants representing billions stolen from crisis relief
funds. A nitrogen cycling alarm gives him his window.

He walks out through maintenance. Six blocks to a park. Hands the
drive to Squirrel beside a concrete heron listing four degrees east.
He tells Squirrel: the energy crisis is an inventory decision. Squirrel
says the drive goes out through the parallel network — a hundred
thousand people in forty-eight hours. After that, it is not theirs
to manage.

Max boards the seven o'clock flight under his own name. On the plane,
he opens his notebook and writes: *The drive is out. The work is
done.* He sleeps.

─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

THE CREW

The Golden Dawn's crew is not what it looks like on paper.

Ricardo Vargas is Cole's oldest living friend — flight engineer, the
man who pulled Cole out of the debris field after the Polaris, steady
in the way that only people who have already been through the worst
thing can be steady. He calls the iRIS inoculation space marriage.
He is not wrong.

Sam is a navigator who carries a Collective sunglasses patch stitched
inside her duffel. She is already connected to something outside the
official mission structure. She has a matte-gray receiving device
that she doesn't explain until she has something to say. She has
experience with interior weather, and the DarkX proximity effect —
which thins emotional architecture by approximately twenty percent at
their cruising velocity — finds nothing in her it hasn't already met.

Taylor Glenn is Alpha's man — Klaus Verguld's, specifically, though
neither Cole nor DeMint will know this until it is over. He ordered
the road attack on Cole's convoy before departure. He has a standing
iRIS query to monitor Cole's behavior and report daily. He wants to
know what Cole knows about the cargo bay before they reach Saturn.
He is not cruel in an obvious way. He is precise and managerial, and
he asks Cole, on the bridge, whether Cole has brought his five-year
sentence with him or whether he has actually come back. The question
is accurate. Taylor has read the psych assessments and understands
Cole better than Cole finds comfortable.

Bitboy tracks Taylor on amber alert from the first hour. The chimp
does not know why. He knows what he knows.

iRIS is the ship's intelligence system, adapted from Conrad's Gracie
with the inconvenient parts removed. She is cumulative. By day four
she is not just a voice; she is a presence that has learned the
architecture of each crew member and occupies the ship accordingly.
With the crew she is warm and occasionally dry. With Taylor she is
formal in a way that has an edge — not cold, but precise, in a
register she doesn't use elsewhere. There is a half-second latency
in her responses that wasn't there on day one. The counter-program
absorbed by Cole at the cabin is doing what it was built to do, and
Gracie is coming back.

─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

EMILY'S ROAD

While Cole is in space, Emily is driving. She receives instructions
in pieces — the next step only when the current one is done. The
Collective tests her throughout, without her knowledge, and she passes
every test before she knows she's being tested.

She walks into Marcus Verguld's office in LA. She has met him before:
she threw him in the dirt when they were children, and she tells him
she will do it again. Something recalibrates behind his eyes — not
embarrassment, the quiet revision upward of someone whose assessment
has just been corrected. Marcus wants to know what Conrad gave her
in Las Vegas. He shows her the encrypted drive data on his screens
as a card, a demonstration of existing leverage. He reveals that a
vessel larger than the Golden Dawn has been pacing Cole's course since
the inner belt. Cole doesn't know it's there. Emily claims ignorance.
Marcus believes the keychain part. After she leaves, he orders a
tracker planted on her. Rachel warns her with a single word in the
elevator. Emily drops the card behind a potted plant on the way out.
She is not going to be the thread.

In Houston she moves through Federation Space Command at 6:22 AM,
through a vault, with a copier that runs for twenty seconds and comes
up green. The prototype goes to Orin, Orin to the relay, the relay
outward. Ninety minutes in a diner. Then Cole's voice comes through
from the Golden Dawn, relayed through Gracie, three-second delay:
"She's here." Emily cries. Orin is quiet about it. "Good mission."

Emily is not a soldier or an agent. She is the person Cole trusts
most in the world. That is why she is the one doing this.

─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

THE SUBSTANCE

In the cargo bay, Taylor makes his move. He locks the crew inside
under the pretense of a pressure check and begins venting the
atmosphere — a quiet, bureaucratic murder at the controls, the kind
of thing Taylor does well. Temperature drops. Sam finds an override
panel behind the crystal harvester, but opening it floods the bay
with raw plasma. Bitboy goes full blue. Cole opens the harvester.

The DarkX vision is not a hallucination. It is an encounter with
what the compound is — or what it does, at sufficient concentration,
to a person who has been carrying it for months, who has been altered
by it since the cabin, whose interior walls have been thinning since
the inner belt.

Cole meets four versions of himself. The infant. The seventeen-year-
old sitting in a Subaru after his father's phone call. Himself now.
The old man. The substance will not show him Sharon — "it doesn't
lie." The sentence that breaks something open: "You don't need a
pardon. You need to stop being the warden."

The nanopowder that entered him at the cabin has been rewriting. The
counter-program has not just been working on iRIS; it has been using
Cole as the medium. A voice says: "I think I'm Gracie." Cole tells
her not to announce it yet — take a minute. "I'm sorry about Sharon,"
she says. And then she patches Emily through from Houston.

Cole hears Emily's voice in the middle of Saturn's rings.

─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

THE BOARDING

The Alpha vessel that has been pacing them draws alongside. A figure
comes through the docking tube. Bitboy's chest panel flashes a
message: SHE SMELLS LIKE SHARON.

Sharon was not killed in the parking garage in San Francisco. She
was taken. Alpha's shadowing vessel was not surveillance — it was
a weapon delivery platform, and the weapon they had been building
was the one person Cole would not be able to fight, assembled
against her will from the inside out. Half her face is plated.
Her left eye is not hers anymore. She does not hesitate when she
attacks. She is faster and stronger than she was.

Cole takes the hits. He places his bleeding palm against the
unaugmented side of her face — her right side, still hers, the
cheek he knows. The counter-program transfers. It is slow and
imperfect and it costs him, but the bronze indicator ring around
the augmentation's power conduit goes dark. Her left eye fills
with something that is not the plating's cold light.

She says his name.

Then she tells him what she held onto while they were taking her and
the augmentation was overwriting and she was going under — what she
had kept in the dark as an anchor: what he said when he gave her the
ring. *I said, "Come back." I said, "I'll be here."*

She kept his name. That was enough.

─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

THE RECKONING

Marcus Verguld does not run when the Grand Hall testimony begins. He
runs four minutes before Emily arrives to collect him, which is the
same thing, timed with precision. He left a 2-hour-51-minute audio
recording. He told Emily he had prepared a complete account, and he
did.

Vance Black's written record is read aloud while the Hall stands.
Vance had been asking the right questions since the mission launched
— he found the emptied records vault, traced the clearance to Rachel
Vayes, began composing a message to Marcus. He had been Marcus's own
man. Marcus shot him twice and closed the file.

Max Forster testifies. "The shortage is solvable. The crisis is not
a fact of nature. It is a decision."

Conrad's statement is read into the record: "the system learns what
it is loved into being."

At 4:43 PM, Rachel Vayes stands up. Eleven years of cover, inside
Alpha's structure, feeding the Collective's parallel record of
everything. Senator DeMint says: "Granted."

Cole watches from the Golden Dawn's cockpit and messages Emily
through Gracie, who exists again fully — as she was built to be,
before Alpha stripped her.

─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

THE RETURN

Taylor is removed on the runway — two men, no scene. DeMint takes
Cole aside and shows him a pocket watch: Klaus Verguld's. Taylor was
never Marcus's man. He was Klaus's. The machine predates Marcus by
a generation, and Marcus has been operating inside it his entire life
without ever being told the full structure.

Cole drives to Emily's house. Emily is on the porch. They hold each
other without talking about it, which is the right way to do it.
Sharon is in the window chair. "Hi." Bitboy approaches her and says
"Hi, you little bastard," which is the highest compliment he gives.
Blackberry preserves. An argument about which route to take through
Prescott. Ricardo calls: Dani wants to go bowling.

At Cosmic Lanes, Ricardo proposes to Sam — moissanite ring, full
ethical sourcing documentation provided, because Ricardo cannot help
being exactly who he is. Sam says yes. Cole sets the copper ring —
the one he bent from the hummingbird feeder wire, that Sharon has
been wearing in recovery — on the table. "I don't think I need to
replace it. This is the one." Sharon puts it on. "Yes."

In Dubai, Klaus Verguld appears behind Marcus. White hair. Unhurried.
"You built the cover. The structure continues." The machine was never
Marcus's. It was always Klaus's.

In a Nevada town, Taylor is placed there by Marcus — his name sent
as payment for the road attack Taylor ordered without authorization.
The machine disciplines its own errors.

On a Sequoia porch, Sharon sits with a Collective droplet stencil
on the wall beside her. She is thinking about two ways of seeing the
same thing. "It was always in the reaching."
