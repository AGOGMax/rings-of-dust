RINGS OF DUST

Chapter Ten-A

Max at the Lab


Max worked at Alpha's materials research division for three years before he understood what they were actually working on.

The official designation was Dark Matter Stabilization. The actual designation, which nobody used in writing, was: they had something they couldn't explain and were trying to make it useful before someone else did.

The something was Dark X. It arrived at the facility in sealed magnetic containers, two kilograms at a time, from a processing site that wasn't on any map Max had been shown. It was black-gray, granular, slightly iridescent in direct light. It behaved like no material he'd worked with — it responded to magnetic field variations the way iron shavings respond to a magnet, except the responses were unpredictable, and the field gradients required were precise to a degree that made the testing equipment protest.

Alpha wanted it stable.

Dark X in its raw form held together for eleven days. After that it degraded — not catastrophically, just gradually, losing its structure, reverting to something that tested as a complex carbon compound with anomalous isotopic ratios. Eleven days was the window. Alpha had been trying for four years to extend it.

Max's job was the stabilization matrix. He ran the salts, the additives, the magnetic curing protocols. He ran them carefully, methodically, the way he'd been trained. He documented everything. He ate lunch at his desk. He was, as his supervisor noted in his last review, thorough.

He was also, by the time the Polaris disaster cleared the news cycle, bored.

---

He took a sample home on a Tuesday. This was not authorized. It was also not, in his assessment, particularly dangerous — the container was sealed, the quantity was small (thirty grams), and he had a specific idea he wanted to test that required more time than the lab schedule allowed. He put the container in his bag between his lunch box and his headphones, walked through security, and went home.

His apartment ran on a residential fuel cell array. Most did. The cells were stacked in a utility closet off the kitchen, four of them, standard civilian grade, the kind that got replaced every three years whether they needed it or not.

He set the container on the kitchen table. He made coffee. He opened his laptop.

The container was labeled with a red stripe and a lot of numbers. He'd labeled it himself that morning.

He left it on the table overnight.

In the morning, he felt it before he could name it.

Not sound. Not heat, exactly — something closer to the sensation of a radio scanning through static, searching for a frequency just out of range. A low resonance below the threshold of hearing, the feeling of a node reaching toward a network that wasn't quite in phase yet. He had been asleep. It woke him. He lay still for a moment, identifying it the way you identify something you've never felt before but somehow recognize, then got up.

The container on the table was warm. He picked it up — warmer than the room, warmer than it should have been. But the warmth wasn't the signal.

He opened the utility closet.

The fuel cell array's status panel — which he had never looked at in three years of living there because it never changed — was reading a charge level he'd never seen on a residential unit. Not a malfunction. Not an error. A charge level. The kind that residential fuel cells did not achieve.

He stood in the kitchen and listened to the resonance, which was still there — the Dark X in the container and the fuel cells in the closet doing something together at a frequency he could feel in his sternum and couldn't hear with his ears.

Eleven days later, the feeling stopped.

The panel read steady. The cells were quiet. Something had completed. He understood this later — the eleven-day sync period, the time a newly assembled fuel cell required to align its internal architecture to the Dark X frequency before it could output power. He had felt the process happen in a kitchen in the middle of the night, without instruments, without authorization, without any preparation except having been bored enough to take something home.

He got a notepad and started writing down what he was going to need.

---

He worked for thirty-three days.

He didn't call it that at the time — thirty-three days — but that was what it was. He ordered equipment through his personal accounts in small enough quantities that none of the invoices looked like a materials research project. He locked the fuel cells together. He documented the result. He ran it again. He verified the charge levels. He charted the crystal growth on the inside of the cell casings — thin at first, then accelerating, unmistakably the same isotopic signature as the Dark X he'd started with, and growing at a rate ten times faster than any unlocked cell produced.

The fuel cell was producing new dark matter. Not outside — inside. The crystals were growing in the locked space between the cells, accumulating in the dark interior of the casing at a rate that had no precedent in any stabilization model he'd ever run.

Not a lot. Not quickly. But ten times faster than it should have been, at a rate that, if it held, would compound.

He stared at the chart for a long time.

Then he went into the lab the next morning and told his supervisor he needed time on the main testbed.

---

He walked them through it in the order he'd discovered it. The eleven-day sync. The thirty-three-day lock — the point where the internal crystal growth jumped to ten times the unlocked rate and held there. The charge output. The darker matter accumulating inside the locked casings. He had photographs. He had numbers. He showed them the numbers in the clear, careful way he'd always showed them things — the same way he'd presented three years of stabilization data — and watched their faces.

His supervisor's face did something that was not surprise.

The man next to his supervisor — introduced as a representative from Alpha's executive division, whose name Max did not catch — had no expression at all, which was its own kind of expression.

"Where did you run the initial test?" his supervisor asked.

Max told him.

The room went quiet in a specific way.

He was fired that afternoon. Not for taking the sample — the sample was not mentioned. He was fired for, as the paperwork stated, a failure to adhere to research protocols and the unauthorized dissemination of proprietary findings. He was told, in language that was careful without being explicit, that the research he had conducted was the property of Alpha Corporation and that any further discussion of his findings — internally, externally, in any medium — would be treated as a breach of his existing non-disclosure agreement and prosecuted accordingly.

He was escorted to his desk and given a box.

He walked home.

He was still carrying the box when Red and Squirrel knocked on his door.
