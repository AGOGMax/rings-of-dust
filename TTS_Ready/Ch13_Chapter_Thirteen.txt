RINGS OF DUST

Chapter Thirteen

Emily checked the car before she got in. This was Sharon's rule and she applied
it now from memory: walk the perimeter, look underneath, check the door handles
for anything that didn't belong. She did it methodically, the way Sharon had
shown her — not rushed, not dramatic. Just thorough. The note was on the
driver's seat.

Emily — Pier 9. — R

She transferred her contacts to a new phone at the nearest mobile shop. Had the
old one wiped. Installed the encrypted messaging suite before she pulled back
onto Highway 101.

The vineyard hills rolled past the windows in a way she hadn't noticed before.
She had driven this road a hundred times and never actually seen it. Brushing up
against death has a way of returning you to the world, she thought. The road to
the pier required paid parking, which still annoyed her even now, with a new
phone and a knife-weight in her chest and Sharon four days dead.

A planked footpath led down to the pier into the smell of the ocean. Seagulls in
the distance. The late afternoon light turning the water into hammered bronze.

Rachel was already there, leaning against the pier railing with the particular
stillness of someone accustomed to waiting in visible spaces without being
noticed. Up close she was taller than she looked on television. The anchor-desk
camera had never quite caught the quality of her attention — the way she took in
a space without appearing to look at it.

"You kicked a hornet's nest," Rachel said. "A big one."

"I thought it was over." Emily stood beside her at the railing. Below them, the
water moved against the pier's pillars in its slow patient rhythm.

"It was never going to be over after the crystals." Rachel kept her voice below
the wind. "There's no cell service in this spot. Not for any of the major
carriers. I've been using it for two years."

Emily looked at her. The news anchor who was somehow here, at a pier, with a
dead signal and a way of talking that belonged to someone much further up the
chain than she had any reason to be. "Who are you, Rachel? Not the TV version."

Rachel was quiet for a moment. She looked at the water. "Someone who has been
inside both rooms long enough to know what's actually in them." She turned to
Emily. "Alpha Corp will take any opening they get. They're patient. They're
resource-rich. And Marcus Verguld is very, very bored with the arrangement he
currently has." A pause. "Our people were supposed to intercept Greenbaum before
he found you. Your friend moved faster than we expected."

"Her name was Sharon."

"I know her name." Rachel said it with a directness that was not unkind. "Sharon
Teez. Collective-adjacent since the patent wars. She ran the best unauthorized
parts network the grey market ever had. We tried to recruit her formally three
times. She said no all three times." A small silence. "She was stubborn in the
best possible way."

Emily felt the familiar pressure behind her eyes and refused it. "What now?"

"Your brother is orbiting Saturn collecting the most valuable resource in the
known universe. He is doing this inside a ship whose AI backbone was installed
by Alpha Corp, with a crew member who answers to Alpha Corp. He is in danger
that he is currently managing but cannot manage indefinitely." Rachel turned to
face her directly. "The man who designed the iRIS system before Alpha Corp got
to it — a scientist named Conrad — built a failsafe into the architecture. A
counter-program. Proof of what Alpha Corp has done and a key for undoing the
damage inside the ship. That counter-program exists as a physical prototype at a
Federation research station in Texas."

"You want me to go get it."

"Vance Black — Operations, Federation Council — is already on his way there. If
the Federation gets the prototype, it disappears into a filing cabinet and the
evidence disappears with it. The Federation is not our ally in this. They think
they are, but Marcus has spent fifteen years making sure they aren't." Rachel
held her eyes. "The Collective needs that prototype to reach Cole before Taylor
does whatever he's been sent out there to do."

"And you can't go yourself."

"I have to stay exactly where I am, visible, on camera, in Marcus's periphery,
for this to work." She turned back to the water. "You, on the other hand, need
to be somewhere no one is looking."

Emily watched the horizon. The sun was sitting on the edge of it, bleeding into
the haze. "Sharon used to say I was doomed to be average."

"Sharon was wrong about that," Rachel said simply.

"She said it affectionately."

"I know. She was still wrong."

The palm trees along the pier path swayed in the early evening wind. Emily
looked at them and then at the water and then at Rachel, who was watching her
with the particular patience of someone who has already made their own decision
and is waiting to see what someone else decides.

"No flights, no cards," Rachel said. "Use the burner. The Collective has a car
staged two blocks from your place — it's already clean. The plates are current.
There's a bag in the trunk: cash, a second phone, directions." She reached into
her jacket and produced a small card — just a number, handwritten. "This is not
my number. But someone will answer. When you get to Texas, use the east entrance
of Federation Space Command. It's labeled maintenance access on the directory.
Someone will be there."

Emily took the card. "If something goes wrong—"

"Something will go wrong," Rachel said. "That's not the question. The question
is whether you're the kind of person who keeps going when it does." She looked
at her steadily. "Based on what I saw in that parking garage, I think you
already know the answer to that."

A pause settled between them. The light was going fast now. Emily turned and
walked back toward the parking lot. Rachel followed a few steps, then stopped.

"The Collective needs people who choose it," Rachel called after her. "Not
because their brother is in danger. Because they've decided the world they want
to live in is worth building." A beat. "Cole would do the same for you. But
that's not what I'm asking."

Emily didn't turn around. "I know," she said. She kept walking.

She got into the Collective car — clean interior, full tank, a bag in the trunk
exactly where Rachel said it would be. She sat for a moment with her hands on
the wheel, looking through the windshield at nothing specific. Then she started
the engine and pulled out.

Highway 10 east. The city peeled away behind her. The desert opened up on either
side with the patience it always had, the patience of something that has been
here longer than any of the things driving across it and will still be here
after. Emily counted joshua trees for a few miles until the counting stopped
meaning anything.

She thought about Sharon teaching her to check a car before getting in. Sharon
teaching her the hand signals for the Collective's lower ranks, which Emily had
treated as trivia for a year and now wished she'd memorized better. Sharon
driving this exact stretch of desert two years ago at four in the morning
because Emily had made a call she shouldn't have made and Sharon had answered
without asking why.

The yellow lines came at her in the headlights, steady and relentless.

Somewhere past Indio, her grip on the wheel softened. Her eyes had been open for
thirty hours and her body was filing the final arguments for closing them. She
caught herself thinking about the water at the pier, the sound of it, the
rhythm—

The rumble strips hit like a slap. BRRRRRRRT — the steering wheel went wild in
her hands, the passenger tires chewing gravel shoulder, a scrubby desert bush
shrieking against the car's flank. Emily wrenched it back to pavement, heart in
her throat, adrenaline rewriting everything in her nervous system at once.

She pulled over at the next exit. Gas station. Fluorescent lights. She sat at
the pump for a minute before getting out.

Inside, she bought two energy drinks and a bottle of water and stood at the
counter and let the fluorescent hum of the place do its work. The kid behind the
register didn't look at her. The refrigerator compressors ran. Outside, a semi
pulled through.

She looked at the number on the card Rachel had given her.

She dialed.

Two rings.

"Go ahead." A woman's voice — not Rachel's. Measured. Unhurried. The voice of
someone for whom odd-hours calls were simply calls.

"This is Emily Nova. I'm eastbound on I-10, just past Indio. I'm going to Texas.
I'm going to use the east entrance." She paused. "I almost fell asleep at the
wheel."

A brief pause. "Pull over and sleep for three hours. Set an alarm. You're no
good to anyone in a ditch." Another pause. "The timeline holds. You have room."

"Okay."

"Are you all right, Ms. Nova?"

Emily looked out through the gas station window at the dark desert and the
highway beyond it and the headlights of a car passing on its way to something
that had nothing to do with her.

"Not yet," she said. "But I think I'm going to be."

She clicked off. She bought a road atlas — old habit of Sharon's, always have a
paper backup — and drove the car to the edge of the lot, away from the lights.
She reclined the seat. Set a three-hour alarm. Closed her eyes.

The desert held its quiet around her, the way the desert does. Patient,
enormous, indifferent in the best sense — no judgment in it, just distance, just
dark, just the stars coming out one at a time above the joshua trees.

She was asleep in under two minutes.
