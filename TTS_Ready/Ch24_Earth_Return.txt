RINGS OF DUST

Chapter Twenty-Four

Earth Return

The Golden Dawn broke atmosphere over the Pacific at 0617.

The entry was textbook. Gracie ran the sequencing with the particular economy of
motion she had developed over the last six months — the ship's systems
articulate and responsive, the heat shield protocols precise, the hull talking
back to the atmosphere in the language that all hulls speak during re-entry,
which is the language of resistance and negotiation. Cole had his hands on the
controls and barely used them. Gracie had it. He let her have it.

Sharon was belted in the medical bay, which they had converted to a secondary
crew station for the descent. Sam had arranged it — the positioning, the harness
configuration that accommodated the augmentation. Sharon had looked at the setup
and said: *thoughtful,* which was her way of acknowledging that Sam had done
something considerate without making it a thing.

Ricardo was at the co-pilot station, arms folded, watching the atmosphere go
orange through the viewport with the expression he wore when he was feeling
something and had decided to feel it quietly. Bitboy was in his harness beside
Cole, both sets of arms crossed over his chest, watching the orange through the
forward glass with enormous interested eyes.

The G-forces built. The ship pressed them into their seats. The orange outside
the viewport went white and then went the blue of high altitude and then went
the blue of actual sky — the real sky, the one with weather in it, the one that
had birds in it, the one they had not been inside for almost two years.

Cole looked at the blue.

He had thought, coming back from his first deep-space mission, that the re-entry
was the wrong way to feel it — the violence of the descent, the heat and the
noise and the press of gravity reclaiming you, was not a dignified way to return
to the world. He had spent years thinking the right way to come home would be
slower, quieter, more deliberate.

He understood now that the right way to come home is any way that works.

The Golden Dawn settled through the cloud layer. The Pacific appeared below
them, gray and vast and running endlessly to the horizon. Cole could see the
California coast — the dark line of land becoming distinct, the fog sitting in
the valleys, the mountains catching the early light.

"Federation Space Command," Gracie said, in the calm register she always used
for the things that mattered most, "in range for final approach. Runway seven is
clear."

"Copy," Cole said.

"Welcome home," she said.

He kept his hands on the controls.

---

The return had been eight weeks.

Sam spent the first of them at her station, the middle ones arguing with
Ricardo about routes they were not driving, and the last of them trying not
to think about the fact that they were getting closer to Earth — which meant
getting closer to the end of this particular closed container of
circumstances, and she was not ready for that.

Ricardo had shown up at her station on day nine with two cups of the ship's
coffee, which was not good coffee but which was hot, and had set one down
beside her without asking and sat in the second chair and looked at the star
field with her for a while. He did not explain why he was there. Sam did not
ask. He came back the next day. And the day after that.

He was not what she would have predicted. She had categorized him early in
the mission: capable, loyal, loud in the way that covered something quieter
underneath. She had been right about all of it and wrong about the
proportions. The quieter thing was larger. Ricardo was a man who felt things
at full volume and had spent a significant portion of his life pretending
otherwise, and in the particular thin-walled atmosphere of a ship running
DarkX at cruise velocity he had stopped pretending. It suited him better.

She was not a person who filled silences with noise. Neither, it turned out,
was he — not when it mattered. They had sat through enough of them together
to know.

On the twenty-second day he said: "I'm going to be honest with you about
something." She had looked at him. He had said it — plainly, without
qualification, the way Ricardo did things when he had decided to do them.

She had not said anything for a long time. Then she said: "I know."

The last three weeks of the journey, Sam had her own bunk and another option
and chose the other option most nights, which was the clearest data she had
collected in some time. She slept better in those eight weeks than she had in
the three years before them, which she filed without analysis. Some things are
better observed than explained. She thought: *this is a thing I'd like to
keep.* She thought about that, with the ship humming around them and the stars
in the wrong configuration outside the porthole and Ricardo's arm where it was.

She kept the thought. She would figure out the rest later.

---

Taylor Glenn was removed from the Golden Dawn at the runway.

He walked off under his own power, which was consistent with Taylor. He had been
under quarters-restriction for the duration of the return journey, and he had
spent those eight weeks in a manner that Cole, when he thought about it, found
entirely consistent with the man he had understood Taylor to be since the cargo
bay: not remorseful, not performing anything, simply processing the arithmetic
of his situation in his careful deadpan way and arriving at the position that
the most efficient path forward was compliance.

He walked off the aircraft flanked by two Federation task force agents. He
nodded to Cole as he passed. Cole nodded back.

The pocket watch was found by the task force during the standard post-removal
inventory of Taylor's quarters.

Cole was informed about it later, by DeMint, who had flown in from Washington
that morning and was standing on the runway apron with his coat still on from
the flight when the Golden Dawn taxied to its bay. DeMint described the watch
with the particular precision of a man who has been thinking about what to say
for the last three weeks.

It was a pocket watch. Old-style, mechanical, the kind that required winding.
The case was engraved on the inside lid with a coat of arms — two rings over a
star, the motto below it in a language that was not English. Underneath the coat
of arms, in small letters, a name.

*Klaus.*

"Verguld," Cole said.

"Yes." DeMint looked at the runway. "Klaus Verguld. Marcus's father. Founded
Alpha in 1991. The original vision was energy research — the company's own
records describe it as a project to advance human access to natural resources
for the benefit of all mankind." He said this with the particular flatness of a
man who has read those words and knows what they became. "The watch was a known
object in Alpha's institutional history. It was displayed in the lobby of
the Zurich office until 2038, when the lobby was renovated and the display case
was removed." He paused. "It was removed by Marcus."

Cole looked at the runway. He thought about Taylor in that briefing room,
fourteen months ago, with his deadpan expression and his practiced indifference
and the hand that had moved to his SmartCom with the casual ease of a man
checking something he already knew was there.

He thought about twenty years of service to an institution built on a
father's watch and a motto in a foreign language.

He thought about his own twenty years. What it cost to serve something that
turned out to be less than what you believed. What it took to keep serving after
you knew.

"What happens to him," Cole said.

"That depends on what he says." DeMint looked at the building. "He has
information about Alpha's operational network — people, locations,
timelines. That has value." He paused. "He's not the kind of man who'll be moved
by the magnitude of the charges. He'll make a calculation. The question is what
he values more — the mission he was sent on, which is over, or the future he
still has."

"He knew about Teez," Cole said.

"Yes."

"He knew she was alive and where she was."

"Yes."

Cole looked at the watch in DeMint's hand. The coat of arms. The two rings over
the star. The name in small letters.

"Keep me informed," he said.

---

The cabin was in the Sierras, in the pines above the valley. Two rooms and a
kitchen and a woodstove and a view of the ridge that changed with the light.
Sharon had been here for seven weeks.

Emily was on the steps when Cole arrived.

She was sitting on the top step with a coffee and the road atlas, which had a
dozen new pen marks he could see from the gravel. She watched him get out of
the car. She watched him walk toward her. When he was close enough she stood
up and they held each other in the way you hold someone when you have not seen
them for almost two years and a great deal of what you have to say can be said
without speaking.

He held her for a long time. She held him back.

When they separated she looked at his face — the same careful inventory Sharon
had done, that Emily had always done, the particular watchfulness of a person
who had spent a long time caring about someone from a distance and needed to
confirm the actual evidence.

"You look different," she said.

"Yeah."

"Older."

"It's been two years."

"Not like that." She studied him. "Like something got lighter." She paused. "I
don't have a word for it."

"I know what it is," he said.

She looked at him for another moment and seemed to decide that the full
explanation could wait for later, when there was more time and more coffee and
the particular quality of an evening conversation that didn't need to end.

"She's inside," Emily said. "She's been at the atlas since this morning."

He had almost reached the door when it opened from inside. A
man came out — middle-aged, carrying a folded jacket, moving
with the purposeful calm of someone who had finished what he
had come to do. He looked up when he saw Cole.

Cole didn't know the face. He knew the reports.

"Hexo," the man said. As if no further introduction was
required. Perhaps it wasn't.

"Cole."

Dr. Hexo put on his jacket. "She worked on grip calibration
most of the morning. Fine motor. It's holding today." He said
it with the directness of a man reporting on a job. The job
was also a debt. Both things were true at the same time. "She
had the pen in her right hand for an hour."

Cole looked at him.

"She already told me," Hexo said. "What I was doing it for."
He settled his collar. "She said she didn't need to hear it
from me. She said I was allowed to keep working on it here, if
I needed to. I don't know whether that was permission or
instruction."

"Probably both," Cole said.

Hexo considered that. Then he reached into his jacket pocket
and held something out. Cole looked at it.

The copper-wire ring. Small. A smooth pebble bent into the
top. The wire still holding its shape.

"She had it on when she came in," Hexo said. "I took it off
before the augmentation." A pause. "I thought someone should
have it."

Cole took it. He closed his fingers around it.

Hexo walked toward the gravel without looking back. Cole
watched him for a moment, and then went inside.

---

The room was bright with the afternoon light that came through the window. Sharon
was in the chair by the window, which appeared to be her consistent choice — it
faced east, toward where the ridge went up into the pines, and in the afternoon
the light came from behind the cabin and lit the room sideways, golden and
direct. The road atlas — a different copy from Emily's, a larger format — was
open on the table, and in her right hand she held a pen.

She was tracing a route. The augmented arm moved with the careful precision of
something being trained — small deliberate corrections, controlled grip pressure,
a continuous low-level attention to the mechanics of itself. Seven weeks of running the hardware, learning the range.

She looked up when Cole came through the door.

The augmented eye tracked him before the left one did — the bronze indicator
fully dim, the conditioning link severed, the hardware operating as an
independent optical system with its own cadence. The tracking was faster than
the left eye by a fraction she was learning to account for: two sets of incoming
data at slightly different intervals, and the brain deciding which one to lead.
You pay attention to the differences. You practice. You get better.

She looked at him.

The left eye was fully present.

She set the pen down.

Cole crossed the room and sat down in the chair across from her.

For a moment they were quiet in the way two people are quiet when they have been
carrying the same thing from different distances and have put it down in the
same place.

"Hi," she said.

"Hi," he said.

She looked at his face the same way Emily had — the same inventory, except where
Emily's was the inventory of a sibling reading for damage, Sharon's was
something older and more specific, the inventory of a person reading for the
particular presence she had been looking for in the dark for seven months.

"You look like yourself," she said. "More than before, actually."

"People keep saying that."

"It's true. You're all here." She turned her left hand on the armrest, palm up —
not a gesture, just a position. "There were days, before, where I could tell
that parts of you had gone somewhere else for the day and you were operating on
what was left."

"I know."

"I used to find it very annoying."

"I know that too."

Something shifted at the corner of her mouth. He knew that shift. He had been
carrying it in his interior for fourteen months and it looked exactly the same
as he remembered and like no approximation of it had ever been accurate.

"There are gaps," she said. "I want to be honest with you about that. Some of
them are closing — Dr. Hexo says the memory architecture is intact, the
conditioning put pressure on the access pathways, not the storage. It's like — "
she paused, finding the analogy — "the books are there. Some of the shelves got
rearranged. I keep opening doors that lead somewhere unexpected."

"That's all right," he said.

"I know it's all right. I'm telling you anyway, because you should know the
shape of it." She looked at the window. "I have you. I have Emily. I have the
cabin and the fire and the blackberry preserves, which someone has very
helpfully brought me three jars of since I've been home, I have no idea who."

"Ricardo."

"Of course." She almost smiled. "I have that lot," she said quietly —
"the end of a dead road at night, which I'd prefer not to but can apparently
not get rid of. I have large chunks of
everything before the mission that feel clear." She looked at him. "What I'm
missing is mostly the sequence. I know the events but sometimes I'm not sure of
the order, or the timing, or which things belong to which year. It's
disorienting. I'm working on it."

"We can do this slow," Cole said.

"I know we can." She said it without needing to be reassured. "I'm not telling
you because I'm scared. I'm telling you because you should know the map before
we start traveling."

Cole looked at her. He thought about the old man telling the boy: *there will be
a day when you sit beside something coming back in pieces, and you will
understand what it took for him to die.* He thought about the love and the fury
and the particular sensation of both at the same time.

He felt them now. He felt something that was not quite fury but was adjacent
to it — directed at Marcus, at Alpha, at the machine that had taken her. And
underneath it, a thing that was quieter and larger: the recognition that she
was here. That she was sitting across from him in a chair by the window, with
her left hand open on the armrest, being honest about the shape of what they
were working with, and she was here.

He thought about his father.

He thought about the Harley in the driveway. About the word *cancer.* About what
it took for a man to die — not just the dying, but the preceding months of
trying to protect the people who would outlive him from the knowledge that they
were about to lose him. The particular terrible love of not wanting to hurt
them. The fury that it hadn't worked. And the release that the old man had given
him in the cargo bay: *the cell door's been open the whole time.*

He sat in the chair across from Sharon and felt the love and the fury at the
same time, and then he felt the release.

He had not known until this moment that forgiveness was not a single motion.
That you could forgive one thing and still be held by another. That the release
happened in sequence — one door, and then, when you had moved through it,
another door behind it that you hadn't known was there.

This was the other door.

His father had not had a different choice. His father had loved him with
everything he had. His father had been a man with a limited body and a long
illness and a family he was trying to protect until the protection became
impossible, and then he had let go because letting go was the last thing a
person could do for the people they loved.

Cole had been seventeen and he had not understood that.

He understood it now.

"Hey," Sharon said.

He looked at her.

"Where did you go," she said.

"Somewhere I needed to be," he said.

She looked at him with the left eye and the augmented eye, both steady.

"Good place or bad place?"

"Good place," he said. "I'm back now."

She nodded. She turned her hand on the armrest again, palm up, and he moved his
chair forward and took it.

They sat for a while with the afternoon light coming through the window and the
pines outside moving in the wind above the ridge and the road atlas on the table
with all its new pen marks, and they didn't need to say anything for a while
because some things don't need to be said with words when two people are sitting
in the same place at the same time having arrived there from a very long
distance.

Then there was a sound from the doorway.

Cole did not turn immediately. He knew the sound. He had known the sound for
years — the specific quality of movement that four mechanical limbs made on a
wood floor when they were trying to be quiet and failing because four mechanical
limbs had an irreducible minimum of noise that no amount of effort had ever
reduced.

Sharon looked at the doorway.

Bitboy was in it.

He was standing at the threshold with the particular posture of an animal that
is trying to assess whether it's welcome in a room — body weight slightly back,
mechanical arms folded, the amber-brown eyes doing their careful scan. One of
the arms was still the replacement brace from the cargo bay incident — Ricardo
had offered to have it properly replaced and Bitboy had refused the sedation
required for the procedure twice and they had agreed to revisit the question at
some future point that had not yet arrived.

He looked at Sharon.

Sharon looked at him.

And Sharon said, with the left eye and the augmented eye both entirely clear, in
the exact voice she had always used for it:

"Hi, you little bastard."

Bitboy made a sound that had not previously been categorized in any of the
behavioral documentation the Federation had compiled on his species. It was
somewhere between a bark and a whimper and a sound a person might make if they
had been trying not to cry and had just lost the argument with themselves about
it.

He crossed the room in four long bounds and climbed into her lap and pressed his
face against her shoulder.

Sharon put her arms around him — both of them, the left arm and the augmented
right, both — and held on.

Cole watched them and did not try to describe what it felt like.

There was not a word for it yet. There would be, eventually. He would find it.

---

Emily came in twenty minutes later with two more coffees and a jar of blackberry
preserves, which she set on the table beside the road atlas.

"I told him about the route," Emily said, pointing at Cole. "She's going to tell
you about the route."

"He went through Prescott," Sharon said, from the chair. Bitboy had migrated to
the window ledge but was still within arm's reach and keeping track. "He does
this every time. He always adds forty minutes to avoid the freeway, and then he
complains about the drive."

"I don't complain," Cole said.

"You say 'long drive' in a tone that means complaining."

"That's just what the drive is."

Emily sat on the edge of the window bench and watched the two of them and drank
her coffee with the expression of a person for whom this was exactly the right
amount to be the third person in the room for.

Outside, the pines were going dark with the late afternoon, the light dropping
behind the western ridge and taking the warmth with it the way it always did
this high up. The Sierras did not do gradual evenings. The air was going cold at
the edges and the cabin would need the woodstove soon and that was fine. That
was what the woodstove was for. The road atlas was on the table. The coffee was
warm.

Cole looked at the jar of blackberry preserves and thought about evenings in this
cabin, a long time ago, the fire going and everyone there at once. He thought
about a kitchen table full of books and diagrams and a mother who had been a
chemical engineer before she decided she was a mother. He thought about a
Harley Davidson in a driveway.

He thought: *I'm going to be okay.*

He had thought it before, in the cargo bay, with Sam's hand on his shoulder and
Ricardo's weight against his side and Bitboy crying in a way he had never cried
before. He had thought it and meant it and then had months of space to find out
whether it was true.

It was true.

He was still carrying things. He would always be carrying things — the crew of
the Polaris, the cabin in the Sierras, the county road in the dark with the
hazards clicking. But the carrying was different now. The warden had put down
the keys. The cell door had been open the whole time.

He was the one doing the carrying.

That, the substance had told him, was a great deal.

He looked at Sharon. She was telling Emily something about the gaps — about a
memory that had come back that morning, specific and unexpected, the smell of
the Sequoia pines at six in the morning when the dew was still on them. She was
describing it with the precision of someone noting an interesting data point:
not making it larger than it was, not making it smaller. Just accurate.

That was who she was.

That had always been who she was.

He thought: *I know who you are. I keep losing the where. I'm working on it.*

He thought: *me too.*

He thought: *we can do this slow. We can do this as slow as it needs.*

Sharon looked over at him and said: "You're doing the thing again."

"What thing."

"The going somewhere else while staying in the room thing."

"I'm right here."

"You're mostly right here." She looked at him with the full precision of both
eyes. "Which is better than before."

"It is better than before," he agreed.

She held out her hand.

He took it.

Outside, the last light dropped behind the ridge and the pines went from gold to
shadow, and the cold came down off the high country the way it did every evening
up here, clear and clean and certain, and in the cabin the four of them — Cole,
Sharon, Emily, Bitboy on the window ledge making sure everyone was accounted
for — sat in the particular quiet of people who have arrived somewhere they
needed to get to and are not yet done resting.

---

That evening, on the drive back down the mountain, Ricardo called.

"So," Ricardo said.

"Yeah," Cole said.

"How is she."

"She's Sharon."

Ricardo was quiet for a moment. "That's all I needed to know." Another pause,
and in it the full weight of a man who had been carrying the knowledge of Sharon
Teez's name on a driver's license in a totaled SUV on a mountain road for
fourteen months, and had said nothing, and had driven. "I told her everything."

"How'd she take it."

"She cried. And then she said she wanted to go bowling."

Cole drove for a moment in the mountain dark.

"When," he said.

"She said whenever you want. She said bring everybody. She said—" and here
Ricardo's voice did the thing it did when he was working to keep it from going
somewhere that would require him to acknowledge the feeling—"she said you've
earned it."

"We all have," Cole said.

"Yeah." A long pause. "Cole."

"I'm here."

"I'm glad you came home."

Cole drove down through the pines, the road narrow in the headlights, the valley
lights beginning to show far below through the trees in the way of something
patient that had been waiting in the same place for a long time. He thought
about a Harley in a driveway and a manual transmission Tercel and the plastic
chair in a hospital corridor and the batting cage behind the first park he had
loved, and he thought about everything that had been waiting for him while he
was gone, and everything that was still going to happen.

"Me too," he said.

"She wants to know if Sharon can bowl."

Cole thought about Sharon's augmented arm. He thought about the particular set
of her shoulders when she decided she was going to do something.

"God help the bowling alley if she can't," he said.

Ricardo laughed. The sound of it filled the car.

Cole drove down the mountain.
