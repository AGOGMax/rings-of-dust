RINGS OF DUST

Chapter Fifteen

Surprise, Arizona

The motel was the kind of place that had given up on being anything specific. The carpet was the color of a decade-old decision. The curtains matched nothing but each other and blocked light the way guilt does — imperfectly, in strips. Emily had slept on top of the covers in her clothes and woke up not sure, for a half-second, which direction east was.

She lay still and let her body file its report. The familiar inventory: legs heavy at the ankles, the low hum of fatigue that lived behind her sternum like a pilot light that never went out. Not bad. A five, maybe. She had worked through eights. She sat up slowly, the way she had learned to — not from pain, but from the body's knowledge that it was being managed rather than simply used, and that management was the price of the things she wanted to do.

She had found the motel at two in the morning, somewhere past the Surprise city limits. The Collective car's tank was at a quarter. She had paid cash at the desk, given a fake name out of habit, and been asleep by the time the air conditioner finished cycling to temperature. That had been four hours ago.

The road atlas was on the nightstand where she had left it. Sharon had kept one in the glove box of every car she had ever owned. It is not paranoia, she would say. It is just not being dependent on things that can go dark. Emily had bought this one at the Indio gas station. She had used it to plot last night's stretch. The pen marks she had made on the Texas page were already starting to feel like a conversation she needed to finish.

The burner buzzed.

She answered on the second ring.

"The situation has changed." Rachel's voice was the same as the pier: below the wind, precise, no wasted syllables. "Vance Black arrived at the research station late last night. He located the prototype. He did not retrieve it the way we expected — he went alone, after hours. The director doesn't know."

Emily sat on the edge of the bed. "What does that mean."

"It means he's operating off-book. Which means whoever he reports to does not know he has it yet. That's a narrow window." A pause. "He's not leaving today. He is scheduled for a Federation briefing in Houston tomorrow morning. He'll carry the prototype to Houston. He won't check it."

"He'll have it on his person."

"In a standard-issue case. Field standard, magnetic latch, biometric." Another pause. "There's a contact at the Houston facility who can copy it. Not steal — copy. Vance keeps his version. The Collective gets a duplicate. If you reach the east entrance before nine tomorrow morning, they can walk you through the approach."

Emily looked at the road atlas. Surprise, Arizona to Houston, Texas. She ran her thumb along the spine of the page.

"That's eleven hundred miles."

"Yes."

"Rachel." She kept her voice even. "I'm not trained for this."

There was a silence that was not unkind. "I know what you are and aren't. The person at the east entrance will handle the technical approach. What I need you to do is be present and mobile and not stop before you get there." Another pause. "You've been doing that part since the parking garage."

The line clicked.

Emily sat with the phone in her hand. Outside, a truck reversed in the parking lot, beeping. The light through the curtains was the flat gray of early dawn, not yet gold, the sky still deciding.

She thought about the east entrance. Maintenance access. She thought about Vance Black — white polo, designer glasses, white sneakers, the way he had stood in the Golden Dawn briefing room with his hands in his pockets and said one week. She thought about what she knew how to do. She had never fired a gun. She had once driven a delivery van for Sharon through a rainstorm at four in the morning and gotten four cases of prohibited optical components from a dock warehouse to a client's garage without a single form on file. She had extracted herself from a parking garage in San Francisco while a man who had just had Sharon killed was still in the building. She had driven here from a gas station in the dark, alone, on three hours of sleep, on a road she'd never been on.

None of that was training. All of it was experience.

She stood up. Her legs reported back with their usual bureaucratic delay — present, functional, request noted.

She repacked the bag in four minutes. She ate a granola bar from the zippered side pocket because she had learned that skipping breakfast was the kind of small decision that became a large problem around hour ten. She refilled her water bottle from the tap, drank half of it, refilled it again. She took her medication — the three capsules she carried in a contact lens case, the ones she had refilled in Pasadena with the last script Andrew had called in before everything accelerated. She checked the cash envelope. She counted it without moving her lips.

She was in the car before the sun was fully over the horizon.

I-10 east was a straight line punched through the Sonoran Desert, and it did not apologize for this. The landscape was the color of unfinished pottery, studded with saguaro in the first hour, then the cactus thinning out as the elevation dropped, then the Chiricahua passes, then New Mexico opening up like a room someone had forgotten to furnish. Emily drove at four miles over the limit and kept to the right lane except to pass. She had set the road atlas on the passenger seat, Houston circled in the same pen she had used last night. The GPS on the burner was off. That was another of Sharon's rules, and she was keeping them now with the particular fidelity of someone maintaining a practice in a person's memory.

Sharon had taught her to drive. Not the technical part — their mother had handled that, in a parking lot off the 134, with the patience of a woman who knew that Emily would need to be taught the same thing three times before she trusted herself to do it. Sharon had taught her the other part. How to read a truck's mirror to know if the driver could see you. How to feel a car handling differently before you thought to think about it. How to use the whole road without using all of it. They had been seventeen and nineteen, Sharon already running what she called errands and Emily was not yet old enough to ask about.

Emily had been in the passenger seat of Sharon's car — a tan Civic with a cracked rear bumper that Sharon drove like a surgeon's instrument — watching the freeway pass.

I'm doomed to be average, Emily had said. It was after tryouts for the academic decathlon. She had gotten as far as the third round and then missed three questions in a row on the European history section. Not through ignorance. Through panic. She could hear the answer forming in her head and watch it dissolve under the lights and the quiet of the room.

Sharon had not responded for almost a full minute. Emily had been looking out the window. Then:

You're not doomed to anything. Doomed is for people who sit down. A beat. You panic because it matters to you. That's not a flaw. That's the thing you need to work with.

But I choked.

You choked once. Under specific conditions. So now you know the conditions.

Emily had not found this particularly comforting at seventeen. At thirty-two, alone in a car in New Mexico, she was finding it something closer to useful. She knew the conditions now. She knew what happened to her body under pressure — the familiar cascade, the fatigue that sharpened rather than dulled when the stakes rose, as if her cells made a last call to reserves she didn't know she had. She knew she would make it to Houston. She would figure out the rest when she got there.

She had not, at seventeen, believed Sharon was right. She believed her now. Not because she had earned it — you don't earn beliefs, you accumulate evidence for them until the weight tips. She just no longer had the option of believing the other thing.

Without Sharon, there was no one left to be wrong about her.

She stopped for gas in Lordsburg, New Mexico. Her legs held on the first step down from the car, which was all she asked of them. She filled the tank, stretched along the side of the car with her palms on the roof, counted to forty, and went inside for a bathroom and a second water.

The man was already at the pump when she came out.

He was somewhere in his forties, wide through the shoulders, wearing a canvas jacket that had seen more use than a jacket that age should show. He was standing at the pump two spots down, watching the counter turn. She noticed his shoes — not because they were unusual, but because they were too specific. Work boots, medium brown, the lug sole worn at the outer heel in a pattern that came from covering ground rather than standing in it. They were the shoes of someone who walked for work, not someone who had walked far enough to need a ride.

She did not look at him again. She got in the car.

He was at the on-ramp.

The thumb was out, the stance easy, the canvas jacket catching the light. He was facing the right direction. He looked like a man who had done this before. Emily drove past him, watched him in the mirror for three seconds, and then watched the road.

She thought: a man who walks in shoes like that does not need a ride on an interstate.

She drove four more miles and then pulled to the shoulder.

She watched the mirror. The car behind her slowed, swung around her. Two more cars. Then his figure appeared in the distance, growing.

She rolled the window down when he reached her.

"Lordsburg to Las Cruces?" he said. Easy, unhurried. Not reading the situation, giving the situation a shape.

"Sure," she said.

He got in. He smelled like coffee and something industrial, not unpleasant. He set a small pack on the floor between his feet and buckled his seatbelt without being asked. He looked at the road ahead. He did not make conversation.

She pulled back onto the highway.

For a few miles she drove without speaking. She watched the way he held himself in the seat — relaxed in the specific way that trained people relax, where the relaxation is a posture rather than a state. His hands were in his lap. His eyes went to the mirror at regular intervals, the same intervals she used. Not surveillance. Habit.

"How long have you been behind me?" she asked.

A beat. Not the beat of someone deciding whether to lie. The beat of someone doing a quick review of what was already known.

"Since Benson," he said.

Benson was forty miles before Lordsburg. Emily had stopped there for coffee. He had been behind her, and she had not noticed, and that was the version she was supposed to not notice. Then he had made himself visible at the pump. That was the version she was supposed to notice.

"Rachel sent you," she said.

"Yes."

She kept her eyes on the road. The desert ran on both sides without comment.

"She could have told me," Emily said.

"She could have." He turned his head toward the window, not away from her — a subtle difference. "She wanted to see what you'd do with an undeclared asset. Whether you'd take the ride and not notice, take the ride and notice, or pass."

"And if I'd passed."

"I'd have followed from a distance."

"And if I'd noticed but not said anything."

A pause. "That would have been the concerning response."

She absorbed this. The highway dipped between two cuts of brown rock and climbed back out into open sky.

"What do I call you," she said.

"Orin. With an i." He said it like a thing he'd said many times and had stopped finding interesting. "Certified Technician, Collective North American Circuit. Clearance four." A beat. "You're clearance two, if Rachel's moving you to three for this operation."

"What's three."

"Authorized field participation. Witness protection, asset acquisition, active relay." He said it without inflection. "Below four, you're not asked to move against anyone. You're asked to be somewhere and get something there." He looked at the road. "That's this."

Emily drove. She thought about clearance three. She thought about the east entrance and biometric cases and what it meant to be authorized for something that required a word like authorized.

"Do you know what she's asking me to get there," Emily said.

"The prototype. Conrad's counter-program." He had already known. "I know the facility layout. I know the contact. I know what happens after." He paused. "I don't know your brother."

"You don't need to."

"No," he said. "I don't."

They drove for a while in a silence that was not uncomfortable. The high desert flattened out toward the state line. Emily ate the second granola bar. Orin declined when she offered the box without looking at it. He had his own water and drank it at even intervals, the way someone manages hydration on a long day. She noticed this the way she had noticed his shoes — not as evidence of anything, just as information about the kind of person he was.

"Your condition," he said, not as a question.

"It's managed." Her voice was flat.

"I'm not questioning the operation. I just want to know the rest schedule."

She thought about it. "Every three hours. Twenty minutes. I need to get out and move." She looked at the road. "I don't need you to drive."

"I know." He looked at the horizon. "Every three hours is fine."

Texas opened up after El Paso like the end of a preamble. The sky was bigger here, or it seemed bigger — the land flat enough in every direction that you got more of it above you, the horizon a complete circle instead of an edge. Emily had driven across it once before, with Sharon, heading east for a reason she couldn't fully remember now. They had stopped at a roadside stand selling whole smoked brisket wrapped in butcher paper and eaten it at a picnic table in the parking lot at ten in the morning because Sharon said time zones were a social construct.

She told Orin this.

He said nothing for a moment and then said, "I heard she was good."

"She was better than that." Emily kept her eyes on the road. The phrase she wanted was not available to her. She was still finding the right container for it. "She was the kind of person who made you think the world was slightly more manageable than you'd decided it was. And then you had to keep recalibrating when she was in it." She let a breath out. "And now I'm recalibrating again."

Orin said, "The Collective tries to have that effect."

"Not the same thing." She said it without sharpness. Just accurately.

He was quiet for a moment. "No," he said. "It isn't."

They stopped east of Van Horn. Emily walked the length of a rest stop and back twice, the flat light pressing down on everything with an impartial weight. Her legs were cooperating. The pilot light was where she'd left it. She did the math on the day: six hours in. Five more to Houston. She had managed worse.

She thought about Cole.

She thought about him the way she had learned to think about him during deployments — not with worry exactly, more with a kind of held awareness, the way you keep a candle burning without watching it. She knew him well enough to know that he was managing. She knew him well enough to know that managing covered a wide range of situations that would terrify anyone who was not Cole Nova. She knew that he had iRIS in his head and Taylor in his crew and a ship full of DarkX and fourteen months until he was close enough to a return window. She knew that Conrad's counter-program, in the right hands, could reach him.

She was the hands.

This was not comforting. It was, however, something to do.

She got back in the car. Orin was already buckled. She pulled back onto the highway without announcing it.

The light changed somewhere around Sonora — the flat overhead white of afternoon going amber, the shadows of highway signs getting long. Emily did not stop tracking the road but she stopped working to track it; something in her body had settled into the drive, the way it does after enough hours, and the landscape moved past her without requiring anything.

She thought about Sharon and average.

Doomed to be average had been the formulation that landed, but it wasn't the original sentence. The original sentence, the one Sharon had said when Emily was thirteen, in their kitchen, after Emily had come home from a school presentation gone badly and sat at the table with her forehead on her arms and refused dinner, was something closer to: You know what your problem is? You see the distance between where you are and where you want to be and you call it failure. Most people don't even see the distance. You're not average. You're just impatient.

Emily had thought that was wrong.

She had kept being thirteen about it for about seventeen years.

The doomed to be average version had come later, when Emily was older and knew better how to be self-deprecating, and Sharon had used it because by that point they both knew the terrain well enough to navigate in shorthand. It had not been a verdict. It had been a joke between two people who had already decided the other one was not going to let them get away with anything.

Sharon was not here to not let her get away with anything.

Emily was going to have to do that for herself.

The Houston city limits appeared as lights on the flat horizon, the particular density of a city at dusk — the tall buildings lit against the darkening sky, the glow of highways and industrial lots spreading out in every direction, and somewhere in the eastern approach, the Federation Space Command complex with its paired satellite dishes and its lighted antenna array, visible from the highway as a lit cluster distinct from the city's ordinary sprawl.

Orin said, "Take 59 north toward the inner loop. The contact will meet you at the east entrance at six-thirty tomorrow morning."

"I know." Emily watched the lights grow. "Rachel told me."

"Tonight you sleep." He said it like a briefing note, not an order. "Not for three hours. The full night. Your body is running on fumes and you're not going to be useful at six-thirty if you're running on the next set of fumes."

"I know that too."

"I know you know it." A pause. "I'm just saying it out loud in case saying it out loud helps."

Emily did not tell him that it did, slightly. She took 59 north and the city rose around her, familiar and anonymous, the way all cities are when you arrive in them for the right reasons. She was thinking about the east entrance. The maintenance access door. The contact who would be there with information she did not yet have. She was thinking about a biometric case and a man in white sneakers who did not know she was coming and might help her anyway, or might not, and she would find out in the morning.

She was not thinking about whether she was the right person for this.

She had made that decision in a parking lot in San Francisco when she had gotten up off the pavement and kept going. She had made it again at the gas station east of Indio. She had been making it with every mile since Lordsburg. At some point you stop re-evaluating a decision and start executing it, and she had crossed that line somewhere in the Sonoran Desert, and she was not going back.

The lights of Federation Space Command came into view to the east — familiar infrastructure, the kind of complex that announced itself without trying, all function and scale. She had seen it from the outside before and never thought much about it. She thought about it now. Inside that complex was something Conrad had built to undo damage that Alpha Corp had done. Inside that complex was a path to Cole.

She found a motel three miles from the facility, the same class of anonymous refuge as the one in Surprise — which felt appropriate, somehow, as if the universe had a sense of continuity about unremarkable places. She checked in under the same fake name, paid cash, carried the bag to the room herself when Orin offered to carry it.

She sat on the edge of the bed and checked the burner one more time. Nothing. She set it on the nightstand. She set the road atlas next to it.

She thought about Sharon saying you're stronger than you think.

She thought about what it would take to believe that, not in the abstract, not as something Sharon had said and Rachel had confirmed and Orin had implied by choosing to follow her, but in the practical operational sense, in the morning at six-thirty at the east entrance of Federation Space Command with a biometric case and eleven hundred miles behind her.

She thought: maybe believing it isn't required. Maybe you just act like it's true until the evidence catches up.

She set an alarm for five-fifteen. She stretched out on top of the covers. She turned off the light.

Outside, the city made its usual arrangements. Trucks on 59. The antenna lights of the Federation complex blinking their patient red against the dark. Somewhere above the weather and the light pollution and the long curve of the atmosphere, the Golden Dawn was moving through the dark between here and Saturn at a speed that made the distance between them feel like a measurement from another kind of mathematics entirely.

She closed her eyes.

Stronger than you think, Sharon had said.

Emily wasn't sure she believed it yet.

But she had made it to Houston. She had made it to Houston on three hours of sleep and eleven hundred miles of desert highway with a mitochondrial condition that had been trying to convince her for twelve years that the world was three sizes too big for her, and she had parked the car, and she had walked up the stairs, and she was here.

She would figure out belief in the morning.

She was asleep before the thought finished.
