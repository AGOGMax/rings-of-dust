RINGS OF DUST

Chapter Three-D

The Void


The ceremony was at 0300 on the seventh day.

Nobody announced it. Nobody sent a notification or posted a time or made any visible preparation that Jill could track. At 0245 she woke up in her bunk aboard the Hound's Tooth — not because anything had disturbed her, but because she had been sleeping lightly for two days and something in the quality of the station's ambient sound had shifted. A settling. A stillness that wasn't absence but attention.

She dressed. She went to the main tunnel.

They were already there.

All thirty-two of the Void-Walkers, and eleven of the Loyalists, including Petra, who stood at the far end of the group with the expression of someone who disapproved of the form while accepting the substance. Tyler was at the east end of the tunnel, near the preparation chamber door. The four cells were on a cart beside him — the same ones she had seen two days ago, their crystalline exteriors catching the amber light.

Nobody spoke.

Jill found a place at the edge of the group and stood there.

---

Tyler began without preamble.

He was not loud. He did not need to be — the tunnel was quiet enough that a normal voice carried the full length of it. He spoke the way someone speaks when they have said something many times and the repetition hasn't dulled it; when they have been waiting for the moment to arrive and now that it's here the words come without effort.

He spoke about Elsa Klinge.

He spoke about the fourteen years she had spent building this colony out of rock and belief and the eleven notebooks she had left behind. He spoke about the day she had arrived with eleven people and one resolution: that what she had found deserved to be handled with care rather than stripped for profit. He spoke about the last entry in the last notebook, written three days before she died, in handwriting that Petra had described to him as shaky but precise.

He did not read from the notebook. He had it memorized.

*The cells are not ours. We assembled them. We stabilized them. We gave them conditions in which to become what they are. But the process they run — the sync, the lock, the Jackpot, the void alignment — this did not come from us. We found it. We observed it. We learned to work with it the way a farmer learns to work with rain: not by controlling it but by understanding when to plant.*

*I believe the void receives what we give it because the void is not empty. I believe the energy that moves through these cells moves through everything — through us, through the line of who we are and who we came from and who we will be. I believe this because I have watched the math long enough to understand that the math is describing something larger than the math.*

*I am going to die in this asteroid. I am not afraid of that. I am going to die and the energy I carried through this life is going to move — forward, into the cells I helped stabilize, into the notebooks my friends will keep, into the people who come after and find this place and understand what it is. I am going to be received by the same void that receives everything I give it.*

*The gold will keep coming. I want my colleagues to keep sending.*

---

The silence after lasted long enough that Jill stopped counting.

Then Tyler lifted the first cell from the cart. He carried it himself — no ceremony, no procession, just a man carrying something heavy and handling it with the care you handle irreplaceable things. He walked to the pressure membrane at the end of the east tunnel. He stopped.

He looked back at the group.

"She came back for a reason," he said. "We're sending her the best we have."

He did not say who "she" was. Nobody asked.

He turned to the membrane and pushed the cell through.

There was the sound of pressure equalizing. The faint hiss of the membrane resealing. And then nothing — the cell was on the other side of the wall, moving into the void at whatever velocity the push had given it, and the void was receiving it, and whatever happened on the other side of that exchange was now beyond observation.

He carried the second cell through. Then the third. Then the fourth.

When the last one was gone he stood at the membrane for a long time with his hands open at his sides. Not in prayer. In the specific posture of someone who has finished something that required everything they had and is letting the empty feeling that comes after simply be there.

He turned around.

He looked at Jill.

"You don't have to believe in the ancestors," he said. "You just have to watch the math."

"What math," she said.

"Eleven days," he said.

---

She stayed.

She didn't decide to. She just didn't leave. The morning after the ceremony she ran the full diagnostic on the Hound's Tooth — actually ran it this time, every system, every readout — and found nothing wrong, which she had expected, but the process took three hours and by the time it was done it was midday and she was hungry and the kitchen had food.

She told herself she was leaving tomorrow.

She told herself this for ten days.
