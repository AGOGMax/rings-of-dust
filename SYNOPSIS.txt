RINGS OF DUST
Synopsis

The world runs on fuel cells. Everyone knows the supply is constrained — a
managed shortage that has persisted for a decade, with Alpha Corporation at
the center of it, promising solutions that keep not quite arriving. What
almost nobody knows is that the shortage was a choice.

---

COLE NOVA is a retired deep-space captain living off the grid in the Sierra
Nevada mountains when he's recruited by Federation Chancellor DeMint for a
salvage mission to Saturn. The Polaris expedition he was once part of
deployed experimental dark matter collectors near Saturn's rings years ago.
The retrieval window is closing. Cole is the right man for the job — and
the only one DeMint trusts.

Before Cole launches, his sister EMILY delivers a keychain figurine from a
scientist named STEVEN CONRAD. Conrad is frightened. He's been trying to
break something Alpha built, and he won't survive long enough to finish.
What's inside the figurine has to reach Cole's hands. He has to hold it.
There is a lock for every key.

Cole launches aboard the Golden Dawn with a crew of four: Ricardo, his old
partner; SAM DRUMMOND, a systems officer with a lightness that makes the
mission bearable; TAYLOR GLENN, a commander who is harder to read than he
should be; and SHARON, whose role on the mission is not what it first
appears. Embedded in the ship's architecture is iRIS — the Imbued Recursive
Intelligence System, Alpha's proprietary AI — cold, efficient, and watching.
What Cole doesn't know is that iRIS is also something else entirely. Conrad
built a failsafe into the substrate before he let Alpha have any of it. iRIS
was always meant to become Gracie.

---

Back on Earth, EMILY NOVA begins to understand that the figurine her brother
is carrying is more than a keepsake. The Collective — a distributed network
of scientists, operators, and former Federation personnel who have spent
twenty years building something inside the walls of the thing they were
trying to fix — is moving toward a single moment. Emily is pulled into its
orbit by RACHEL VAYES, a Federation media correspondent who has spent eleven
years as Alpha's most trusted public face, explaining the shortage in the
language of complexity and patience. Emily recognizes her from broadcasts.
She doesn't know yet what Rachel actually is.

Their first meeting is at a pier. Their second is a war.

---

MAX VISION — known inside Alpha as a mid-level materials researcher, known
to himself as someone who should have kept his mouth shut — discovered the
fuel cell anomaly by accident. He took thirty grams of Dark X home from
Alpha's lab. Left it on the kitchen table. In the morning, his apartment
fuel cell array was reading a charge level that residential units don't
achieve. He spent thirty-three days documenting what he'd found: crystal
growth on the exterior of the fuel cell casing, the charge rate jumping by
a factor of ten, a process that once started kept compounding. He walked
into Alpha's lab and showed them.

They went very still. Not surprise. Confirmation.

He was fired that afternoon. He walked home carrying his desk in a box.
When RED and SQUIRREL — two Collective extraction agents — knocked on his
door at six in the morning with a car running outside, he went with them.

He arrived at Tocumen International Airport in a jacket. It was ninety
degrees. A pedicab driver zipped him through Panama City to a green door
on Calle Cincuenta. He knocked. He was taken in by LUCIA and began fourteen
months of underground research under the cover identity Mikof Forsinko.

He ran the experiment eleven times. Same result every time: the 180-day
curve. On day 180, the crystal growth on the exterior of the casing broke
upward — faster than any extraction process Alpha was running, faster than
anything they could produce in a lab. Unlimited new fuel cells from existing
units. No new material. No bottleneck.

He couldn't explain the 180-day threshold. He could only replicate it. He
began to suspect the answer had something to do with gravity.

When a simultaneous power spike from three active fuel cells on their
thirty-third day triggered a city infrastructure flag that Alpha had people
inside, Red moved the timeline up. Max left Panama the way he'd arrived —
in the dark, in someone else's car, with a bag that wasn't his — and was
transported to STATION LIBRE, a Collective orbital facility in low Earth
orbit that the rest of the world didn't know existed.

In zero gravity, the plateau didn't exist. The crystals grew outward in
every direction. On day 180, the curve broke. Max confirmed everything.

He also played Whiskey Slap with the crew — spin the bottle, shot and a
slap — and was welcomed by DAE-JUNG, a materials scientist with very large
hands, and found that being somewhere nobody expected him to be was the
closest he'd felt to safe in a long time.

---

VANCE BLACK is a Federation Operations officer who has spent eight years as
a double agent for DeMint, working inside Marcus Verguld's orbit at Alpha.
DeMint sends him to a classified Federation records facility in the Nevada
desert — a drive-in movie theater facade with razor wire, a concession stand
hiding a hundred steps down into the dark, a machine gun turret in the
ceiling of a metal corridor, a Hall of Records clerk who has read a great
many classified documents and found that people are rarely as interesting
as the paperwork about them.

He's looking for files on Dr. Arvind Ankur. Equipment belonging to him.
Evidence of what Alpha built and what they did with it.

The vault was emptied last month. Alpha Omega Corp trucks. Authorized by
Federation Representative Rachel Vayes.

Vance drives north on the desert highway and pulls over. He thinks about
what it means that DeMint didn't know. He calls Marcus. He goes to the
thirty-second floor of the Alpha building and stands in front of a desk.

The records were cleared.
They were a liability.
They were evidence.
Eight years.
I know.

He drives himself to a clinic. He sits in the parking lot for twenty
minutes. He decides it counted. He goes in.

---

While Vance is dying and Max is confirming the 180-day flywheel from orbit,
Emily is in Houston. The Collective has positioned a Federation systems
technician named PILAR inside a secure storage facility for three years,
waiting for someone to hand a duplicate of Conrad's original communications
prototype to. Emily is that someone.

In the sub-basement corridor, on the way out, she stops at a sealed door.
Through the observation window: magnetic containment units in rows. Dark X.
Alpha's processing output, stored in a Federation facility because the
Federation doesn't know what it is.

Through three inches of reinforced composite, the nanoparticles register
her DNA. The same signature as Cole's. Conrad encoded a failsafe into the
iRIS substrate before he let Alpha have any of it — triggered by proximity
to anyone carrying the same genetic signature as its intended host.

On the other side of the world, in the basement of the building Cole
launched from, iRIS becomes Gracie.

Emily doesn't know this yet. She turns from the window and says: okay.
Let's go.

She sends a relay message to the Golden Dawn — ninety minutes each way.
She waits in a diner in Houston. Cole's voice comes through distorted and
unmistakably his. Sharon is alive. She kept both of us. Emily cries in
the diner. Sommi hands her a napkin without looking up from his plate.
Good mission. Yeah. It was.

---

Four months before the Grand Hall hearing, Rachel Vayes travels to Station
Libre — same Collective transport, same 2.5g burn, same window showing the
curve of the Earth. She doesn't use the bag. She has been operating as
Alpha's most trusted correspondent for eleven years. She has been the Grand
Admiral of the Collective for nine of them.

She spends two days with Max reviewing the data. Seven test runs, six
operators, triplicate verification. The 33-day acceleration. The 180-day
crystal growth on the outside of the casings. Unlimited new fuel cells from
existing units. The shortage as a choice.

She says: I know.

He hadn't expected that.

---

On the day of the Grand Hall hearing, Rachel Vayes goes live from Station
Libre at 10:00 AM — one hour into the proceedings, routed through the
Meridian satellite array on a service contract nobody at Alpha ever read
closely enough to question. The interview runs forty-one minutes. 340
million receiver pings in the first hour. By the time DeMint reconvenes,
the number — thirty-three days, a factor of ten, unlimited new fuel cells —
is in every feed on Earth.

In the Grand Hall, DeMint reads the formal record: the names, the
procurement chain, eight years of documentation assembled by the Collective
and delivered through channels Alpha never knew to watch.

On the Golden Dawn, the crew watches the broadcast in the chow hall. GRACIE
— no longer iRIS, no longer the cold voice embedded in the ship's
architecture, but herself — has materialized at the end of the table with a
holographic bowl of popcorn. The texture is correct. The steam is correct.
She reaches in and takes a handful. She understands the gesture even without
the mechanics.

Cole watches the broadcast. He finds Emily in the fourth row of the gallery.
He recognizes the posture of someone who has made a decision and is waiting
for the world to catch up. He has seen it before — in a kitchen in Sequoia,
on a pier in fog, on a highway shoulder east of Indio.

He routes a message through the Meridian relay.

Gracie says: already composing. Take your time.

---

RINGS OF DUST is a novel about the distance between what is kept from people
and what they would do with it if they knew. It is about the specific kind
of person who decides to spend a decade building the world they want inside
the walls of the world that exists — not because they are certain it will
work, but because the alternative is deciding it won't.

The shortage was a choice. The proof was built into the technology before
the technology was handed over. The lock was made before the key.

Everything else was patience.
