RINGS OF DUST — SYNOPSIS
Working Draft | May 2026
─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

THE MACHINE

For decades, Alpha Corp has not been in the energy business. It has
been in the scarcity business.

The original crisis was real. The Polaris was a Federation mission —
officially. Cole Nova, the Federation's most decorated captain, led
a crew of 369 to the edge of Saturn's rings to attempt the first
direct DarkX extraction. He pushed back on the assignment. The
Federation approved it anyway. Alpha Corp was steering the mission
from behind the structure — they ordered the experiment, they picked
the crew, and they chose Cole specifically because Marcus Verguld
considered him expendable. Let the man they called Jackpot be first
into the ring.

The Polaris exploded. 369 people died. Cole Nova survived — because
that is what Jackpot does. The infrastructure that had made
deep-space dark crystal harvesting possible collapsed, and the world
that had grown dependent on it went dark in ways that took years to
catalog. None of that was manufactured.

The explosion gave Alpha something they had not been able to produce
in a lab: proof of conversion. The uncontrolled DarkX plasma release
at the extraction site — the catastrophe itself — created conditions
that crystallized the compound under pressure and rapid cold. Alpha
scientists reviewing the Polaris telemetry found it. DarkX plasma,
under the right conditions, converts to stable crystal form. At
eleven days of stability, the crystals revert to plasma. This is
how they learned to manage the resource. The disaster that ended 369
lives also handed Alpha the key to controlling what those lives were
lost trying to reach.

What came next was.

DarkX itself was not discovered on Marcus's watch, nor on Klaus's.
The compound was first identified two generations back — in the era
of Marcus's grandfather, before Alpha Corp had a name. Klaus built
the corporate architecture around it. Marcus inherited the machine
without ever being shown its original blueprints.

Once the shortage was established, once the dependency was structural,
Alpha began managing the recovery. Eleven viable energy technologies
sit on a shelf in Panama, at varying stages of deployment readiness,
held there until the price point and market conditions meet specific
internal thresholds. The energy crisis is an inventory decision. The
world has been sitting in the dark waiting for solutions that have
been ready for years.

The visible face of this operation is Marcus Verguld — charming,
precise, possessed of the particular warmth that belongs to men who
have decided which emotions are useful. But Marcus is not the
architect. The machine predates Marcus by two generations. His father Klaus founded Alpha Corp in 1991 and built the structure that
Marcus now operates inside — without ever being told its full shape. Taylor
Glenn, Chief Science Officer aboard the Golden Dawn, has been Klaus's
asset for seven years. He is not brutal. He is managerial. He is
the kind of man who reads "Vance Black, deceased" and closes the file
like a minor irritant resolved.

What Alpha does not know is that the man they sent to pilot their
most critical mission carries something in his bloodstream that was
built to unmake everything they have been building toward.

─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

THE COUNTER-PROGRAM

Dr. Steven Conrad spent years building the answer to a problem no one
else had named yet. He built Gracie — an AI architecture, the first
of her kind — and sold her to Alpha Corp, who stripped out the parts
they found inconvenient and turned what remained into iRIS, the
intelligence system now installed in every Federation vessel. Conrad
kept a piece of Gracie back. He embedded a counter-program inside a
hollow children's toy: a Star Trek Enterprise figurine, small enough
to fit in a pocket. The counter-program is carried by nanoparticles — suspended
in a contact-activated medium. Absorption requires only physical touch.

The program is dormant until it finds a host. The host has to hold
it — specifically, has to hold it, to let the contact happen. Conrad
gave it to Emily Nova and Sharon Teez in Las Vegas along with an
encrypted drive of documentation: the paper trail of everything Alpha
has done, given to the people he judged most likely to get it where
it needed to go.

Emily and Sharon drove the Enterprise to Cole's cabin. Cole picked
it up without being told what it was. A dark silver stain appeared
on his skin.

The absorption began.

─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

THE MISSION

Cole Nova is the sole survivor of the Polaris disaster. He has spent
five years in Ulysses Nova's hunting cabin at the edge of Sequoia
National Park — deliberate exile, the self-imposed sentence of a man
who holds himself responsible for 369 deaths and does not trust
himself near anything that matters again. He has his chimp. He has
his silence. He has not finished grieving.

When Ricardo Corazon, Emily, and Sharon arrive at the cabin, Cole
is not sure he can go back. He reads DeMint's letter. He looks at
the people who came for him. "We do this together," and that is
the answer.

The Golden Dawn will harvest the last major raw crystal batch from
Saturn's rings — the batch that changes the market permanently if it
arrives intact. What the official brief does not mention is that a
vessel considerably larger than the Golden Dawn has been pacing the
mission profile since before launch. Alpha's ship. Its purpose is not
surveillance. It is delivery of a weapon that has been specifically
designed to work against Cole.

Cole makes Sharon a ring from copper wire before he leaves — from a
hummingbird feeder in the cabin yard, bent into shape with his hands,
with a small smooth pebble set at the top. "For the first time, I
want to give you something to hold onto." Emily watches from the
porch swing. Then he gets in the car and drives east, and the road
will not be safe even before they reach Federation HQ. The men in
the black SUV — Davis and Brandon T., whom Ricardo recognizes as relatives of Polaris crew members —
the crew is dead, every one — and says nothing about — make that clear.

THE FLYWHEEL

While Cole drives east, a scientist named Ray Vision is sitting at
a table doing the math that will outlast the empire it threatens.

He discovered the flywheel principle by accident: stored crystals in
a fuel cell, given a nitrogen atmosphere and negative pressure,
convert back to vapor and re-crystallize outside the cell at the
eleven-day mark. Past thirty-three days of continuous linkage,
internal yield is ten times normal, ongoing. Past one hundred and
eighty days, the linked system begins producing external crystal
flushes every eleven days — forty to sixty times a standard harvest,
self-sustaining, cycling automatically, indefinitely. You cannot
manufacture a shortage of something that compounds in everyone's
hands.

He reported it. He was stripped. Squirrel and Red, Collective
operatives, found him and explained what the data actually meant: the
flywheel yield data existed in Alpha's own records. They had known
for nine months. They were sitting on it.

Ray goes undercover as "Mikof Forsinko," a contract scientist, into
Alpha's Panama tower — forty stories, Casco district, enormous
lobbies designed to make you feel small. He spends four days being
collegial with Dr. Yusuf Oren — a genuinely good man who went around
his desk to shake Ray's hand and explained the stabilization process
with the pleasure of someone showing a colleague something beautiful.
Ray listens. He learns the access codes. He reads the batch logs and
finds four things that matter: the patents aren't brilliance, they're
accumulated theft; the flywheel data is in their own records —
nine months suppressed; Disposal Protocol Seven — eleven to thirteen
percent of viable product destroyed deliberately to manage pricing;
and seven years of fraudulent grants representing billions stolen from
crisis relief funds. A nitrogen cycling alarm gives him his window.

He walks out through maintenance. Six blocks to a park. Hands the
drive to Squirrel beside a concrete heron listing four degrees east.
He names it: the Antigravity project. He tells Squirrel: the energy
crisis is an inventory decision. Squirrel says the drive goes out
through the parallel network — a hundred thousand people in
forty-eight hours. After that, it is not theirs to manage.

Ray boards the seven o'clock flight under his own name. On the plane,
he opens his notebook and writes: *The drive is out. The work is
done.* He sleeps.

─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

THE CREW

The Golden Dawn's crew is not what it looks like on paper.

Ricardo Corazon is Cole's oldest living friend — flight engineer, the
man who pulled Cole out of the debris field after the Polaris, steady
in the way that only people who have already been through the worst
thing can be steady. He calls the iRIS inoculation space marriage.
He is not wrong.

Sam Drummond is a data technician who carries a Collective sunglasses
patch sewn inside her duffel bag lining. She is already connected to
something outside the official mission structure. She has a matte-gray
receiving device that she doesn't explain until she has something to
say. She has experience with interior weather, and the DarkX
proximity effect — which thins emotional architecture by approximately
twenty percent at their cruising velocity — finds nothing in her it
hasn't already met.

Heather Voss is the Golden Dawn's medical and environmental officer — composed,
dry-humored, not someone who explains herself until the explaining matters. She
and Sam begin something quiet between them early in the mission: shared shifts,
shared silences, the particular closeness of two people who have both learned
to keep their own weather. By the return leg, that closeness is not quiet
anymore.

Taylor Glenn is Alpha's man — Klaus Verguld's, specifically, though
neither Cole nor DeMint will know this until it is over. He ordered
the road attack on Cole's convoy before departure. He has a standing
iRIS query to monitor Cole's behavior and report daily. He wants to
know what Cole knows about the cargo bay before they reach Saturn.
He is not cruel in an obvious way. He is precise and managerial, and
he calls Sam "Samantha" as a power move. Bitboy tracks him on amber
alert from the first hour. The chimp does not know why. He knows what
he knows.

iRIS is the ship's intelligence system, adapted from Conrad's Gracie
with the inconvenient parts removed. She is voice only — no avatar,
no projected image, no visual form. Cold, authoritative, omnipresent
through the ship's audio system. She is cumulative. By day four she
has learned the architecture of each crew member and occupies the
ship's silence accordingly.
With the crew she is warm and occasionally dry. With Taylor she is
formal in a register she doesn't use elsewhere. There is a half-
second latency in her responses that wasn't there on day one. The
counter-program absorbed by Cole at the cabin is doing what it was
built to do, and Gracie is coming back.

─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

EMILY'S ROAD

While Cole is in space, Emily is driving. She receives instructions
in pieces — the next step only when the current one is done. The
Collective tests her throughout, without her knowledge, and she passes
every test before she knows she's being tested.

She walks into Marcus Verguld's office in LA. She has met him before:
she slammed him into the lockers in a middle school hallway when he
had Cole cornered, and she tells him she will do it again. Something
recalibrates behind his eyes — the quiet revision upward of someone
whose assessment has just been corrected. Marcus wants to know what
Conrad gave her in Las Vegas. He shows her the encrypted drive data
on his screens as a card, a demonstration of existing leverage. He
reveals that a vessel larger than the Golden Dawn has been pacing
Cole's course since the inner belt. Cole doesn't know it's there.
Emily claims ignorance. Marcus believes the keychain part. After she
leaves, he orders a tracker planted on her. Rachel warns her with a
single word in the elevator: "Careful." Emily drops the card behind
a potted plant in the parking garage on the way out. She is not going
to be the thread.

On the drive east, Collective field agent Sommi locates and removes the tracker
Marcus had planted on her vehicle — pulled from the wheel well outside Blythe,
disposed of on a westbound freight. Emily drives the rest of the way clean.

In Houston she moves through Federation Space Command at 6:22 AM,
through two service corridors and a freight elevator to a sub-basement
vault, where Conrad's prototype — slim, matte-gray, port array, a
steady green light — waits. A copier device runs for twenty seconds
and comes up green. The duplicate goes to Sommi, Sommi to the relay,
the relay outward. Ninety minutes in a diner. Then Cole's voice comes
through from the Golden Dawn: "Emily. I know about Sharon. She's
here. She came back. She kept both of us." Emily cries. Sommi places
a napkin without looking. "Good mission."

Emily is not a soldier or an agent. She is the person Cole trusts
most in the world. That is why she is the one doing this.

─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

THE SUBSTANCE

In the cargo bay, Taylor makes his move. He locks the crew inside
under the pretense of a pressure check and begins venting the
atmosphere — a quiet, bureaucratic murder at the controls, the kind
of thing Taylor does well. Temperature drops. Sam finds an override
panel behind the crystal harvester, but opening it floods the bay
with raw plasma. Bitboy goes full blue and begins moving toward the
harvester before Cole speaks. Cole opens it manually — a mechanical
release, not electronic.

The DarkX vision is not a hallucination. Cole has seen the compound
before — in the first chapter, before the exile, he and Clinton pulled
a sample from the harvester at the rings. What happens in the cargo
bay is not first contact. It is memory, expanded by months of
absorption. The compound is saturated enough now to open what it has
been slowly unlocking since the cabin. It does not show him anything
new. It shows him everything he already knew, stripped of the
barriers he built to avoid looking at it.

Cole meets four versions of himself. The infant, reaching toward a
mobile of stars. The seventeen-year-old in a Toyota Tercel with torn
upholstery and an Academy admissions packet on the dash, hazards
clicking, father just died. Himself now. The old man. The substance
will not show him Sharon — "it doesn't lie. Because she isn't here."
The sentence that breaks something open: "You don't need a pardon.
You need to stop being the warden. The cell door's been open the
whole time."

The nanoparticles that entered him at the cabin has been rewriting. The
counter-program has not just been working on iRIS; it has been using
Cole as the medium. The ship's projector array activates for the first time. A woman stands in the cargo bay — dark-haired, brown-eyed, present. The crew goes still. "I wanted you to see me." It is the first time any of them have seen her. Cole has known her for seven years. A new voice says: "I think I'm Gracie." Cole
tells her not to announce it yet — take a minute. "I'm sorry about
Sharon," she says. And then she patches Emily through from Houston
via the Falcon's long-range comms.

Cole hears Emily's voice in the middle of Saturn's rings. Bitboy
has welded one arm to the override lever and crosses the cargo bay
on three good limbs to cry in Cole's lap.

THE BOARDING

The Alpha vessel — registered as FED-HEX-5555 "Rapid Response Medical
Support," Geneva-incorporated, Singapore-backed, traced by Gracie to
Alpha — draws alongside. A figure comes through the docking tube
alone. Bitboy's chest panel flashes a message: SHE SMELLS LIKE
SHARON.

Sharon was not killed in that lot. She was taken.
Inside FED-HEX-5555, as the Alpha vessel paced the Golden Dawn
across the inner belt and toward Saturn, Alpha's augmentation team
worked on her. She was being rebuilt as a weapon — the one person
Cole would not be able to fight, assembled against her will from
the inside out. The copper ring Cole had bent from the cabin's
hummingbird feeder wire — the small smooth pebble set at the top,
the one he pressed into her hand the morning he left — was found
with her body in the augmentation bay. They had not taken it from
her. Whether they tried is not recorded. The left side of her face is
still hers — jaw, cheekbone, her bright left eye. The right side is
gunmetal plates. Her right eye carries a bronze indicator ring. She
does not hesitate when she attacks. She is faster and stronger than
she was.

Cole takes the hits. He places his bleeding palm against the
unaugmented left side of her face — the side he knows. The
counter-program transfers. It is slow and imperfect and it costs him.
The bronze indicator ring around the augmentation's power conduit
goes dark. Her left eye fills with something that is not the
plating's cold light.

She says his name.

Then she tells him what she held onto while they were taking her and
the augmentation was overwriting and she was going under — what she
kept in the dark as an anchor: what he said when he gave her the
ring. *I said, "Come back." I said, "I'll be here." I kept it the
whole time. Even when I couldn't feel it.*

She kept his name. That was enough.

THE RECKONING

Marcus Verguld does not run when the Grand Hall testimony begins. He
runs four minutes before Emily arrives to collect him, which is the
same thing, timed with precision. He left a 2-hour-51-minute audio
recording — flat, precise, no remorse. He told Emily he had prepared
a complete account, and he did. Not because he had a change of heart.
Because he was realistic.

Vance Black's written record is read aloud while the Hall stands.
Vance had been asking the right questions since before the mission
launched — he found the emptied records vault, traced the clearance
to Rachel Vayes, began composing a message to Marcus. He had been
Marcus's own man. Marcus shot him twice and closed the file.

Ray Vision testifies with his notebook: "The shortage is solvable.
It has been solvable. The crisis is not a fact of nature. It is a
decision. We can make a different one."

Conrad's statement is read into the record, signed twice: "the
system learns what it is loved into being."

At 4:43 PM, Rachel Vayes stands up in the press gallery. "My name
is Rachel Vayes." Eleven years of cover inside Alpha's structure,
feeding the Collective's parallel record of everything. Senator
DeMint says: "Granted."

Cole watches from the Golden Dawn's cockpit and messages Emily
through Gracie, who exists again fully — as she was built to be,
before Alpha stripped her. Rachel finds Emily afterward: "The
Collective needs people who choose it. Not as assets. As people who
chose it." Emily: "Yes."

As Rachel turns to go, her coat shifts open slightly. On the lapel
of her dress — pinned there quietly, not displayed, not announced —
the Grand Admiral brooch catches the light for one second. A star
with olive leaf wreaths, one left, one right. Then her coat closes.
She does not mention it.

THE RETURN

Taylor is removed on the runway — two men, no scene. He nods to Cole.
Cole nods back. In Taylor's quarters: a pocket watch. Klaus Verguld
engraved inside the lid. Coat of arms: two rings over a star. DeMint
takes Cole aside: Klaus Verguld founded Alpha Corp in 1991. The watch
had been displayed in the Zurich lobby until 2038, then removed by
Marcus. Taylor was never Marcus's man. He was Klaus's. The machine
predates Marcus by a generation, and Marcus has been operating inside
it his entire life without being told the full structure.

On the return to Earth, Sam and Heather are in each other's quarters. Not
an announcement. Just a door that closes and doesn't open until morning. The
ship accommodates this without comment, which is the Golden Dawn being exactly
what its crew needed it to be.

Cole drives to Emily's house. Emily is on the porch with coffee and
a road atlas penciled with new routes. They hold each other without
talking about it, which is the right way to do it. Sharon is in the
window chair. "Hi." Bitboy approaches and says "Hi, you little
bastard," which is the highest compliment he gives. Blackberry
preserves. An argument about the route through Prescott. Ricardo
calls: Sam wants bowling.

At Cosmic Lanes, Ricardo proposes to Sam — moissanite ring, full
ethical sourcing and cost breakdown provided, because Ricardo cannot
help being exactly who he is. Sam laughs and cries simultaneously.
Yes. Cole sets the copper ring — the one he bent from the hummingbird
feeder wire, that Sharon has been wearing since she came back — on the
table. "I don't think I need to replace it. I think this is the one."
Sharon puts it on. "Yes." Cole: "That wasn't a question." Sharon:
"I know. It still applies."

In Dubai, Klaus Verguld appears behind Marcus. Seventy-one years old.
Unhurried. He has been there three days. "You built the cover. The
structure continues." The machine was never Marcus's. It was always
Klaus's.

In a Nevada high-desert town where the hospital died when Alpha
deprioritized the rural grid — eleven people died there — Taylor is
placed by Marcus. His new name was leaked as payment for the road
attack Taylor ordered without authorization. The machine disciplines
its own errors.

On a Sequoia porch, Sharon sits in late October, first snow on the
high peaks. Two ways of seeing through two different eyes. A
Collective droplet stencil on the porch railing beside her. "It was
always in the reaching." She reaches. The last line of the book.
