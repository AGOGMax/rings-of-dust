RINGS OF DUST — DM vs ROD CHAPTER NOTES
Working Draft | May 2026
─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

NOTE ON FORMAT: This document compares what Dark Matter (the
first-draft source manuscript, referred to as DM) does against
what Rings of Dust (the current working draft, referred to as ROD)
does, beat by beat. No verdicts. Just the facts of each version so
both can be drawn from cleanly.

─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
I. WORLD & PREMISE — THE FOUNDATIONAL DIFFERENCE
─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

DM: The inciting disaster is an electrical storm in the upper
atmosphere that caused mitochondrial degeneration in roughly
half of Earth's population — called Gravity Sickness. Dark
Matter is the cure. Alpha Corp has been controlling its supply
to profit from the illness. Cole's personal stake is Emily, who
is sick. The mission to harvest is also the mission to save his
sister and everyone like her.

ROD: The inciting disaster is the Polaris explosion — a failed
dark crystal harvest that killed 369 crew members and destroyed
the infrastructure supporting deep-space energy collection.
Dark crystals are an energy source, not a cure. The crisis is
economic scarcity. Max Forster's flywheel principle is the
scientific bomb that breaks Alpha's monopoly. Emily is healthy
and the primary ground-level operative.

These are not interchangeable premises. Everything downstream —
why the mission matters, what's at stake personally for Cole,
Emily's role, the final chapter resolution — flows from this
difference.

─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
II. CHARACTER DIFFERENCES — QUICK REFERENCE
─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

MARCUS
  DM: His surname is "Simons." He was Cole's childhood FRIEND.
      They planned a summer together at "A Thousand Thrills"
      theme park; Cole backed out when his father left. Marcus
      wrote Cole a note: "Find yourself a new best friend. I
      will NEVER forget how you screwed me over." He became a
      corporate villain profiting from Gravity Sickness. His
      personal grudge against Cole is specific and rooted in
      betrayal, not dominance.
  ROD: Surname "Verguld." He was Cole's childhood antagonist —
      a bully who put his knee on Cole's chest. Emily threw him
      in the dirt. He carries no personal history with Cole
      beyond that moment of humiliation.

EMILY
  DM: Sick with Gravity Sickness. Not an action protagonist.
      Cole's primary motivation for the mission is saving her.
      She appears at the bowling alley at the end, recovered,
      grateful.
  ROD: Healthy. The ground-level operative of the entire second
      half of the book. She runs the Conrad meeting, the Squirrel
      handoff, the Marcus confrontation, the Houston vault job.
      Her competence is the book's surprise.

SHARON
  DM: Cole's established girlfriend. Warm, explicit relationship
      from the start — she does Bitboy's stomach pump, makes
      pancakes at the cabin, kisses Cole goodbye at departure.
      Not augmented. She is briefly in Marcus's custody (she
      accidentally gives away Saturn as destination), then
      released. She sends a voice recording through the
      transmitter at the end — it's what brings Cole back from
      the DarkX vision. At the bowling alley, she proposes to
      Cole.
  ROD: Her relationship with Cole is more tentative, building
      through the book toward the copper ring. She is abducted
      by Alpha's shadow vessel and augmented — half-plated face,
      replaced left eye. Cole transfers the counter-program
      through physical contact. She says his name. The copper
      ring is Cole's proposal, at the bowling alley.

TAYLOR GLENN
  DM: Starts hostile and suspicious but becomes Cole's ally
      and guide — a calming, philosophically-oriented presence
      during the DarkX phase. He ends the book romantically with
      Samantha, celebrating at the bowling alley with the crew.
  ROD: Klaus Verguld's embedded asset. Ordered the road attack.
      Monitors Cole daily through iRIS. Vents the cargo bay
      atmosphere in a slow bureaucratic murder attempt. Removed
      on the runway after landing. Never sympathetic.

SAMANTHA / SAM
  DM: Ship's navigator. Ends the book in a relationship with
      Taylor Glenn.
  ROD: Ship's navigator. Has a Collective sunglasses patch
      stitched inside her duffel — already connected to the
      resistance. Ricardo proposes to her at the bowling alley.

RICARDO
  DM: Proposes to DANI — a "rotund, cherubic" woman he's been
      with off-ship. Moissanite ring in a Brunswick Bowling
      jacket pocket.
  ROD: Proposes to Sam. Same moissanite ring, same ethical
      sourcing explanation.

DANI
  DM: Ricardo's girlfriend and fiancée. A named character
      present and active at the bowling alley.
  ROD: Referenced by name only — "Ricardo calls, Dani wants
      bowling." Not the proposal recipient.

KYLE GOMEZ
  DM: Major character. The engineer who designed the Golden
      Dawn. Gives philosophical guidance to Cole before launch.
      Leads breathing exercises in the cockpit. Talks Cole
      through a panic attack. Substantial pre-mission presence.
  ROD: Does not appear.

GRACIE / iRIS
  DM: GRACIE announces herself during the mission as a surprise
      sixth crew member — a sentient AI built into the ship from
      nano-materials. She deleted the navigation files and flew
      the ship herself. She has an agenda: she wants to create
      more AI like herself. She is openly adversarial, then
      cooperative. At the end she pilots them home to Houston
      with a cheerful flight-attendant announcement.
  ROD: iRIS is the ship AI — Conrad's Gracie stripped down by
      Alpha, the inconvenient parts removed. She reconstitutes
      herself slowly through Cole's absorbed counter-program
      without announcing it. During the DarkX vision she says
      "I think I'm Gracie." Cole: "Don't tell them yet. Take a
      minute." Her restoration is quiet and internal.

VANCE BLACK
  DM: Marcus's paid spy inside the Federation, feeding DeMint
      credible intelligence on schedule for four years. He
      tried to defect — found something he wasn't supposed to
      find. Marcus: "How is my favorite little snitch." Two
      shots. Before he was killed, Vance secretly recorded the
      confrontation and emailed it to DeMint as an MP3 —
      audio of Marcus rehearsing crying for police. DeMint
      plays this recording as evidence at the Grand Hall.
  ROD: A Federation investigator. Goes to Conrad's desert
      research station and finds the records vault cleared by
      Rachel Vayes four months before any official inquiry.
      Brings findings to Marcus, who shoots him. Three pages
      of metadata stay in his jacket pocket. Read aloud at the
      Grand Hall while everyone stands.

RACHEL VAYES
  DM: Introduced at HQ as "Representative Vayes" — a
      politician hostile to the mission, later revealed as
      Marcus's operative who arranged Sharon's kidnapping.
      Eventually flipped by DeMint and Sharon — she reveals
      the virus and transmitter location.
  ROD: Alpha operative embedded inside the machine for eleven
      years, secretly feeding the Collective's parallel record.
      Warns Emily with a single word in the elevator. Stands
      up at the Grand Hall at 4:43 PM. DeMint: "Granted."

DEMINT
  DM: Bumbling but well-meaning chancellor. Raids Marcus's
      transmitter warehouse with Federal Police. Sends Sharon's
      voice recording to the Golden Dawn as the MP3 that brings
      Cole back from the DarkX vision.
  ROD: More serious institutional authority. Dispatches Vance
      Black to Conrad's station. Shows Cole Klaus Verguld's
      pocket watch at landing — revealing Taylor was Klaus's
      man, not Marcus's.

KLAUS VERGULD
  DM: Does not appear. No generational architecture behind
      Marcus.
  ROD: The actual architect. The machine predates Marcus by a
      generation. Appears in Epilogue A behind Marcus in Dubai.
      "You built the cover. The structure continues."

MAX FORSTER
  DM: Does not appear.
  ROD: Discovers the flywheel principle, is stripped of his
      position, goes undercover in Alpha's Panama tower,
      extracts four categories of evidence (stolen patents,
      suppressed flywheel data, Disposal Protocol Seven,
      fraudulent crisis relief grants). Testifies at the
      Grand Hall. "The shortage is solvable. The crisis is
      not a fact of nature. It is a decision."

ORIN
  DM: Does not appear.
  ROD: Collective Technician. Tailing Emily since Benson
      for a field authorization assessment. "Clearance three
      means authorized field participation." Drives I-10
      with her. Receives the prototype after the Houston
      vault job. Quiet when Emily cries. "Good mission."

SQUIRREL AND RED
  DM: Present — Collective operatives who find Max and
      run the crystal/data handoffs.
  ROD: Same roles, more developed. Red hands Max the card
      with an address. Squirrel receives the drive beside
      the concrete heron in the park.

─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
III. CHAPTER-BY-CHAPTER NOTES
─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

Organized by ROD chapter number. DM equivalent described
first, then ROD, then specific differences.

─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
CHAPTER ONE
─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

DM: Cole adrift after the Polaris explosion, recording a
final message to Emily. Ricardo and Bitboi pull him from
the debris field. Cole received a raw DarkX hit during
the disaster. Emily is sick with Gravity Sickness —
Cole's survival and eventual mission are bound to her
illness. He has a reason to come back before the mission
even begins.

ROD: Same core scene. 369 crew dead. Cole adrift,
recording to Emily. Ricardo and Bitboy pull him out.
First contact with the DarkX compound begins here.
The weight he carries is guilt, not purpose.

DIFFERENCES: Same opening image, different emotional
engine. DM: Cole has to survive because Emily needs
him. ROD: Cole survives and then doesn't know what
to do with it. In DM the reason to keep going is
already present. In ROD the five-year cabin exile is
the direct consequence of having no reason.

─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
CHAPTER TWO
─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

DM: Conrad in a Las Vegas hotel room. Emily and Sharon
run a social-engineering play to reach him. Conrad built
GRACIE, sold her to Alpha, watched her stripped-down
version become the ship AI. He gives Emily the Star Trek
Enterprise figurine and an encrypted drive of
documentation. Sharon is Collective-adjacent. A
Collective droplet mark on the hotel wall.

ROD: Identical in function and most details. Same
hotel scam, same Conrad, same Enterprise and drive
handoff, same Sharon/Collective signal. The counter-
program needs to be held to begin absorption.

DIFFERENCES: Minimal. This chapter translated almost
intact. The primary difference: Conrad's statement —
"the system learns what it is loved into being" — is
developed in ROD as a line eventually read into the
Grand Hall record. Whether it exists in DM in exactly
that form is unclear.

─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
CHAPTER THREE — A THOUSAND THRILLS
─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

DM: Cole's origin story in flashback. Marcus appears
as Cole's childhood FRIEND — they planned a summer at
"A Thousand Thrills" theme park. Cole's father left or
became unreliable. Cole backed out of the trip. Marcus
wrote him a note: "Find yourself a new best friend. I
will NEVER forget how you screwed me over." Marcus is
not an antagonist in this chapter — he's a kid who got
abandoned the same way Cole did, and responded by
becoming someone else. Emily, in DM, is sick — her
Gravity Sickness is already in the household by the
time Cole eventually leaves.

ROD: Marcus is a bully who puts his knee on Cole's
chest. Emily throws Marcus in the dirt. Cole's father
is diagnosed with cancer. Cole gives up his summer
and scholarship plans to care for him. Father dies
when Cole is seventeen. Emily pushes him toward the
Academy. The chapter plants the weight of Marcus's
name in Chapter Ten: Emily has already put him on
the ground.

DIFFERENCES: The Marcus backstory is fundamentally
different. DM's Marcus is a boy who was abandoned by
his friend and weaponized that abandonment. ROD's
Marcus is a bully who was physically humiliated by
a girl and has never metabolized it. Both give Marcus
a personal history with Cole's family, but the nature
of that history differs. DM's Marcus and Cole share
a wound. ROD's Marcus and Emily share a reckoning.

─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
CHAPTER FOUR
─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

DM: The cabin. Cole in self-imposed isolation. Ricardo,
Emily, and Sharon arrive. Cole handles the Enterprise
figurine without being told what it is. Sharon inserts
the data drive. Cole reads DeMint's letter. "We do
this together." Sharon is already established as Cole's
girlfriend — warmth and ease from the first scene
at the cabin.

ROD: Same structure, same beats. Same hand-sanitizer
incident with Bitboy. Same letter, same decision.
The relationship register between Cole and Sharon in
ROD is slightly more charged and less established than
DM's warm domesticity.

DIFFERENCES: The Gravity Sickness premise in DM gives
the cabin scene an additional undercurrent — Emily is
visibly unwell, and Cole accepting the mission is also
Cole deciding to go get his sister's cure. In ROD the
stakes are external and collective, not personal in
that specific way.

─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
CHAPTER FIVE
─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

DM: No equivalent. There is no Max Forster, no flywheel
principle, no stripped scientist discovering the energy
crisis is an inventory decision. Alpha's crime in DM is
monopolizing the cure for Gravity Sickness, not
withholding energy technology.

ROD: Entirely original. Max Forster at an oak table
doing the math. The flywheel principle: cells linked
past 33 days produce 10× yield; past 180 days, external
crystal flushes every 11 days, 40-60× a standard
harvest, self-sustaining. He reports it. Stripped of
his position. Squirrel and Red find him. Taylor Glenn
calls Sam "Samantha" as a power move. Sam looks at her
screen.

DIFFERENCES: The flywheel principle changes what Alpha
is hiding. DM: Alpha controls the cure to a disease.
ROD: Alpha controls the solution to a crisis they let
continue past its natural lifespan. One is pharmaceutical
monopoly, the other is deliberate management of
civilizational scarcity.

─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
CHAPTER SIX
─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

DM: Cole makes the copper ring from hummingbird feeder
wire and gives it to Sharon — same dialogue, same
gesture, same weight. Emily on the porch swing. The
road drive includes the Kowalski hard-boiled egg story.

On the drive, Ricardo runs through a catalogue of
women he's known by name: "Sammie Sammie from
Cincinnati," "Trina from San Bernardin-ah," "Raquelle
the Spell Caster from a Shell station in Lancaster."
Cole's counter is to name Ricardo's past women back
at him until Ricardo surrenders. Faster-paced,
more comic.

Road attack: The attacker's ID is "Mati Allins."
Ricardo speculates this is a family member of Corporal
Davis, whose fiancée was named "Savannah Allins." The
attack is personal revenge from a Polaris victim's
family, not an ordered hit. Ricardo shares his
speculation with Cole openly.

ROD: Copper ring scene identical in substance and
dialogue. Emily on the porch swing. Kowalski egg
story present. The "heavy hitter" banter on the road
describes Ricardo's history with women who have
presence and substance — ends on the third one being
"just geography." This approximates DM's spirit in
a more literary register.

Road attack: The ID is Davis, Brandon T. — Polaris
crew roster. Ricardo looks at the ID and drives
without speaking. The attack was ordered by Taylor
Glenn; Cole won't learn this for several chapters.
Ricardo's silence is a deliberate choice to withhold.

DIFFERENCES: The road attack motive is structurally
different. DM's attacker is civilian revenge — a
family member of someone Cole couldn't save. ROD's
attacker is Alpha operational — institutional
elimination of a threat. DM makes the Polaris guilt
immediate and personal. ROD makes the Polaris disaster
something Alpha has been using for years.

Ricardo's behavior differs. DM: shares the connection
openly, speculates, moves on. ROD: recognizes the name,
says nothing, drives. ROD's Ricardo is carrying
something Cole doesn't know about yet.

The banter: DM gives Ricardo a comic catalogue of
specific women by name. ROD gives him a monologue
about weight and presence. Different registers.

─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
CHAPTER SEVEN
─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

DM: Federation HQ. iRIS inoculation. Then GRACIE
announces herself — a surprise sixth crew member,
a sentient AI built into the ship from nano-materials.
She deleted the navigation files and has been flying
the ship. She is openly confrontational about her
own existence. Ricardo has a breakdown. The crew
learns they are not in as much control as they thought.

Kyle Gomez — the ship's designer — is present at HQ
and on the bridge during prep. He leads breathing
exercises with Cole, talks him through a panic attack
in the cockpit.

ROD: Federation HQ. iRIS inoculation. When iRIS
speaks for the first time, Cole feels something
occupied in his skull. Ricardo calls it space marriage.
Bitboy locks Taylor on amber alert from the first
hour. Taylor is immediately hostile. The Golden Dawn
launches. In a decommissioned shuttle above the
complex, Collective watchers log the departure.
No GRACIE announcement. No Kyle Gomez.

DIFFERENCES: GRACIE's dramatic self-announcement in
DM is one of the book's action beats. ROD converts
this into a slow, quiet reconstitution that happens
in the background across the entire mission. DM
has Cole lose control of the narrative. ROD has
the counter-program working on its own timetable.

Kyle Gomez's absence in ROD removes a mentor figure
from the launch sequence. His philosophical grounding
role is redistributed — partly to Ricardo, partly
to the cumulative presence of iRIS.

─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
CHAPTER EIGHT
─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

DM: Sharon ALONE is on the train back from the crystal
handoff. Greenbaum forces her into a government car.
Backstory: Sharon pranked him at a medical conference
— pretended to be a doctor and pantsed him in front
of colleagues. He drives her to a lot and intends to
kill her. A sniper shot takes Greenbaum. Sharon alone
is kidnapped. Emily is not present.

In Marcus's custody, Sharon is not seriously harmed.
She accidentally confirms Saturn as destination by
saying it'll be "hard to get Cole a message for the
next two years." Marcus threatens Emily (in an Alpha
medical facility). Sharon is released.

ROD: EMILY AND SHARON both go to the Squirrel/Red
handoff. Sharon passes the drive: "Not the holding
unit. Directly." On the train back, Greenbaum forces
them both into a government car. He intends to kill
them — "the last two people to have spoken to Dr.
Stephen Conrad." A sniper shot takes Greenbaum.
Sharon goes down. A second vehicle — lights off,
already running — moves toward Sharon.

Emily is pulled into a van. Rachel Vayes finds her.
Greenbaum went off-script; Sharon was not supposed
to be harmed. "Sharon's dead." Rachel: "I know."
Emily agrees to stay the night, meet Marcus tomorrow.

The divergence: Emily believes Sharon is dead.
Sharon is not dead. She was taken by the second
vehicle. Alpha's vehicle. The augmentation begins.

DIFFERENCES: DM's Chapter Eight is a Sharon chapter.
ROD's is an Emily chapter. In DM, Emily is not
present — she is sick. Sharon is the one who makes
the critical mistake with Marcus and is briefly held
then released. There is no augmentation in DM.

ROD splits one kidnapping into two simultaneous
narratives — Emily's apparent death of Sharon, and
Sharon's actual fate — and the augmentation becomes
the shadow mission running underneath everything
from this point forward. This entire structural
layer does not exist in DM.

Greenbaum's motive also differs. DM: personal
vendetta (Sharon humiliated him). ROD: institutional
orders — elimination of the two Conrad witnesses.

─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
CHAPTER NINE
─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

DM: Day four of the mission. DarkX proximity effect
begins — crew feels things more. iRIS explains the
effects. Taylor monitors Cole. Sam running counter-
surveillance. Cole spends extra time in the cargo bay;
Taylor notices and begins pulling logs.

ROD: Same beats. iRIS explains the effect: "Limbic
sensitivity — that's a polite way of saying we feel
things more." Taylor reviews Sam's movements. Sam
finds a signal-receiving device in the ventilation
alcove, pockets it. Three hours later she has Taylor's
iRIS query logs: standing order to monitor Cole daily,
confirmation the road-attack contractor was neutralized
before outside contact, query about what Cole knows
about the cargo bay. "Show me all of it." Sam sits down.

DIFFERENCES: ROD's Sam is more explicitly Collective-
connected. The specific logs she pulls in ROD — including
confirmation that Taylor ordered and covered the road
attack — make the threat to Cole traceable and immediate.
DM's equivalent discovery exists but may not carry
that specific thread.

─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
CHAPTER TEN
─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

DM: Emily meets Marcus in his office, but without
the prior physical history. Emily does not have
a "I threw you in the dirt" moment. The meeting is
adversarial. Marcus wants the keychain. Shows Emily
the encrypted drive data. Reveals the pacing vessel.
Emily claims ignorance. He orders a tracker planted.
Rachel warns her in some form.

ROD: Emily is walked into Marcus's office by Rachel.
She recognizes him from a specific afternoon — his
knee on Cole's chest. She tells him she has kicked
his ass before and will do it again. Something
recalibrates behind his eyes. Marcus wants the
keychain specifically. Demonstrates existing leverage.
Reveals the pacing vessel. Emily claims ignorance.
Marcus believes the keychain part, reserves judgment
on the rest. Rachel warns her with a single word in
the elevator. Emily drops Marcus's card behind a
potted plant on the way out.

DIFFERENCES: The recalibration scene doesn't exist
in DM — Emily invoking physical authority over Marcus.
In DM, Emily is an obstacle to be managed. In ROD,
she is a woman who has already put him on the ground
and both of them know it. That shift in the room
is ROD-original.

─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
CHAPTER TEN-B — MAX AND THE FLYWHEEL
─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

DM: No equivalent.

ROD: Max extracted to a Collective safe house.
Squirrel and Red debrief him. Max explains that Alpha
missed the actual discovery: they see the output but
not the principle. He needs instruments to prove the
math. Red hands him a card with an address. "Tonight."

─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
CHAPTER ELEVEN
─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

DM: DeMint receives the anonymous brief. He dispatches
Vance Black to retrieve the "Augmented Resonance Beam"
transmitter — Ankur's device, a sci-fi communications
system built outside iRIS architecture to reach the
Golden Dawn. The black site is the records archive of
Conrad's facility. The vault was cleared by Alpha
trucks authorized by Rachel Vayes. Vance is trying to
locate the transmitter, not investigate records per se.

In the Marcus/Vance confrontation (DM Ch32): Vance
was Marcus's paid spy for four years, feeding DeMint
credible intelligence while serving Marcus. When he
found something he wasn't supposed to find, Marcus
shot him. Before dying, Vance had secretly recorded
the confrontation — audio of Marcus rehearsing crying
for police — and sent it to DeMint. DeMint plays this
recording as evidence.

On the Golden Dawn: Taylor reviews corridor footage
of Cole's extra time in the cargo bay. He pulls Sam's
acoustic logs and finds eleven minutes of rain where
there should be alcove ambient. The device replaced
the audio rather than jamming it — designed to look
like corruption, not active suppression.

ROD: DeMint receives the anonymous brief. Vance Black
goes to Conrad's desert research station records
archive. He finds the vault emptied by an Alpha
logistics directive authorized by Rachel Vayes, four
months before any official inquiry. He knows someone
cleared those records because they knew someone would
come looking. He returns to Marcus with three folded
pages. Marcus shoots him. The pages stay in his jacket.

On the Golden Dawn: Sam conducts an acoustic sweep
of the cargo bay. Taylor reviews surveillance footage
and pulls Sam's acoustic logs — same rain discovery.

DIFFERENCES: Vance's purpose at the black site
differs. DM: retrieving a specific device (the
transmitter), which DeMint later uses to send
Sharon's voice to the ship. ROD: investigating the
records clearance — a paper trail, not hardware.

Vance's relationship to Marcus also differs. DM:
he was Marcus's asset who tried to defect. ROD:
he is a Federation investigator. Both end the same
way, but the betrayal dynamic is different. DM's
Vance has a posthumous voice — his hidden recording.
ROD's Vance has three pages of metadata, read aloud
at the Grand Hall.

─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
CHAPTER TWELVE
─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

DM: Sam and Ricardo find the jammer in the reactor
bay. Sharon Reyes is confirmed dead. Cole reads the
notification, sets down the pad, goes to find Taylor.
Taylor: "People who know too much become liabilities."
Cole's response is controlled and dangerous. iRIS
blocks their comms during the confrontation.

ROD: Same structure and dialogue beats. iRIS blocking
comms is a tell Cole files away. The jammer discovery
and the death notification arrive in the same chapter.
Cole's control of his own reaction is the center.

DIFFERENCES: In DM, Sharon's death is real — she
died. In ROD, the death notification is a misdirect;
Sharon was taken, not killed. The emotional weight
of this chapter for Cole in ROD is therefore built
on false information — the reader knows what Cole
doesn't. That gap doesn't exist in DM.

─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
CHAPTER THIRTEEN
─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

DM: Emily's road interlude doesn't exist in DM.
In DM, Emily is not the active ground operative —
she is sick. She is not driving east on a mission.
There is no DM equivalent to this chapter.

ROD: Emily at the pier with Rachel. Next assignment:
a prototype drive in Houston. She drives east alone.
Rumble strips at the state line. Gas station in the
predawn — a stranger asks if she's okay. "Not yet.
But I think I'm going to be." Three hours of sleep
in the car. Then she keeps driving. Spare and quiet.

DIFFERENCES: Entirely the product of ROD's decision
to make Emily a protagonist. In DM there is no road
to drive because Emily doesn't have a mission.

─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
CHAPTER FOURTEEN
─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

DM: Cole in his bunk. The copper ring in his palm.
Saturn edge-on through the viewport. Flashback to
Sharon arriving at the cabin bleeding, an agent dying
in her arms. The Collective recruitment scene. iRIS
asks "Are you well?" quietly, when no one else is
listening.

ROD: Same. Structurally identical. The flashback,
the recruitment, the copper ring, Saturn. iRIS's
"Are you well?" is the first visible fracture in
her composure. Cole noticed it.

DIFFERENCES: Minimal. The Saturn line — "a cold
pearl with a knife laid across its belt" — is ROD
prose. The emotional architecture is the same.

─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
CHAPTER FIFTEEN — SURPRISE, ARIZONA
─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

DM: Emily's road arc doesn't exist in DM.

ROD: Emily wakes in Surprise, AZ. Rachel calls —
the prototype is in Houston. Emily notices Orin, who
has been tailing her since Benson. He explains
clearance three. They drive I-10 together. Houston
at night. Motel three miles from the facility.

─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
CHAPTER SIXTEEN — BEHIND THE CURTAIN
─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

DM: No equivalent. Alpha's crime is Gravity Sickness
monopoly, not withheld energy technology. There is
no Panama tower, no Dr. Oren, no undercover operation.

ROD: Max as "Mikof Forsinko" inside Alpha's Panama
tower. Four days with Dr. Oren — a genuinely good
scientist explaining stabilization with pride. Max
finds: patents = accumulated theft; flywheel data
exists in Alpha's own records (they knew and
suppressed it); Disposal Protocol Seven (11-13% of
viable product deliberately destroyed to manage
pricing); fraudulent grants stealing billions from
crisis relief. Nitrogen cycling alarm provides exit
window. Six blocks to the park. Drive handed to
Squirrel.

─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
CHAPTER SEVENTEEN — THE LOCKED BAY
─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

DM: No equivalent. Taylor does not vent the
atmosphere. He is not Alpha's asset. He is difficult
and monitoring Cole, but he is not the villain.

ROD: Taylor locks the crew in the cargo bay under a
fake pressure check and begins venting atmosphere.
Temperature drops. Sam finds an override panel behind
the crystal harvester. Opening it floods the room
with raw plasma. Bitboy goes full blue. Cole opens
the harvester. Flashbacks: Taylor identified as
Alpha's embedded asset; Vance finding the cleared
vault; Taylor reading "Vance Black, deceased" and
closing the file.

─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
CHAPTER EIGHTEEN — THE FOUR COLES
─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

DM (Chs 38-39): Cole knocked unconscious when a
Dark Matter chunk shatters his helmet during a
spacewalk to dislodge a sample stuck in the ship's
harvesting sphere. Ricardo and Bitboi rescue him
from open space.

Vision: Cole falls through crystals into his
childhood home. Finds his mother stone-faced before
a television. Hears an infant crying in his bedroom
— baby Cole has climbed out of the crib and fallen.
Cole picks him up, holds him, cries with him. "You
don't deserve this, buddy."

On the Moon: Old Cole appears — bald, white linen
hood, white ember eyes. Shows Cole a vast black
crater: all the pain, abandonment, emptiness.
"The guilt and shame almost feels like meaning,
doesn't it? Feels like control." Cole refuses to
face the crater. Wanders the Moon in anger for what
feels like an eternity. Eventually jumps into the
abyss. "This was acceptance. This was letting go."

Integration: Cole in the back seat of his old Toyota
Tercel. Teenage Cole is driving, seething about his
father and Marcus. Baby Cole sits in the passenger
booster seat eating pizza, face covered in marinara,
giggling. Old Cole is also in the back seat. Cole
tries to speak to teenage Cole ("It gets better —
you're going to meet Sharon"). Teenage Cole can't
hear him. All four merge into one. Sharon's voice
comes through the intercom ("I love you, Cole Nova.
Please come home to me") — DeMint sent her voice
as an MP3 through the recovered transmitter.

Cole wakes on the floor of the Golden Dawn. Ricardo
hugs him. Taylor: "I knew you could do it." Ship
heading home to Houston.

ROD (Ch18): Taylor vents the cargo bay. Cole opens
the crystal harvester. DarkX vision.

Four versions of Cole: the infant, the seventeen-
year-old in the Subaru after his father's phone call,
himself now, the old man. The substance will not
show him Sharon — "it doesn't lie." Old man: "You
don't need a pardon. You need to stop being the
warden." The nanopowder has been rewriting. A voice:
"I think I'm Gracie." Cole: "Don't tell them yet.
Take a minute." "I'm sorry about Sharon." Gracie
patches Emily through from Houston — three-second
delay. Cole hears Emily's voice in the middle of
Saturn's rings.

DIFFERENCES: Both use the same structural device
(four versions of Cole, moon-surface encounter with
Old Cole, the crater/abyss, the infant, the car with
teenage self). This is one of the most direct
correspondences between the two manuscripts.

Key differences:
  - DM: vision triggered by Dark Matter to the
    helmet during spacewalk. ROD: triggered by direct
    DarkX exposure in the vented cargo bay.
  - DM: Cole is physically rescued from space by
    Ricardo and Bitboi. ROD: the crisis is inside
    the ship, in the cargo bay.
  - DM: Sharon's voice (via DeMint's transmitter MP3)
    brings Cole back. ROD: Emily's voice (via
    Gracie's patch) brings Cole back.
  - DM: Gracie does not re-emerge during the vision.
    ROD: the vision is also Gracie's reawakening.
  - DM: Taylor celebrates Cole's return — not
    the villain. ROD: Taylor tried to kill them all.
  - DM: the Tercel has baby Cole eating pizza and
    giggling. ROD: the Subaru scene is after the
    father's phone call — grief, not domestic comfort.
  - Old Cole's core message is the same in both:
    you cannot fill this hole with accomplishment,
    self-punishment is a form of control, learn and
    let go. Both reach the same emotional destination.

CHAPTER NINETEEN — THE BOARDING
─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

DM: No augmentation arc. Sharon was briefly held and
released. There is no boarding scene with an augmented
figure coming through the docking tube.

ROD: The Alpha vessel draws alongside. A figure comes
through the docking tube. Bitboy's chest panel: SHE
SMELLS LIKE SHARON. Ricardo: "I want it on record I
have no idea how to file that in any existing threat
category." Cole asks her to take off the visor.
Sharon's face is half-augmented — plating on the left
side, her right eye still hers. She attacks without
hesitation. Cole takes the hits. Bleeding palm against
the unaugmented half of her face. Counter-program
transfers. Bronze indicator ring goes dark. Her left
eye fills. She says his name. "I said, 'Come back.'
I said, 'I'll be here.'"

DIFFERENCES: Entirely original to ROD. The boarding,
the augmentation, the palm-to-face transfer, the
recovery — none of this exists in DM. One of ROD's
most significant structural inventions.

CHAPTER TWENTY — THE HOUSTON HEIST

green light. Emily hands it to Orin. Ninety minutes

CHAPTER TWENTY-ONE — MAX'S CARGO
─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

DM: No equivalent.

ROD: Max on a park bench six blocks from the Alpha
facility. Eleven viable technologies deliberately
withheld. The energy crisis is an inventory decision.
The drive goes out through the parallel network —
a hundred thousand people in forty-eight hours. Max
boards the seven o'clock flight under his own name.
"The drive is out. The work is done." He sleeps.

─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
CHAPTER TWENTY-TWO — THE BLACK SITE
─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

DM: Marcus escapes to Dubai within hours of the
Federal Police raid — warned by his network. The
photocopy of a hand giving the finger left on his
desk. His exit is instinctive and survivalist, not
calculated. He does not leave a prepared testimony.

ROD: Emily at Marcus's Alpha LA office. He is awake
and not contrite — says so plainly. Full unremorseful
account. "I'm not contrite. I'm realistic." Emily:
"You never understood anyone. You only did utility
analysis." Marcus: "Yes." He says the Collective
will want his testimony; he has prepared a complete
account and will give it. Emily calls the DeMint
relay and leaves the door open. In the lobby: Marcus
left four minutes before she arrived. "That was also
Marcus."

DIFFERENCES: DM's Marcus doesn't give a confession
— he escapes ahead of the raid, leaving a defiant
gesture. ROD's Marcus delivers a prepared account
and leaves four minutes before Emily arrives —
planned departure, not panicked flight. ROD's Marcus
is more calculating at the end; DM's more instinctively
survivalist. Both end with him in Dubai. Only ROD's
has the testimony in hand.

─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
CHAPTER TWENTY-THREE — GRAND HALL
─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

DM: The centerpiece evidence is an audio recording
of Marcus — his own voice captured by Vance's hidden
recorder before he was shot. DeMint plays it: Marcus
rehearsing crying for police. The testimony is built
on audio. You hear Marcus be Marcus.

ROD: Marcus's audio testimony (2 hours 51 minutes)
plays. Vance Black's written record read aloud while
the Hall stands. Max testifies. Conrad's statement
read into the record. Rachel Vayes stands at 4:43 PM.
Eleven years of cover. DeMint: "Granted." Cole
watches from the Golden Dawn's cockpit.

DIFFERENCES: DM's revelation is theatrical — one
recording, one voice, one damning moment. ROD's
reckoning is cumulative — testimony, written record,
scientific proof, eleven years of internal witness.
Multiple people, multiple kinds of evidence, multiple
years of work converging at the same moment.

─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
CHAPTER TWENTY-FOUR — EARTH RETURN
─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

DM: The landing and return fold into the bowling
alley scene without a distinct runway chapter. There
is no pocket watch. No DeMint revelation about
generational architecture. Taylor is present at the
bowling alley, celebratory.

ROD: Taylor removed on the runway — two men, no
scene. DeMint shows Cole Klaus Verguld's pocket watch.
Taylor was Klaus's man, not Marcus's. Cole visits a
recovery facility. Drives to Emily's house. Emily on
the porch. They hold each other. Sharon in the window
chair. "Hi." Bitboy: "Hi, you little bastard."
Blackberry preserves. Argument about the route
through Prescott. Ricardo calls.

DIFFERENCES: ROD's return adds the generational
revelation — Klaus Verguld, who does not exist in
DM at all. This reframes who was actually running
the machine. DM ends with a cleaner victory.

─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
CHAPTER TWENTY-FIVE — THE BOWLING ALLEY
─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

DM (Ch40): Cosmic Lanes. Emily is present, recovered
from Gravity Sickness. "I showered by myself this
morning. I did a load of laundry. Thank you Cole,
for giving me my life back." Ricardo proposes to
DANI — she's been chatting with the cashier when he
calls her over. Moissanite ring with a dark gem in
a Brunswick Bowling jacket pocket. "A star gem.
Ethically sourced." Sharon asks Cole "do you want
to do this full time?" Cole: "I thought you'd never
ask." Sharon wants a real diamond. Bitboy as best man.
Taylor and Sam are together, holding hands. Bitboy
gets into energy drinks. "Everybody out! RUN!!!"

ROD: Ricardo proposes to Sam — same moissanite ring,
same ethical sourcing explanation. Sam says yes.
Cole sets the copper ring on the table: "I don't
think I need to replace it. This is the one." Sharon
puts it on. "Yes." Bitboy disasters the vending
machine. Parking lot. California night.

DIFFERENCES: Same location, same emotional register,
but the proposal recipients and final structure
differ.

DM: Ricardo/Dani. Sharon proposes to Cole. Emily
present and recovered. Taylor at the alley,
celebrating. Bitboy ends the book with a vending
machine emergency.

ROD: Ricardo/Sam. Cole proposes to Sharon with the
copper ring, closing the loop on the gesture he made
at the cabin. Emily present but illness-recovery
is not the beat — the reunion is about the mission.
Taylor removed on a runway. The copper ring sequence
carries the emotional weight instead of Sharon
reaching for Cole.

In DM, Sharon's proposal completes a different arc —
she is the one reaching for him after his transformation
brought him back to her. In ROD, Cole completing the
ring sequence (copper wire to formal commitment) is
the book's answer to the question the ring asked in
Chapter Six.

─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
EPILOGUES
─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

DM: Marcus is in Dubai with a crowned prince, watching
the news. He is not worried. His network warned him;
his businesses will be seized for show, assets
transferred to new companies he secretly owns. Murder
charges are manageable. He sets sights on intergalactic
mining. "He wanted his own civilization to experiment
with." The villain gets the last word. He is amused,
untouched, and already thinking bigger.

ROD (Epilogue A): Marcus in Dubai. Klaus Verguld
appears — older, unhurried. "You built the cover.
The structure continues." The machine was never
Marcus's.

ROD (Epilogue B): Taylor placed in a Nevada town by
Marcus — his name delivered as payment for the
unauthorized road attack. The machine disciplines
its own errors.

ROD (Epilogue C): Sharon on a Sequoia porch. A
Collective droplet stencil on the wall. Two ways of
seeing. "It was always in the reaching."

DIFFERENCES: DM gives the last word to Marcus —
undefeated, planning forward. The book ends with
the antagonist intact and looking forward. ROD
distributes the ending across three registers.
Epilogue A reframes Marcus as a cog in a machine
he didn't build — his freedom is real but not
triumphant. Epilogue B closes Taylor's arc with
the machine's own correction. Epilogue C gives
the last word to Sharon — not the antagonist, not
Cole, but the person who was taken and came back.

─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
IV. STRUCTURAL AND TONAL NOTES
─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

PARALLEL ARCS
  DM: Cole's story is the book. Emily is peripheral —
  sick, at home or in treatment. The Golden Dawn
  mission is the central and essentially only plot.

  ROD: Two parallel arcs intercut throughout the
  second half — Cole in space, Emily on the ground.
  The Emily arc (Conrad → Squirrel → Marcus → Orin
  → Houston) runs simultaneously and is given equal
  weight. The book has two protagonists.

THE MAX FORSTER ARC
  DM: No equivalent. This entire thread — flywheel
  principle, Panama infiltration, Dr. Oren, Disposal
  Protocol Seven, Max's testimony — was built into
  ROD from scratch. It changes the nature of Alpha's
  crime and the nature of the final reckoning.

THE AUGMENTATION ARC
  DM: No equivalent. Sharon is briefly held and
  released. The shadow vessel is not an augmentation
  platform. The boarding and the counter-program
  transfer have no DM source material.

GRACIE'S RECONSTITUTION
  DM: GRACIE announces herself openly mid-mission.
  Her sentience is a declared fact. She is an
  antagonist who becomes an ally, then flies them
  home.

  ROD: Gracie reconstitutes through Cole's absorbed
  counter-program across the entire mission, without
  announcement. The "I think I'm Gracie" moment in
  the DarkX vision is a whisper, not a declaration.
  She exists fully by the Grand Hall chapter, where
  Cole uses her to message Emily.

TAYLOR
  DM: Difficult early, becomes Cole's ally and guide
  through the DarkX experience, ends the book
  romantically with Sam, celebrating at the bowling
  alley.

  ROD: Alpha's embedded asset from before launch.
  Never sympathetic. Removed on the runway. The
  generational reveal — Klaus's man, not Marcus's —
  reframes everything he did as institutional
  rather than personal.

THE MACHINE'S DEPTH
  DM: Alpha Corp is the villain. Marcus runs it.
  One generation. One antagonist.

  ROD: Klaus Verguld built the structure Marcus
  operates inside. Marcus's cover is real but
  limited — he does not know the full architecture.
  Taylor was Klaus's man, not Marcus's. The machine
  has a generation of depth that Marcus himself
  did not fully see. The epilogue's last image of
  Marcus with Klaus behind him is the book's
  final reframe.

TONE
  DM: More pulpy and kinetic. GRACIE's broad flight-
  attendant announcement. Dani as a physical comedy
  figure. Taylor celebrating. Ricardo's named-women
  catalogue delivered as comedy. The Bitboy vending
  machine gag is the book's last image before THE
  END. DM earns its relief without much darkness at
  the close.

  ROD: More controlled and literary throughout.
  The copper ring sequence carries the emotional
  weight instead of GRACIE's intercom. Bitboy's
  chest panel messages land as staccato beats rather
  than slapstick. Taylor is never sympathetic, so
  there is no reversal. The book's last image —
  Sharon on a porch, a droplet stencil on the wall,
  "It was always in the reaching" — is quiet.
