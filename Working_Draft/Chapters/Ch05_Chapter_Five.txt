RINGS OF DUST

Chapter Five

It had started as laziness.

The protocol was clear: submit for containment within twenty-four hours of
crystallization. That window had closed ten days ago.

Max had converted the batch on a Tuesday — standard process, salt compounds and
liquid nitrogen in a negative-pressure chamber until the DarkX plasma locked
into solid form. Beautiful crystals. Dense, dark, faintly luminous at the edges.
The kind of thing that took three hours to make and thirty seconds to ruin. The
kind of thing a junior researcher had no business holding onto without
authorization.

The official containment unit was across campus. Getting there meant logging the
transfer, signing through two security checkpoints, and explaining why a junior
researcher had an unauthorized batch of Darker Matter crystals at all. Max
looked at the crystals. He looked at the clock. He looked at the spare fuel cell
sitting on the shelf behind him.

He packed the crystals inside it, sealed the cell under negative pressure,
topped it off with nitrogen to keep things stable, and shoved it in his locker.
*I'll deal with it tomorrow.*

Tomorrow became the next day. The next day became a week. A week became ten
days, the mandatory turnover window long gone, the crystals now approaching
reversion on their own schedule whether he was ready or not. Each morning he
stood at the locker for a full minute before he could make himself open it.

Max slumped over his workstation, eyes strained from hours of staring at data
screens. The fluorescent lights above cast a sterile, cold glare on the polished
floor. His mind was a storm of thoughts, still reeling from what he'd seen. The
fuel cell—his impulsive shortcut—hadn't just held; it had transformed.

Sitting hadn't worked. Relaxation is a distant bitch. A double-edged sword. His
feet wanted to run in any direction other than the one he forced his mutinous
legs to continue. He rounded the corner, his pulse quickening. The locker loomed
ahead, a secret buried beneath anxiety and cautious hope. His fingers fumbled
with the lock, the combination momentarily escaping him as nerves tangled his
thoughts. When the door finally swung open, his breath caught in his throat.

The fuel cell was glowing. A soft, eerie light pulsed from the panel, a
heartbeat in the silence. He lifted it carefully, hands trembling as he peered
through the viewport. The vapor inside was dense, swirling lazily like a storm
caught in a jar.

"No way," he muttered, almost dropping the cell in shock. The crystals he'd
sealed there were gone, replaced by something entirely different. His mind
raced, a dozen explanations swirling like the mist in the cell, none making
sense. He should have reported it immediately, but the fear of repercussions—of
losing everything—held him back.

Instead, he tucked the cell back into the locker, slammed the door, and forced
himself to breathe. He needed time, needed to think. The lab was a maze of risks
and rewards, and Max knew better than most how quickly those scales could tip.

The following weeks were a blur of late nights and whispered conversations, the
fuel cell hidden away like contraband. Max monitored it obsessively, his heart
pounding every time the lab door creaked open. Each day, the cell's charge grew,
the vapor inside thickening, alive with a strange, magnetic energy. He was on
the edge of something huge that could change everything.

But when the 11th day came, and the mist cleared, revealing a cluster of
delicate crystals clinging to the outside of the cell's base, one word escaped
him before he could stop it.

"Jackpot."

He said it the way a man says something he didn't know he was going to
say—quietly, to no one, to the empty lab, to the crystals themselves. Then the
dread moved in behind the word and filled all the space the triumph had just
vacated. He knew what this meant. This wasn't just a fluke; it was a
breakthrough—a dangerous one. He scribbled notes furiously, documenting every
detail, his thoughts a chaotic jumble of triumph and fear.

Then came the decision he couldn't put off any longer. He had to tell someone,
had to share what he'd found. He chose his department head, hoping that his
discovery's potential would outweigh the protocol breach.

It didn't.

Security was on him within minutes, their hands rough as they stripped him of
his badge and lab coat. Max barely registered the words—the accusations, the
threats. He was too stunned, his mind still fixated on the image of those
crystals, their potential.

"You'll keep quiet about this, or we'll make sure you never work again.
Anywhere," one of the guards snapped, his voice a low growl. Max blinked, the
weight of the threat slowly sinking in. They weren't just talking about his
career. This was bigger, more dangerous than he'd realized.

Outside, the night air was sharp against his skin, the chill cutting through the
shock. He stood in the parking lot, staring at the looming silhouette of the
research complex. It felt like he'd been expelled from some exclusive club, and
the doors slammed shut behind him.

At that moment, he knew that Alpha Corp would bury his discovery and lock it
away behind layers of corporate secrecy. But he couldn't forget what he'd seen.
Couldn't let it go.

Back inside, Alpha Corp's upper echelons were already dissecting his research.
The data was clear: the combination of nitrogen, negative pressure, and the fuel
cell circuits had triggered a reaction that turned Dark Matter crystals back
into its vapor state, generating massive energy. They added liquid nitrogen,
accelerating the formation of the crystals and cutting the cycle down to 11
days.

The process was erratic. Some cells bloomed with crystallites, others failed
spectacularly. Researchers started betting on which cells would produce, a
twisted game of chance in a world where control was everything. The "gardens,"
as they called the fuel cells, became a grim parody of hope—a hope that could
only thrive in secret, behind locked doors.

Every 11 days, they harvested what they could, the crystals growing more
reliable but still volatile. The cells themselves deteriorated under the
constant strain, leaking energy and sometimes exploding. Any remnants were
transferred to fresh cells, and the losses""tailings," they called them—were
painfully significant.

They knew what they had, though. The data was buried deep, accessible only to a
select few. Stock prices soared on speculation and rumor, the true nature of
their discovery hidden beneath layers of corporate bureaucracy.

"  "- "

Meanwhile, at Federation Headquarters, the crisis deepened. Chancellor DeMint
stared at the holographic display, the red zones creeping across the globe like
a spreading disease. The energy crisis was a noose tightening around their
necks, and the room buzzed with the grim knowledge that time was running out.

"We're looking at six months, maybe less," Dr. Salazar said, her voice cutting
through the tension. "Without the new fuel cells, we're facing catastrophic
blackouts."

DeMint's jaw clenched. "What about production? Can we ramp it up?"

"Not without more DarkX," Taylor interjected, his tone clipped. "The
stabilization period is still 90 days, and our reserves are almost gone. We're
scraping the bottom of the barrel."

Sam, a data technician who had been silent until now, leaned forward. "We've
been experimenting with the accumulations on the cells. We can extend the
stability for a few more days, but it's not enough. We need their data."

Taylor didn't look at her. He addressed the room. "Extending accumulation cycles
based on experimental intuition isn't a strategy. It's a feeling. And feelings
don't hold up at a briefing table." A half-beat pause. "Or in the field."

Sam's eyes stayed on her screen. "Might want to patch that," she said. "Your
firmware's running a few versions behind." She pulled up a new readout and
turned it toward Dr. Salazar without looking at Taylor once. "The numbers are
right here."

Dr. Salazar's expression didn't change, her gaze steady and unyielding. "Alpha
Corp has already shared everything they can. The proprietary information is
classified."

Sam's hands curled into fists. "We're all fighting the same battle. The least
they could do is give us a chance."

Taylor's voice was low, almost apologetic. "It's not that simple."

"Isn't it?" Sam snapped. "They hold all the cards, and we're just supposed to
wait until they decide to deal?"

The room fell silent, the weight of the unspoken truth settling over them. Alpha
Corp controlled the future, and without them, the Federation ran out of options.

--- --- ---

Back in his apartment, Max sat at his kitchen table, staring at the stolen fuel
cell. The crystals inside glowed faintly, a promise of power just out of reach.
He knew what he had to do. He couldn't let this go or let Alpha Corp bury his
work.

He would go underground and find a way to continue his research outside their
control. He knew the risks, knew he was playing with fire, but he also knew that
if he could crack this, he could change everything.

Max took a deep breath, the decision settling like a stone in his gut. There was
no turning back now. He had to see this through and find a way to get this
information into the right hands.
