RINGS OF DUST

Chapter Three-C

The Faithful


The fifth day. She had her cells. She had not left.

She told herself it was the docking bay's proximity to the main tunnel — easier to reach the ship quickly if she needed to. She told herself it was the food, which was still real and which she was not done eating. She told herself she was running preliminary diagnostics on the Hound's Tooth before the return run to Titan, which was true in the limited sense that she had opened the diagnostic panel and then sat looking at it without actually running anything.

She was staying because on the fourth night Tyler Durden had sat across from her in the kitchen and talked for two hours, and she had listened, and when she went back to her ship she had not been able to sleep.

Not because she believed him. Because she couldn't find where he was wrong.

---

The woman who had been easiest to talk to was named Yun. She was twenty-six, maybe twenty-seven, and she had been at the colony for three years, and she had the specific quality of someone who had thought very carefully about a thing and arrived at a position she was comfortable defending.

"You don't think it's strange," Jill said. They were in the work bay, running cable. The Void-Walker installations — the membrane equipment at the east end — required regular maintenance, and everyone rotated through it.

"Which part," Yun said.

"Any of it. All of it. The sending things into a void and waiting for gold."

Yun looked at her for a moment. "Do you think it's strange that the cells sync on an eleven-day clock? That one of seven hundred and forty always has the Jackpot on it?"

"That's mechanics."

"It's mechanics we can't fully explain," Yun said. "Tyler says the line between mechanics and miracle is just the distance between what you've measured and what you haven't yet."

Jill had heard versions of this argument before. Usually they led somewhere she didn't want to go. "And the gold."

"And the gold," Yun said. "Which is also mechanics. Which Elsa mapped." She set down the cable spool. "The void at the end of the east tunnel has a specific gravitational signature. When a stabilized cell goes through the membrane, the dark matter interacts with the void — redirects inbound asteroid traffic along a vector Elsa calculated forty years ago. It's in her notebooks."

"So it's physics," Jill said. "Not faith."

"Both," Yun said. "Elsa understood the physics. Tyler understood what the physics was for." She picked up the spool again. "The cells are real. The gold is real. The question Tyler asks is: why. Why does the void respond this way to this specific material? Why eleven days? Why always one in seven hundred and forty?"

"Nobody knows."

"Tyler thinks the cells are in a conversation with something," Yun said. "The void, the ancestors, the chain of who we came from and who we'll become. He thinks the eleven-day clock isn't random. He thinks it's a heartbeat."

Jill was quiet.

"You don't have to believe it," Yun said. "You just have to notice that the gold keeps showing up."

---

Tyler took her to the preparation chamber on the fifth afternoon.

It was a room she hadn't seen yet — small, off the main tunnel, lit differently from the rest of the colony. Not surgical-white like the lab spaces. Warmer. The kind of light you associate with things that are meant to be seen slowly.

Four fuel cells were racked along one wall. Not ordinary units — these had been running for more than a hundred and eighty days. Their casings were covered in crystalline growth, exterior deposits from dozens of eleven-day cycles. They were, by any technical measure, extraordinarily valuable.

Tyler stood beside them the way you stand beside something you're about to part with.

"We don't sacrifice every Jackpot," he said. "We choose. The best ones. The ones that have been running the longest and producing the most." He looked at her. "You're thinking we're throwing away value."

"You are throwing away value," she said.

"We're giving it back." He touched one of the cells — not possessively. The way you touch something before releasing it. "Elsa believed that energy doesn't disappear. It moves. The dark matter goes into the void, the gold comes from the void, the cells keep producing. Something on the other side of the exchange is receiving what we send." He paused. "She believed that people work the same way. That the energy of a life — everything you built, everything you gave — doesn't disappear when you die. It flows forward. Into your line. Into the people who come after you."

Jill looked at the cells. "She believed she'd come back."

"She believed all of us come back," Tyler said. "Into our own bloodlines, if we've given enough. Into the people we love. Into the hands of whoever is willing to continue the work." He was quiet for a moment. "She's buried forty meters from where we're standing. She came out here and built something and died in it. I never met her. But I've read every word she wrote, and I understand why she came out here instead of staying where she was."

"Where was she before?"

"Working for the people who are trying to control the grid," he said. "She left. She came here. She spent the last three years of her life building an understanding of the cells that nobody inside those walls ever achieved." He looked at Jill. "She gave everything she knew to the void. The notebooks she left behind are what we still have."

Jill looked at the four cells, their crusted exteriors, the dark matter slow-growing in the amber light.

"When is the ceremony," she said.

"Two days," he said.

She did not ask why she was being told this. He did not explain.

---

She asked Petra about the notebooks that evening.

Petra was at her bench, logging the day's readings. She did not look up.

"No," she said.

"I'm not asking to keep them."

"I know what you're asking." Petra set down her pen. "You want to understand if the physics in those notebooks is real enough to explain the gold, and if it is, you want to know whether that changes your opinion of what Tyler is doing with it."

Jill waited.

"The physics is real," Petra said. "Elsa was the most rigorous scientist I have ever worked beside. Every number in those notebooks is correct. The void's gravitational signature, the cell interaction, the eleven-day redirect — all of it verifiable, all of it exact." She picked up her pen again. "The other parts of the notebooks are not something I show to people who are leaving."

"What other parts."

Petra looked at her directly for the first time. "The parts that are harder to verify," she said. "The parts that she wrote at three in the morning after her husband died and her family stopped speaking to her and she had been out here long enough to stop needing their approval." She held Jill's eyes for a moment. "The parts about why she came here. What she believed the cells were. What she believed happened when you gave something you couldn't afford to lose."

She looked back at her readings.

"When you come back," she said, "ask me again."

Jill opened her mouth and then closed it.

She went to her ship. She sat in the dark for a long time.
