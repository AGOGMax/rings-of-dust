RINGS OF DUST

Chapter Three-E

The Crater


The eighteenth day. Eleven days after the ceremony.

She was in the tracking room at 0347. She had set an alarm, which she had not told anyone about and which she was not entirely sure why she had set. Tyler was already there. Yun was there. Four other Void-Walkers she knew by face and not yet by name. And Petra, at the back of the room, standing where she always stood — slightly apart, slightly sideways, as though the data on the screen was something she was being asked to testify about rather than watch.

Nobody had told Jill to come. Nobody had needed to.

At 0402 the proximity sensor lit.

The asteroid resolved on the tracking display from the same vector as the previous one — she had reviewed the logs, all six years of them, the same approach corridor each time, the same impossible deceleration as it entered their field, the same final holding position just outside docking range. It was always this way.

This one was larger.

Not by a little. Twice the mass of the previous one. Maybe more.

"He sent more than last time," Yun said quietly. "He sent four. We've never sent four."

The asteroid rotated slowly on the display. At 0419 it reached the angle that put its largest face toward their sensors, and the spectrometric reading came in, and the display showed what the reading showed.

The face had an impact crater. Thirty meters across. Dense, uniform, reflective.

Gold.

Not gold-bearing rock. Not gold-flecked mineral. A crater of solid gold on the face of the asteroid, as though something had struck it from the other side with an instrument that left gold behind instead of taking material away.

Petra looked at the display for a long time.

"Jackpot," she said.

Quietly. The way she always said it. The way it always meant the same thing — here is the number, here is what it is, here is what it does to every model I have ever run. She said it and did not look at Tyler, and Tyler did not look at her, and the room was quiet in the way rooms are quiet when the argument has been running for six years and one side has just produced another piece of evidence that neither side entirely knows what to do with.

---

Jill left the tracking room.

She went to the docking bay. She sat in the Hound's Tooth in the dark with the hatch closed and the interior lights off and she sat there for a long time.

She was not a person who believed in ancestors receiving fuel cells through a void. She was not a person who believed in reincarnation into bloodlines or the energy of a life moving forward into the people who came after. She was a person who ran a trade circuit in the outer belt and checked her instruments and made her runs and did not spend a lot of time on questions that didn't have answers she could work with.

She had watched a man carry four fuel cells through a pressure membrane and send them into the dark.

Eleven days later, a gold asteroid — twice the size of any previous one — had arrived from the same impossible vector it always arrived from, and on its face there was a crater of solid gold that nobody could explain, and Petra had said the same word she always said, and the tracking room had gone quiet in the way it always went quiet.

She could not explain the gold.

She had tried. She had spent ten days running the orbital mechanics in her head — inbound trajectories, gravity wells, asteroid composition distributions, the probability of a gold-mass object entering a stable approach vector from the same corridor six times in six years. The numbers didn't work. The numbers hadn't worked the first time she ran them and they didn't work now.

She sat in the dark and thought about Elsa Klinge, buried forty meters away in the asteroid wall, who had mapped the void's gravitational signature and left eleven notebooks and written, three days before she died, that the energy of a life moved forward.

She thought about the part of the notebooks that Petra had refused to show her.

She got out of the ship and went back inside.

---

She found Petra at the bench. Petra looked up when Jill came in. She looked at her for a moment and then looked back at her work.

"No," she said.

"I watched it arrive," Jill said.

"I know. I was standing next to you."

"I can't explain the gold."

"I know that too." Petra was quiet. "That is the problem with this place. The gold keeps arriving and nobody can explain it, and Tyler is very good at providing an explanation, and his explanation requires a great deal of belief in things that are not measurable." She set down her pen. "Elsa's explanation also required a great deal of belief. The difference is that Elsa was a scientist first and a believer second. She believed because the math pointed somewhere and she was honest enough to follow it even when she couldn't verify where it led."

"And Tyler?"

"Tyler believes because he needs to," Petra said. It wasn't unkind. "That doesn't make him wrong. It just means his path there was different from hers."

Jill was quiet.

"The notebooks," she said.

Petra looked at her for a long moment. Then she stood up and went to the supply room. She came back with a sealed case — old, worn at the edges, the kind of case you keep because what's inside is irreplaceable. She set it on the bench.

She did not open it.

"Elsa believed," she said, "that the cells were a medium. The way water is a medium for sound. The dark matter conducts something she couldn't name — not energy in the conventional sense, not a field she could measure — but something that moved through it the way meaning moves through language. She believed this because of the sync period." Petra looked at the case. "Not the charge levels. The sync period. The eleven days when you can feel it in your sternum and can't hear it with your ears. She wrote: 'I have felt this before. Every human being who has ever held a newborn child in the first days of their life has felt this. The feeling of a new node reaching toward the network. The feeling of something that has just arrived learning where it is.'"

Jill said nothing.

"She believed that the cells were doing what human beings do," Petra said. "Finding their way back into the circuit. Moving forward through the line." She put her hand on the case without opening it. "She believed her daughter's daughter would find her way here eventually. She left the notebooks for her."

Jill looked at the case.

"I'm not her granddaughter," she said.

"No," Petra said. "You're not."

She picked up the case and took it back to the supply room.

Jill stood at the bench alone for a while. Then she went to find Tyler Durden.
