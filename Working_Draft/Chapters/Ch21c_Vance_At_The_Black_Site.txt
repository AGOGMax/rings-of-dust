RINGS OF DUST

Chapter Twenty-One-C

Vance at the Black Site


Vance adjusted his sunglasses against the glare of the mid-day sun. He yanked
them off with one hand and steered with his knee as he cleaned the lenses with
his shirt. Four hundred dollars and they smeared like a gas station pair. He put
them back on. The A/C was set to max, and he kept the window down anyway — he
felt claustrophobic with it up.

The desert spread out in every direction. Mountains dimly visible through the
haze. After what felt like too many miles of dirt road, the road stopped being
a road.

He pulled up to what looked to be a partially demolished drive-in movie theater.
Chain link fence around the property, cruel-looking razor wire along the top.
A single brand-new guardhouse with a fresh coat of white paint sat near the gate,
completely out of place in the wreckage around it. The guard that emerged from it
was equally out of place — well-groomed, pressed uniform, rifle leveled at the
windshield before Vance had fully stopped.

"Turn around, sir."

Vance shoved his badge out the window. "Vance Black, Operations. They should
have called ahead about me."

"Get out of the car. Hands where I can see them."

He complied, both hands up, badge in his left. The guard kept the rifle on him
and came around the front of the truck.

"On the ground. Hands flat."

The rocky desert floor was scorching — sand and grit and a particular quality
of heat that had been building since before dawn. Vance laid himself prone and
spread his limbs. Sand got into his mouth.

The guard pulled his arms behind his back and cuffed them. "This is for your
safety, sir."

Vance coughed out wet grit. "How considerate."

The guard checked the badge, jerked him to his feet, went back to the gatehouse
and picked up a phone. A brief exchange. He came back out.

"You can enter. On foot."

"What do I do with the truck?"

"I'll take care of it, sir."

"Where do I go from here?"

The guard motioned to a cement structure that had once been the concession stand.
Graffiti along the sides. The roof partially gone. "Go in there and get yourself
a soda."

Vance looked at the building and back at the guard. "You're serious."

The guard stared at him.

With his hands still cuffed behind his back, Vance tilted his head toward them.

The guard looked genuinely irritated at the implication. "Of course, sir." He
removed the cuffs. Vance cradled his wrists.

"Thanks, Rambo."

The guard raised the rifle again, leveled and pleasant. "No problem, sir. You
have yourself a fine day."

---

The concession stand smelled like dry ketchup and old oil from the hot dog
rollers, a smell that had been baking in the desert heat long enough to become
the smell of the building itself. Barren shelves. Advertisements for brands
that no longer existed. At the far left, a beverage dispenser on a dusty counter
with a stack of styrofoam cups beside it.

Vance set a cup under it. A small amount of thick liquid dripped from one of the
faucets. He sniffed it and threw the cup in the nearest garbage can.

A click from a door to his right. Stenciled on it: SUPPLIES. Now open.

He went through.

The stairwell was narrow — cement walls on both sides, an iron handrail bolted
into the right wall that he decided not to touch. A hundred steps down, the smell
shifting from hot dust to cold mold, the light from above thinning and then gone.
At the bottom, another door with a keycard reader. He placed his badge against it.
Red twice. Then green. The door opened.

A guard on the other side, standing at rigid attention. Vance waved. No reaction.
The man stood as still as furniture. The hallway beyond him was long and circular,
a metal grid pathway running the length of the floor. At the far end, another
door. With a sigh, Vance started walking.

Each step sent a metallic echo clanging through the corridor, doubling and
returning. The overhead lights were spaced at regular intervals and the ceiling
between them was dark. He noticed the machine gun turret mounted above the
entrance when he was almost directly under it. Subtle.

At the far end, another keycard reader. He swiped. The heavy door unlocked.

On the other side, a young female military clerk sat working through a stack of
papers. Cabinets lined the cement wall behind her. Two well-lit curved tunnels
led left and right from the room. She looked up.

"Hall of Records. What are you looking for today, Mr. Black?" Polite, no warmth.

"Files and equipment belonging to Dr. Arvind Ankur."

She arose. "One moment please. Wait here." She turned to the cabinets, opened a
drawer of old reference cards, walked her fingers through the yellowed stack for
a long time. Found the one she needed, traced along it, pulled a thin folder from
a separate cabinet, and brought it to the counter. It landed with a soft plop on
the laminate.

Vance opened it. Newspaper clippings. College transcripts. A birth certificate.
A death certificate, heavily redacted except for the cause of death.

Suicide.

He looked up. "I need more than this. I need to see what was in storage. I'm
looking for a specific piece of equipment."

She cocked her head. "That won't be possible."

"It is required, actually." He looked back at the clippings. One read: *Florida
Man Claims Contact With Aliens And Why It Should Matter To You.*

"I don't think you understand," she said. "His vault was emptied last month."

Vance looked up from the clipping.

"How is that possible. By whom."

"I don't even have to look that one up." She didn't look it up. "It was
transferred by several trucks with the Alpha Omega Corp emblem on the sides.
These files are all that's left. Would you like me to make a copy for you?"

"Who authorized the transfer."

"Federation Representative Rachel Vayes."

Vance set the clipping down.

He looked around the room — the curved tunnels, the cabinets, the clerk with her
stack of papers — and looked back at her and held himself very still.

"You have got to be kidding me."

---

He drove north for thirty minutes before he pulled over on the shoulder of the
road and sat with the engine running and the window down.

The desert ran flat in every direction. No mountains now. Just scrub and rock and
the specific quality of silence that exists in places where nothing is expected
to happen.

Rachel Vayes. He had been following her public work for three years — Alpha's
preferred correspondent, the voice they sent when they needed the shortage to
sound like an engineering problem rather than a choice. He had never once thought
of her as a threat to his side of the ledger. He had not known she had a
Federation liaison designation. He had not known she had access to a facility
DeMint didn't know existed.

He thought about what it meant that DeMint hadn't known.

He thought about the trucks with the Alpha Omega Corp emblem on the sides, and
the specific kind of operation it takes to move a vault's worth of materials
through a Federation facility without anyone in the Federation being told.

He thought about the eight years, and who had known about this facility, and who
had sent him here, and who had not.

He drove back south. He made a phone call.

Marcus picked up on the second ring.

---

Marcus's office was on the thirty-second floor. It had a view of the Strip that
was better than it deserved to be.

"The vault was cleared," Vance said, without sitting.

Marcus looked at him from across the desk. He had the face of a man who had
already done the calculation and was now watching the rest of the equation catch
up to where he was standing.

"It was a liability," Marcus said.

"It was evidence. DeMint sent me to get it."

"DeMint sent you because he didn't know what was there. He still doesn't." A
pause. "And he won't."

Vance had been in rooms like this before. He knew the grammar of what happened in
them. He'd been on the other side of it enough times to understand every stage —
the particular stillness that precedes a decision that has already been made, the
specific quality of a man who has moved past the point where words accomplish
anything.

He had always been on the other side of it before.

"Eight years," he said.

"I know," Marcus said.

That was all.

---

He drove himself to a clinic on the other side of the highway, afterward.

He sat in the car for twenty minutes before he went in.

That part mattered. Not to Marcus. Not to Alpha Omega Corp. To nobody who had
been in the room. But he sat in the parking lot in the Nevada heat, and before
he went in he thought about the hundred steps down into the dark, and the mold
smell, and the clerk's face when she said *his vault was emptied last month* with
the specific neutrality of someone who understood that the paperwork was more
interesting than the people in it.

He thought about the trucks with the emblem on the sides.

He thought about DeMint's face on the day he'd taken the assignment — the
specific quality of a man who trusts the wrong person in the right way for the
wrong reasons.

He thought about eight years, and what they had been for, and whether any of
it counted.

He decided it did.

He went in.
