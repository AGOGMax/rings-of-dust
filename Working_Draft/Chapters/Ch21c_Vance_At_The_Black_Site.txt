RINGS OF DUST

Chapter Twenty-One-C

Vance at the Black Site


The facility's public address was a strip mall on the eastern edge of Henderson, Nevada, across the highway from a distribution center and two blocks from a closed casino. The anchor tenant was a dollar store that had been there since the mid-2000s and showed no signs of stopping. The tenant next to it was a mail and print shop. The tenant next to that — the one with the blank white door and no signage — was not a mail and print shop.

The underground facility was seven levels. The Federation had built it in the late 2030s for purposes that the documentation described as "high-priority records management" and that actually meant: somewhere to put the things that couldn't exist anywhere else.

Vance had been here twice before. Both times with authorization from DeMint's office, both times to pull specific documents for specific cases. The guards knew his credentials. The biometric readers knew his hand. The seven-level elevator knew him the way any system knows the people who have used it enough times that their patterns are part of its baseline.

He went through the entrance process in the standard order: surface checkpoint, sub-level badge scan, biometric confirmation, document declaration — he had none, he was pulling not depositing — final guard station. The guard at the final station was a man named Rourke who had been here every time Vance had been here and who communicated primarily through the particular nod of someone who has learned that most of what people say is less informative than how they stand.

Rourke nodded.

Vance took the elevator to sublevel five.

The records room on sublevel five was a long, climate-controlled space with floor-to-ceiling shelving on three walls and a central processing desk. The clerk on duty was a woman in her forties named Chen who had the manner of someone who had read a great many highly classified documents and drawn from this the conclusion that people, as a category, were rarely as interesting as the paperwork about them.

"Vance Black," she said, without looking up from her terminal. "Credentials verified. What's the access number?"

He gave her the number. The Federation file designation for Alpha Corporation's fuel cell technology procurement records — the ones that should be here, the ones DeMint had sent him to find, the ones that would show exactly which procurement decisions had been authorized, by whom, and through which channels, and for how long, and to whose benefit.

Chen typed. She looked at her screen. She typed again.

"That file has been accessed and transferred," she said. "March 14th. Full vault clearance."

Vance felt something he was careful not to show.

"Transferred to what classification level?"

"Reclassified to restricted. External transfer authorization." She turned the screen so he could read it. "Federation Representative Rachel Vayes. Alpha Corporation liaison designation."

He stood at the desk and read the authorization three times.

Then he thanked Chen, took the elevator back to the surface, and drove north on 215 for thirty minutes before he pulled over on the shoulder and sat in the car.

Rachel Vayes.

He'd seen her on broadcasts for years — Alpha's correspondent, the one they sent out when they needed someone the public trusted to explain why the shortage was complex, why the solution was coming, why patience was the correct position. He hadn't known she had a Federation liaison designation. He hadn't known she had access at this level. He hadn't known, until this moment, that she had cleared a vault that DeMint hadn't known existed.

He thought about what it meant that DeMint hadn't known.

He thought about what it meant that Marcus had known and hadn't told him.

He drove back to Vegas. He made a phone call.

Marcus picked up on the second ring.

---

Vance had worked for Marcus for eight years. In that time he had done things that he had understood, at the time, as necessary, and that he understood now differently. He had always known that he was operating in a space where the categories — legal, illegal, authorized, not authorized — were less fixed than the people using them believed. He had accepted this as the nature of the work.

He had not accepted being disposable.

Marcus's office was in the Alpha building on the thirty-second floor. It had a view of the Strip that was better than it deserved to be.

"The records were cleared," Vance said, without sitting.

Marcus looked at him from across the desk. He had the face of a man who had already done the calculation and was now watching the rest of the equation catch up.

"They were a liability," Marcus said.

"They were evidence. DeMint sent me to get them."

"DeMint," Marcus said, "sent you because he didn't know what was there. He still doesn't." He paused. "And he won't."

Vance had been in rooms like this before. He knew the grammar of what happened in them. He'd been on the other side of it often enough that he understood every stage — the particular stillness that precedes a decision that has already been made, the specific quality of a man who has moved past the point where words accomplish anything.

He'd always been on the other side of it.

"Eight years," he said.

"I know," Marcus said.

That was all.

---

He drove himself to a clinic on the other side of the highway, afterward.

He sat in the car for twenty minutes before he went in.

That part mattered. Not to Marcus. Not to Alpha. To nobody who had been in the room. But he drove himself there, and he sat in the parking lot in the Nevada heat, and before he went in he thought about DeMint's face on the day he'd taken the assignment — the specific quality of a man who trusts the wrong person in the right way for the wrong reasons — and he thought about the eight years, and what they had been for, and whether any of it counted.

He decided it did.

He went in.
