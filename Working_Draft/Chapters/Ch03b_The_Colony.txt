RINGS OF DUST

Chapter Three-B

The Colony


She found the colony by accident, the way most people found it.

The transponder signal was on a frequency she didn't recognize — not in any registry she'd filed with, not on the standard Federation band, not on the miner's auxiliary band she checked when she was past the main belt. She'd picked it up three days out from Titan on a routing she'd taken because it was shorter and she was low on time and not yet low on fuel, which turned out to be the first wrong decision in a series of wrong decisions. By the time the signal resolved into something intelligible, she was low on both.

The colony was on an asteroid — a real one, not a station rock. Natural iron-silicate formation, roughly three kilometers across, with a spin so slow you couldn't feel it. The habitation structure ran along the inner surface of a carved tunnel through the asteroid's middle: an old mining excavation that someone had sealed and pressurized and built something inside. The approach was marked with hand-painted indicators rather than navigation beacons. She landed in a bay that had been bored by hand drill, looking at marks on the rock that said, in several languages: LEAVE YOUR WEAPONS AT THE DOOR.

She wasn't carrying weapons. She'd sold them at Titan.

Her name was Jill.

---

The man who met her at the bay entrance had his hands in his pockets and the expression of someone who was curious about her in a way that wasn't threatening and also wasn't quite safe. He was tall. He had the kind of face that meant something in any room he was standing in — not handsome exactly, but arranged in a way that made you pay attention to what it was doing. He wore a work shirt and work boots and nothing about his appearance suggested authority, which she understood later was deliberate.

"You ran out," he said. Not a question.

"I'm short. Not out. I need two cells to make the run back to Titan supply."

He looked at her for a moment. "We have cells. They're not for sale."

"I can trade." She had a manifest. She handed it over.

He looked at it the way someone looks at a map of a city they don't live in. "We don't need any of this."

"Then what do you need?"

"Mostly," he said, "for people not to want things from us." He handed the manifest back. "Come inside. Eat. We'll talk about what you actually came here for."

His name was Tyler Durden.

---

The tunnel was lit with strips of amber light that gave everything the color of an afternoon she couldn't identify — somewhere warm, somewhere on a planet with weather. The habitation ran along the length of the bore: sleeping quarters, a communal kitchen, a workspace full of fuel cell equipment that she looked at for a long time as they walked past.

"You make them," she said.

"We manage them," Tyler said.

"There's a difference?"

"That's the question," he said.

The kitchen had real food. She ate. He sat across from her and watched with the patience of someone who understood that hungry people weren't ready for conversations.

When she was done, he said: "There are two groups here. The people who were here before I came, and the people who came after."

"Loyalists," a woman said from the far side of the kitchen. She was older — seventies, maybe, white hair cut close, the posture of someone who had made peace with exactly the amount of space she took up in the world. She did not look at Tyler when she said it. She said it to Jill. "We were here first. We maintain what Elsa built. We stabilize the cells, harvest what they produce, and understand what we're working with."

"And your group?" Jill asked Tyler.

"We offer the cells to the void," he said.

"Void-Walkers," the old woman said. No contempt. Only accuracy.

---

Elsa Klinge had founded the colony fourteen years ago. She had been a materials scientist — one of the early researchers in dark matter stabilization, before Alpha Corporation had consolidated the patents and the research direction into a single building in a single city. She had left before that happened. She had filed the Federation papers herself and come out here with eleven people and fourteen years of research notes and spent the remaining three years of her life building the thing she had spent eleven years thinking about.

She had died in the colony she built. Her grave was carved into the asteroid wall at the far end of the main tunnel, marked with the kind of marker you make when you have tools and time and the person mattered.

The old woman — Petra — had been one of the original eleven. She had maintained Elsa's research the way you maintain a building belonging to someone who isn't there anymore: with the care of someone who understands their job is not to change it but to keep it right.

What Elsa had built was an understanding of the cells at a level that no institutional lab had ever reached, because no institutional lab had ever had the time or the distance from commercial pressure to simply watch. She had discovered that newly assembled cells — assembled within eleven days of Dark X stabilization — entered a sync period. You could feel it in the casings if you knew what to listen for: a low resonance, below hearing, the sensation of something searching for a frequency just out of range. After eleven days the feeling stopped. The cells were quiet. Something had completed.

She had discovered the thirty-three-day lock: cells locked together in a Grid, internal crystal growth running at ten times the rate of any unlocked unit. She had mapped the void at the end of the east tunnel. She had written it all down in notebooks that Petra now kept in a sealed case in the supply room, which nobody was permitted to open without asking her first.

Tyler had arrived six years ago. He had not come by accident. He had been looking for the colony specifically.

What he had done, six years ago, was stand in the main tunnel with one of Elsa's fuel cells and a three-page document he'd written himself and delivered the most coherent argument Petra had ever heard for why the cells should not be managed but given.

Not used. Not harvested. Given.

She had told him he was wrong. He had agreed she might be right. And then he had taken one of his own cells — not hers, his — and walked to the end of the east tunnel and pushed it through the pressure membrane into the void.

Eleven days later, a gold asteroid appeared in their tracking field. Close. Slower than inbound objects had any right to be. Twenty meters across, dense enough that its value at mineral exchange rates was enough to fund the colony for four years.

Petra had done the math herself.

She had told Tyler he was still wrong. She had meant it. She still meant it.

Eleven people now followed him.

---

"You sacrifice the cells," Jill said. "And gold arrives."

"Every eleven days," Tyler said. "One asteroid. We have been tracking it for six years. The pattern does not vary."

"And you don't know why."

"We know the why," Petra said. "The cells have mass. The dark matter has gravity. When you push a stabilized cell into a void with the specific characteristics of the one at the end of our east tunnel, the gravity differential redirects inbound asteroid traffic along a new vector. Elsa mapped the void. The math is hers."

"She mapped it to the physics," Tyler said. "I mapped it to something else."

"He means the cells are choosing," Petra said, to Jill, in the flat tone of someone who had this conversation many times. "He believes the cells have intention."

"I believe the cells are in a conversation we haven't learned to listen to yet," Tyler said. He said it without argument. He said it the way you state a coordinate — here, not there. "The sync period. The lock. The void alignment. These are not accidents."

Petra looked at her hands. "That," she said, "is where we disagree."

Tyler looked at Jill. "There's a race to control the grid," he said. "It's been running for a decade. Alpha Corporation on one side. The people who understand what these cells actually are on the other. Everyone else has been told it's a supply problem — a technical constraint, something being worked on. Something almost solved." He paused. "It isn't a supply problem."

Jill looked at her cup.

This was the part, she understood now, that made people call them a cult. Not the void alignment. Not the gold asteroids arriving on an eleven-day clock. This part — where the conviction behind the eyes got too clear and the thing being said stopped being a theory and became a verdict. She had heard versions of this speech in every outer-belt waystation she'd ever stopped at. Grid conspiracies. Energy wars. The managed shortage as a choice rather than a failure.

She had never believed any of them.

"Okay," she said.

Tyler looked at her for a moment. He had the face of someone who had said this many times and was no longer surprised by the response. He let it go.

Petra said nothing. She had heard all of Tyler's speeches. She did not agree with his conclusion. She was not entirely sure it was wrong.

---

She stayed for eleven days.

She told herself it was because she needed the cells. On the fourth day Tyler gave her two without asking for anything in return, which she accepted because she needed them and because refusing would have been a performance. She stayed because on the morning of the eleventh day she wanted to see it.

She stood in the tracking room with Petra and six Loyalists and Tyler and five Void-Walkers and they all watched the same screen.

At 0411 station time, the proximity alert lit.

The asteroid was twenty-three meters across, same vector as the previous five — Jill had reviewed the logs. It resolved from the void and slowed, in the way that inbound objects do not slow, into their tracking range.

"Jackpot," one of Tyler's people said. Quietly.

One of Petra's people said nothing, but Jill watched her face and watched her do the calculation in her head she always did, and come to the same number she always came to, and say nothing.

The room was quiet in the way rooms are quiet when people have stopped arguing and are simply watching something be true.

---

She left on the twelfth day.

Tyler was at the east pressure membrane when she passed. Not blocking the way. Just there.

"You're going to tell people," he said.

"I don't know who I'd tell."

"You'll find someone," he said. He said it like a fact he had already accepted. "Elsa thought it should stay here. I think it can't."

"Which one of you is right?"

He looked at the membrane, at the void beyond it.

"Ask me in eleven days," he said.

---

She did not know, leaving, that the woman whose grave was carved into the rock at the end of the main tunnel had a grandson. That he had spent four years looking for something she had left him. That Jill had passed within forty meters of the marker without reading the name carved into it.

Nobody had told her to look.

That was the thing about the colony. You were not a leech if you came in honest — if you ate the food they offered and asked the questions that were yours to ask and left with what they chose to give you. You were something else if you came looking to take without staying long enough to understand what you were taking from.

Most people who found the colony left with two cells and the memory of an amber-lit tunnel and food that tasted like somewhere they couldn't name.

Some of them came back.

The ones who came back were always looking for the same thing: to be in the room on the eleventh day, when the screen lit and someone said, quietly, the only word that fit.

Jackpot.
