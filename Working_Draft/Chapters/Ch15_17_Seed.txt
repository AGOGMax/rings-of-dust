## Arc Overview

Ch15-17 intercut two timelines:
  (A) Present: the Golden Dawn in deep space — Taylor's mask slowly slipping.
  (B) Flashback: Vance and Taylor's overlapping careers as Klaus/Marcus assets.

Vance is the road not taken for Taylor. He developed a conscience.
Taylor watched what that cost Vance — and chose differently, on purpose.

## Source: Vance visits the black site
[ Source: DARK_MATTER.txt, Chapter 30, "Vance Black Visits a Black Site" ]
[ Placement: Ch15 or Ch16 flashback, before Marcus kills him.
  This is where Vance's conscience fully crystallizes — he came out here
  to get leverage, to do the right thing, and finds he's already been
  outmaneuvered. ]

KEY PROSE — Vance at the Hall of Records:

    The clerk clarified, "His vault was emptied last month."
    Vance, visibly irritated, hissed, "How... How is that possible? By whom?"
    "I don't even have to look that one up. It was transferred by several
    trucks with the Alpha Omega Corp emblem on the sides. These files are
    all that's left."
    Vance looked around wildly, barely managing to look calm.
    "Who authorized the transfer?"
    "Federation Representative Rachel Vayes," she replied.
    Sensing the end of his rope, he looked into her hazel eyes.
    "You have got to be kidding me."

WRITING NOTES:
- Vance came here as a double agent in the making — one foot in Alpha Corp,
  one foot starting to tip toward the Federation and DeMint. He wanted the
  equipment as leverage and as proof of good faith.
- Rachel Vayes beat him to it. She's working the same angle from the
  Federation side, but she serves Marcus just as much as the Federation does.
- Vance now has no leverage. He has a death certificate, a redacted file,
  and the dawning understanding that every exit has been sealed.
- His choice: swallow it and stay useful to Marcus, or confront him.
  He confronts him. That's who Vance is.

## Source: Marcus shoots Vance in cold blood
[ Source: 01_Antigravity_2023-02-16.txt, lines 3979–4087 ]
[ Placement: Ch16 or Ch17 flashback. The chapter's emotional gut-punch.
  Keep it tight — this scene works best when it moves fast. ]

KEY PROSE:

Marcus's office, Alpha Corp HQ. Night. Vance arrives unannounced.

    Vance grew defensive. "You want to know something, Marcus?
    You sell crap that doesn't work. Okay? And your greed —
    your bottomless greed — is going to cost me a court-martial.
    Did you ever consider that?"

    Marcus crowed, "That's the price of doing business. Oh you'll
    get paid. After you serve your term I'm going to get you a
    nice setup here. Big salary, full benefits."

    Vance stuttered, "But I could be going to prison for years
    for failing the Federation."

    Marcus' tone dropped. "Next time you think you can out-sing
    or out-dance me, I want you to think again."

    Vance stamped his foot. "If you think I'm going down without
    you, you are sorely, SORELY mistaken. I've got you by the balls."

    Marcus said, calmly, "Sounds like a threat to me. Pull out
    your phone."

    Vance spat, "Man, screw you!"

    A revolver clicked under Marcus' desk.
    Vance grimaced. "You'd better not try any —"
    Marcus took aim, and an explosion ripped through the air.

AFTERMATH (same source, immediately following):

    As Vance's body slumped over, Marcus pulled up his own phone.
    From blank-faced to weepy, he practiced putting on the waterworks
    a few times before making the call.
    In hysteria, he forced out the words. "Yeah. Hi? This is Marcus
    of Alpha Corp? Um, there's been a shooting and... oh my gosh."
    He really had to sell this.
    "Um, there was an intruder and I feared for my life."
    "Mr. Marcus, we have your address on file and we're sending units
    immediately."
    "Oh my gosh. I am so traumatized by this. Please hurry."
    When he hung up, Marcus went blank-faced. Then he exploded in
    laughter, hugging his ribs when laughing started to hurt.

WRITING NOTES:
- The "dance competition" line is vintage dark comedy — Marcus killed a man
  partly because Vance won a dance-off. Keep it. That's the point: Marcus
  doesn't need a proportionate reason. He kills for status.
- The practiced weeping before the call is the scariest beat. He rehearses it.
- Don't editorialize. Let the scene speak. No narrator intruding after the shot.
- Marcus laughing alone in the dark, holding his ribs — that's the image
  that should land and stay.

## New Material Needed: Taylor learns of Vance's death

[ Placement: Late Ch17, aboard the Golden Dawn or during Taylor's escape
  sequence. A single scene, half a page maximum. ]

WHAT HAPPENS:
- Taylor receives word (comms, a message, however it reaches him in space)
  that Vance Black has been killed. Official story: intruder, self-defense.
- Taylor already knows the truth. He knew what Vance was doing.
  He may have even flagged Vance's growing disloyalty to someone at Alpha Corp.
- His reaction: not grief. Not even surprise. A quiet, satisfied exhale.
  Maybe a faint smirk. The sense that an irritant has been removed.
  "He always thought the rules were negotiable," Taylor might think.
  "They weren't."
- The reader should feel sick. This is the moment Taylor becomes irredeemable.

WRITING NOTES:
- No monologue. No explanation. Taylor's inner life should be visible through
  what he does NOT do — he doesn't mourn, doesn't pause, doesn't look up
  from what he's doing for more than a second.
- Sam should be nearby and should notice his non-reaction. She doesn't say
  anything yet. She files it away. (This plants her eventual break from him.)
- Keep it short. The less Taylor says, the worse he looks.

## New Material Needed: Vance / Taylor friction in earlier flashbacks

[ Placement: Ch15 — one or two scenes, seeded before the black site visit
  and the murder. Establish them as two men who recognized each other
  as the same kind of creature and resented what they saw. ]

WHAT TO ESTABLISH:
- Both were Klaus Verguld assets. Both knew it about the other.
  That knowledge made them more competitive, not more allied.
- Taylor was younger, more ideological, more purely Alpha Corp.
  Vance was older, more pragmatic, and had accumulated doubts over years.
- Taylor found Vance soft. Vance found Taylor naive — a true believer
  is always the most dangerous kind of fool.
- Their friction was never loud. It was the kind that happens in hallways,
  in the way a man doesn't move out of your path, in the way a handshake
  is one second too short.
- One good concrete image: a shared briefing where Vance asks a question
  that implies doubt about an Alpha Corp directive. Taylor answers before
  the presenter can — clean, certain, slightly too eager. Vance looks at him
the way you look at a man who just volunteered for something he shouldn't have.

SUGGESTED LINE (Vance internal, not spoken aloud):
  "Glenn had the answer for everything. The problem with men like that
  is they never ask the right questions."

## Easter Egg: Ryan Greenbaum in the flashback
[ Placement: ONE beat, inside the Vance/Taylor friction scene in Ch15 or Ch16.
  Do not give it its own scene. Do not call attention to it.
  The camera passes through it on the way to something else. ]

WHAT HAPPENS:
- Greenbaum is in the room — a junior Federation officer, unremarkable,
  trying to contribute. He says something during a briefing or debrief.
  It's not wrong. It's just small.
- Taylor dismisses it without looking at him. Not a takedown — just a redirect,
  the kind that erases the person who spoke. Like they never said anything.
- Vance doesn't intervene. He moves on. Greenbaum stands there for a beat,
  recalibrated, and then straightens up and acts like it didn't happen.
- That's it. The scene continues. Vance and Taylor keep going.

WHY IT WORKS:
- Greenbaum has already been established in Ch7 as a man running on
  humiliation and resentment — the elevator scene, the sulfuric cologne,
  the rigid jaw, the "women get away with bloody murder" line. That is
  a man shaped by years of being looked through by people like Taylor.
- This beat is where one of those years happened.
- The reader who catches it gets a gut-punch callback when Sharon is shot.
  The reader who doesn't catch it doesn't notice anything — Vance's arc
  keeps moving without interruption.

WRITING NOTES:
- Do NOT name the connection in the prose. No narrator wink. No Vance
  noticing Greenbaum's expression and filing it away. Just the beat,
  clean, and then the scene moves on.
- Greenbaum should not look angry in the moment. He should look practiced —
  the practiced neutrality of someone who has learned to absorb this
  and not show it. That's scarier than anger.
- One sentence is enough. Two at most. If you're writing more than that,
  you've broken the Easter egg — you've made it a scene instead of a seed.

SUGGESTED IMAGE:
  Greenbaum offered something — a number, an observation, a small piece
  of data. Taylor talked over the end of it. Not to Greenbaum. Just...
  past him. Vance was already moving to the next point on the board.
  Greenbaum nodded like he'd agreed.
