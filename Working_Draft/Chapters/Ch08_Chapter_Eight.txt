RINGS OF DUST

Chapter Eight


Emily and Sharon sat in a parked Sprinter van. They were pulled off in a turnout on a county farm road surrounded by wheat and soybean fields. They arrived a little earlier than the designated time- 3:33 pm. Sharon picked at her gas station sandwich; the soggy lettuce was as pathetic as her last attempt at college.

A minivan pulled up behind them, and a pair of Collective agents exited, approaching the back of the Sprinter. They wore shirts and Levis, looking very ordinary except for their gate. Their body language telegraphed confident resolve and a purpose greater than themselves. Sharon and Emily rolled down the windows, and the man on Emily's side reluctantly spoke the code phrase: "No raindrop thinks it's the flood."

With that, Emily unlocked the doors, and the Collective members moved to the back of the vehicle. 

"What are you two anyway? Agents, or lower on the totem pole?" She turned to look at them through the cargo area. While hefting the first set of 6 gym bags on their shoulders, they paused to look at each other, puzzled; they weren't sure if they should divulge that information.

"It's not like I'm a threat; I'm just delivering the crystals."

The one on the left spoke, "I'm a Junior Tech, He's a Senior."

"How many more merit badges are needed to level up to Heavyweight Champion?" Emily said, smirking at the sharpness of her own wit. 

They rolled their eyes and carried the loot back to their ride. Sharon chuckled at how much they looked like chipmunks hoarding acorns for winter. On their return, Sharon threw them a token of amends. "Hey, Technician is pretty good; I'm sure you guys will make it past Agents into the Navigators. Good job for being punctual. These crystals are on 'Day 5', so ensure they get sealed within six days."

"Got it; how come you two aren't part of us?" the Junior asked with a little grunt as he hefted the next bag.

"I'm all business, and she's just far too pretty." Emily blushed at Sharon's quip.

Before Sharon closed the van doors, Emily reached into the inside pocket of her jacket and produced a small drive — thumb-sized, matte black, unmarked. She set it in Sharon's palm without a word. Sharon turned to the Senior Tech.

"This goes directly to your comms lead. Not the holding unit. Directly."

He looked at it. "Understood."

Sharon placed it in his hand with more deliberateness than she'd used with any of the crystal bags. He tucked it into his chest pocket and zipped it closed.

"Speaking of business, you have something for me?"

After placing the last bag in the minivan, the Senior Tech went to the glovebox and grabbed an envelope. "Here you are, Ma'am."

"Ma'am? What am I, fifty?" he shook his head, not wanting to engage. 

"Thank you for your help with the fight." They shut the doors to Sharon and Emily's vehicle and returned to theirs. They skidded as they flipped across the gravel onto the paved road and were soon gone.

After returning the van to an indoor parking lot as instructed, Emily and Sharon walked a couple of blocks to the train station and sank back into the mundane rhythm of life. The train's hard, cold steel seats, marred with remnants of old gum and grime, made Emily long for the familiar comforts of her brother's cabin, albeit equally stained.

 As the train clattered along, the rhythm of the rails became soothing in its monotony as they passed urban brick and steel canvases adorned with sprayed signs of the city's frustration. Among the vibrant tags, a symbol caught Emily's eye—the mark of the Collective. The sprawling museum flashed by in a blur, creating an almost stop-motion animation look against the sun's rays, pulsating defiantly. Even still, Emily recognized a secret handshake known only to those fighting in the shadows. 

"If we're doomed to sit on stained seats," Emily said, her face twisted in distaste, "they could at least be comfortable."

Behind them, an elderly couple sat in stark contrast to their discomfort, their hands intertwined, surrounded by reusable grocery bags. The man's derby hat and checkerboard green vest painted a picture of quiet contentment; his companion's headscarf added a splash of color to the austere surroundings.

The train slowed, and the doors opened to admit a new passenger—a man whose presence was immediately disconcerting. Dressed in a black sport coat that hung on his thin frame and a white dress shirt tucked into slightly askew pants, he fixed his gaze on Emily and Sharon. His approach was deliberate, and he chose the seat directly across from Sharon in a train car that offered many alternatives. Sharon quickly identified the outline of a concealed weapon beneath his coat.

As the train jolted back into motion, the man wasted no time.

"I'm here to escort you to the Federal Council immediately," he declared, presenting his ID badge. "Ryan Greenbaum, Federal Council Police."

Sharon read the badge without touching it. Emily had already seen what Sharon had seen — the embossed logo on the inside of his watch band, briefly visible as he reached. She had seen that same logo two days ago on a lanyard at the conference hotel. She looked at Sharon. Sharon's expression said: I know. Keep moving.

Emily folded her arms. "Are we under arrest?"

"Not exactly," Greenbaum replied.

"Then we are free to go," Sharon said, with the particular calm of someone who knows exactly who they're talking to.

"Not exactly," Greenbaum said with a hungry smile. "I'm taking you into protective custody. The fun part is I get to do it with or without your assistance."

Emily slumped her shoulders, playing the role of a defeated woman. "Can I at least stop by my place and get my things?"

"I'm afraid that won't be possible."

"So, the plan is to force us to go, even if we say no?" Sharon asked.

"Yes," he replied. " With pleasure and without hesitation."

"Fine. We'll go, but no cuffs," Sharon said.

As the train slowed to a stop, Greenbaum stood and walked toward the doors. Sharon held out her wrists as if to suggest he should cuff her; his brain froze for a moment with the mismatch between her words and gesture. The doors opened with a hiss to an empty platform. Sharon and Emily stepped off the train with Greenbaum in tow, heading down the train platform stairs to the parking lot below.

"How was the rest of the conference?" Greenbaum asked.

"The entire thing was pretty forgettable, to be honest," Sharon replied.

At the bottom of the stairs, a white Range Rover with government plates waited for them. Greenbaum swung open the back door with a gesture that seemed more obligatory than courteous, signaling the transition from a semblance of freedom to a more constrained reality. Emily and Sharon, hesitantly stepping into the vehicle's plush interior, found themselves enveloped in the sterile atmosphere, contrasting sharply with the vibrant chaos of the city outside.

"Interesting," Greenbaum remarked. "Can't say I feel the same."

Once inside, Greenbaum selected a preset location on the central display, and the car propelled itself forward into traffic. Despite the churn of her stomach, Emily maintained a facade of indifference; her smile was a mask to disguise the unease knotting her insides. She brushed her hair out of her eyes with a coy smile.

"I'm really sorry. We were just having fun, and..."

"You're sorry?" Greenbaum turned and made eye contact with Emily, much like an eagle with a freshly caught field mouse. "If I'd done something like that to either of you, I'd be in jail. Women get away with bloody murder these days."

Sharon let out an uncontrollable laugh that sounded more like a cough, highlighting the absurdity of Greenbaum's thinking. The cityscape transitioned slowly as they moved, buildings with their glass facades reflecting the late afternoon sun gradually giving way to the open expanse of the outskirts, where the city's relentless energy dissipated into the ether. 

Greenbaum's rigid posture and clenched jaw spoke volumes of his frustration, a wave of simmering anger just below the surface, poised to boil over at the slightest provocation. Emily, her nerves alight with a mixture of dread and defiance, maintained a composed exterior. Though tinged with mockery, her smile did little to veil the storm brewing within. Greenbaum's silhouette, rigid against the backdrop of passing scenery, exuded a cold professionalism.

"The difference is," Sharon said, "we—and most women, for that matter—wouldn't strip down for strangers in an elevator like some coke whore in a dive bar. You have only yourself to blame for playing with us, and we—WE—are not sorry to have taught you a lesson." The memory became vivid again, prompting Emily to laugh. 

The steady hum of the car's engine provided a constant reminder of their journey's progression towards an uncertain destination. Sharon's laughter, bursting suddenly in the constrained space, shattered the veneer of civility and exposed the underlying tensions. Greenbaum, mixing irritation and embarrassment, clearly felt the pressure mounting within the vehicle's confines.

"I almost caught a charge for public indecency," Greenbaum exploded in exasperation. "Well, today, I'm not here about that. I'm just the lucky bastard assigned to bring you in."

"Ryan. Can I call you Ryan?" Sharon asked, "Look, I'm not here to judge anyone. But hey, you do you, buddy."

Greenbaum's ears turned a darker shade of red. "I'm just following orders."

"You really don't hear yourself talk, do you?" Emily narrowed her eyes.

Greenbaum's assertion of duty, merely following orders, failed to quell the rising tension, his words falling flat in the charged atmosphere. Emily and Sharon united in their defiance, challenging not merely the man before them but the very system he represented, wielding their words as sharp weapons with precision and courage.

"Not a single day of freedom has existed since the start of this country, Ryan. You might find yourself stuck in a story you didn't even write. Call yourself an agent or whatever you want. You're not even a supervisor at the kidnapping store," Emily countered.

Greenbaum bristled at the accusation. The sense of isolation intensified as the Range Rover navigated an increasingly sparse landscape. The occasional flicker of streetlights flanked the open road stretching into the distance. Cars, or what little was left of them, littered the roadside—stripped of all valuable parts, sitting in clusters like piles of skeletons on a long-forgotten battlefield.

"Is it, though? With the stroke of a pen, someone who thought they should rule over people declared that the evil you do is good. But you're just a slave like your friends. My goodness, why am I even talking to a slave like you? Ugh," Sharon exclaimed.

"You're only making me happier to have gotten this detail," he retorted. "Keep squawking, bitch."

The Range Rover turned towards a manufacturing district with abandoned factories standing like silent sentinels of a bygone era of productivity. Its headlights pierced the deepening night, illuminating a chain link and shadows that embraced forgotten streets and buildings. Heavy with the stench of rust and decay, the air signaled desolation and danger. Emily spotted a vehicle, untouched by scavengers and understandably so, for it no longer bore any of its intended colors. Instead, it blended into the surroundings: rusted, forgotten, and decrepit, complete yet wholly overlooked.

"Nice spot for a safe house," Sharon commented.

The car's central display sprang to life, indicating they had reached their destination. The Range Rover halted in a cul-de-sac littered with broken glass, dead industry, and a few dim overhead lights casting just enough illumination to lend the whole scene an anxious vignette. The silence inside and around the car felt oppressive. Greenbaum faced Emily and Sharon with sinister calm, his eyes gleaming with malice.

"How does it feel to be the last two people to have spoken to Dr. Stephen Conrad?" he asked with cold satisfaction. "I intend to do the same to you two. But maybe we should have some fun first."

The heavy silence that descended upon the car, punctuated only by the subtle hum of its idling engine, was palpable. Greenbaum's hand, previously resting casually by his side, moved with chilling deliberation towards the concealed firearm under his armpit, the metal glinting ominously in the dim light. Recognizing the imminent threat, Sharon met Greenbaum's gaze, her expression a blend of defiance and calculated calm. 

Engulfed in fear and disbelief, Sharon struggled to suppress the scream welling up within her. The tension encircled each occupant of the car tightly. Suddenly, Sharon flung the car door open and leaped from her seat, compelling Emily to follow with a look that brooked no hesitation. Propelled by adrenaline, Emily mirrored Sharon's actions, their feet hitting the gritty pavement. They stood up, momentarily disoriented by their sudden shift from the car's confines to the vast, danger-filled openness. Greenbaum, cursing under his breath, clambered out of the vehicle. His previously composed movements were now hurried and awkward, the gun in his grasp now pointed threateningly at Sharon as he rounded the car, his expression contorting in rage and desperation.

Sharon squared off against Greenbaum. Her posture implied a grim acceptance. With her heart pounding, Emily watched in frozen terror, frantically searching for a solution that stayed out of reach.

In the stretched-thin silence, a sharp crack split the night — not from Greenbaum's gun, but from the far edge of the lot. Greenbaum's body pitched forward, throat burst, weapon discharging in a wild arc as he fell. Emily's gaze found Sharon in the same instant. Sharon was down.

The van that had looked dead lurched to life. The side door opened before it fully stopped. Hands — efficient, unhurried, entirely unsurprised — had Emily up and moving before she understood she was being moved. These men had been waiting for this. All of it. The shot. The chaos. Her.

She twisted back once. Sharon was on the concrete, still. A second vehicle — not the van, something she hadn't seen before, lights off, engine already running at the lot's far entrance — pulled forward toward where Sharon lay. Emily had no way to know what that meant. She had no time.

They put her in the van. The door closed. The lot disappeared.

Her thoughts were consumed by Sharon. The injustice. And she stilled, switching her mind's state from past and future to the present. Time slowed and seemed to stretch as she carefully absorbed her surroundings. The van, hollow except for two bolted-in seats on the side wall, pressed the double zip ties into her wrists and ankles as she lay in it. Every bump and pothole assaulted her weakened body. Even as the bare, ridged floor warmed, it jabbed her, slipping between her ribs, between her vertebrae. Yet, she remained still and present. Dim light filtered through the perforated steel wall separating her from the cab. 

Across from her, two armed men watched from their seats. Between the suffocating darkness and the sudden light, time became fluid for Emily, each moment stretching into eternity. The cold embrace of the room into which she had been thrust harshly reminded her of her current reality. Her mind raced, and every thought was a desperate attempt to find an escape, a solution, or a way to gain the upper hand. But with each passing second, her situation's weight pressed harder, squeezing hope from her heart. 

Yet, amidst despair, a spark of defiance flickered within her. Emily forced her breathing to slow, each inhale an act of resistance, each exhale a release of fear. The distant sounds of the city outside—a car horn, the muffled drone of traffic, faint laughter—grounded her, reminding her that life, her life, was still out there.

After what might have been an hour, the van's angle shifted upwards slightly as they drove. The burly man who had skillfully taken her by the neck earlier now pulled a small knife from his boot.

"Let's get these ties off you," he said in a deep rasp. The van door slid open, and a sturdy grip encapsulated her shoulder.

"Walk."

She stepped out of the van and onto the ground. The heavy hand on her shoulder steadily guided her through a packed parking garage until the ding of an arriving elevator reached her ears.

The elevator smoothly ascended an unknown number of floors, dinging again upon opening. Emerging into the desolate lobby, Emily's senses sharpened, taking in the sterile smell of antiseptic in the air and the muted echo of their footsteps on the polished concrete floor. 

The vast and unwelcoming space, its emptiness underscored by the soft hum of fluorescent lights overhead, stretched out before her. A long corridor lay ahead, its walls adorned with unremarkable art, the kind that fades into the background, forgotten by all who pass by.

Guided by the firm grip on her arm, someone led her down the hallway, her mind racing as she passed door after nondescript door, each potentially hiding untold stories behind their cold, impersonal facades. The palpable tension in the air, a silent oppressor, weighed heavily on her shoulders, making each step feel like wading through a trench. Finally, they stopped before a door, indistinguishable from the rest except for the ominous sense of finality it seemed to exude. The sound of keys jingling broke the oppressive silence, and Emily's heart sank. The door creaked open to reveal a room that promised little in the way of comfort or safety.

With a forceful nudge, someone propelled her into the room, and the door slammed shut behind her with a finality that echoed ominously in the empty space. The click of the lock sounded like a death knell, sealing her fate. Now alone, she took a moment to absorb her new surroundings, the stark, unadorned walls offering no solace, no hint of what was to come.

Emily's tears carved a path down her cheek. Her ragged and heavy breaths battled against the storm of emotions threatening to engulf her. The echo of Greenbaum's final moments haunted the corners of her mind, replaying with cruel insistence that bordered on torture. Why, she pondered in the depths of her turmoil, had she allowed her heart to harbor such disdain for a man now merely a whisper in the wind of their harrowing journey? 

With a surge of inner strength, she cast aside the chains of regret, donning instead the armor of resolve forged in a fire of adversity. The abrasive sound of the key grating against the lock heralded the entrance of an unexpected guest, her arrival marked by a gust of air that carried a semblance of life into the room's oppressive stillness.

"The orders were executed to the letter," the man's words barely cut through the thick air. "She's here, as requested."

"My orders and your execution diverged vastly. Leave us," Rachel commanded.

Her authority was unquestionable. Once the door clicked shut behind him, leaving them in a bubble of forced intimacy.

"Emily, I'm Rachel Vayes." Her voice was steady but her jaw was tight. "I work for Alpha Corp. The man who brought you here tonight was not a federal agent. He went significantly off-script, and I want you to understand that Sharon was not supposed to be harmed."

"Sharon's dead," Emily said.

Rachel didn't flinch. "I know."

The mention of it sat in the room. Rachel took in the blood on Emily's clothes without letting her expression change.

"Your man going off-script is what got Sharon killed," Emily said. "The bullet came from Greenbaum. Greenbaum only had a gun because your people put him in that lot. If not for those god-damned thugs, Sharon could have talked him down. She could have talked anyone down."

"We stand by our commitment to make this right, Emily."

"Make this right?" Emily said. "Do you have a time machine? A surgeon who can fill the hole in her head?"

"Emily, I'm..."

"Don't even say it," Emily said. "This is going to destroy Cole."

Rachel pulled up a chair and sat across from Emily without ceremony.

"And you want something from me?!" Emily said.

"I want you alive. Right now those are the same thing." Rachel kept her voice level. "Here's what I can give you: you walk out of this building tomorrow. No charges, no federal involvement, no one going near Cole. What I need is one conversation with our director and your cooperation for the next twenty-four hours. You don't have to believe in anything. You don't have to sign anything."

"You're asking me to play along?!"

"I'm asking you to survive," Rachel said. "Play along is just the method."

Emily looked at her for a long moment.

"And Cole?"

"Cole is what this is all about," Rachel said. "And the less he knows about what happened tonight, the better — for him and for you. That's the deal."

The silence settled. Emily looked at the door. She looked at Rachel. She was doing the math and the math kept coming out the same.

"Emily," Rachel said, "we just need you to stay here until tomorrow. One night."

"I can't just stay here," Emily said. "I have a life, people who—" She stopped. The night's events pressed in around the sentence and finished it for her.

"I know," Rachel said. "Tomorrow morning you'll meet our director. His name is Marcus. After that conversation, you go home."

Cole's name was the only thing that might have moved her, and Rachel hadn't used it — which meant she was either smarter than she looked or more dangerous. Emily looked at the door one more time.

"Fine," Emily said. "But I'm not doing this for you. I'm doing this for Cole."

Rachel nodded. 

"You'll find we made a bed up for you in the room across the hall," Rachel said as she started toward the door. "I'm in the next room. If you need anything at all, knock on the wall, and I will come right over."

As Rachel stepped out to leave Emily to her thoughts, her heart and mind felt at peace. A moment of clarity only accessible in isolation. She desperately wanted to tell Sharon what had happened and to talk to Cole. Looking around at the darkened corners of the room, she could see only Sharon's blood pooling on the dirty ground with the stars reflected in its glossy, crimson brilliance.
