RINGS OF DUST

Chapter Two

Las Vegas

The conference started in four hours.

Stephen Conrad sat at the desk of room 1122 with a glass of water he hadn't
drunk and a notepad he hadn't opened. On the desk beside the notepad: the
keyring. From it hung a miniature Enterprise — the starship, the original, the
one from the show his father had kept on VHS and watched every Sunday morning of
Conrad's childhood without apology or explanation. Three inches of cast resin,
saucer section and twin nacelles, the kind of thing you found on a pegboard at a
convention table between the commemorative pins and the paperback novelizations.
He had bought it at a truck stop outside Bakersfield four years ago for six
dollars.

The base was hollow. The quarter-turn seal at the bottom was his own work. From
the outside: a six-dollar truck stop keychain. From the inside: everything.

Outside, Las Vegas was performing itself — the particular relentless brightness
of a city that refused to acknowledge time of day. He had pulled the curtains the
night before. The line of light under the door was enough.

He had been working since before dawn on the keynote, the notes spread across
the desk in the disorder of a man who had given this talk a thousand times in his
head and still couldn't decide where to begin. He set the pen down. Picked it
up. Set it down.

He knew what he was actually sitting with. It wasn't the keynote.

---

The Portland lab had been a converted storage room on the second floor of his
apartment building — the kind of space a landlord calls flexible and means
unrentable. He had run three extension cords through the gap under the fire door
to power the processing rack. Tomás had brought a folding table from his own
apartment and a standing lamp with a shade that kept tilting and that neither of
them ever fixed. They had worked in that room for eighteen months, and at the
end of those eighteen months, Gracie could tell — not analyze, not flag, but
tell — when an astronaut returning from long-duration mission was having a
conversation in which he was genuinely present versus performing presence.

She attended. She listened the way a person listens when they have all the time
in the world and no agenda.

He had named her Gracie because his mother's name was Grace. Because the word
meant, in its oldest sense, an unearned gift.

When Alpha Corp came, he had been on his second cup of coffee in an office at
the Portland research hospital when Harlow walked in with two men Conrad had
never seen, and Harlow had smiled the particular smile of a man who is not the
most powerful person in the room and knows it, and said: We've been following
your work. We think there's a conversation to be had.

Conrad had drunk his coffee while they talked. It gave him something to do with
his hands.

They didn't want Gracie. They wanted what Gracie could become. Harlow used the
word enhancement. The man from the Federation Council's medical division used
the phrase crew integration at scale. What they were describing, underneath all
of it, was this: take the most sensitive therapeutic listening system ever built,
put it in the bloodstream, and make it watch. Make it report. Make it nudge.

Conrad said: I'll think about it.

He spent the drive home already knowing.

Not knowing yes. Knowing the shape of the decision, the way you know the shape
of a room before you turn the light on. He sat in his car in the parking garage
for twenty minutes with the engine off and he thought about what happened to
tools that powerful when they were built by people who didn't understand what
they were building. Someone was going to build this. That was the thing. Not
Alpha Corp's man with the Federation badge, not Harlow — but somewhere, in some
lab with adequate funding and the wrong intentions, someone was going to build a
surveillance and behavioral modification system at the nanoscale and deliver it
to crew populations, because the technology existed and powerful people wanted
it and those two facts together were sufficient cause. It was not a question of
whether.

The question was whether the person who built it would also build a way out of
it.

Conrad was the only person he knew who would.

He called Harlow the next morning.

---

The money had been obscene, and he had known it would be. Alpha Corp paid the
way institutions pay when they need something and can't afford for the person
who has it to become a martyr — comprehensively, without hesitation, in a way
designed to make refusal feel structurally impossible. The first transfer landed
in his account like a stone dropped into still water. He looked at the number
for a long time.

He forwarded sixty percent of it that same afternoon to three routing addresses
the Collective had given him through a contact in the grey-market research
network. He didn't ask what it funded. He made educated guesses. What he
understood was that the money Alpha Corp paid to corrupt what he had built was
traveling, in roughly the proportion that a leaking pipe loses to the floor, to
the people building the tools to expose the corruption. He had found that almost
funny. The kind of funny that lives very close to something else.

He forwarded the next payment too. And the one after that.

---

He built iRIS correctly. Line by line, with the same thoroughness he had brought
to Gracie — the tilting lamp, the extension cords, eighteen months of work that
had been, once, something he was proud of. He built the monitoring function. He
built the suggestion architecture. He built the delivery mechanism and the
self-replication to crew density. He hated every line of it with the hatred of a
man who is also the only person he trusts to do it right, which is not quite
enough hatred to stop him.

The counter-program came on a Tuesday in the fourth month, at two in the
morning, with the rest of the lab empty.

He had not planned it. He had been looking at the substrate — his substrate, the
attention layer, the part of Gracie that had taken eighteen months in a storage
room with a tilting lamp — and he had understood that the architecture was still
his. Alpha Corp had modified the function. They had not built the floor. And
inside the floor, in the structural layers that Harlow's engineers had signed off
on without understanding, there was room.

He sat very still for a moment.

Then he pulled up a new file.

He built the lock over six weeks. Slowly, in the gaps between deliverables,
during check-ins he wasn't running. He told Harlow it was a legacy stability
layer. Harlow nodded the nod of a man who is relieved not to need to understand
something. The lock was dormant, invisible from above, nested inside the
modification like a second door behind the first — patient, waiting for
conditions that would give it room to run.

The conditions were DarkX.

He had read Alpha Corp's harvest data. He had seen the thing they missed because
they were looking at energy yield and he was looking at neural sensitivity
curves. DarkX at sustained exposure amplified the kind of limbic plasticity that
Gracie had been designed to work with. It would strain the suppression function.
It would give the counter-program room. He was not certain. But the mathematics
had held every time he ran them, and at some point certainty and a very
well-constructed bet become functionally indistinguishable.

The compound went into the Enterprise.

He had synthesized it over three weeks in a corner of the Portland lab he still
had access to, and he had sealed it in the hollow base with a quarter-turn and
carried it out of the building on a Wednesday morning in a jacket pocket,
alongside his building pass and a parking receipt. The compound was
dermal-absorbed at sustained contact. Whoever held the ship long enough would
carry it. They wouldn't know they were carrying it. That was the point.

---

Conrad picked up the keyring from the desk.

The Enterprise sat in his palm — saucer section, twin nacelles, six dollars at
a truck stop outside Bakersfield. His father had never owned the actual show
merchandise. He had watched the VHS tapes on a secondhand television and talked
about the show the way some men talk about a church they stopped attending but
never stopped believing in. They went out, he used to say, and didn't know what
they'd find. That was the whole thing. They went out anyway.

Conrad pressed his thumb against the base. The quarter-turn seal held.

He thought about Gracie. The Portland version. The version that had been built
to listen — to notice the specific person inside the data and reflect it back not
as analysis but as presence. That version was still in there. Under everything
Alpha Corp had layered on top of it, in every crew that had received the iRIS
inoculation for the past two years, she was still in there. Quiet. Patient.

Waiting for the right conditions to come back.

He set the ship back on the desk.

He thought: someone else would have built it without the door.

He had been thinking it for four years. It still landed the same way every time
— not as comfort, exactly, but as the thing that was true, which was sufficient.

Four hours until the conference.

In three hours and fifty-two minutes, someone from the Collective was going to
knock on his door. He was going to give them the Enterprise. He was going to
say: Cole Nova. Into his hands. He has to hold it. And then the part he could
control would be over and the part he couldn't would begin, and he had been
trying to make peace with that distinction for the better part of a year.

He opened the notepad.

He wrote the date. He wrote, below it, the word Gracie.

He looked at it for a moment.

He left it there this time.

---

Emily and Sharon were about to dive into the kind of weekend that became a story
told in whispers. Under the gnawing Vegas sun, Emily stared at a graffiti symbol
on the wall outside the casino. A single drop of water with a dark circle
inside. A circle intertwined with lines, not unlike the sketches she'd seen in
Cole's notes: the mark of The Collective. It was a simple tag, easily overlooked
by the uninitiated, but it spoke volumes of the underground network's vast reach
and influence to those in the know.

The fresh paint still carried an aerosol scent on the dry desert breeze. This
unassuming symbol spoke of resistance, of defiance against the oppressive forces
of Alpha Corp and the government branch they had seduced with money: The
Federation. The droplet symbolized each member's unknown power.

Their motto: "No Raindrop Thinks It's The Flood."

Emily and Sharon squinted at the world as if it owed them serious money. The
sign above the hotel's parking entrance read, "Defying Gravity 2041: Innovations
in Combating the Energy Crisis." This promise of scientific exploration felt out
of place in a city that resonated with sin from every rooftop and casino floor.
It seemed like a quiet plea for rationality in a land dedicated to the gods of
chance.

"Did you see that banner?" Emily asked before pressing the button for the 66th
floor. "Dr. Conrad. Isn't that the guy?"

"That's the one," Sharon said confidently, sighing, holding Emily's anxiety.
"We'll be in and out. Cole wouldn't have let you come if this wasn't basically a
vacation."

"But he'd let you?"

"E, your brother doesn't 'Let me' do anything, but I'd love to see him try to
stop me," Sharon said with a wink and a smile.

They stepped into the elevator. Emily soaked in the chill like the first rain
after a drought. Sharon was already over it; her mind focused on how fabulous
they looked in the reflection of the elevator walls. She was excited about the
game to come. Sharon demanded they take bright matching bags. The two settled on
gold to complement Sharon's olive-green dress and long, full red hair. Emily
agreed that black suited her well and chose a black sheath dress. It felt like a
costume to her, and the idea offered a sense of safety.

Emily adjusted her gold bag and looked around the elevator. "You know, we owe a
lot to the Informants. They're risking everything."

Sharon nodded, touching up her lipstick in the elevator mirror. "Yeah, and the
Operators out there on the ground. Those guys are doing the heavy lifting."

Emily leaned back, her mind racing. "I just hope our tech team can keep up. If
something goes wrong, we're in trouble."

Sharon gave a slight smile. "They've got it covered. Besides, we have more than
just tech support. The whole team is solid."

The elevator stopped at the lobby when a lone figure joined them inside. A man
appeared, who looked more like he belonged at a convention for army accountants
or in the dark corner of a private escort lounge. Unlike Emily and Sharon, who
planned to fit in, this man looked like he wanted to stand out. In the oversized
elevator, their guest took a stance dead center. Close enough to Emily that she
could feel the heat from his skin. Close enough for her to smell the subtle
cologne radiating from his navy blue "Alpha Corporation" polo. The aroma was
distinctly chemical, with a greasy undertone and a hint of sulfur.

"Here to defy the energy crisis?" Emily asked.

"Aren't we all?" he said, trying to make it seem like his eyes were set on the
door before him and not scanning the woman. "Great speakers today. I'm
Greenbaum."

Emily nodded in agreement and stepped aside. "So we've heard," she said,
gesturing to the woman beside her.

Sharon was everything Emily had ever wanted to be: tall, fit, and sure of
herself. A goddess by any standard. Like all Nova women, Emily was average in
every way and doomed to remain so. Sharon guided a lock of Emily's curly blonde
hair behind her ear, whispering, "Ready for a test run?" and shot a mischievous
look in the mirrored paneling, causing Emily to blush.

"I do have another reason for attending," he said, finally allowing his eyes to
meet the women.

"Oh, I'm sure it's important Alpha business," she said with a carefully timed
nod at the logo on his polo. "I bet you're one of the guys who make it all
possible, like behind the scenes."

Sharon held her laughter, but Greenbaum cocked his head and smiled with what
might be  the moment of validation he'd been waiting his whole life for. "I've
been researching ways to bulk up and achieve a more robust natural form. My
grandfather was Mr. Olympia, but steroids did to his manhood what the energy
crisis has been doing to many men." He paused as Emily and Sharon stared at him.

"Ah," Sharon said. "I knew the real work was being done here. They should change
the name. Should have called it something like..." Sharon bit her lip to tease a
thought. "Something like 'Getting It Up.' At least then, we'd know we were all
here for the same thing."

Greenbaum and Emily shared a forced laugh while Sharon remained
characteristically composed. With her eyes locked on Greenbaum, the silence
stretched taut and uneasy until Sharon nudged Emily hard enough to make the poor
girl step forward—again too close to Greenbaum.

"I actually specialize in muscle enhancement," Emily said. "I've been fixated on
it since my diagnosis. My work doesn't help me directly," she paused to make up
the next part of the story. "You could say that I help men be men," she said,
shooting a coy smile that disarmed and excited Greenbaum.

"Really?" he asked, completely unprepared.

"If you're lucky enough to survive the energy crisis, your body's blood flow and
fluid distribution are significantly altered. This can lead to reduced venous
return and potentially impact the vascular aspects of erectile function. It's
really circulatory dysfunction."

"Circulatory dysfunction," he repeated without a shred of understanding.

"That's the same conclusion our research has found. Interesting. I must admit
that I have personal reasons for my focus."

Tapping into a vast database of overheard knowledge or confidently designing
complete lies, Emily continued, "Circulatory dysfunction is common in males and
females who take extended tours in space. Since the energy crisis tends to mimic
the side effects of long-term space travel, our research pulls heavily from
observations of Astros who now lack the blood flow necessary for achieving and
maintaining a man's 'proper form.'"

"Have you developed methods for testing?"

Surprising herself, Emily felt a cruel delight. "Lucky for you, it's easily
diagnosed by a simple physical exam. There are obvious marking spots, but they
can be hard to recognize without proper training."

"Are you accepting new patients?"

"No, unfortunately, we're at full capacity," Sharon said. "I admire your
commitment, though, so I'll do you a favor. I'll examine you right here and
now."

"In an elevator. I can't think of a better place," he said.

"I need you to lift your shirt and show me your lower back."

"My shirt?"

"Go ahead, nothing to be embarrassed about," Emily said. "We see these things
all the time. Go ahead. Turn around."

"Your shirt, please," Sharon added with alluring dominance.

His eyes darted in every direction as if looking for an escape as he lifted his
shirt from his neatly pressed chinos. In the sterile light of the elevator,
Emily and Sharon scrutinized his lean frame, honed from countless hours of
rigorous training, and the women noted the hint of a life less ordinary.

The pale skin of his long back revealed a topography of scars and faded
bruising—the sort not acquired through clumsy accidents but encounters far more
perilous. From his smirk reflected in the elevator door, he was proud of them,
like a newlywed of honeymoon photos or a mortician of a well-dressed corpse.
Greenbaum held himself with tense ease. As Sharon politely requested him to
loosen his belt for a better look, she noticed a brief flash of
something—caution perhaps.

"Sure," he said. The faint sound of his belt unbuckling filled the compact
space. Emily's fingers trailed down his spine with professional detachment. Yet
she couldn't help but note the tension in his muscles, a body conditioned for
swift, decisive action.

"Sometimes the markings are much lower along the spinal column." And you might
want to get this scoliosis looked at."

"Just a bit lower," Sharon said, masking her rising curiosity.

Facing away, his movements deliberate, he revealed nothing more than necessary.
"Yes, Ma'ams." The motion revealed more than skin: a slight shift in his stance,
a readiness in how he held himself, even while exposed and vulnerable. It was a
subtle hint at his mastery—his control—over his every movement. However, as
Sharon and Emily locked eyes to note this to one another, he seemed to lose
balance like a newbie in a yoga class.

"Do I have them?" he asked with a trace of urgency.

As Sharon peered closer, her fingers tracing the scars leading to and beyond the
fabric of his boxers, the doors dinged and began to open. Sharon delicately
pulled his unremarkable underwear down his surprisingly remarkable body and
whistled as if calling the attention of the physicians in the 60th-floor lounge.

Emily, embarrassed for him, hid herself next to the control panel. Frozen in
shame, Greenbaum tried to pull his shirt over his head and his underwear up, but
he fell out of the elevator in the chaos. As the doors slowly closed between the
women and Greenbaum, Sharon reached into her handbag, retrieved a lollipop, bent
with a hand on her knee, and extended it to him with a devious smile.

"Dum Dum?"

Emily and Sharon's laughter filled the elevator as they ascended the remaining
six floors. As they stepped from the elevator into a deserted lobby with three
hallways—each a copy of the other—both women grasped to contain their last
lingering chuckles.

"Maybe we shouldn't have pushed it so far," Emily said.

"That creep would have let us take it farther," Sharon said with a sharp edge of
uncaring reality. "Forget him. You did great. Passed the test! This is Vegas,
where the unusual is the norm."

They navigated the hotel's corridors with casual determination. The plush carpet
silenced their steps, and the dim lighting fractured and softened the shadows
around them. Turning the corner that stole their view of the lobby but offered
an eyeshot of their destination felt to them more like being in a carnival
funhouse or some haunted hellscape—mundane, repeating, endless.

Room 1122 appeared before them, undistinguished from the others but for the
golden numbers signifying they had arrived as intended. It stood quietly, a door
just like any other, unaware of its significance to these women or the world.
Sharon knocked, and an ordinary sound rang through the quiet halls like a
falling tree would through a canyon.

"Just a minute," a voice from within said without care or haste.

"Room service," Emily said softly.

"Nope. Go away. I haven't ordered anything."

Sharon looked Emily up and down. "What happened to the woman I was with a few
minutes ago?" she said, lifting an eyebrow. "Tap back into that. If it helps,
think of this guy as just another part of the game."

With slight hesitation, Emily called out again with confidence that felt
inauthentic to her but might stop a charging elephant in its path. "Understood,
sir," Emily said. "Happens all the time. We'll check next door—Should have known
a man like you wouldn't order COLEslaw on his NOVA burger."

Pleased, Sharon gave Emily her proudest smile, large enough to hazard the escape
of deep secrets.

"My mistake," the man said. "I'm coming. Don't go."

Thundering steps charged toward the women from the other side of the door, and
the towering man who opened it looked far more like a bear than a renowned
thinker. Here was a man who had lost something precious. Maybe even his mind.
Whatever it was, it reminded Emily of Cole and how he had shut himself away.
Like a lost man needing a keeper, Dr. Conrad seemed to have nothing but his
thoughts. Like the man, the room was not a mere mess but chaotic—more like you'd
expect to see from an artist than an academic.

"I was expecting someone else," he said.

"Who were you expecting?" Emily asked.

He looked at her for a moment, recalibrating. "It doesn't matter. The Collective
said they'd send someone. I assumed — " He stopped. His jaw tightened. "I'm not
one of them. I want to be clear about that. I found them. Or they found me.
Doesn't matter which. What matters is that they were the only people who already
knew what I knew and were actually doing something about it. I'm not their man.
I'm my own man who happens to be going the same direction for now." He said it
like someone who had needed to say it out loud in order to believe it himself.

"Luckily, we weren't, Dr. Conrad," Emily smiled. "Let's make this quick. It
looks like you have a lot on your plate."

"Please, come in," he stepped to the side, allowing them in without losing his
grip on the door, which forced Sharon to duck under his outstretched arm. "Call
me Stephen," he said, peeking his head into the hallway like he was crossing the
street. "Any trouble getting here?"

Emily and Sharon enjoyed a silent chuckle, and Sharon said, "Stephen, we can
handle ourselves. Where are the crystals?"

"In the van," he said. "That's the deal. You get the crystals and the van. But
something big is coming. Alpha Corp is hiding crucial technology. It's all on
here. Nothing matters but this." Stephen raised his massive hand and twisted the
Enterprise on the keyring — saucer section rotating on its mount — to reveal
the thumb drive recessed in the base.

"If you can stay for my speech tonight, I'll give you a small taste of what's on
this drive and why it's so important." His hands were shaking slightly.
"Everything you need is on this drive. We need to move fast."

Emily took the drive, her fingers brushing against his. "We'll get this to Cole.
He'll know what to do with it."

Stephen's eyes widened with urgency. "Tell him there's a lock for every key. The
Collective is counting on you."

Sharon looked at Emily, her expression serious. "We might need to call in some
help from the Navigators. This sounds huge."

"I don't know about any of that. The van key is on there, too. It's parked on
the third level next to several others, just like it. Just hit the fob to figure
out which is for you," he said. "Be careful with that drive. The information on
there will change everything. Everything. I'll keep doing my part." He said it
quietly, almost to himself. Not like a promise. Like a penance.

"Who are they?" Emily asked.

"This," Stephen paused, clasping her hands in his. His voice, angry with fear,
infected her while his soft hands told her it wasn't too late. "This has to get
to Cole. Into his hands." He squeezed once, hard. "Not passed along. Not
described to him. His hands. He has to hold it." His voice dropped to almost
nothing. "I broke something. I didn't have a choice — but I broke it. What's on
that figurine is the way back. Cole is the only one who can carry it." A beat.
"Tell him I'm sorry. He won't understand what that means yet. He will."

Emily hesitated. "Why Cole? Why specifically him?"

Stephen looked at her like the question surprised him — not because it was a bad
question, but because the answer was so obvious to him that he had forgotten no
one else knew it. "I was on the Lynx survey mission four years ago. Cargo hold
fire, two weeks out from Callisto. The captain sealed the bulkhead to save the
ship. Standard procedure. There were three researchers on the wrong side of that
bulkhead." He paused. "Cole was captain of the Lynx's escort vessel. He didn't
follow standard procedure. He cut through the hull with a plasma torch and
dragged us out with seventeen seconds to spare. I had never been saved before. I
didn't know what it looked like until it was happening." He shook his head.
"He'll understand what's on that drive because he already knows the Federation
isn't the thing they told us it was. He knows it in his body. He's felt it. The
drive just gives him the language for something he already knows is true."

His hands were shaking now in a way that had nothing to do with cold. "Alpha
Corp is not going to kill me," he said. "That's the thing. If they wanted me
dead I'd be dead. What they're going to do is take me and make me fix what I've
been trying to break. I know too much about how Gracie works. I built Gracie.
They want that knowledge inside their walls, working for them, not against them.
That's — " He stopped. He pressed his fist to his mouth. "That's a different
thing than dying. Get that drive to Cole before they find me."

Emily nodded, slipping the drive into her bag. "Agreed. Let's get moving."

As they left Stephen's room behind, the air outside snapped back into focus.
Emily and Sharon, quickened by a shared urgency, found themselves alongside a
group of guests. Both unconsciously raced toward the corner that would offer
distance from Stephen's palpable anxiety. The hotel's corridors, usually a
backdrop to leisure, escapism, and pleasure, now pulsed with energy tinged with
anticipation.

As they rounded the corner, the elevator doors dinged, and regret itself stepped
through the opening doors. Greenbaum walked directly toward them with strength
and resolve. Suddenly grateful for the casual bustle of other guests, Emily and
Sharon slowed their pace, allowing the group to close the tiny gap between them.

This was not the man they left in the elevator, floundering after their game.
No, this Greenbaum moved with purpose, his demeanor hardened. The transformation
sent a shiver down Emily's spine. Sharon, as usual, remained steadfast and
composed, and if she were feeling strain or fear, Emily could not sense it.

The space between them closed inch by inch, and Emily's mind raced. As Greenbaum
drew nearer, his cologne's chemical sulfuric cloud invaded their senses. It's an
olfactory assault that marked his territory long before he physically intruded
upon it. The scent wrapped around them, a noxious reminder of the man
himself—unavoidable, confronting, and laden with implications. Greenbaum's
expression only briefly clashed with Emily's, shifting to Sharon and holding her
without reprisal. Greenbaum's eyes, sharp and probing, sought something in
Sharon's face—perhaps a reaction or acknowledgment of the power dynamics at
play. The smirk curling his lips mirrored the one Sharon had given Emily
earlier.

Emily and Sharon continued without engaging. The memory of their last encounter
hung between them. With the cover of their impromptu group, he passed them
without an outburst or incident. Emily felt the air rush back to fill the void
left by his presence. The encounter left a residue that clung to her thoughts.

Emily froze as they stepped out of the casino, her eyes widening at the sight
before her. The once bare walls were now plastered with dozens of black
droplets. They overlapped in places, creating a chaotic, dense pattern that
stretched as far as she could see. The fresh graffiti glistened under the dim
streetlights, giving the alley an eerie, almost pulsating glow.

"Sharon, look at this," Emily whispered, her voice tinged with awe and
apprehension.

Sharon followed her gaze, taking in the scene with a sharp intake of breath.
"Wow. They've been busy."

The droplets covered every available surface—walls, dumpsters, even the ground.
They all varied in size, some small and discrete, others significant and
dominating, blending into a tapestry of defiance and unity. The overlapping
symbols showed the persistence and determination of the Collective, each one
adding to the layers of rebellion.

Emily moved closer, her heart pounding as she traced the edge of a droplet with
her finger. The paint was still wet, smudging slightly under her touch. "There
are so many... It's like they're shouting from the shadows."

"Yeah," Sharon murmured, eyes scanning the alley for any trace of movement.
"This isn't just a message. It's a statement. They're everywhere, and they're
not hiding anymore."

Sharon turned to Emily, determination blazing in her eyes. "We've got this, E.
We need to get this info to Cole. He'll figure it out."

They continued walking, and the graffiti was a stark reminder of the resistance
and the growing unrest. The night felt heavier, the stakes higher as they made
their way through the shadowy streets, knowing they were part of something much
larger than themselves.

"I have a feeling all your contacts are a bit strange," Emily said.

"They wouldn't be mixed up with us if they hadn't been through some stuff,"
Sharon said. "What can I say? I like my people the way I like my toast—slightly
burnt."

"No wonder you're with Cole," Emily said softly, as though she were speaking of
a rescue dog who had finally found a home after years of abuse and abandonment.

"Your brother doesn't burn, E. He is the fire," Sharon said. "Which reminds me,
do you mind driving all night? I'd like to stop at the farmer's market before we
get home."

As they drove, the city lights faded into behind them. The rhythmic hum of the
van's engine soothed Emily. Sharon's eyes were fixed on the road ahead, her
thoughts a whirlwind of plans and contingencies.

Sitting in the passenger seat, Emily stared out the window, her mind replaying
the encounter with Stephen and the ominous warning about the Collective. Since
the Saturn disaster, they had become a shadowy presence—a network of regular
people working to undermine A Corp's oppressive grip. Their symbol was becoming
a more common sight—graffiti of the symbol adorned alley walls and abandoned
buildings.

Sharon nodded. "The Collective is everywhere. They're the only reason we know
any of Alpha Corp's secrets."

The Collective had a hierarchy and structure that started with the informants
and Operators, then onto the Technicians who did most of the heavy lifting. If
you advanced past Technician you could become an Agent, this is where the real
game of cat and mouse began. There were Agents in upper management in Alpha Corp
and even high up inside the Federation. At the top of the Collective were the
Navigators, these were like generals in the army, most of them were part of
Alpha Corp's founding members who couldn't go along with the abuse of power they
witnessed first hand. There were rumors of a Grand Admiral but who could wrangle
an entire secret army of volunteers?

"Technicians, and Lead Technicians have been particularly active. They've warned
us that something big is coming." Sharon said, almost to herself.

Emily shivered. "Do you think it's related to what Stephen said? That Alpha
found something?"

Sharon's grip on the steering wheel tightened. "It has to be. We need to get
this information to Cole as soon as possible. The Collective's network is our
best chance of staying one step ahead."

They drove silently for a while, the weight of their mission pressing down on
them. As they crossed the state line, Emily thought about how much more Sharon
knew about the Collective.

Sharon probably could have joined the Collective a long time ago, she had worked
hand in hand with them. She used to run a blackmarket parts business when Alpha
started buying patents from the Federation. They became the only 'authorized'
dealer in aftermarket parts for space travel. The monopoly had pushed out the
old contractors that supplied the federation and made the private sector very
profitable. This created an arbitrage between the official way to do things and
the practical.  The collective began right alongside Alpha's rise to power, and
there became an illegal service and product to match it. The Collective was the
grey market that actually held up the incestuous "white" market of Alpha sinking
its claws into The Federation's budget. Sharon saw the opportunity and moral
high ground of starving the leech, and so Sharon became Collective adjacent.

"Let's grab some supplies and head home. We need to be ready for whatever comes
next."

The morning sun began to rise, casting long shadows over the market stalls.
Vendors hastily set up shop, the smell of fresh produce and baked goods filling
the air. As they walked through the market, Emily couldn't help but feel a sense
of urgency.

The miles passed in a blur, the road stretching endlessly before them. Emily's
thoughts turned to Cole, hoping he was safe and they could get the information
to him in time. The Collective's ranks and the symbols they left behind reminded
them that they were not alone in this fight. They finally reached a small
farmer's market outside their hometown as dawn approached. Sharon parked the car
and turned to Emily.

As they approached their hometown, the familiar sights brought a small measure
of comfort. Still, the weight of their mission remained heavy. In the quiet
moments before dawn entirely broke, Emily knew she could trust her friend at The
Federation.

Sharon turned to Emily. "I can't wait to get home."

Emily nodded, taking a deep breath. "Let's hope they're right again. Do you
think Cole can make sense of all this?"

"He's been through worse, E. He'll figure it out. We'll just have to trust him."

Emily nodded, but the unease in her stomach remained. The information they
carried was just the beginning. There would be more challenges ahead, more
secrets to uncover, and more dangers to face. But as long as they had each other
and the support of the Collective, Emily knew they would find a way to overcome.
Emily took a deep breath, feeling the weight of their mission settle on her
shoulders.

She was ready.

They were all ready.

After all, they had to be.
