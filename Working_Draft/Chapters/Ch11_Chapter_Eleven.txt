RINGS OF DUST

Chapter Eleven


Like the murmur of distant thunder, the containment building's underbelly resonated through the floor and into Chancellor DeMint's bones. His fingers twitched, restlessly plucking at his beard in jagged rhythms. Pluck. His lips curled faintly, and he let the pricks wash over his in silence like a  broken clock. Tethering himself to time. Solid. Controllable. Real. 

Pale light streaked across DeMint's face, throwing sharp shadows under his eyes and cheekbones, distorting his features into something hollow. His shadow stretched grotesquely, flickering across the room with each step, warping into monstrous proportions before shrinking back to human form. He stopped mid-step, shoes squeaking against the polished floor as the machine emitted a louder hum. The glow rippled outward. His heart skipped a beat in sync with the pulse. His hand dropped from his beard, curling into a tight fist, knuckles white with tension. The low hum crawled under his skin, feeding the gnawing inevitability that spread through him.

"iRIS has gone dark," he muttered, his breath crumbling under the weight of his words.

Vance shifted his stance, moving just inside the machine's glow. His rigid, the tension radiating from his form, but his face remained unreadable. 

"The last message from Officer Taylor was routine. No anomalies. We're still within mission parameters," Vance replied, though even his words seemed to hang in the air, brittle and unsure.

DeMint's steps faltered. His hand dropped. His jaw tightened. 

"Routine?" he said. "We've lost all contact. That's. Not! Routine."

Vance's gaze flicked upward, tracking the machine as it spun. 

"What about the satellites?" DeMint's said before he exhaled slowly. Heavily.

 "We don't know where the Golden Dawn is exactly. Sending a message would be like throwing a bottle into the sea," he said, the resignation clear in his tone.

DeMint stopped dead in his tracks, the weight of Vance's words sinking in. "What if Alpha can't fix this?" DeMint asked, his voice edged with an unfamiliar vulnerability. "They've got the code behind a wall of patents. We're stranded."

Vance hesitated, his hands twitching at his sides, fingers curling unconsciously. "There's another way. It's risky," he admitted, his voice quieter now, almost as if he was afraid of what he was about to suggest.

DeMint's eyes narrowed, suspicion and desperation mingling in his gaze. "What way?"

"Dr. Stephen Conrad," Vance said, the name itself carrying a weight, as though simply uttering it was a form of rebellion. "He was working on something beyond iRIS before he disappeared. There's a prototype. I think it could bypass iRIS."

DeMint's stare hardened, his fingers curling reflexively at his side. "You think this prototype will help us reach the Golden Dawn?"

Vance nodded, though there was a hesitation there, the slightest flicker of doubt. "It's the only real shot we have."

Silence descended upon the room again, thick and suffocating, wrapping around them until even the softest sound felt like an intrusion. The only break was the machine's constant hum, low and mechanical, like the breath of some unseen force. DeMint's eyes drifted back to the machine, watching as it spun, the motion too smooth, too even, too controlled. Something was wrong—he couldn't shake the feeling. Was it just his imagination playing tricks on him, or was there something off in how it moved?

He turned to Vance with a curt nod. "Do it." The words were cold and detached, but there was no mistaking the threat that laced them. His voice lowered, carrying a weight that hung in the air between them. "But if you fail, I'll have your head. Understand?"

Vance didn't flinch, didn't blink. His face remained impassive, his eyes fixed on DeMint with unshakable resolve. "Understood."

DeMint's hand moved to his pocket as the faint buzz of his phone hummed against his leg. He ignored it. Without another word, he spun on his heel, the hem of his coat sweeping through the air behind him as he strode toward the door. His footsteps echoed briefly against the metal floor before the hum swallowed the sound. The door slid shut with a soft hiss, closing him off from the room—and from the weight of his own decision—leaving Vance alone in the dim, sterile glow of the machine.

The hum deepened, resonating through the walls and floor as if the machine itself had become aware of the silence that followed. It was a sound that seemed to creep into every corner of the room, pressing against the edges, vibrating through the very foundation. Vance stood motionless, his eyes locked on the machine, feeling the sound stretch and expand, vibrating up through his boots into his chest until it felt like it had taken on a physical form, binding him to the moment.

And far away, on the Golden Dawn, that same hum threaded through the ship's corridors, vibrating through the walls and into Officer Taylor's bones as he sat at his post, his fingers hovering just above the controls. His eyes scanned the screens, searching for anything that might explain the unease gnawing at the back of his mind. The diagnostics blinked back at him, serene and untouched, the lights green and unbroken, like nothing had changed.

Too clean, Taylor thought, a knot tightening in his gut. Too perfect.

His shoulders tensed as the ship's systems reported back with the same unnerving precision. His fingers twitched over the controls, but he hesitated. The numbers were flawless. Everything was flawless. Too flawless.

"Systems functioning within acceptable parameters," iRIS's voice echoed through the command deck, calm as ever, like the hum that persisted through the ship.

Taylor's jaw clenched as the glow from the screens illuminated the faint lines of tension on his face. His hand hovered over the keys, but still, he hesitated. The silence pressed down on him, too perfect, too heavy. Something wasn't right.

"Nothing to report," iRIS chimed again, its voice unnervingly calm.

Taylor's gaze flicked over to the other crew members, their movements too smooth, too synchronized. The hum of the ship persisted, threading through the silence like the last tether to reality, but it felt wrong now. It wasn't something he could point directly—no, not yet—but it gnawed at him, deep in his gut.

He reran the diagnostics. The same results blinked back at him, sterile and polished, as if someone had scrubbed the system clean. His gut twisted tighter. No one else saw it, not yet, but he did.

The hum persisted, drumming through his bones. Something was coming.
