RINGS OF DUST

Chapter Ten

Rachel walked Emily into the building without speaking. Through the lobby,
into an elevator, three floors up, then into a long dark hallway that ran
in both directions. A cheerful fat Buddha statue sat underlit at each end.
Small, warm lights shone sharply downward from the ceiling in focused
beams, leaving the walls in dark. Two men in dark suits peeled off the
wall as they entered — one ahead, one behind. They did not announce
themselves. They did not look at Emily. That was the point. At the end of
the hall, a large set of double wooden doors stood decorated with brass
hardware and long polished bar handles. Rachel gripped one, breathed, and
pushed forward.

The guards took up positions outside the doors. Emily noted the placement
without turning: flanking the exit, not the entrance.

The room was wide and dim, the kind of dim that came from choosing not to
turn on half the lights. Floor-to-ceiling windows pulled cloud and sky
into the far wall. Along both sides, a dozen televisions ran news feeds
and sports scores at ambient volume. On one screen, a news anchor sat
composed at her desk. Emily looked at her. Then at Rachel. Rachel's color
shifted slightly and Emily filed that away.

The man was kicked back in a swivel chair with his feet on the wide
mahogany desk — the posture of someone who had designed this impression.
A glass of orange juice sat at the desk's corner. Four ice cubes. Emily
counted them without meaning to.

He got to his feet.

"Emily Nova." He moved toward her with the wide, unhurried stride of a
man who had never once been made to hurry. "I'm Marcus Verguld."

She knew that face.

Not from files. From a specific afternoon: Cole with a bloody lip and
this boy's knee on his chest, and her grabbing the collar and putting
him on the ground in front of his friends. He had looked up from the
dirt like he genuinely could not believe what had just happened to him.
She had not given him time to decide what to do about it.

He had grown up. The dirt was better carpeted now.

"I've kicked your ass before," Emily said. "Be aware that I will do it
again."

Something moved behind Marcus's eyes — not embarrassment. The quiet
recalibration of someone whose assessment had just been revised upward
by a measurable amount.

"You have a good memory," he said. He settled back onto the edge of the
desk and looked at her with the unhurried attention of a man who had
nowhere else to be. "Sit down."

"I'd rather stand."

He studied her a moment. Then he looked at Rachel, who stood near the
doors with her face arranged carefully toward the middle distance.

"You were in Las Vegas," Marcus said. "You and Sharon."

Sharon's name in his mouth with the same flat weight as Las Vegas and
Conrad, as if Sharon were a coordinate and not a person, not someone who
had been taken from an empty lot three days ago while Emily
was three states away and not there. Emily did not move.

"You met with Steven Conrad. He gave you something." He turned toward her
fully. "I want to know what it was."

"I don't know what you're talking about."

"The keychain," Marcus said. "Small plastic figurine — the Enterprise.
You held it. You drove it to Cole's cabin and put it in his hands." His
voice had the quality of a man reading from a list he had memorized a
long time ago. "What's inside it."

She thought about the hollow base. Conrad's specific instruction, calm
and level: *it has to reach Cole's hands. He must hold it.* She had
thought that was sentiment. She had thought Conrad was being theatrical
about a trinket.

She genuinely did not know.

"He farts in his sleep," Emily said, "and he's two months late on the
phone bill, and he has an unhealthy relationship with a four-armed monkey
that drinks hand sanitizer. I don't know anything about a keychain."

Marcus looked at her with the expression of a man who was neither amused
nor annoyed — simply cataloguing.

He turned to the desk. A few finger taps on the glass surface inlaid in
it. The banks of screens on the walls changed.

Emily recognized it immediately.

The encrypted drive. The research archive she and Sharon had been handed
in Vegas — the file structure, the visual signature of the export format,
the specific documentation of things that were not supposed to exist. She
recognized all of it.

She had put this in the hands of the people who needed it. It was already
where it was supposed to be.

Marcus was showing it to her like a card.

"We expected this through certain channels," he said. "It found other
channels. Contingency plans were made." He glanced at her. "You
recognize it."

"I've never seen that in my life."

"No." And she understood he did not mean *I don't believe you.* He meant
*that's the right answer, and I haven't decided yet if it's the true one.*
He was filing it separately from what he thought she knew.

He moved to the window. The clouds moved behind him.

"The Golden Dawn launched twelve days ago," he said. "Harvest sequence
underway. The last major raw crystal batch coming off Saturn's rings —
the one that changes the market permanently, if it arrives intact." He
paused. "There is a vessel considerably larger than the Golden Dawn that
has been pacing Cole's course since he crossed the inner belt. Cole
doesn't know it's there."

The room changed.

Not the room. Her position in it. The calculation restructured itself
around a single fixed point.

"What do you want," she said.

"What Conrad gave you. What he told you and Sharon in that hotel room
that you haven't told anyone." He came away from the window. "That is the
piece I don't have. And until I have it, I can't tell you what happens to
that mission when the larger vessel makes its move."

The room was very quiet.

Emily thought about Cole at nine, looking up at her from the dirt. She
thought about Sharon's hand across a table in Vegas. She thought about
Sharon in that empty lot at the edge of the city and the message that came
afterward and the specific quality of a grief that hadn't been allowed to
land yet because there had been too many miles to drive, too many things
to do, and now she was standing in this room and the grief was still
waiting and she was still not done.

She thought: *the only way to help him is to get out of this room.*

"I don't know what was in the keychain," she said. "I handed it to Cole
and I drove away. Conrad didn't explain it to me."

Marcus watched her for a long moment.

"I believe the keychain part," he said. "The rest of it, I'm reserving
judgment on." He moved back to the desk. "You're more useful to me as a
question mark than as a problem. So you're going to walk out of here
tonight."

He held out a small card. Plain. No logo. A number printed on one side.

"If anything comes back to you — about what Conrad gave you, about what
he built, about any of it. There's a number."

Emily looked at it.

She took the card.

"Ms. Vayes will see you out."

---

After the doors had closed and the elevator had engaged and the sound of
footsteps on tile had faded from the corridor:

"Put one on her before she reaches the street," Marcus said. "The small
one. She won't feel it."

The man standing in the far corner of the room — not the guards; the
other one, the one who had been there before Emily arrived — nodded once
and moved toward the secondary exit.

Marcus sat back down. He picked up the orange juice. He looked at the
screens, still lit with the contents of the drive, and he thought about
Conrad. A man he had underestimated twice and did not intend to
underestimate a third time. He thought about what a careful man builds
into a children's toy when he knows someone might come looking.

He drank the juice.

The ice was already melting.

---

In the elevator, Rachel held her face toward the floor numbers. Her eyes
moved once toward the security camera mounted in the upper corner.

"Careful," she said.

Emily took the hint.

The parking garage was cold. Emily slipped the card behind the potted
plant on the landing without breaking stride. She understood what it was.
He did not believe she knew anything — believed it enough to let her walk
— but he wanted a thread.

She was not going to be the thread.

She turned to Rachel. A taxi was pulling into the garage entrance.

"What is Marcus going to do to Cole?"

Rachel's face held nothing. "Nothing happened," she said quietly. "For
both of us. Nothing happened."

Emily felt something that was not quite disgust — more the particular
exhaustion of having been in a room with a man who understood the price
of everything and had very deliberately not paid it.

"Nothing happened," she said.

She got in the taxi.
