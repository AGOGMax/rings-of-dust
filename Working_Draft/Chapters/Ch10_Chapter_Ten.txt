RINGS OF DUST

Chapter Ten


Emily and Rachel walked into a long, dark hallway that extended in either direction. A cheerful fat Buddha statue sat underlit at each end. Small, warm lights shone sharply downward from the ceiling, casting down brilliant beams of light to the floors in focused beams. Yet the walls managed to escape any of the light. Directly ahead, a large, heavy set of double wooden doors loomed. They were decorated with sharp brass swathes and long polished bar handles. Rachel gripped one with her hand. Breathing deeply, she pushed forward.

Kicked back in a swivel chair, with his feet atop his wide mahogany desk, a man in a gray suit lounged. Beyond him, the floor-to-ceiling windows revealed giant passing clouds.  On either wall, a dozen televisions showed live news feeds and sports. Racheal was sitting at an anchor desk on one of the screens. Emily looked at her, finally realizing where she recognized her from. Racheal blushed. Emily noticed. The man quickly got out of his seat and stood up. 

"Emily Nova, It's a pleasure to meet you." He moved towards Emily with casual, wide strides. "I'm Marcus Verguld."

A chill went down Emily's spine. She stalled to buy herself time.

"How much are you going to pay me to stay quiet about your goons?"

Marcus shifted towards Rachel. "Ms. Vayes, can you please elaborate?"

"There were complications, sir."

"Oh? What kind?" Marcus said.

"The kind that requires a," Rachel's shoulders hunched forward, "a clean-up crew, sir."

With a grand wave of his hand, Marcus dismissed her. 

"Oh, yes. Already handled. Don't you worry your little head about that?" 

He sat on his desk with his shoulders back wide. 

"Now, Emily. Look, business is just business. I won't let you blackmail me because that simply does not work. Just tell me what I want to know about your brother."

"He farts in his sleep, and snores. He's late on paying the phone bill. He drinks too much. Oh, and he has an unhealthy relationship with a four-armed monkey that drinks hand sanitizer." Emily offered.

He shuffled around on the desk, moving his hands as if juggling his ideas. He waxed lyrical. 

"Let's imagine the news story. Your little agent got mugged, didn't he? Greenbaum, was it? He must have told the wrong person about his upcoming payday. He was there to collect your bribe money, was he not? To keep your side hustle under wraps? And, uh, you were kidnapped for the cash, and maybe you were dumped in a field. Sometimes people get kidnapped and are never seen again, you know?"

Emily gulped hard.

"Lucky for you, our favorite public star, Ms. Vayes, just so happened to be driving by and saved a woman in need. Now, obviously, you don't want your illegal activities exposed. This will play out very well for us and very poorly for you." Marcus said.

"I don't know what you're talking about." Emily defended.

"Sure you do," Marcus insisted. "Does our unreliable friend Cole know about your dirty deals?"

Emily saw a gap in Marcus's armor. A subtle tell that he is no stranger to discussing complex topics with little to no real knowledge of the details.

"With a past like his," Marcus said, "do you really want to add your dirty laundry to his pile?" 

"Cole's a big boy. He can take care of himself. Besides, he's a little busy at the moment."

"Don't you know I have eyes and ears everywhere? Don't you know the Federation is crawling with MY employees? There's only one reason to take on a mission as expensive as Saturn right now."

Emily could tell he was bluffing. Something didn't add up.

Marcus went to his desk. The glass surface inlaid in it served as a touch screen. With a few finger taps, the bays of screens on the walls flooded with Cole's face and information. Everything from medical records to his Federation transcripts and grade school photos was laid bare.

"Does this information look familiar?" Marcus said.

It did. In fact, it was a copy of the thumb drive she and Sharon were given in Vegas. On one of the screens, known relatives were displayed.

"We expected a drive with this info to be delivered along with the crystals you dropped off," Marcus said. "Luckily, we are getting pretty good at contingency plans."

"We," Emily said. "Not I. Me and Sharon delivered your god-damn Dark Matters right before she was shot and you kidnapped me. Cole kept the drive."

Emily felt her heart pounding. 

"Cole's heart is gonna break when he hears about Sharon."

The glint in Marcus's eye told Emily that she had just played her last card. Her knees almost buckled in regret. Her head spun. 

"Let's make some phone calls. We'll get you a ride home, and you can think of this whole ordeal as a bad dream," he said.

Now that Marcus had evaluated the threat that Emily posed, she was no longer of value. He was bored with her. 

Emily glanced at Rachel. 

"People will be looking for me."

Marcus pursed his lips.

"A little paperwork, and that goes away too. You get your life back, and the Federation leaves you alone. So what do you say?"

Emily didn't know what to do or say. Her instincts told her she had to leave. She'd have to pull herself together to fix this.

"Yeah."

Marcus hissed through his teeth, "Rachel, please see that Ms. Nova is taken care of."

Rachel's pale expression told Emily that they needed to get moving. Rachel took Emily by the elbow and escorted her out the door. In the elevator, Emily couldn't help but vent her frustration.

"Rachel, what was that?" Emily demanded.

Rachel didn't look at her but kept a stolid face toward the elevator buttons, counting down. Rachel looked towards the security camera in the elevator

"Careful," she said, and Emily took the hint.

The elevator opened to the parking garage. Emily turned to really observe Rachel.

"What is Marcus going to do to Cole?"

A taxi came into full view and pulled up to Rachel and Emily.

"Please. Nothing happened. For both of us, nothing happened, all right?"

Emily felt disgusted with herself. 

"Nothing happened. Got it."

Max ran his dry fingertips along the rough edges of his weathered oak table, feeling the familiar grit beneath his skin. The surface, scarred from years of restless nights and unanswered questions, was as uneven as his thoughts. A single light hung overhead, casting sharp, unforgiving shadows that made the room feel even smaller than it was. The faint hum of the lamp above him was the only sound, its flicker a reminder that nothing around him was as stable as it seemed.

In front of him, the stolen fuel cell gleamed dully under the light's hesitant glow. It was untouched since the day he'd left Alpha Corp—abandoned, like everything else. Max stared at it, half-expecting it to give him answers, though he knew better by now. The thought of selling it tugged at him, a practical solution for the bills piling up. The black market would pay a good price. But the longer he looked at it, the more something twisted in his gut—a strange cocktail of anger and, worse, hope.

Max hadn't realized how small his apartment had become until the two men stepped inside. Chip crossed the threshold first, his heavy boots thudding against the worn hardwood floor, each step feeling like it pulled the air from the room. Dale followed, his gaze sweeping across the space, taking in everything with a kind of quiet intensity that made Max's skin prickle.

They didn't sit, didn't even make a motion toward the shabby couch half-covered in clothes and old blankets. Instead, they hovered near the table, drawn to it like everything in this room eventually was. The fuel cell still sat hidden beneath the blankets, but its presence loomed, casting a shadow over the room that Max couldn't ignore.

Chip leaned forward slightly, his posture relaxed but with a tension lurking underneath, like a spring waiting to uncoil. "We're not here to waste your time, Max. We know what you're sitting on, and we know what Alpha Corp did to you."

Max's eyes flickered toward the hidden cell. He could feel the weight of it, even now, as if it had somehow grown heavier since they entered. "And what do you expect me to do about it? I'm just trying to stay alive," he muttered, voice low and sharp, the edge of bitterness unmistakable.

Dale tilted her head, her eyes narrowing slightly. "Survival's not going to be enough for much longer," she said, her voice cool, calculated. "You know that. You've seen what Alpha Corp is capable of. You've felt it. But what you discovered? That can change things. It can change everything.

Her words hung in the air, thick and heavy, pressing against Max's chest. He wanted to scoff, to tell them to get out and leave him with his quiet, miserable existence, but something in Dale's tone—something in the way she looked at him—made the words catch in his throat. She wasn't just talking about fuel cells or DarkX. This wasn't just about tech. This was personal.

He dragged his hand along the table's surface, the worn wood rough against his fingertips, grounding him. The cell was right there, within arm's reach. All he had to do was pull the blanket away and show them. But once he did, there'd be no going back. He'd be in it—deep. And that scared him more than Alpha Corp ever had.

"What exactly do you think I can do?" Max asked, though his voice was quieter now, more uncertain.

Chip and Dale exchanged a glance. It was subtle, but Max caught it—a quick flicker of understanding passing between them, like they had prepared for this moment. Chip straightened, crossing his arms over his chest.

"We need access to Alpha's labs," Chip said, his voice steady, unwavering. "We need someone who knows their systems, their weaknesses. Someone who's seen behind the curtain. You've been inside. You know things no one else does."

Max's breath stilled. The mention of Alpha's labs brought back memories he'd tried to bury—cold, sterile hallways, the constant hum of machines, the pressure to perform, to innovate, to push beyond the limits no matter the cost. He could still feel the weight of their expectations, the suffocating urgency of it all.

"And what happens if I say no?" Max asked, though part of him already knew the answer. There was no walking away from this. Not anymore.

Dale's gaze softened, just a fraction. "You can say no, but that's not going to stop Alpha Corp from coming after you. They already know what you're capable of. And they won't just blacklist you next time."

The words hit him like a punch to the gut. He'd been clinging to the idea that if he just kept his head down, if he stayed out of sight, Alpha Corp would eventually forget about him. But the truth was staring him in the face now, in the form of two strangers from the Collective, telling him that staying hidden wasn't an option. Not anymore.

Max exhaled slowly, the weight of it all settling in his bones. He reached for the blanket, fingers brushing the edge as he hesitated. This was it—the moment everything changed. There was no going back.

With a slow, deliberate motion, he pulled the blanket away, revealing the fuel cell. The metal gleamed under the dim light, cold and unfeeling, yet full of dangerous promise. Max could feel Chip and Dale's eyes on him, but he didn't look up. Instead, he stared at the cell, as if willing it to give him an answer.

"This is all I have," Max said quietly, his voice barely above a whisper. "It's not enough."

Dale stepped forward, her expression unreadable. "It's more than you think."

Max's hand hovered over the cell, fingers twitching as if he wanted to grab it, to hide it again, to pretend this wasn't happening. But he didn't. He couldn't. Because deep down, beneath all the fear and anger and hopelessness, there was that flicker again—the one he hated. Hope.

And hope was dangerous.
