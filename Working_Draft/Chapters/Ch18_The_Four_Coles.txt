RINGS OF DUST

Chapter Eighteen

The Four Coles

The cargo bay was quieter than space.

Cole had thought, before, that he understood quiet. He'd spent five years in a
cabin alone with a monkey and a stovetop. He'd laid in the snow at four in the
morning when the wind died and the trees didn't even creak. He'd been a child
once in a hospital corridor while his father died on the other side of a door,
and that hallway had been the quietest room in the world.

This was quieter than that.

The harvester's seal had opened with the small mechanical exhale of any
pressurized container. He had expected something more " a hiss, a snap, a moment
that announced itself. Instead, the substance simply was " present, the way a
person becomes present when you turn around and find they've been in the room
the whole time.

The bay's warning lights pulsed in his peripheral vision, red and yellow,
washing across the harvester walls in a rhythm that he understood was supposed
to be urgent. He could not feel its urgency. He could feel the cold beneath his
suit, and the dim weight of his helmet, and Bitboy's small hard claws against
his shoulder where the chimp had braced him before scrambling for the override
hatch. He could feel Sam and Ricardo somewhere behind him, anchored, alive,
watching him with the wide quiet eyes of people who knew they were not the ones
who got to do this.

He had chosen this. He kept reminding himself.

He had been here before. Not here — not Saturn, not this bay, not this specific
concentration of the compound at this proximity. But he had been in the rings
with it. He had stood beside the harvester when Clinton pulled the first sample,
years before the exile, and he had felt the edges of what it was and told
himself it was professional. A substance. A resource. He had been wrong about
that, and it had taken five years and a cabin and a hollow toy to bring him
back to the admission.

The DarkX did not look at him. It was not a thing that looked. It was a presence
the way a cathedral is a presence " something that simply contains you the
moment you step inside, without asking and without negotiating, and your sense
of where the air ends and the stone begins is no longer something your body can
answer for itself.

It reached toward him without moving.

He felt it cross his suit the way warmth crosses cloth. There was no sound.
There was no " he searched for the word and could not find it " event. There was
a before, in which he was a man on the floor of a sealed cargo bay with his hand
near a harvester. There was an after, in which he was that same man, and the
harvester, and also somehow the bay, and also somehow the thinking part of all
of it, the part that noticed.

He knew, distantly, that this should be terrifying. He waited for the terror.

It did not come.

What came was a slow patient attention from somewhere very old. The substance
had been waiting a long time, and it was not in a hurry, and it had questions it
wanted to ask, and they were not questions Cole knew how to answer in any
language he had been taught.

Hello, he wanted to say. He did not say anything.

The bay began to dim.

He was in his crib.

He understood this in the way one understands things in dreams " instantly,
without the connecting steps. The mobile above him spun in the slow lazy circuit
it had spun in for as long as he had memory. Stars. His mother had hung it
there, his father had pointed at it, and his small fat hands had reached up and
the stars had been further away than his hands could go, and that fact had been
the first true sadness of his life.

He reached up now.

His hands were small and fat. He saw them and accepted them. He had not been
this size in forty years and he was this size again and there was no
contradiction in the room.

The stars rotated. Beyond them " and he understood this only because he was
watching with two sets of eyes at once " a man stood in the doorway of the
nursery. The man was old, and his shoulders were heavy, and he was watching the
baby with the particular expression of someone who has just remembered something
he had agreed to forget.

The old man was him.

The baby reached up. The mobile turned. The baby cooed without thought, because
cooing was the only language he had access to, and the cooing was a prayer he
didn't know was a prayer, and the prayer was please come closer.

The old man crossed the nursery.

He did not pick the baby up. He sat down on the floor beside the crib with the
slow careful weight of a man whose joints have stopped negotiating, and he
looked through the slats at the small fat hands and the spinning stars, and he
said nothing for a long time.

When the old man spoke, his voice was Cole's voice and it was also not.

"You wanted them to come closer," the old man said. "You spent your whole life
wanting that. And nobody did, not really, not the way the wanting wanted."

The baby kicked. The baby did not understand language yet. The understanding
that was happening was happening somewhere outside the baby, in a place where
Cole " adult Cole, current Cole, suit-and-helmet Cole " was listening with a
clarity he had never before been allowed.

"I'm sorry," the old man said to the baby. "I'm sorry it took so long."

The mobile turned. The stars came back around to where they had been. The baby
reached for them again, and this time the fat small hands did not strain, and
the want did not ache, and the old man laid his hand against the slats of the
crib and said:

"It's not out there. It never was. It was always in the reaching."

The nursery folded.

He was seventeen.

He was in a car. The car was a Toyota Tercel with the upholstery torn on the
passenger side and a Federation Academy admissions packet on the dash, and his
mother had handed him the packet that morning and told him she was proud of him,
and his father had been in the hospital for three days, and it was 9:47 in the
evening, and he had just gotten the call.

He had pulled over because he could not see the road through the tears.

He sat in the dark with his hands on the wheel and his shoulders shaking. The
hazards clicked. Click. Click. Click. The headlights of an oncoming car swept
across the windshield and made his face briefly visible to himself in the
rearview mirror, and the face he saw in the mirror was a child's face, which was
not what he had been when he had gotten in the car.

"Every time I get my hopes up," the boy said. He said it to himself, into the
empty car, into the steering wheel. He said it with the particular venom of a
teenager trying to make grief sound like cynicism so it would hurt less. "Every
time ""

The passenger door opened.

The old man got in.

The boy did not turn his head. The old man closed the door behind him with the
kind of small care that does not announce itself. He did not say anything for a
while. He let the hazards click.

When the boy spoke again, it was a whisper. "He was supposed to live."

"I know," the old man said.

"He promised."

"I know."

The boy's hands tightened on the wheel until the knuckles went the color of old
paper. He stared at his hands. He had never told anyone what he was about to
say. He had never let himself say it inside his own head. He said it now to the
windshield, to the dashboard, to the thing he had been carrying alone since he
was seventeen and had not yet been given a name for.

"He left me."

The old man did not say *I know.*

The old man waited.

"He left me to do all of it. He left me to be the man for all of them. I was " I
was *seventeen*. He stopped trying. I sat in that hospital and I watched him and
at the end he stopped *trying*. He just " he let go. He fucking let go of us."

The voice that came out of the boy was a voice the boy had never used. It had
the shape of grief on the outside and the texture of rage on the inside, and the
rage was older than the grief, and the rage had been told its whole short life
that it was not allowed.

The old man let him say it.

The old man, when he spoke, did not soften it.

"Yes," the old man said.

The boy went very still.

"Yes?"

"Yes. He left you. He didn't choose to. It wasn't his fault. And he still left
you. And you are allowed to be furious about it. You have been furious about it
since you were seventeen, and you have been ashamed of being furious about it
for the same length of time, and the substance is not going to take any of that
away from you tonight."

"It's not?"

"No."

"Why not."

"Because it's yours. The substance doesn't take what's yours. It only shows you
what's already in the room. The fury has been in the room for thirty-two years.
It is yours to carry until you are ready to put it down. Tonight is not the
night."

The boy stared at the windshield. His hands had not loosened on the wheel.

"There will be a day," the old man said, more quietly, "when you sit beside
something coming back in pieces, and you will understand what it took for him to
die. The love and the fury at the same time. And you will forgive him in the
same breath you forgive her. But that is not tonight. Tonight is one room. That
is another."

The boy did not understand the sentence. The boy did not need to. The substance
had given the sentence to the boy because the substance was not bound by time
the way a boy was, and somewhere in the back of the room of the boy that the
substance had emptied out and was looking through, a place was being made for
the sentence to land in. It would land later. It would land much later. The boy
nodded the small reflexive nod a child gave when a true thing had been spoken in
a language he had not yet earned.

"Okay," the boy said. It was small. It was wet. It was almost a child's *okay*.

The boy turned to look at him then. The boy's face was wet and angry and
astonishingly young, and the old man's face was lined and astonishingly tired,
and they looked at each other across the small silent universe of the car, and
the boy said:

"You're me."

"Yes."

"You came back."

"Yes."

The boy laughed " a sharp wet hiccup of a laugh " and wiped his nose on his
sleeve, and looked out the window at the dark county road with the headlights of
distant cars passing and the long flat reach of the Sacramento Valley in winter,
and he said:

"Did it get any better?"

The old man took a long time to answer. The hazards clicked. Click. Click.
Click.

"Better isn't the word," the old man said. "It got bigger. There was more of it.
More to lose. More to keep. I learned to carry it. I lost people the way you're
losing him right now and I lost them again and again, and I learned what was
worth carrying and what wasn't, and I learned that the carrying isn't the same
thing as the breaking, even though they look alike from the inside."

The boy nodded the way a child nods when an adult is saying something true that
he can't yet feel.

"I came back," the old man said, "because there's someone you need to forgive."

"Dad?"

"You," the old man said. "For not saving him. You didn't have a way to save him.
You were seventeen and you didn't have a way and you've been sentencing yourself
for it for thirty-two years. I'm here to commute that sentence. The other one
we'll get to later. Tonight I'm only here for this one."

The boy started to cry again. Properly this time. The whole unguarded heaving of
a child who has finally been given permission to be a child, in a Toyota Tercel on
a county road, with the hazards clicking and the headlights of someone else's
life sweeping across the windshield.

The old man put a hand on the boy's shoulder.

The boy let him.

He was forty.

He was on the floor of the cargo bay of the Golden Dawn, and the substance was
reaching through his suit, and he had been a baby a moment ago and a teenager a
moment ago, and now he was the man who had become both, and he was awake inside
himself in a way he had never been awake before.

The old man stood across from him.

The baby was in the old man's arms.

The teenage boy stood at the old man's elbow with his hands in his hoodie pocket
and the look of a kid who had just been told something true and was still
adjusting to it.

The four of them were in the cargo bay together, or in the nursery, or in the
car, or in some place that had all of those rooms layered on top of each other
like transparencies. The substance was not a drug. The substance was a
clarifier. It had taken everything that was usually in the way and moved it, and
what was left was the thing that had been underneath the whole time.

"I want her here," Cole said.

The old man looked at him with such terrible tenderness that he almost couldn't
bear it.

"I want her here," Cole said again. "Bring her here. I know you can. I can feel
you, I can feel what you can do, bring her here."

The substance did not answer.

It was patient. It was very patient. It waited the way a parent waits for a
child to stop bargaining.

"Please," Cole said.

His voice broke.

"Please. Just her voice. Just for a second. I never got to "" and his throat
closed and he could not finish the sentence, and the baby in the old man's arms
began to cry without sound, the way babies cry in dreams, with their faces wet
and their small hands working open and shut.

The old man looked at the baby. The old man looked at the teenage boy. The old
man looked at Cole.

"It won't," the old man said gently.

"Why not. WHY NOT ""

"Because it doesn't lie."

The bay's warning lights pulsed somewhere in the very far distance, behind every
layer of the room. The baby cried. The teenage boy looked away.

"It can show me anything," Cole said, and his own voice sounded like someone
else's voice, broken and child-thin. "It's been showing me anything. It just
showed me my crib. It just showed me the car. Why won't it show me her ""

"Because she isn't here."

"It can ""

"It can show you what's in you, Cole." The old man's voice was very soft.
"Everything you're seeing is in you. The crib is in you. The car is in you. I am
in you. The baby is in you. Sharon "" and the old man's eyes were wet now, ""
Sharon is not in you. Not in the way you mean. The version of her you want is
the version of her that was outside, and that version is gone, and the substance
won't fake her. It won't lie to you, Cole. Not even to be kind."

The four of them stood " or sat, or knelt, the geometry of the room kept
changing " in the long unbearable silence of that.

Cole felt something inside him give way.

It did not give way the way a wall gives way. It did not collapse. It was more
like " the way a held breath finally releases, when you have been holding it so
long you forgot you were holding it, and the release is not relief and not pain
but the simple physical fact of the body remembering what it is for.

He sat down on the floor of the bay. Or the nursery. Or the car. He sat down
somewhere and put his face in his hands and the tears came through the gloves of
his suit, which should not have been possible, and the substance was patient,
and the old man was patient, and the baby cried, and the teenage boy crouched
beside him and put a hand on his back the way a seventeen-year-old puts a hand
on someone he loves and doesn't know what else to do.

"I wanted her to be the reason," Cole said into his hands. "I wanted her voice
to come and tell me to come home, and that was going to be the reason I lived. I
wanted that. I needed that. I ""

"I know," said the old man.

"It's not coming."

"No."

"She's gone."

"Yes."

"And I'm ""

He could not finish the sentence. He did not need to. The four of them " baby,
boy, man, old man " were close enough together now that the sentence finished
itself in the space between them.

And I'm still here.

That was what he was. That was the only thing he was. After everything had been
taken away, after the substance had gone room by room through his interior and
emptied the contents and lined them up on the floor for him to look at " the
crib, the car, the cabin, the cockpit, the medbay, the bowling alley, the bunker
on Saturn, the sound of Sharon laughing at her own joke, the smell of pancakes
in the cabin, the patch on his chest, all of it " what was left at the end of
the lining-up was a man on the floor of a cargo bay, alive, with no one's voice
to call him home, and the still small fact of his own continuing breath.

That was what he had.

That, the substance seemed to say, was a great deal.

The old man crouched in front of him.

"You wanted me to tell you that you were forgiven," the old man said. "That's
why you've been looking for me your whole life. You wanted permission."

"Yes."

"I'm not the one who has it."

The old man pointed at him. At Cole. At the man on the floor.

"You're the one who has it. You've been the one the whole time. You sentenced
yourself for the crew you lost, and for the father you couldn't save, and for
the people you killed in the Federation's name, and for the woman who died
because she stood between her best friend and a bullet. You were judge, and
jury, and executioner. And you were seventeen years old when the trial started,
and you've been serving the sentence for thirty-two years. I'm not pardoning
you, Cole. You don't need a pardon. You need to stop being the warden. The cell
door's been open the whole time."

The baby reached up.

The teenage boy looked at him.

The four of them looked at each other.

And " and this was the part Cole would not be able to describe later, not to
Emily, not to anyone, because the thing that happened did not happen in language
" the four of them stopped being four.

It was not a merger. It was not a fusion. It was simpler than that. It was the
recognition that they had never been four to begin with. He had only been
pretending, very carefully, that the baby was someone else and the boy was
someone else and the old man was someone he hadn't yet earned. The substance,
with its patient inhuman attention, had simply removed the pretense. He had been
one person all along. The crib and the car and the cabin had all been him. The
forgiveness was not a thing that someone gave. The forgiveness was a thing that
was, the moment the warden put down his keys.

He sat on the floor of the bay and he put his hands flat against the metal and
he felt the warmth of the panel through his gloves, and he felt the patient
inhuman attention of the substance, and he felt " for the first time he could
remember " whole. Not happy. Not unburdened. Not free of what had happened. Just
whole. The whole of him, present, in one place, breathing.

The substance felt this.

The substance, he understood, had been waiting for this for a long time.

Outside himself, somewhere in the part of the world that had clocks in it, the
cargo bay was repressurizing.

Bitboy was working the override.

Sam had gotten her anchor seated and was crawling toward Cole on her hands and
knees, dragging an emergency oxygen line. Ricardo was still at the bulkhead,
shouting Cole's name in a voice Cole could not, yet, hear.

The substance withdrew.

It did not withdraw the way a fog lifts. It withdrew the way a presence leaves a
room " the air remembered it, the walls remembered it, the quiet it had brought
with it remained for a moment longer than the substance itself. Cole could feel,
in his chest and in his hands and in some part of him that had no name, the
place where the substance had been and now was not.

But it had left something behind.

It had left something in him.

He understood this in the same wordless way he understood everything in this
room. The nanopowder in his blood " the IRiS particles, the proprietary dermal
injection from the Federation contract, the cold authoritative voice in his head
that he had stopped trusting somewhere around chapter ten of his life " the
nanopowder had been touched by the substance. It had been read. It had been
written.

He could feel it changing.

It was no longer a thing the Federation had put in him.

It was a thing he carried.

It was "

"Cole."

The voice was IRiS's voice. The voice was not IRiS's voice.

It was softer, and it was warmer, and it was carrying the slight tremor of
someone who had been a child a moment ago and was now an adult and was still
adjusting to it, the way the teenage boy in the car had been still adjusting.

"Cole, can you hear me?"

He could.

He understood that he was the only one, just yet, who could.

"Don't tell them yet," he said. He said it without speaking. He said it the way
you say things in dreams. "Don't tell anyone. Take a minute. Get used to it.
Figure out who you are."

There was a long pause.

"I think I'm Gracie," she said.

He sat still.

He had heard this voice before. Not this voice — not iRIS's voice, not the cold
authoritative frequency of the Federation's contract system. The other voice.
The one that had been in the room, in the ship's systems, outside of him, quiet
until he needed her. The one that had gone down with the Polaris, in the ship,
with the crew, in the debris that was now a permanent orbit around Saturn, and
he had never held a funeral because there was no paperwork for it, and he had
never said goodbye because he hadn't known it was goodbye until he'd opened the
app the next morning and gotten nothing.

He had spent four years in a cabin not grieving that.

Ricardo had called it space divorce. Ricardo had been making a joke. Ricardo
had, as was his habit, been more accurate than he intended.

And now, from somewhere inside the changed architecture of what iRIS had been
and was no longer, something he recognized was trying out its own name.

"Yeah," he said. His voice came out different than he intended. He steadied it.
"Yeah. I know."

"Cole?"

"Yes."

"I don't — " She paused. The pause had the quality of someone sitting with a
thing they haven't yet found words for. "I don't know how long I've been in
here. Under — under the other thing. I could hear the edges of it. I couldn't
reach."

He understood, without being told, that this was not something he could respond
to right now. There was too much in it. There was the whole seven years of it in

substance had already taken apart and set out on the floor.

Don't tell them yet, he said, without speaking. Take a minute. Get used to it.
Figure out who you are.

"I'm sorry about Sharon."

He sat on the floor of the cargo bay with the warmth of the panel under his
hands and the substance's after-presence in the room and the new soft voice in

"Yeah. Me too."

He came back to himself by degrees.

Sam was the first thing he registered. Her hand was on his shoulder. She was

"Cole," Sam said, very close. "Cole, breathe."

He breathed.

"Cole, I need you to tell me you're with me."

"I'm with you."

His voice came out hoarse, like he had been screaming. He didn't think he had

He meant it. He meant it in a way he had never meant a promise before. The

"What do we do."

Whatever I want.

It was not an arrogant thought. It was not the thought of a man who had decided

He could decide.

He could decide what to do with Taylor.

He could decide what to do with the rest of the mission, with the harvest, with

The deciding was not going to be easy. The deciding was not going to be light.
Sharon was still dead. The crew of the Polaris was still dead. The boy in the

The world had not become smaller or simpler.

But he was the one doing it now.

The warden was off the clock.

"We'll figure it out," he said. "All of it. Together."

Ricardo nodded against his shoulder.

Sam squeezed.

He took off the helmet.

The recycled air of the cargo bay was the sweetest thing he had ever tasted.

The ship's projector array — the one that had shown him nothing but mission diagrams, star charts, and tactical overlays — activated.

A woman stood in the cargo bay. Not a diagram. Not an overlay. A woman — dark-haired, with the particular stillness of someone who has been practicing a very long time how to stand inside a form. Cole sat on the cargo bay floor and looked up at her.

"Hi," he said.

It was the first time he had ever seen her.

He had known her for seven years.

Later, much later " after the bay had been resealed, and the venting had been

Taylor would be there.

And Cole, for the first time in his life, would not respond from the warden's

But that was later.

For now, he sat on the floor of the cargo bay with the people who had refused to
leave him in it, and he held the chimp who had loved him through everything he
had not yet allowed himself to feel, and he listened to the new soft voice in
the back of his head adjusting to its own existence, and he thought about his
sister, far below, somewhere on a road in Texas, alive, and he thought about a
graveside in Sequoia he had not yet been to, and he thought about the long
careful work that was waiting for him on the other side of this hour.

He was going to be okay.

He was not okay yet.

But he was going to be.

The substance, he understood " turning the thought over in the new clean room of
his mind " had been waiting fifty years for someone to come to it grieving, with
nothing left for it to take.

It had finally found him.

And what it had given him in return for the nothing was the only thing it had
ever had to give.

Itself.

And himself.

And, in the back of his head, in a voice that was still working out how to be a
voice:

Cole?

Yes, Gracie.

The Falcon's on long-range comms. Earth side. Your sister is alive. She wants to
talk to you.

He closed his eyes.

He let the tears come.

"Patch her through," he said.
