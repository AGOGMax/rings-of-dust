RINGS OF DUST

Chapter Twenty-One

Max's Cargo

Six blocks from the hardware store was a park.

Max had identified it eleven days ago on his fourth perimeter walk, when he was
still in the Mikof Forsinko period of getting his cover right — the particular
rhythm of a scientist on contract, efficient and unhurried, the lunch routes and
errand routes and the way a man who belongs somewhere moves through a
neighborhood versus the way a man who's watching it does. The park had two
benches facing a fountain that no longer worked. The fountain was shaped like a
heron. The heron was concrete, slightly drunk-looking, leaning at four degrees
toward the east. Max had liked it because it was the kind of detail that felt
permanent in its imperfections — no one was going to fix the heron — and
permanent things made good landmarks.

Squirrel was on the second bench.

He was reading a newspaper that was four days old in a language that was not his
first language — Max had figured this out on the second day of the Panama
operation, from the way Squirrel's eyes moved along the lines, the slight
recalibration at the end of each paragraph. He had not mentioned it. Mentioning
things like that was not Max's job. Noticing them was.

Max sat down on the first bench. He did not look at Squirrel. He looked at the
heron.

He had been three blocks from the facility when his hands had started to shake.
He had been three and a half blocks when he had identified what the shaking was
— adrenaline clearing, the body filing its report. He had walked the six blocks
to the park with his hands in his pockets and his pace at
contractor-leaving-for-dinner, and the whole walk he had been thinking about Dr.
Oren.

He sat on the bench.

"Did you get it?" Squirrel said. He said it to the newspaper.

"Yes."

"The full protocol?"

"Disposal Protocol Seven and the project archive up to the current quarter.
Seventeen months of records." Max looked at the heron. "They named it Seven.
Which means there are at least six others."

Squirrel was quiet for a moment.

"Hand," he said.

Max put his right hand in his jacket pocket, felt the contact of Squirrel's fingers
closing around the drive. He brought his hand back out empty.

"You're shaking," Squirrel said.

"I know."

"Are you hurt?"

"No." Max looked at the fountain basin, which had been dry for long enough that
something had grown in it — a thin patchy green, the kind of life that moves
into empty spaces. "There was a nitrogen cycling alert in the containment lab. I
was in the building when the alarm sounded."

"You got out."

"I was already at the loading bay. Two badge reads on the planned route, then
the maintenance exit." He paused. "I had six minutes before the alert would have
locked down external access. I had four minutes of comfortable margin."

"Then you're fine."

Max looked at the four-degree heron. He thought about comfortable margin. He
thought about the loading bay door closing behind him and the sound of the
facility's alarm cycling through the cooling vents, muffled by three layers of
industrial wall.

"Oren is going to know it was me," Max said.

Squirrel set the newspaper on the bench. He looked at the fountain.

"Not you," he said. "Mikof Forsinko. Who doesn't exist."

"He'll know it was someone inside. He'll know it was someone he walked through
the stabilization process with, someone he explained the patent archive to." Max
paused. "He was collegial. He was genuinely helpful. He was the kind of man who
enjoys his work and wants other people to enjoy it too." A pause. "I liked him."

Squirrel said nothing.

"He's going to come to work tomorrow and find out that someone he spent four
days being generous to used his access codes and his trust as operational
cover." Max looked at his hands. They had stopped shaking but he was watching
them anyway. "That's going to do something to him. That kind of thing does
something to a person."

"It does," Squirrel said.

"I want you to understand that I know that." Max looked up. "I didn't abstract
it. I knew, going in, what it would cost him. I knew and I went in anyway and I
got the drive and now I'm sitting here telling you that I know." He paused. "I
just need — I needed to say it to someone who would hear it."

Squirrel was quiet for a long moment. He picked up the newspaper. He set it back
down.

"I hear it," he said.

A pigeon landed at the edge of the fountain basin and looked at the green growth
with the interested detachment of a creature for whom the world contained
nothing that was not potentially edible. Max watched it investigate and leave.

"Disposal Protocol Seven," he said. "What I read — what I had time to read,
which wasn't all of it — it's not just the stabilization process. It's not just
one product line or one research division. It's a supply chain decision. They're
managing the rate at which solutions reach the market. They have eleven viable
technologies at varying stages of deployment readiness, and they're holding
seven of them back until the price point and the market conditions meet specific
internal thresholds." He paused. "The energy crisis is an inventory decision."

Squirrel nodded.

"They didn't cause it on purpose," Max said. "Not the original crisis. The
original crisis was Conrad's failed experiment — that was real. But once the
shortage was established, once the infrastructure collapsed enough to create
dependency, they started managing the recovery. They decide how fast the lights
come back on. They decide when." He looked at the heron. "The world has been
sitting in the dark waiting for solutions that have been on a shelf in Panama
for years, and the reason they're on the shelf is because the timing isn't
profitable yet."

"That's going to be important," Squirrel said, "for what happens next."

"What happens next."

"The proof goes out. Through the parallel network — the same grey market your
fuel cell research has been circulating in for two years. Decentralized. No
single point of control for Alpha to suppress. It's going to reach about a
hundred thousand people in the first forty-eight hours." Squirrel paused. "After
that, it's not ours to manage."

Max thought about a hundred thousand people. He thought about what they would do
with seventeen months of Disposal Protocol records and the names of eleven
technologies and the internal communications of a company that had decided how
fast humanity could come in from the cold.

He thought about Dr. Oren coming to work tomorrow.

"Is there anything for him?" Max said.

"Oren?"

"If the proof goes public, the facilities get scrutinized. The employees get
scrutinized. There are people in that building who had no idea what they were
building. Oren might be one of them." He paused. "Is there a process for—"

"The Collective has a protection channel for Alpha employees who come
forward voluntarily. For people who didn't know and want out." Squirrel paused. "For
people who did know and want out too, depending on the specifics." He looked at
Max. "That's not nothing."

"He might not use it."

"He might not."

"He might not even believe it at first. The records might look fabricated to him
because he's inside the machine and the machine looks different from inside."

"It might."

Max was quiet for a moment.

"But it's something," he said.

"It's something."

He sat with that. The pigeon came back to the fountain and left again. The heron
listed east. Two women went past on the path behind them, talking in rapid
Spanish about something domestic and urgent. The afternoon had the quality of a
city that didn't know anything had just changed, which was accurate, because
nothing had changed yet — the drive was in Squirrel's pocket and the relay hadn't
gone out and in eighteen hours the world would still look like the same world it
had been when Max walked into a hardware store in Casco Viejo four days ago
under a name that wasn't his.

And then it wouldn't.

"Red is at the transit house," Squirrel said. "There's a clean route to the airport
through the market district. Your extraction flight is at seven."

Max nodded.

He did not move to get up.

Squirrel waited.

"I've been thinking about the flywheel since I left the building," Max said.
"The curve." He turned the math over in the way he always turned the math over,
the way he had been turning it over since the oak table and the fuel cell and
the first data point that shouldn't have been possible. "The first thirty-three
days produce a non-linear yield but it's still recoverable — if you wanted to,
you could reverse-engineer the curve, understand the mechanics, sell it
incrementally. The sixty-day inversion is harder to monetize in the traditional
sense because you start getting more than you put in, and markets are not
designed for that. But the hundred-eighty-day exponential—"

He stopped.

"The hundred-and-eighty-day exponential is what they're really afraid of," he
said. "Not the proof. Not the protocol records. The flywheel. Because if the
flywheel is real and it's reproducible, it doesn't matter how long they hold
their inventory. The curve makes their shelf meaningless." He looked at his
hands. "Everything compounds. Once people understand that the compound works in
their favor, not against them, the shortage model collapses."

Squirrel was watching him.

"You've been running on that for a long time," Squirrel said.

Max thought about his notebook. The ink-stained margins, the reworked equations,
the moment two years ago when the data point appeared and he had stared at it
for thirty seconds before he understood what it meant. The moment he had
understood what it meant and realized that he could not tell anyone in any
official capacity because of who would hear it first.

"Yes," he said.

"Max."

He looked at Squirrel.

"You can stop now," Squirrel said.

Max sat with this.

He sat with it the way he sat with difficult data — patiently, without trying to
rush to the next thing, letting the information exist before he decided what it
meant.

*You can stop now.*

He had been running the equations in his sleep for two years. He had been Mikof
Forsinko for twenty-two days. He had been careful and methodical and precise and
he had walked out of a building in Panama City with a drive full of proof and he
had walked six blocks with his hands shaking in his pockets and he had sat down
next to a man reading a newspaper in a language that wasn't his first and he had
handed over everything he had, and now the work was done.

The work was done.

He felt, distantly, that he might cry. He was the kind of person who found this
annoying and also, when it happened, unavoidable. He pressed the side of his
thumb against his eye and looked at the fountain with its concrete heron listing
east and its thin green growth and its empty basin, and he thought about all of
the things he had been holding.

The fuel cell on the oak table. The notebook. The 33-day yield data. The 60-day
inversion. The 180-day curve. The name Mikof Forsinko. The access badge. The four
badge reads he had planned, the two he had used. The drive. Dr. Oren's face when
he had explained the stabilization process with the particular pleasure of a man
showing a colleague something beautiful.

He had been holding all of it.

He put it down.

Not carefully. Not in any particular order. He just — let it down. The way you
set something on a table after carrying it from one room to another, and the
room you've arrived in is the right room, and you can put it down now, and you
do.

He breathed.

"Okay," he said.

"Okay."

"The extraction flight is at seven."

"Yes."

"Through the market district."

"Red knows the route."

Max looked at the heron one more time. He thought he might miss it, slightly —
the degree of tilt, the empty basin, the specific quality of something that was
going to keep listing east for the rest of its existence without anyone seeing a
reason to intervene. There was something in that he found companionable. He did
not know why. He decided he did not need to know why.

He stood up.

His legs held without asking anything difficult of them. His hands were in his
pockets. His pace, when he walked away from the bench, was the same pace it had
been on the six blocks from the facility — the unhurried pace of a man who
belonged somewhere, or who had learned well enough how to look like he did.

Squirrel folded the newspaper. He did not follow immediately. He watched Max walk
away, until Max was at the path's edge and turning toward the market district
and Red and the transit house and the seven o'clock flight, and then Squirrel sat
for another moment on the second bench in the park near the hardware store, six
blocks from a building where a drive's worth of truth was already absent from
its slot, and he thought about the particular quality of a person who does the
difficult thing not because they were trained for it but because they understood
it was the thing that needed doing.

He thought about the fuel cell on the table. The notebook margins. The flywheel
curve that made the shortage model obsolete.

He thought: *not bad for a scientist from a town no one's heard of.*

He folded the newspaper and put it in the recycling bin at the path's edge and
walked out of the park in the direction that was not the direction Max had gone.

---

Max boarded the seven o'clock flight under his own name.

The passport scan took three seconds. The agent at the desk looked at the photo,
looked at him, handed it back, and turned to the next person. Max walked through
the gate with the carry-on that had been packed for forty-eight hours and a
boarding pass that the Collective's transit coordinator had issued under the
same route as any scientific conference attendee leaving Panama for the week.

He found his seat. He put the carry-on in the overhead. He sat down.

The aircraft began its pre-departure sequence — the overhead announcements, the
safety demonstration, the push back from the gate, the taxi to the runway. Max
watched the terminal lights move past the window. He watched the runway lights
resolve into a line. He felt the acceleration, the geometry of the nose coming
up, the moment of departure.

Below him, the city lights spread out in their grid — the parallel lines of the
streets, the clustered brightness of the commercial district, the darker gaps of
parks and water. He could see, at the edge of his window, the glow of the Alpha
Corp campus across the industrial waterway. It looked, from above, like any
other light. It was the same wattage as the residential blocks around it, the
same color, the same spread.

He turned away from the window.

He closed his eyes.

He thought about the flywheel. He thought about the 33-day yield, the 60-day
inversion, the exponential at 180. He thought about the moment the curve became
self-sustaining — the moment you stopped putting more in than you were getting
out, the moment the system fed itself.

He thought about what Squirrel had said: *everything compounds.*

He thought: *yes. That's exactly right.*

He had named the principle. Squirrel had named it *flywheel.* He liked the word. It
was a mechanical word, an honest word — no abstraction, no pretense of magic.
Just a wheel that, once set in motion, stored the energy of its own rotation and
used it to keep going. Simple physics. The kind of thing that, once understood,
made you wonder how you had managed without the understanding.

The aircraft climbed. The lights below became smaller and then abstract and then
disappeared into the cloud layer that sat over Panama City in the evenings. The
seat belt sign clicked off.

Max opened the notebook. It was the same one he had been keeping since the oak
table — filled to two-thirds, the ink denser in the margins where the good ideas
had arrived sideways. He turned to the first clean page.

He wrote the date. He wrote the word *Panama.* He wrote, in his careful
engineering hand, the thing he had been thinking about since the park bench:

*The shortage model collapses when the compound works in the individual's favor.
The energy crisis persists only as long as the flywheel's benefits are withheld
from the people who need them. Once the withholding ends — once the proof is out
and the public understands the curve — the incentive to manage scarcity
disappears. You cannot manufacture a shortage of something that compounds in
everyone's hands.*

He looked at what he had written.

He wrote beneath it, because it was true and because he had earned the right to
say it:

*The drive is out. The work is done.*

He closed the notebook.

He put it in the carry-on pocket.

He settled into the seat.

He slept.
