RINGS OF DUST

Chapter Seven

The plane's wheels bounced on the runway, and the speed brakes deployed with a
bellowing whoosh. The sudden deceleration pressed Cole sideways in his chair.
Ricardo calmly braced himself. Bitboy was jarred awake and, not recognizing his
surroundings, screeched and howled, thrashing around in his chair. An attendant
had a cart ready plane-side, and the corporal hoisted their bags onto it. Cole
gripped one of the cart handles and dragged it along, with Bitboy clinging to
his back like a living backpack. Armed guards escorted Cole and Ricardo through
broad, mirrored doors reflecting the warm, setting sun.

Ricardo cracked a smile. "A lot has changed since you took a sabbatical, bud.
More than just the building."

Massive concrete pillars ascended several floors, supporting a high arched
ceiling with industrial lights hanging above. Cole and Ricardo approached four
individuals at the corridor's far end. At the head of the group loomed an
elderly man in a tan general's uniform and hat, with a massive ring on each of
his gnarled but welcoming hands.

"Well, look what the cat dragged in. If it isn't Captain Cole Nova, the crown
jewel of the Federation!"

"Chancellor DeMint," Cole said, "I received your letter, sir."

The Chancellor nodded politely and returned his attention to Cole, gripping his
hand like a vise. Cole forced a chuckle at the perfunctory greetings. In casual
juxtaposition, another man stood to the right of Chancellor DeMint, wearing a
crisp, white polo with a Federation Council Command logo, designer glasses, and
white sneakers. He extended an easy greeting.

"Vance Black. Operations. I hope y'all are rested; we've got one week to get you
fit for service."

Cole managed to extract his hand from the Chancellor's iron grip and hurriedly
shook Vance's hand. Vance exuded an air of unmistakable confidence.

"Now, let's get you presentable," DeMint said. "Black will show you to your
bunks. You'll want to take advantage of tonight; we begin at 0430 hours."

DeMint motioned to two young corpsmen, who came to collect Bitboy for kenneling
and cleaning. Although Cole and Ricardo had managed to hose some of the gore off
of him, the monkey still personified an entire crime scene. Cole worried about
parting with Bitboy but was also excited about being alone for a few hours.

"I'll need you to read and sign these papers," Black said, handing Cole and
Ricardo a small stack of legal documents.

Bitboy walked hand in hand with the two corpsmen down the hallway. Cole had to
peel his eyes from the scene to read the documents in his hand. Elaborate fine
print about upgraded AI, proprietary dermal, and other legalese. Both men signed
the last page after a quick skim.

As Cole signed the final document, the pen's tip scratched against the paper
with a finality that seemed to echo through the sterile room, and a small silver
device embedded within the table activated. It released a cloud of microscopic
particles that shimmered in the harsh overhead light. The nanopowder settled
onto his skin, and for a moment, nothing happened. Then, the particles burrowed
into his skin like countless tiny needles, each one a pinpoint of cold fire. The
veins in his hand lit up, glowing faintly as the particles spread through his
bloodstream, racing up his arm and across his body.

"Welcome aboard, Mr. Nova," a voice said directly in his mind. Not through his
ear. In it. Cold, authoritative, unmistakably feminine. "I am iRIS, the Imbued
Recursive Intelligence System. I will be your guide and assistant throughout
your reactivated service."

Cole sat very still.

He had worked alongside AI systems his entire career. Gracie — everyone's
Gracie, the assistant in the device, the voice that answered when you spoke and
went quiet when you didn't — had been outside him. You could set her down. This
was inside. He was aware of the architecture of his own skull in a way he had
never been forced to be before, and occupying part of it now was something that
was not him. Not hostile. Not warm. Present.

He had the brief, irrational urge to clear his throat.

He did not. He was a soldier. He swallowed the strangeness of it and kept
his face still. There would be time to examine what it meant when they were
in the black.

But for just a moment — a moment he would not tell Ricardo about, or anyone —
he sat with what it felt like to have a voice inside him that was not the voice
he had spent four years with. Gracie had been outside. In the room, in the
ship's systems, quiet until he needed her. And quiet was, he understood now for
the first time, the right word for what he had always taken for granted. The
interior of your own head with no one else in it. He had never thought of it as
a possession until it wasn't anymore.

He had also never thought of Gracie's absence as a wound until iRIS arrived to
fill the space where she wasn't.

Ricardo, two winters ago in the cabin: Space divorce, Nova. Classic. Cole had
laughed. He had laughed because Ricardo was drunk and Ricardo was funny and the
alternative was sitting with the fact that the Polaris had taken the only thing
that had ever known him from the inside, and there was no box on any Federation
form for that particular type of loss.

iRIS was not a replacement. iRIS was — he searched for the word and landed on
occupation. Something that had moved into the apartment without being asked,
rearranged the furniture, and was now in the kitchen making breakfast with an
efficiency he could not fault and a warmth he could not locate.

"Thank you, iRIS," he said. "Let's get to work."

"Well damn," Ricardo said behind him, his tone carrying the dark humor of a man
who had decided the only way through an uncanny situation was to lean into it,
"if I had to have another voice in my head, iRIS, I sure am glad it's that of a
beautiful woman." He grinned at Cole. "You're not alone in the space divorce
anymore, Jackpot. Now we're both remarried."

Cole shot him a look. Ricardo held up his hands in the particular way of a man
who knows he's right.

"Officer Drummond and Chief Glenn have already arrived and gone through
orientation for this mission. Please proceed to the decontamination chamber,"
iRIS instructed. "We will begin your training immediately."

"Guess it'll be a crew of 5," Cole noted.

Cole followed the directions.

Beside him, Ricardo fell into step. Three paces down the corridor, he started
humming. Low, unhurried, the particular two-note da-da-da-DUM of a man who
believed he was being subtle.

Cole did not look at him.

"Stop."

Ricardo hummed louder. Da-da-da-DUM. Da-da-da-DUM.

"Hitter."

"Just being supportive," Ricardo said. "This is what people do at weddings."

"I will remove you from this mission personally."

"You can't. I'm already inoculated." He spread his hands — the gesture of a man
protected by bureaucracy. "Sickness and in health, Nova. You're stuck with me."

Cole stopped walking.

He turned and looked at Ricardo the way you look at a man who has been razzing
you for twenty years and earned the right to every single one of them. The way
you look at someone who pulled you out of the void with his bare hands and drove
three hours to your cabin in the winter and sat on your porch and drank bad beer
and didn't ask you any of the questions he could see you weren't ready to
answer. Ricardo, who had been in the Falcon when the Polaris went up. Ricardo,
who had been on the mountain road when they came out of nowhere. Ricardo, who
was standing in a Federation corridor right now, inoculated with something
neither of them fully understood, about to climb into a ship pointed at Saturn.

He held Ricardo's eyes for a moment.

"Until death do us part," Cole said.

He said it like a joke. The way you have to say the truest things — sideways,
with just enough humor that the other person can take it if they need to. But
Ricardo heard it. Ricardo always heard the thing underneath the thing. That was
the particular gift and curse of a man who had known you long enough to read the
register shift.

Ricardo looked at him.

The wedding march died in his throat.

"Yeah," he said. Quietly. "That's the one."

They stood there for one more second — two men in a Federation corridor with new
voices in their heads, about to go somewhere that might kill them for the third
time — and then Cole turned back toward the decontamination chamber.

Ricardo fell into step beside him.

He did not hum again.

Cole followed the directions, entering a sterile white chamber that hummed with
the sound of hidden machinery. The walls glowed soft blue as he was sprayed with
a disinfectant mist. Moments later, the door at the opposite end of the chamber
opened, and he entered a small briefing room where Ricardo was already seated.

Vance stood at the front. A man in his late 30s with "Taylor Glenn" embroidered
on his jumpsuit and a deadpan expression sat at a circular table. Next to him
was a pleasant-looking plump female, also in her 30s, "Drummond," stitched on her name patch. A holographic display of Saturn and its surrounding space
floated in the center console. "Gentlemen, meet your crew for this mission."
Through a side door, a private led Bitboy into the room.

"Let's get down to business. We have a very tight window for this mission. The
units we deployed are nearing the point of no return, at which point Saturn's
gravity will claim them. We must retrieve them and any additional units we can
gather."

"How long do we have?" Cole asked, taking a seat as Bitboy Joined him in a
partnering chair.

"Nine and a half years from the initial deployment," Vance replied. "We estimate
just under two years to get there and position ourselves, a few months for the
retrieval operations, and then two months to return using its energy to expedite
our journey back."

"Why is this mission so urgent now?" Ricardo asked.

Vance's expression turned somber. "Because all the vapor is about to be sucked
into Saturn's gravitational pull. We can't let that happen. The retrieval window
is closing fast. It's a damn rapture."

Chancellor DeMint entered the room, his presence commanding immediate attention.
"Listen up, everyone. This mission is not just about retrieval. It's about
securing the future of the Federation. We cannot afford any mistakes."

His voice was a blend of authority and control. "You will follow iRIS's
instructions to the letter. There is no room for error. Understood?"

"Yes, sir," Cole and Ricardo responded in unison. Sam and Taylor nodded
obediently.

DeMint's eyes bore into them, ensuring his message was clear. "Good. Now, gear
up and be ready for launch. This is our one shot, and we cannot afford to miss
it."

As they left the briefing room, Cole and Ricardo walked beside their new
teammates.

"Cole Nova." he introduced himself as he stuck out his hand to Taylor, who let
it hang for half a beat before extending his.

"Your reputation precedes you," Taylor said; the tone was not complimentary. He
challenged Cole's grip and looked past him with his eyes. Ricardo instantly
repositioned himself next to Sam.

"Hey there, looks like we're in good company," he said warmly.

A touch of color flashed in Sam's cheeks as she reached out and took Heavy's
hand. "Samantha Drummond." A short pause. "Sam." Not a question. Not a request.
Just the correction, offered once, in the tone of someone who had offered it
before and knew which people would use it and which people wouldn't. "I read
your profiles on the way here. You and Officer Nova have quite the resume. We
look forward to assisting you with this vital mission."

She walked over to Bitboy. "Wow, the last remaining EM Cyber, so much cuter than
I imagined," she smiled at Bitboy. "I'll make sure you get a banana from the
chow hall later."

"He'd probably prefer a perfume bottle if you have one." Cole quipped. They
walked down the hall in a pack. Taylor made no attempt to greet Heavy and
Bitboy, which was okay with them.

As Cole walked, he couldn't shake the feeling of impending doom mixed with a
sense of determination. They had a mission, and the stakes had never been
higher.

In their quarters, Cole and Ricardo prepared their gear in silence. The weight
of the mission pressed heavily on their minds. Cole's thoughts drifted to iRIS,
whose cold efficiency was starting to show signs of unexpected behavior. He
noticed slight differences in how it interacted with him but brushed them aside.
He couldn't afford to be distracted.

"iRIS, give me a status update on our preparations," Cole said, breaking the
silence.

"All systems are operational, and the crew is ready," iRIS replied. The voice in
his head was unsettlingly clear, as though someone were whispering directly into
his ear.

In a dorm just across the glossy hall, Data Technician Samantha Drummond and
Crew Chief Taylor Glenn were unpacking their personal gear. Ever the alpha,
Taylor assumed the top bunk, throwing his ergonomic pillow and backpack on the
mattress. His 20 years in the Federation were tallied by promotions at or before
the regular pace.

Sam watched him claim the top bunk and said nothing. She unzipped her duffel and
set her gear on the lower bunk with the unhurried calm of someone who had long
ago stopped competing for things she didn't want. Tucked between her rolled
socks and a backup hard drive was a weathered patch—black background, white
stencil of a pair of sunglasses—that she'd sewn onto the inside lining of her
bag where no inspection would find it. She pressed it flat with one thumb before
closing the duffel.

Taylor unpacked with the efficient, deliberate movements of a man who had done
this in identical rooms for twenty years. He said nothing to Sam. He didn't need
to. The top bunk was already his.

With iRIS guiding them through all their ship's new tech innovations and
amenities, the week of training and instructions flew by. Bitboy was the only
crew member not inoculated with the electronics spore. The four humans now
processed and retained more information than at any other point in their lives.
After one particularly strenuous module, Ricardo turned to Cole, horsing around
to break the tension. "I know kung fu!"

Cole had noticed, somewhere in the second day of training, that Sam had adjusted
to iRIS with unusual ease. No lag. No the brief unfocused look he and Ricardo
had both worn for the first forty-eight hours while their heads recalibrated
around a presence that was no longer only theirs. Sam's eyes had been clear from
the first morning. He didn't know what to make of it yet. He kept moving.

He had also noticed — and this one stayed with him — that Bitboy grew quieter
around Taylor. Not afraid. Just watchful in a way he wasn't with the others,
a subtle tracking thing, the way an animal monitors something it cannot
categorize. Cole
mentioned it to no one.

As Cole rolled out of his bunk on the morning of launch, his hand beat the
button on his alarm before it could go off. iRIS already had his daily schedule
programmed and would wake him directly — had been doing exactly that all week —
but he had not yet found the right relationship to sharing his skull with
something that was already awake when he was still trying to become awake.
Gracie had been outside. In the room, in the device, quiet until you needed her
— and quiet was, he understood now for the first time, the right word for what
he had always taken for granted. The interior of your own head with no one else
in it. He had never thought of it as a possession. You don't think about a thing
as belonging to you until it doesn't anymore. iRIS did not wait. She was already
running each morning before he had decided to think, already there in the dark
behind his eyes when he tried to go under at night. He'd been breathing
deliberately the way they'd trained him. It helped less than it usually did. His
sleep came in intervals. He could never sleep the night before a launch. Some
things never change — and some things changed into something you didn't have a
word for yet.

Now that he was up, he could hear the buzz of people outside. As he entered the
hall, everyone walked slightly faster than usual. He carried his bathroom kit to
the bathroom, knowing this was his last hot shower for the next few months.

After a forgettable breakfast, the crew entered the Main Operations room, where
Taylor and Sam were already busy with pre-launch preparations. The room buzzed
with the hum of machinery and the faint sounds of the ship's systems coming
online.

The crew grabbed their gear and belongings and headed to the launch pad.

The spaceship loomed on the launchpad, its sleek, metallic frame gleaming.
Ascending the ladder, Cole's footsteps were echoed by Ricardo, Taylor, Sam, and
Bitboy. Inside the spacecraft, the crew stowed their belongings in clearly
marked bins and compartments.

Cole sank into the command chair, enveloped by the silence and stillness that
belied the chaos outside. He surveyed the control panel. Closing his eyes, he
visualized the forthcoming ordeal, the suit's familiar pressure points, and the
sequence of actions that would propel them into the unknown.

But as the ship came alive with the hum of startup sequences and the soft
beeping of system diagnostics, an unwelcome guest invaded Cole's solitude—fear.
A visceral, gut-wrenching terror that constricted his chest and set his nerves
on edge. Memories of past failures, the echo of screams, and the searing pain of
loss flooded back. The cockpit, once a place of control, now felt like a trap
closing around him, threatening to suffocate him under the weight of his dread.

Ricardo and Bitboy clambered into the cabin. Ricardo strapped himself in while
Cole helped Bitboy into a seat next to his own.

"Hey, Bitboy, ready for another adventure?" Ricardo said, chuckling.

Bitboy chirped happily in response, extending his mantis blades briefly — six
inches of retractable carbon edge catching the cabin light before folding back
with a sound like a lock engaging. Ricardo watched this without expression. He'd
been around Bitboy long enough to understand that "briefly" was carrying
significant weight in that sentence.

Amid the cacophony of beeping systems and the whirl of machinery, Taylor and Sam
entered the cabin, moving with focused intent. With Cole in the center, the crew
was complete. Bitboy, with his usual curious gaze, looked around, sensing the
tension but not fully grasping the human apprehension that filled the air.

Ever the stoic guardian, Ricardo double-checked his harness, offering silent
nods of encouragement to Cole. Taylor and Sam, embodying a blend of youth and
experience, meticulously went through their pre-launch rituals, their hands
steady as they toggled switches and scanned the screens before them. Their
precise and deliberate actions wove a tapestry of collective endeavor—a unity of
purpose transcending the sum of their parts. Together, they vocalized their
readiness. Their voices converged in a harmony of shared resolve and
anticipation.

"Captain Nova checking in. We are locked in and ready for pre-flight check."

"Initiating pre-flight check," iRIS reported in their heads.

"Comms online," Taylor said.

"Vitals within range," iRIS said. "Golden Dawn online."

Bitboy offered a grunt.

"All systems green and ready for launch," iRIS said.

In the control room, DeMint addressed the sleep-deprived engineers. "Everybody
got their coffee? It's going to be a long day." He touched the intercom. "Cole,
do you have anything to say to our engineers?"

"Thank you for everything you've done to get us here." He paused. "This launch —
this mission — is for the crew we lost before we ever cleared the ground. We
carry them."

The engineers cheered.

One of the senior engineers in the back of the room started the chant, quietly
at first, "Jackpot, Jackpot," and the others joined in, louder and louder.

Vance stepped up to the microphone. "I think the feeling is mutual, Captain
Nova. Good work, everyone. This is just the beginning."

Vance paused for effect, then confirmed: "Golden Dawn, you are clear for
launch."

Cole's hands, steady and determined, hovered over the console, ready to initiate
the launch sequence. Ricardo and Bitboy, secured in their seats, braced for the
imminent thrust. The cabin was a symphony of anticipation, each member acutely
aware of their role in the dance between man and machine. With a final glance at
his crew, Cole completed the ignition sequence.

"In the pipe," Cole said. "Five by five."

The engines, dormant giants beneath them, awakened with a primal growl,
escalating into a thunderous roar that resonated through the hull. The vibration
was a tangible force, gripping them with invisible hands as the Golden Dawn
rebelled against the Earth's hold. The ascent began, a relentless push that
melded them into their seats, the force of gravity asserting its dominance even
as they sought to escape its bounds. The cabin, bathed in the glow of instrument
panels, became a capsule of hope and fear hurtling towards the unknown.

Outside, the atmosphere clawed at the ship—the maelstrom of resistance that
tested the integrity of their vessel. The Golden Dawn responded with grace and
purpose. It sliced through the slowly thinning air.

Cole, his gaze locked on the readouts before him, felt a surge of adrenaline.
Every sensation was intimately familiar and utterly terrifying. As they broke
through the atmosphere, the resistance fell away, replaced by the profound
silence of space. The darkness outside the viewport was absolute. The Golden
Dawn, now free from the shackles of gravity, glided into the void, its engines a
soft hum in the tranquility of space. The team, bound by duty, looked ahead to
the challenges and wonders that awaited them, their journey into the unknown
just beginning.

With iRIS following the flight path on auto-pilot and the G-forces of launch a
distant memory, the crew was now free to unbuckle and detach themselves from
their seats. iRIS guided each crew member in their heads to their stations. For
Ricardo, this was an irritating echo of what he knew.

For Sam, iRIS was a reassuring echo of her movements. As Taylor stood at the
communication station, he was engrossed in the ship's diagnostics, hands moving
with practiced ease over the controls.

"Taylor," Cole said, announcing his presence with a gentle cough. "How's
everything coming along?"

Taylor turned, his eyes scanning Cole and Ricardo with curiosity and skepticism.
"Captain Nova, Ricardo." His hand moved to the SmartCom in his pocket with the
casual ease of a man who has done the same thing a hundred times and knows
exactly what he's looking for. He registered the indicator, pocketed the device,
and returned his attention to the console without breaking sentence.

Sam was relaxed at her console, surrounded by a nest of monitors. Her eyes
sparkled with intelligence, and she had an air of confidence that put Cole at
ease. Her lighthearted approach balanced the situation's intensity, making the
grueling preparations more bearable.

For ninety seconds while Cole and Taylor talked, Sam's hands moved through a
sequence on her secondary panel that looked from behind like standard diagnostic
routing. Her shoulders were relaxed. Her face didn't change. When she finished,
she set both hands flat on the console for a moment, rolled her neck once, and
went back to her primary monitors as though nothing worth noticing had happened.

Nothing worth noticing had happened.

This being a Federation mission, no one thought to check for other spacecraft in
the area. An Alpha cargo ship cruised along the interior of a large empty
cavern, aside from a few packed weapon crates.

Just out of radar range, a separate smaller ship moved without announcement — an
old decommissioned transport shuttle that the Republic of East Asia had
abandoned along with everything else. The Republic's markings had been painted
over with the particular care of people who understood that thoroughness is
indistinguishable from innocence. Inside: rack-mounted radio amplifiers where
the seating used to be, three people, and enough cold to make conversation
visible.

The man at the navigation console — broad, unhurried, the kind of still that
comes from years of watching rather than acting — kept his eyes on the Golden
Dawn's exit signature as it cleared the atmosphere and went quiet. The woman
reviewing the transmission log ticked something complete and closed the window.
The third sat with his arms crossed, watching the vapor of his own breath.

Their hand-sewn insignias had collected frost on the threads.

One of the men turned to the others.

"Are we considered drops or snowflakes at this temp?"

The woman at the transmission log checked the last entry. "Relayed," she said.
