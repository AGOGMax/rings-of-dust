As Cole and the crew got used to having iRIS live rent-free in their heads, work went smoother. When the crew was grouped in close proximity, iRIS would sound over the ship's intercom system as though it knew the etiquette of whispering in someone’s ear in public as being rude and appearing dodgy.  

Cole suspected the AI was also affecting the crew’s subconscious minds while they slept because he was finally getting a good night's rest on the ship.

As they worked, Taylor tapped away at the starboard control panel. The tiny screen above it read, “Loading File: 6%.”

 “Dammit, iRIS,” Taylor hollered. “This thing just isn't loading. Why is this computer so slow? Even the clocks are malfunctioning. Did Bitboy mess with my station while I was away?”

Irritated but trying to stay composed, Cole shot back, “Quit blaming the chimp.”

 “Somebody messed something up! Who was it!?” Taylor said, evident frustration ringing in his voice.

“All systems functioning normally,” iRIS stated in a low, slow growl.

“Ok, now something’s definitely wrong,” Taylor said, losing his temper.

Samantha, channeling her inner mother, said, “Calm yourselves.”

 Taylor and Cole checked themselves.

 “It’s not what you say; it’s how you say it,” Taylor said, talking more to himself than anyone else. “I have to watch my tone. This feels like it’s taking forever. We might as well mark the days on the walls with chalk.”

“The system is working as programmed. Humans experience time dilation when nearby Dark Matter reaches the cruising speed we are currently traveling, 10% of light speed.” iRIS growled slowly.

Cole remembered how strange he felt the day he sampled from the harvester. It felt like the crown of his head was floating yet still attached. His usual strong barrier of stoicism was weakened, almost letting his suppressed emotions well up to the surface. It was beautiful and unfamiliar. With the effects accelerating, he didn’t know if he could keep his composure in front of the crew.

 
“You know the only reason you and your buddies are aboard is because of your previous trip to Saturn,” Taylor said. “This command should be mine.”


In fact, Cole and his original crew were used as Guinea pigs to see what would happen if they separated Darkx. They were expendable. Now Alpha Corp is trying to kill Cole, Bitboy, and Heavy Hitter off while Taylor gives the bulk of Darkx to Alpha Corp.


“If you want to be the leader,” Cole said, his voice steady. “Go ahead. You don’t need to be at the top to be in charge.”


Taylor crouched forward. “Have you lost it? Did you hit your head during the launch sequence?”


“That and plenty of other shit,” Cole said. “I’m saying leadership isn’t something you start doing because you have command. If you can’t find a way to lead laterally, you likely don’t have the tools to lead vertically.”


“Wisdom flows easily from on high, eh, Cole?”


“We can revisit this later, but think about it. No one’s stopping you from being in charge here. If you want it, do it.” Cole wanted to keep his cards close but was more reactive with the Dark Matter kicking in.


“That’s oddly motivating,” Taylor said, “but don’t think your mind games will work on me.”


“Oh no, of course not,” Cole said. “Why would I want to help you?”


Cole turned to Sam, who was diligently working on another console. &quot;Sam, how’s everything looking on your end?&quot;

Sam looked up, a smear of grease on her cheek. &quot;We’re looking good.” then gave a thumbs up.

&quot;Great work, Sam,&quot; Cole said. &quot;Keep me updated.&quot;

Cole went to the cargo bay to ensure none of the containers with salt and other seed minerals were leaking. A task he could have easily delegated to iRIS, but he needed a hidden refuge to escape to as his anxiety started creeping up on him. Just as he confirmed the storage bay was clean and containers stacked neatly, he sat with his demons for a beat in the near-silent bay and headed back to the control room.

As he turned the penultimate corner, he could hear Taylor speaking confessionally. His eyes scanned Sam's chair, which was now vacant, and stopped moving out of instinct.

 “Your master is the most long-winded, egotistical maniac I have ever met.”

 “If my maniacal ego sends me to hell, I'll save Cole a seat beside me. Maybe there’s even a hell within hell, like Russian nesting dolls. Why do we have to suffer in his company? This pissing contest isn’t producing any results. I’d work together to see if we can figure out what’s going on and how to fix it.”

Bitboy’s blue LED eyes went in and out of focus, and he cocked his head, searching for audible commands in Taylor’s drivel. 

Taylor felt the heat of irritation rising. 

“You want to know what I really think of your daddy? I think he’s so busy trying to be the hero that he’s afraid to be himself. I think Cole’s a coward. A coward pretending to be wise and strong. I see right through him, and it looks like weakness.”

“You think I'm a chicken?” Cole said without a shred of defensiveness in his voice.

Taylor spun around, startled, but continued with his rant.

“When I see you fronting, acting all courageous, I know it's–you are–fake.”

Cole felt the truth behind the words piercing him, emotions welling up. He sat with them, absorbed them internally, and held his eager tongue.

Taylor softened his tone and lowered his head to meet Cole eye-to-eye. 

“I can only imagine the pressure of being 'Cole Nova,' the most decorated pilot in Federation history. That kind of attention would give anybody a savior complex. How's that working out for you? Five years wallowing in self-pity?”

Cole remained quiet, listening to know himself.

“While you were hiding in your cabin–hiding from the world and your responsibilities, I was training. You didn't earn your place here. So, yeah, I resent the fact that the Federation gave you a spot at all. I’m sick of hearing about “Jackpot,” the Federation's golden boy, the great explorer. Those days are over, but that’s the Cole Nova we need right now. Not a shell-shocked has-been that crawls into a bottle whenever he needs to escape the past.”

Inexplicably, Cole could accept Taylor’s harsh criticism, and he wasn’t finished dishing it out. 

“So, you killed over 200 people. So what? Hell, you think you’re the first person to carry a burden like that? Half of Earth’s population is fighting for energy sources now. But you don’t come out of hiding ‘til your sister’s life is on the line. Priorities, man! You could save billions, but it took your sister being threatened to get you to man up.”

Cole felt like an armadillo on a highway. At the same time, he was shocked by a wave of relief. Taylor was exposing all the monsters and pain he couldn’t face before. It felt like taking a breath of fresh air and feeling warm sunlight on his face after years of being buried alive. A kid half his age was publicly calling him out in excruciating detail. 

“So what is it that you want from me?”

“I want the truth. That's it.”

Cole recognized this pattern. “The scientific method... on a smaller scale.”

“Do your beliefs actually match with the evidence? You appointed yourself judge, jury, and executioner, sentencing yourself to years of torment. Were you really qualified to convict yourself back then from a place of pain? Are you qualified now? Can you finally be completely honest and fair with yourself?” Taylor questioned.

Cole looked away, tears hidden and held back behind his eyes.
