RINGS OF DUST

Chapter Nine

The iRIS effect was cumulative.

By day four it was no longer a voice. It was a presence — something that had
learned the architecture of the crew and had begun occupying it without
announcement. It knew when to speak over the ship's intercom and when to lean in
close. It had calibrated itself to each person individually, which should have
been reassuring and which Cole found, instead, faintly wrong in a way he could
not yet name.

The other thing he could not name was what the Dark Matter was doing to him.

iRIS had explained it, clinically, on day two. Proximity to the Dark Matter
payload at cruising velocity — ten percent of the speed of light — produced
measurable neuroplastic cascades in human neural tissue. The effect was
documented in the mission parameters as a known variable and had not been
flagged for crew review, which told Cole something about how much of the mission
parameters he had not been given. What it meant in practice was that whatever
emotional architecture a person had built over a lifetime of effort was, at
present, running approximately twenty percent less load-bearing than usual. The
walls were thinner. The light got through differently.

Ricardo had put it more plainly in the galley on day three. "Limbic sensitivity.
That's a polite way of saying we feel things more."

"Great," Cole had said. "Crying in space."

"Some of us," Ricardo said, and left it at that.

Sam had said nothing. Sam had adjusted to the effect with what Cole could only
describe as unusual ease. She moved through her shifts with the same precise,
unhurried competence she had shown from the first day, and if the Dark Matter
was doing anything to the floor of her inner world, she did not show it. He had
watched her once or twice, looking for the seams in it, and hadn't found any.
Sam was no stranger to this kind of interior weather. She had simply assessed
the conditions and begun sailing.

Bitboy was the anomaly.

The chimp was fine. His readings were stable, his threat assessment protocols
cycling normally, his appetite for anything ethanol-adjacent unchanged. But he
had developed a new behavior that Cole had noticed and not yet named.
Whenever Taylor entered a room, Bitboy went still. Not the stillness of sleep or
inattention — the particular stillness of a thing that has made a decision to
watch. His dim-blue LED eyes would track Taylor across the compartment and then
stay on him at rest. No vocalization. No arm deployment. Just the flat attention
of something waiting to understand what it was looking at.

Cole understood the feeling.

---

On the bridge, Taylor worked the starboard control panel with the concentrated
frustration of someone who had decided the problem was external. The file
counter on his secondary screen had been sitting at six percent for eleven
minutes.

"Dammit, iRIS." He didn't quite shout it. "Every clock on this panel is off.
These aren't malfunctions, these are—someone has been at my station."

"All systems functioning within normal parameters," iRIS said. The voice was
measured. Unhurried. Cole noticed it did not say *all systems functioning
normally*, which was the standard response string. It said *within normal
parameters*, which was technically identical and subtly different.

"That's not an answer. Bitboy's been at my—"

"Quit blaming the chimp," Cole said, from the port console, without looking up.

Taylor turned. "I didn't say it was definitely—"

"You implied it. You imply it every time something is slow or missing or not to
your liking. The chimp doesn't touch your station. iRIS, confirm."

"Chief Science Officer Taylor's station logs no access events outside authorized
personnel in the past seventy-two hours," iRIS said. There was, Cole noticed, a
half-second latency between question and response that had not been there
yesterday.

Taylor exhaled and turned back to his panel. "The clocks are still slow."

"The perception of time dilation is a documented effect of sustained proximity
to Darker Matter at our current velocity," iRIS said. "Ten percent of light
speed produces measurable neuroplastic response in human subjects. What you are
experiencing as a delay is more likely an alteration in your own perception of
duration."

Taylor stood very still for a moment. Then: "So the clocks are fine and I'm the
problem."

"The clocks are functioning within—"

"I heard you the first time." Taylor tapped at the panel. "I have to watch my
tone," he said, more to himself than anyone. Then, quieter: "Everything feels
like it's taking forever."

Cole said nothing. He kept his eyes on his own readouts and let the silence
settle. He had noticed something else about iRIS in the past forty-eight hours.
With the crew, it mirrored back — warm, informative, occasionally dry. With
Taylor, it was formal in a way that was not exactly cold but had an edge to it.
A precision that was not present elsewhere. He held it next to Bitboy's stillness and did not yet draw a line between
them.

---

He went to the cargo bay in the early afternoon cycle. The cargo containers with
the salt compounds and seed minerals needed a visual check — iRIS could have run
it via sensors, but Cole needed the two minutes of dark and silence and
mechanical purpose. He moved between the containers, reading the seals,
listening to the hum of the ship doing what ships do at speed in the cold middle
of nowhere.

He finished the check and walked back to the bridge at the same pace he'd
arrived.

---

Taylor was waiting for him.

Not physically waiting — he was at his console, doing something that looked like
work. But he turned the moment Cole entered, which meant he had been listening
for the door, which meant he knew Cole had been in the cargo bay. Whether he
knew anything more than that was what Taylor wanted Cole to wonder.

"I owe you an apology," Taylor said.

"No you don't."

"I blamed the chimp. You called it correctly."

Cole sat at his station. "Forget it."

Taylor moved toward the center console, putting himself in Cole's peripheral
vision — close enough to be heard without the room. "You know I've been
reviewing the logs from the Polaris mission. Your mission."

Cole tapped through his readouts.

"Three hundred and sixty-nine crew members," Taylor said. "That's a weight a person
either carries or gets buried by."

"Is there something you need from me?"

"I'm trying to understand what I'm working with. You spent five years in a
mountain cabin." He let the cabin part sit for a moment. "I've read the psych
assessments. Voluntary isolation, high-functioning avoidance. You built a very
convincing case that you were fine."

Cole turned and looked at him directly. "Taylor."

"You appointed yourself judge, jury, and executioner. Sentenced yourself to five
years and served it. And now you're here, and I can't tell if you're back or if
you brought the sentence with you." Taylor's voice was neutral — not kind, not
cruel, simply accurate in the way that a blade is accurate. "That's not a
personal observation. That's a command risk assessment. If you're still in the
cabin, I need to know."

Cole held his gaze for exactly long enough.

"Run your assessments," he said. "Let me know what you find."

He turned back to his station. From the corner of his eye he caught Bitboy, who
was seated against the starboard wall. The chimp's dim-blue eyes were on Taylor
and did not move.

Taylor went back to his console.

---

He found Sam in the forward maintenance alcove forty minutes later. She had a
panel off the ventilation housing and was running diagnostics on the secondary
environmental feed. Her toolkit was open on the deck beside her. There was also
something else on the deck beside her — a matte-gray device, rectangular, small
enough to palm. Its indicator light was blinking at a slow, even interval.

Sam looked up when Cole crouched down at the alcove threshold. Her eyes moved to
the device and then back to him in a way that didn't rush itself.

She picked it up and put it in her breast pocket.

"It's receiving something," she said. "I can't tell you the source yet. When I
can, I will."

Cole looked at where the device had been on the deck. Then he looked at Sam.

"All right," he said.

She nodded and went back to the diagnostic panel. He stayed at the threshold for
a moment.

"The Dark Matter," he said. "You're not having any trouble with it."

"No."

"You know what it is."

"I have some experience with this kind of interior weather." She ran a
calibration sequence. "It's not comfortable. I'm just familiar with it."

Cole leaned against the alcove frame. Outside the small porthole at the far end
of the corridor, the black ran in all directions without a visible edge. "The
ring," he said. "The copper wire. Before launch." He had not said it to
anyone. He was not entirely sure why he was saying it now.

Sam set down the calibration tool. "Ricardo told me," she said. "You gave her
something to hold onto before you left."

"He told you about her."

"Yes." The flatness of it was its own information. She set down the tool and
looked at him steadily. "I'm sorry, Cole."

He didn't answer. He looked out the porthole. The black held its position.

"I can't wait to meet her when we get back," Sam said. "I bet you two are an
adorable couple."

Cole looked at her. The corner of his mouth moved — not quite a smile, but
close enough. "I can't wait to introduce you," he said.

Sam picked up her tool and went back to work.

Cole pushed off the frame and headed back toward the bridge. At the corridor
junction, he stopped.

"Sam."

She looked up.

"When the source comes through," he said, "don't wait."

She held his eyes for a moment. "I won't."

---

Three hours later, Sam found him at the nav console.

She had the device in one hand and a data pad in the other. She set the pad
in front of him without preamble.

"Taylor's iRIS query log," she said. "The system records every input
interaction — requests, directives, behavioral flags. You get his side. Not
iRIS's responses." She paused. "His side is enough."

Cole looked at the pad. The timestamps were mission-accurate.

Day two. First entry.

*Run continuous behavioral assessment on Nova. Flag any change in his
communication patterns, his interaction frequency with crew. If his behavior
toward me shifts in any way, I need to know immediately. Report daily.*

Second entry.

*Road incident pre-departure — I need the contractor confirmed neutralized
before any outside contact was made. Confirm this.*

Third entry.

*Cargo situation. What does he currently know about the bay? I need his
awareness level before we reach Saturn. I can't move until I know what he
knows.*

Cole set the pad down. He looked at the porthole. The dark outside held its
position.

"How long have you had this?" he said.

"Day three I had the behavioral flags. The road query came through this
morning." She held his eyes. "There's more. I can walk you through all of it."

"Show me all of it," he said.

She sat down.

The ship ran on. Bitboy was asleep in the crew chair with two arms tucked
under his head and two arms hanging loose, and the indicator on his chest
panel cycled green, and the hum of the engines was the only thing between
them and the rest of the dark.
