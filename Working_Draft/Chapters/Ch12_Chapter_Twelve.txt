RINGS OF DUST

Chapter Twelve


The reactor bay was the quietest part of the ship at the end of the day cycle. The hum there was lower, more foundational — not a sound so much as a frequency you felt in the soles of your feet. Sam sat against the cold metal wall with her legs stretched out in front of her, watching the space where Ricardo paced.

He had been pacing for about four minutes. He was working something out.

"You ever wonder," Sam said, "what the actual shape of this is. Not what we were told the shape was. The actual thing."

Ricardo stopped. "What do you mean."

"I mean we were handed a mission brief and a set of objectives and a timeline, and those things were assembled by people who had their own objectives and their own timelines, and we are on the inside of something we only partly see." She was not anxious about it. She was observational. "That's not unusual. Most people are on the inside of something they only partly see. I'm just wondering what we're in the middle of, specifically."

Ricardo frowned. The question had lived in the back of his head for a while and he had managed not to look at it directly. "You think we've been lied to."

"I think we've been given a specific portion of the truth. Which is different from a lie. But it produces similar navigational problems." She looked at her hands. "What would you do, if the mission turned out to be something other than what we signed on for?"

Ricardo considered it, genuinely, without deflection. He sat down across from her. "I'd finish it anyway. Because the crew is real. Because what happens when we get that yield to the Federation is real, whatever the story around it is."

Sam nodded slowly. "Yeah."

They sat with that for a moment.

"Cole's not here," Ricardo said.

"No."

"He's been in the forward crew quarters since second shift." Ricardo's jaw tightened. "Something happened today."

"I know," Sam said. She stood up. She patted the pocket where the matte-gray device lived, checked the indicator, and looked at Ricardo with the particular steadiness of a person who is not going to explain everything but is going to say enough. "I need to find him."

---

Cole was in the forward crew quarters standing at the narrow porthole with his back to the room when Sam knocked on the frame.

He didn't turn around. "Come in."

She closed the door behind her. She stood for a moment, calibrating.

"The device picked something up," she said. "About an hour ago. A message from the same routing I can't fully trace yet. But the content was—" She stopped. She started again, differently. "It was about Sharon."

Cole's hands on the porthole frame went very still.

"She was killed four days ago," Sam said. "The message said it happened during the delivery. Before Emily was taken. The message said Emily survived and was released." She was watching his back. She did not soften it and she did not deliver it hard. She just said it, completely, and let it be what it was.

Cole did not move.

The ship hummed. Outside the porthole, the black ran in every direction without a visible edge. The copper-wire ring was in his breast pocket. He had felt it there through his shirt every hour of this mission.

He said nothing for a long time.

Then, very quietly: "How."

"I don't know the specifics. The message was brief."

His hands came off the porthole frame. He turned around, and whatever she had expected his face to look like, it looked like something else — not grief, exactly. Not yet. The grief was there but it was moving through something first. Something harder and quieter.

"Thank you," he said. His voice was steady. She could see that the steadiness was costing him, and she did not remark on it.

She stayed for a moment. She didn't try to fill it.

Then she said, "Take whatever time you need. I'll be in the bay." She put her hand on his arm, briefly — not a consolation, just an acknowledgment. Present. Real.

She left.

Cole stood in the room for a long time. He looked at his hands. He thought about the ring made of copper wire that he had pressed into her hand in the driveway at the cabin and she had closed her fingers around it and said *I will*. He thought about the way she said his full name — *Cole Nova* — not as a joke exactly, but with the weight of someone who believed the name meant something. He thought about sitting on the porch, watching the headlights come up the dirt road, not knowing yet that she was on her way.

He had a list. He had learned, in the weeks after his father died, to keep a list of everything about a person that you did not want to forget. He had started the list for Sharon in his head already, had not known he was doing it until now.

He pressed his fist against the porthole glass. The cold of it came through immediately.

Then he pushed off the wall and went to find Taylor.

---

The bridge was running on minimal light at this hour. Taylor sat at the starboard console with a holoscreen open in front of him, and he had the posture of a man who is working but was also listening for the sound of a specific door. When Cole came through the entryway, Taylor's hands did not stop moving and he did not turn around immediately. He gave it a beat.

"Captain." He said it without looking. The word landed like a test.

Cole moved to the center console and stood. He was not close enough to be threatening. He was close enough to be clear.

"I have a question," Cole said.

Taylor turned his chair. His face was composed — the careful composition of a man who had decided in advance what kind of conversation this was going to be. "Go ahead."

"Did you know she was in danger."

Taylor's gaze held steady. He did not blink. He did not recalibrate. He had already decided what his face was going to do and it was doing that. "We're all in danger. That's the nature of—"

"Sharon. Did you know."

A pause. Taylor clasped his hands in his lap. "What I know," he said, slowly, like he was choosing the weight of each word, "is that this mission has more stakeholders than the crew manifest reflects. And that people who get caught between those stakeholders — people who are carrying information that more than one party wants — those people are at risk. Sharon was carrying information."

"That's not an answer."

"It's the truest thing I can say to you right now." He met Cole's eyes without flinching. "Sharon knew things. About the delivery, about the crystals, about the people she was working with. People who know too much become liabilities in a war. That isn't a Collective truth or an Alpha Corp truth. It's just what happens."

Cole watched his face. He was watching for what had already happened — for what Taylor had known before Cole walked in here, for the shape of the gap between what Taylor said and what Taylor knew. He did not find it. Taylor was too careful, had too much practice, had already made the calculation that ambiguity served him better than a position.

"I'm going to find out exactly what happened," Cole said. "I want you to know that."

Taylor nodded. "I would expect nothing less." He said it without irony, and that was the most unsettling version of it.

Cole turned to the communications panel. He reached for the relay controls — not because he expected them to work, but because he wanted to know. The screen blinked and then went dark in the particular way of something that had been configured not to cooperate.

He stood there for a moment. He looked at the dark screen.

"iRIS," he said.

"Working," iRIS responded. The reply came back a fraction of a second slower than usual.

"Open a relay channel to Federation HQ."

"Communications are experiencing an alignment issue at the current approach vector. Signal clarity is insufficient for—"

"Log the attempt."

"Logged."

Cole looked at Taylor. Taylor looked at Cole. Neither of them said what both of them were thinking.

Cole walked out.

---

Eleven hundred miles south of Federation airspace, Emily Nova was in the back of a taxi with her head against the window and her eyes closed. The cab smelled like pine air freshener and old coffee. She had been awake for thirty-one hours and the road under the tires was a rhythm she could almost follow but not quite, and the almost-following of it kept dragging her toward the edge.

She jerked awake when her head slid on the glass.

The city lights were behind them. She could see the highway and the dark on either side and the yellow dividing lines running past in a steady pulse. She had seen this before — the yellow lines and the dark — and she pressed her palm against the window to wake herself up further.

In her other hand she was holding the number Rachel had given her in the parking garage. A handwritten sequence on the back of a Verguld Media parking receipt — not Rachel's number. Rachel had been very clear about that. *Someone will answer. Tell them where you are and where you're going.*

She dialed it.

It rang twice.

"Go ahead." A woman's voice. Not Rachel's. Measured, unhurried, with the particular quality of someone accustomed to receiving information at odd hours.

"This is Emily Nova," Emily said. Her voice came out rougher than she expected. "I'm in a taxi. I'm going to Texas. To Federation headquarters."

A brief pause. "We know who you are. Are you clear of Verguld?"

"Yes."

"All right." Another pause — not uncertainty, the pause of someone consulting something. "When you arrive, don't go to the main entrance. There's a secondary receiving entrance on the building's east face, flagged on Federation maps as maintenance access. Someone will be there."

"Who?"

"Someone who knows what you're carrying." The voice shifted slightly — not warmer exactly, but more direct. "Are you all right, Ms. Nova?"

Emily looked out the window. The yellow lines kept coming. The dark kept holding its position on either side. She thought about what she had said to Marcus in his office — the thing she had said without thinking, the thing she had known the moment it left her mouth was a mistake. *Cole's heart is gonna break when he hears about Sharon.*

"I don't know yet," she said.

The line closed.

Emily leaned her head back against the seat. She watched the highway. She kept her eyes open this time, watching the yellow lines come and go in the dark, and did not let herself drift.
