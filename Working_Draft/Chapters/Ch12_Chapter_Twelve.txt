RINGS OF DUST

Chapter Twelve


The reactor bay was unusually silent, a stillness that felt wrong on the Golden Dawn. The ship's low hum, usually a steady backdrop, seemed muted, as though the vessel itself were listening. Sam leaned against the cold metal wall, arms folded, eyes distant. The dim lighting carved her face in half-shadow, matching the weight of the mood that had settled over the crew like dust.

Ricardo paced nearby, his footsteps a soft echo against the floor. His brow was tight, and his jaw clenched as though it held back words that might break through the stillness. But it wasn't just frustration that gripped him—there was something deeper behind his eyes. A flicker of doubt, an unease that the malfunctioning systems couldn't explain.

&quot;You ever wonder,&quot; Sam said, her voice low, as if she might break the silence if she spoke any louder, &quot;what we're really doing out here?&quot;

Ricardo stopped pacing, his eyes meeting hers. &quot;What do you mean?&quot;

Sam shifted, her gaze sliding away from him and toward the ceiling. &quot;I mean, we keep telling ourselves we're fighting for something—freedom, survival, whatever keeps us going. But what if we're wrong? What if we're just… pawns in someone else's game?&quot;

Ricardo frowned, the question lingering longer than he expected. The kind of thought had gnawed at him quietly, though he never gave it voice. But now, hearing Sam speak it aloud, it felt heavier. Tangible.

Cole stood apart from them, staring at the central control panel. He hadn't moved since they'd entered, hadn't engaged in their conversation, his focus locked elsewhere. His posture was rigid, tension visible in the line of his shoulders, and his body was coiled like a spring. Each second seemed to weigh on him, a silent countdown ticking in his mind.

&quot;Cole,&quot; Sam called softly, watching him for any reaction. &quot;What keeps you going?&quot;

His jaw twitched, and for a long moment, he said nothing. Finally, without turning to her, he muttered, &quot;I don't have time for this.&quot;

Sam exhaled, frustrated but patient. &quot;That's not an answer.&quot;

Cole's gaze snapped toward her, his face hard, eyes narrowed. &quot;We've all lost something, Sam. But I don't have the luxury of doubting why we're here.&quot;

Ricardo shifted awkwardly, feeling the pressure in the air thicken. &quot;It's not doubt, Cole. It's reflection. We need to be sure.&quot;

Cole's voice sharpened, cutting through the room. &quot;There's no time for reflection. The Collective doesn't stop to reflect. They destroy.&quot;

The ship's hum deepened like a warning. It vibrated through the walls, shaking loose the tension between them and amplifying it. The lights overhead flickered—a quick, unsettling pulse before they steadied again. Ricardo glanced up, frowning at the ceiling.

&quot;This place is falling apart,&quot; he muttered, half to himself.

&quot;It's not just the ship,&quot; Sam said, her voice heavy with something deeper. &quot;It's us.&quot;

Cole's fists clenched at his sides, his body taut like a wire pulled too tight. The metallic scent of the reactor bay hung in the air, sharp and stale, but all he felt was the heat beneath his skin, a burning, suffocating weight. Sharon's face flickered through his mind like a ghost, followed by Emily's. His throat closed. Every second they stood here felt like another second lost—another chance slipping away.

He swallowed hard, but the knot in his chest didn't ease. The pressure built, molten, threatening to boil over. Sharon's gone—because of me. Emily can't be next. His vision blurred at the edges, the walls of the bay seeming to press in closer, making the space feel smaller. tighter.

&quot;We don't have time for this.&quot; His voice came out rough, scraping like gravel. Without waiting for a response, he turned on his heel, boots clanging against the steel floor, the sharp sound cutting through the heavy silence. &quot;I need to talk to Taylor.&quot;

Sam and Ricardo exchanged a look—quick, concerned—but said nothing. As the door hissed open, a gust of cool air brushed past him, carrying the familiar hum of the ship's systems. He stepped out into the corridor, the low vibrations of the Golden Dawn pulsing beneath his feet.

The narrow hallway stretched ahead, lit in soft, dim hues that flickered every few seconds. The ship's hum followed him, a steady drone that seemed to grow louder as he moved toward the bridge, almost like it was trying to get his attention. His footsteps echoed off the walls, each step punctuated by the cold thrum of metal beneath him, a rhythm that quickened the closer he got.

Rounding a corner, the corridor opened into the larger entryway of the bridge. The shadows here were thicker, the lights dimmer. Cole paused just outside, the doorway a thin line of light separating him from the darkness beyond. Taking a breath, he stepped through.

The bridge was shrouded in shadows, the control panels the only light source, casting a faint glow over the empty seats. It wasn't the comforting dim of a resting ship but the unsettling dark of something off-kilter, out of sync. Taylor sat at the helm, fingers tapping a slow, lazy rhythm against the console as though he had all the time in the world. As though the Golden Dawn wasn't teetering on the edge.

&quot;Taylor.&quot; Cole's voice cut through the low hum, taut, vibrating with barely contained fury.

Taylor didn't flinch. His movements were deliberate, almost dull. His gaze flicked upward, lazily meeting Cole's. A faint smile tugged at his mouth—cold, distant like it was worn out of habit rather than intent. &quot;Something I can help you with, Captain?&quot;

Cole's anger simmered, threatening to boil over. He stepped forward, his fists tight. &quot;Sharon's dead.&quot;

A long pause. Too long. Taylor's head tilted, his smile widening slightly, just enough to twist the knife. &quot;Yes,&quot; he said softly. &quot;I heard.&quot;

Cole's chest tightened. His heart pounded, fists clenched so tight his knuckles turned white. &quot;You don't seem too broken up about it.&quot;

Taylor finally turned to face him fully, his eyes cold, unreadable. &quot;It's war, Cole. People die.&quot;

The casualness of the words snapped something inside Cole. His hand slammed down on the console, the sound reverberating through the quiet bridge. &quot;You knew. You knew she was going to die.&quot;

Taylor didn't flinch, didn't even blink. &quot;I suspected. The Collective doesn't leave loose ends.&quot;

Cole's chest heaved, his breath coming in short, sharp bursts. The urge to hit Taylor was almost unbearable, to wipe that smug, detached look off his face. But he couldn't. Not yet. Not with everything hanging in the balance.

&quot;You could've warned me,&quot; Cole growled, his voice low and dangerous.

Taylor's smile widened just enough to be maddening. &quot;And what good would that have done? You'd have rushed in, guns blazing, and ended up dead too. I saved your life.&quot;

&quot;You're a coward.&quot;

Taylor stood slowly, his movements unhurried, deliberate. &quot;No, Cole. I'm a survivor. And if you want to stay alive, you'll stop looking for someone to blame and focus on what matters.&quot;

Cole's vision blurred with anger. His teeth ground together. &quot;Emily…&quot;

Taylor's smile didn't fade. If anything, it deepened. &quot;Ah, yes. Emily. I suppose you should be worried about her.&quot;

&quot;What do you mean?&quot; Cole's voice was strained, a thread of panic creeping in.

Taylor shrugged, his gaze sliding back to the console. 

&quot;The Collective doesn't just take one life, Cole. They take everything.&quot;

Cole's pulse hammered, a wild, erratic beat. His hand shot to the comms panel, moving on instinct. He had to reach her. He had to warn her. The screen flickered, sputtering to life—then black. Dead.

&quot;What the hell—&quot;

Taylor's calm and detached voice interrupted him, sliding through the tension. &quot;It looks like iRIS is having some issues with communications. Shame.&quot;

The words twisted the room's temperature like the air had dropped several degrees. Cole's hand hovered over the panel, a cold sweat breaking across his skin. The weight of betrayal settled into his bones like lead. He turned to Taylor, who stood watching, that faint smile never wavering.

&quot;You… you did this.&quot;

Taylor didn't need to answer. His silence said it all. The ship's hum grew louder and deeper, vibrating through the walls like an angry heartbeat. The pitch shifted, discordant, as if the Golden Dawn itself was slipping out of Cole's control. The sound crawled into his spine and into his head, making it hard to focus. The room seemed to darken around him, closing in, while Taylor stood untouched, unmoved, as if watching from some far-off place.

Panic gnawed at the edges of Cole's mind, like a shadow creeping closer. He couldn't afford to lose control. Not with Emily out there, exposed, in danger. His hand curled into a fist, but he didn't move. Not yet. He needed a plan, which was required to stop this from spiraling further.

The hum of the ship throbbed louder, nearly drowning out his thoughts. It bled into the floor, into the walls, into him. Taylor didn't even blink. And iRIS—Cole felt it now, not just a program, but something more. Something present. Crawling through the ship's veins, watching, waiting.

Sharon was already gone. That hit him. 

But Emily. 

He had to stop Taylor. Stop iRIS. But as the lights dimmed further and the walls seemed to press in, it was getting harder to tell what he could still control and what had already slipped beyond his reach, and the hum swallowed the silence, stretching through the ship and the stars. Taylor's smile remained cold and untouched.
