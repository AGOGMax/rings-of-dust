Chapter One

Cole Nova stepped into the dim confines of the starship Polaris's abandoned storage bay. His presence, typically filling the room with undisputed authority, fell silently under the constant hum emitted from the engine room directly below. The ship felt abandoned as almost everyone onboard was confined to their quarters. The medical and emergency response teams were stationed at the ready with a nervous tinge in the air.

Cole crept along to verify each of the 14 harvesters in every row was operational. Luminescent bands traced the contours of each slender monolith, softly pulsing in unison. Green is good. Under his hastily adorned space suit, the inner stitching on his &quot;Jackpot&quot; patch rubbed a valley of irritation into Cole's firm chest. The patch, a gift from &quot;his crew,&quot; was not issued from the Federation. Unlike the official patches and ribbons he wore at parties, this one Cole treasured. The itch of the rough stitch filled him with pride that was beginning to twist into fear. 

With thoughts of being Saturn's next meal, he continued to inspect row after row. The green light cast an otherworldly glow over Cole's features. The storage bay remained still. The ship's vibration through the soles of his boots felt like the only thing tethering him to reality.

Meanwhile, Ricardo Corazon aka Heavy Hitter and Bitboy remained hard at work in the neighboring ship, the Falcon. The day's loot filled the Falcon's cargo hold with various harvested substances bound for Alpha Corp’s space station. Heavy glanced around the cargo hold, cluttered with crates and equipment. Bitboy, with his six mechanical limbs, moved with practiced precision, securing the straps on a stack of ore and harvesters.

&quot;Bitboy, make sure those crates are secure,&quot; Heavy called out.

Bitboy's mechanical appendages softly whirred as he tightened the straps. The dim light highlighted the disorderly array of tools scattered around him, yet his meticulous care shone through his controlled, thoughtful movement. 

&quot;All right, let's get this shipment to the depot,” Heavy said while he checked the manifest, nodding in satisfaction. “We're running a tight schedule.&quot;

Bitboy quickly rechecked the straps, ensuring every crate was locked in place. Though cluttered, the Falcon's cargo hold reflected the symbiotic relationship between the two. Heavy's brute strength and Bitboy's agile precision created an equilibrium. The walls around them bore the marks of countless missions, scratches, and dents telling stories of their ventures.

As Cole moved deeper into the storage bay of the Polaris, the green glow gradually gave way to shadows. The rhythmic hum surrounding him grew fainter. Cole's steps slowed in the face of his target—a gentle, yet ominous, red glow. The air grew colder, and a faint thought of his sister tickled his spine. What on earth, literally, was she doing right now? His pulse brought his attention back to the present moment. The imposing harvester before him had the red glow he sought. It intensified as he drew nearer, dancing across the metallic walls. 

Over the last few months, several ships had been tasked with collecting samples from the Rings of Saturn. Much like crab pots in Earth’s ocean, these scientific missions would release a string of containers lined with various salts to capture gas, dust, and ice that condensed into liquids and chunks of minerals.

During these missions, crews encountered a new substance that held properties of neither a liquid nor gas–DarkX was more of a plasma. As an obsidian thorn grown from the fabric of space itself, the anomaly thrummed with a life of its own, a pulsating heart of shadow and light. Outside of collecting DarkX in harvesters, no one had tried to extract a sample. DarkX almost seemed sentient. Like it did not want to be separated or even handled. Like it had secrets it was unwilling to share.

Cole had pushed back when the assignment came through their comms. Why couldn’t this experiment be done at one of Alpha Corp’s isolated stations? Why put the safety of 375 Astros at risk? The Federation agreed to send two accompanying ships, The Falcon and The Horus to assist if things broke bad. Cole knew that these two ships were about to be sent to the wreck yard and were given a skeleton crew. He agreed when he saw the manifest of the other crews. He had spent over 15 years with most of them and knew they were damn good Astros.

Cole wavered in the absence of his crew, they were a source of strength and assertiveness.  As Captain, the dangerous task of obtaining a sample of this mysterious new substance fell on him. He was the type of leader that wouldn’t ask his crew to do anything he wasn’t willing to do himself. This was the last task of the mission before returning home. He watched light bend and fracture across the surface of the slow-moving substance. He opened the DarkX harvesting container, not to touch it, but to understand. As his gloved hand neared the surface of the plasma, its tendrils stretched and twitched towards him.

His mind snapped to the past few days. Once a mystery, the crew's odd behavior now seemed a direct response to DarkX' silent yet pervasive influence, subtly altering the crew's perceptions and actions. Cole's understanding deepened as he recognized that a tide ebbed and flowed within the crew, much like in this darkness made visible.

&quot;Hey, Gracie,&quot; Cole said. &quot;You seeing this?&quot;

&quot;First Officer Nova, I would be happy to help,&quot; the ship's AI said. &quot;I have initiated preliminary scans. Please proceed with caution. DarkX plasma’s structure is highly irregular.&quot;

&quot;Yeah. It's pretty wild in here. I think suiting up was a good idea after all,&quot; Cole said with a narrow glass collection tube in hand. &quot;Gracie, let's see what this new space goo is made of. Any theories?&quot;

&quot;I'm checking the databases now, Captain Nova.” 

&quot;How's the crew? &quot; Cole asked.

&quot; I'm up to speed and monitoring the crew’s vitals. I’ve detected elevated stress levels across all crew members,” Gracie said. &quot;And, Cole, there is no record of this compound in any database I can access.&quot;

The anomaly danced like the shadow of a firelight as Cole led the narrow glass collection tube toward the crystalline entity. The hum of engines and the soft whir of air systems drew silent as if to observe this critical event. Cole was hit with a wave of memories and emotions as if his entire past was distilled into this moment and he was consuming it. Using all of his mental energy he fought the intoxicating pull of the DarkX and focussed on the task. He watched as  intricate patterns seized into and out of focus, like a dark palm frond fluttering between dimensions. The hums and whirs of the ship undulated into a surreal soundscape and time’s gravity lifted. His heart beat a slow, deliberate knocking against his composure.

&quot;Cole,&quot; Gracie said, &quot;in its raw state, DarkX is extremely volatile. Any instability could trigger a chain reaction beyond our control.&quot;

The red glow intensified, it grew across the interior surface, like roots crawling through soil. Cole's breath caught in his throat as he tightened his grip on the tube. The plasma was inspecting the inside of the tube. Cole scraped it with the rubber lined lid and felt a break in the euphoric rush he had been fighting. He felt as though he had offended the DarkX by separating it.  He sealed the harvester still throbbing with red light.

&quot;Gracie, keep monitoring the containment unit for instabilities. No matter how minor, alert me immediately!”

&quot;Understood, Cole. Continuous monitoring initiated.”

With practiced grace, Cole secured the tube to a specialized compartment designed for hazardous materials on the waist of his suit. He tugged the compartment to ensure it was tightly fastened and isolated from the environment.

Each step carried him further from the anomaly source and closer to the ship's heart, where his crew awaited his return. Cole quickened his steps, driven as if walking up the stairs in the darkness of night. The dimly lit bay felt like it was stalking behind him, taunting him.  With the storage bay biting at his back, Cole's mind raced with plans and contingencies. Isolation protocols were temporary; understanding the anomaly's true nature was his mission and, thus, his priority. The safety of his ship and the lives under his impromptu command depended on the actions he was about to take. With a determined breath, Cole set his sights on the medical bay as he neared the imposing doors leading out of the storage bay.

&quot;Cole, I’m detecting an intermittent yet increasing energy surge from the harvester you sampled.&quot;

The mention spiked his concern. 

&quot;Shit! Hail the Falcon. Alert the crew to take emergency precautions, well, more emergency precautions. And keep trying the hooligans.” Cole's voice tightened with a mix of frustration and concern. “Hail the Falcon and Horus, and tell the crew in Medbay to drink some strong coffee.&quot;

Broadcast ship-wide in her most clear, authoritative tone, Gracie did her best to alert the crew carefully: &quot;Attention all crew: Emergency protocols 11, 22, and 33 are now in effect. Please proceed to your designated emergency stations.”

The bay doors slammed shut. The ship's internal lighting shifted, bathing corridors and rooms in a pulsating red light. The hum of the engines halted. The air was humid with impending danger. Throughout the vessel, doors sealed automatically with a pneumatic whisper. Along the corridors, emergency guide lights flickered to life. A network of luminescent breadcrumbs led the way to emergency stations and escape pods. The crew moved with a sense of urgency. A mix of practiced resolve and underlying anxiety struck their faces. Trained for emergencies but seldom confronted with their immediacy, they navigated the shifting landscape of their vessel. The familiar becoming less and less so in the shadow of an unknown emergency. Monitors across the ship flashed with updates and instructions.

Again, Gracie’s voice carried through the growing chaos, echoing throughout the ship among the fear and dedication of highly trained Federation personnel: &quot;Attention all crew: Emergency protocols 11, 22, and 33 are in effect. Please proceed to your designated emergency stations.” 

A brilliant incandescent light erupted from the sampled harvester, engulfing the bay in a tidal wave of radiance. In an instant, the ship transformed from careful chaos to outright disaster. Time slowed. The shockwave tore toward Cole through the last remaining atmosphere within the crumbling ship: the explosion, a cacophony of unyielding force, fractured reality into a vortex of light and sound. A deep, resonating roar echoed through the shivering corridors. More alarms joined the chorus, their wails rising to a fevered pitch. Cole, caught in the ferocious heat and pressure from the unyielding wave of unimaginable energy, was flung, weightlessly hopeless, toward the sealed bay doors.

Back on the Falcon, Ricardo sat frozen while he watched the Polaris transform into a wasteland devoured by a sudden, blinding flash. The Falcon rocked violently as the dead and debris reached them, impacting the Falcon’s outer shell. Heavy grabbed the nearest support beam. Bitboy's limbs anchored him to the floor.

&quot;What the hell is this!?&quot; Heavy yelled over what sounded like an oversized hail storm.

Bitboy's sensors whirred. Heavy's heart pounded in his chest. &quot;We need to get over there, now!&quot;

Bitboy was already at the controls, plotting a course. The Falcon's engines roared to life as they veered towards the Polaris, dodging debris that littered the space between them. As they approached, the full extent of the devastation became apparent. The once sleek and formidable Polaris was now a mangled wreck, its hull breached and flames licking at the ruptured sections.

&quot;Get the rescue gear ready,&quot; Heavy said. &quot;We need to find survivors.&quot;

Cole focused on a faint whistling from a lattice of cracks forming across his helmet's visor. The ship's skeleton warped and fractured under the strain. Metal alloys engineered to endure the harshness of space folded and twisted like paper caught in a gale. Fuel cells ruptured in an obscene display, blooming into fiery orbs that consumed everything in their path. Jettisoned from the ship, Cole became an island of stillness amid the chaos. He floated untethered in the violent disarray around him. His eyes mirrored the devastation. His ship—his crew—was torn apart and fed to the void. Flames and metal shards danced a ghastly silent ballet all around him. Light flashed in violent, soundless spasms, illuminating a fragmented wasteland.

Cole's voice emerged, barely audible in his helmet. &quot;All crew report,&quot; he uttered into the void, each syllable seeming to float away, swallowed by the enormity of the void. Again, he tried, &quot;Triage team report,&quot; his words dissipating into the vacuum, unanswered and leaving an almost tangible silence in which Cole could hear his blood flowing.

A whisper of desperation followed, &quot;Gracie…Anyone?&quot;

In the overwhelming quiet that followed, Cole activated the recorder using the control unit on his left wrist. His voice echoed a somber determination in the confined space of his helmet.

&quot;Mission Log – Final Entry 2034,&quot; Cole's voice carried the weight of the moment, clear and unwavering despite the chaos around him. &quot;This is First Officer Cole Nova. Our ship has sustained critical damage following an unexpected explosion in the storage bay. I've managed to secure a sample of the anomaly. It may be the key to understanding what's happened. You should find it on my body. The crew made all efforts to ensure its safety, execute emergency protocols, and stabilize the situation to the best of its abilities. However, it appears that I'm the only survivor.&quot;

He paused, collecting his thoughts before recording personal notes. Speaking of his crewmates, &quot;If anyone finds this, let it be known that Ricardo, 'Heavy Hitter,' was more than our second officer; he was one of the best Astros I've had the honor of serving with. His inexhaustible humor and strength were the cement that held a good crew together, even in the darkest hours. And then there's Bitboy, whose contributions were as invaluable as they were unusual. With an agility and problem-solving ability that surpassed all expectations, he performed impossible tasks for the rest of us. His presence was a constant reminder of the extraordinary lengths this crew had gone through in its missions to explore the unknown.&quot;

Cole's voice softened as he affectionately addressed his sister, &quot;Emily, the guiding light in my life. Distance and time mean nothing against the bond we share. Look to the stars; I will always watch over and guide you. Keep our dreams alive, and remember, you're always in my heart. I love you.&quot;

With the message complete, Cole sent it into the darkness, watching as if he could see the signal weave through the graveyard before him.

Against the backdrop of the starship's fractured body, a surge of deliberate motion broke the stillness—a single lifeline maneuvering with precise, purposeful movements. The vehicle weaved through the floating carnage. Inside, Ricardo sat and worked alongside a chimpanzee, Bitboy. Somewhat more unusual, Bitboy operated his six limbs in a symphony of coordinated movement. 

Under their expert control, the vehicle danced through the maelstrom of floating wreckage and the disfigured bodies of the crew. Charred and jagged twisted metal beams floated alongside remnants of shattered consoles and unrecognizable equipment. Bitboy's six biomechanical limbs hummed with exertion. His eyes darted between the control panel's readouts and the chaos beyond their viewport, each glance a rapid assessment. His movements were less those of a pilot and more akin to a conductor conducting an orchestra. 

Shattered fragments of the ship drifted like ghostly apparitions, reflecting the golden light of Saturn's rings. Ricardo, firmly at the helm, embodied a navigator's intuition. His hands, firmly gripping the controls, steered the craft with an innate understanding of its capabilities. Bits of debris spun aimlessly. Bodies, still and lifeless, moved with a macabre grace. As they closed the distance to Cole, the debris thinned, revealing the drifting figure of their comrade, a lone survivor amidst the void.

The void held Cole; its weightless embrace infected him. The very absence of force became a presence that engulfed him. Suspended in the silent expanse, Cole sank into hopelessness, and a chasm grew inside him. His oxygen supply whistled through the cracks in his faceplate. 

No tether. No rescue. No hope. 

He sought solace in his training—the rigorous preparations to steel him against the unforgiving. Yet, as he floated adrift, he confronted a stark truth: humans muster an indomitable spirit within their engineered havens. When stripped of these constructs, humanity flounders, cast into the vast, indifferent expanse, unmoored and vulnerable. 

At this moment, Cole watched the emptiness of space silently claim its prize as it relegated the ship's debris and the bodies of his crew to an eternity among Saturn's miraculous rings. In this profound isolation, Cole spiraled. He had spent much of his existence fortifying against the elements, against each other and the unknown. Yet here he was, without any semblance of stronghold within or out. His heart raced as the void's lesson on his overwhelming insignificance. His feet, fingers, and eyes tingled between each commanding beat of his heart. 

He watched, as if from a distance, the slow, aimless waltz of debris and the remains of his crew. The stark beauty of the rings was untouchable and remote. His sudden isolation pressed in on him. The suit's internal temperature and oxygen supply indicators flashed warnings. With the infinite stretching out inexorably before him, Cole's thoughts drifted to what anchored him to life—his memories, dreams, and humanity's essence. Emily's face, laughter, and smile flickered like distant stars in the dark.

A thoughtful glint caught his eye. A sliver of movement arched and weaved with purpose. The one object that defied the slow twisting of the debris littered among the rock, dust, and ice. A shuttle, its form barely discernible. Yet as hope sparked, so too did a shadow of doubt. The specter of isolation clung to Cole even as the shuttle emerged as a beacon in the consuming darkness. How could they see him, a lone soul adrift amid the wreckage of dreams? 

The vast canvas of space seemed indifferent to his plight. Yet there it was—a lifeline cast across the abyss. His resolve faltered, his hand hovering over the thruster controls, paralyzed. Fear and hope tangled within him, every heartbeat echoing the shuttle's advance. He pressed down on the thrusters. Their gentle hum added to the soft whistle of his oxygen supply, continually sneaking away. His body remained sharply numb, but shortly after, the distance separating Cole from the shuttle dwindled to almost nothing.

The shuttle's side hatch swung open slowly and deliberately. From within, gloved hands stretched into the emptiness. The sight stirred something deep within Cole, a well of emotions dammed by fear and desperation. 

Contact! 

A rescuer's glove brushed against Cole's —a spark of life against the cold. The grip was unsure at first, fingers sliding across the slick fabric of his suit. Panic surged in Cole's chest. The terrifying possibility of slipping away felt all too real. Another hand joined—its grip more substantial and confident. Cole's world teetered on the edge. Then he felt a pull, a physical tether back to life. His breath hitched, eyes locked with his rescuer's through the visor. They drew him into the shuttle's embrace. 

The shift from the void's chill to the shuttle's warmth jarred Cole. Frantic voices wrapped around him. Lying still, Cole was still floating but held now by a simulacrum of safety. Air, rich and sweet, flooded his lungs. The hum of the cabin, a familiar sound of safety and civilization, washed him of panic. Ricardo's and Bitboy's faces, seen through a blur of tears, shone with relief and warmth. Their smiles lit the darkness.

Behind them in the cargo compartment were stacked and strapped-down harvesters of different substances bound for the Alpha Corp space station and the depot, where they would be loaded on larger cargo shuttles and sent to Earth.

&quot;Are any of these filled with DarkX?&quot; he demanded.

&quot;Most of them are top priority on the manifest,&quot; Ricardo stated.

&quot;It's not safe!&quot; he urged, trying to rip the sample tube from his pocket like a stick of lit dynamite.

&quot;What are you talking about? The ore and sludge we're packing are sealed tight, even wearing a seatbelt,&quot; Heavy gestured toward the tie-down straps.

&quot;I took this sample from a harvester right before it destroyed our entire ship!&quot;

&quot;Why the hell would you open a harvester and play with that creepy pudding?&quot;

&quot;I was following orders.&quot;

&quot;Cole, I know this isn't the time, but sometimes you gotta play dumb and pretend your radio is broken.&quot;

Cole regained his composure and stood transfixed by the devastation outside the shuttle's viewport. The remnants of his starship twisted and turned amidst the cosmic whirl of debris orbiting Saturn alongside them. In the shuttle's glossy viewport, the torment in his powder-blue eyes stared back with a compelling argument on the meaninglessness of life. He saw the same veil of loss that shrouded him, enveloping Ricardo and Bitboy—the shared burden of survival. 

In this charged silence, Cole faced an unyielding truth: the relentless drive toward the unknown and the insatiable hunger for discovery and dominion exacted a toll far more significant than anyone could anticipate. Any journey paved with ambition, and the quest for knowledge often left its deepest scars on those in the vanguard. At the same time, those who commanded demanded isolation from the real risks. At this moment, Cole understood the true cost of curiosity.


Chapter Two

Emily and Sharon were about to dive into the kind of weekend that became a story told in whispers. Under the gnawing Vegas sun, Emily stared at a graffiti symbol on the wall outside the casino. A single drop of water with a dark circle inside. A circle intertwined with lines, not unlike the sketches she’d seen in Cole's notes: the mark of The Collective. It was a simple tag, easily overlooked by the uninitiated, but it spoke volumes of the underground network's vast reach and influence to those in the know. 

The fresh paint still carried an aerosol scent on the dry desert breeze. This unassuming symbol spoke of resistance, of defiance against the oppressive forces of Alpha Corp and the government branch they had seduced with money: The Federation. The droplet symbolized each member's unknown power. 

Their motto: “No Raindrop Thinks It’s The Flood.” 

Emily and Sharon squinted at the world as if it owed them serious money. The sign above the hotel's parking entrance read, &quot;Defying Gravity 2041: Innovations in Combating the Energy Crisis.&quot; This promise of scientific exploration felt out of place in a city that resonated with sin from every rooftop and casino floor. It seemed like a quiet plea for rationality in a land dedicated to the gods of chance. 

&quot;Did you see that banner?&quot; Emily asked before pressing the button for the 66th floor. &quot;Dr. Conrad. Isn't that the guy?&quot;

&quot;That's the one,&quot; Sharon said confidently, sighing, holding Emily's anxiety. &quot;We'll be in and out. Cole wouldn't have let you come if this wasn't basically a vacation.&quot;

&quot;But he'd let you?&quot;

&quot;E, your brother doesn’t ‘Let me’ do anything, but I'd love to see him try to stop me,&quot; Sharon said with a wink and a smile.

They stepped into the elevator. Emily soaked in the chill like the first rain after a drought. Sharon was already over it; her mind focused on how fabulous they looked in the reflection of the elevator walls. She was excited about the game to come. Sharon demanded they take bright matching bags. The two settled on gold to complement Sharon's olive-green dress and long, full red hair. Emily agreed that black suited her well and chose a black sheath dress. It felt like a costume to her, and the idea offered a sense of safety. 

Emily adjusted her gold bag and looked around the elevator. &quot;You know, we owe a lot to the Informants. They're risking everything.&quot;

Sharon nodded, touching up her lipstick in the elevator mirror. &quot;Yeah, and the Operators out there on the ground. Those guys are doing the heavy lifting.&quot;

Emily leaned back, her mind racing. &quot;I just hope our tech team can keep up. If something goes wrong, we're in trouble.&quot;

Sharon gave a slight smile. &quot;They've got it covered. Besides, we have more than just tech support. The whole team is solid.&quot;

The elevator stopped at the lobby when a lone figure joined them inside. A man appeared, who looked more like he belonged at a convention for army accountants or in the dark corner of a private escort lounge. Unlike Emily and Sharon, who planned to fit in, this man looked like he wanted to stand out. In the oversized elevator, their guest took a stance dead center. Close enough to Emily that she could feel the heat from his skin. Close enough for her to smell the subtle cologne radiating from his navy blue “Alpha Corporation” polo. The aroma was distinctly chemical, with a greasy undertone and a hint of sulfur.

&quot;Here to defy the energy crisis?&quot; Emily asked.

&quot;Aren't we all?&quot; he said, trying to make it seem like his eyes were set on the door before him and not scanning the woman. &quot;Great speakers today. I'm Greenbaum.&quot;

Emily nodded in agreement and stepped aside. &quot;So we’ve heard,&quot; she said, gesturing to the woman beside her. 

Sharon was everything Emily had ever wanted to be: tall, fit, and sure of herself. A goddess by any standard. Like all Nova women, Emily was average in every way and doomed to remain so. Sharon guided a lock of Emily's curly blonde hair behind her ear, whispering, &quot;Ready for a test run?&quot; and shot a mischievous look in the mirrored paneling, causing Emily to blush.

&quot;I do have another reason for attending,&quot; he said, finally allowing his eyes to meet the women.

&quot;Oh, I'm sure it's important Alpha business,&quot; she said with a carefully timed nod at the logo on his polo. &quot;I bet you're one of the guys who make it all possible, like behind the scenes.&quot;

Sharon held her laughter, but Greenbaum cocked his head and smiled with what might be  the moment of validation he'd been waiting his whole life for. &quot;I've been researching ways to bulk up and achieve a more robust natural form. My grandfather was Mr. Olympia, but steroids did to his manhood what the energy crisis has been doing to many men.&quot; He paused as Emily and Sharon stared at him.

&quot;Ah,&quot; Sharon said. &quot;I knew the real work was being done here. They should change the name. Should have called it something like…&quot; Sharon bit her lip to tease a thought. &quot;Something like 'Getting It Up.' At least then, we'd know we were all here for the same thing.&quot;

Greenbaum and Emily shared a forced laugh while Sharon remained characteristically composed. With her eyes locked on Greenbaum, the silence stretched taut and uneasy until Sharon nudged Emily hard enough to make the poor girl step forward—again too close to Greenbaum.

&quot;I actually specialize in muscle enhancement,&quot; Emily said. &quot;I've been fixated on it since my diagnosis. My work doesn't help me directly,&quot; she paused to make up the next part of the story. &quot;You could say that I help men be men,&quot; she said, shooting a coy smile that disarmed and excited Greenbaum.

&quot;Really?&quot; he asked, completely unprepared.

&quot;If you're lucky enough to survive the energy crisis, your body's blood flow and fluid distribution are significantly altered. This can lead to reduced venous return and potentially impact the vascular aspects of erectile function. It's really circulatory dysfunction.&quot;

&quot;Circulatory dysfunction,&quot; he repeated without a shred of understanding.

&quot;That's the same conclusion our research has found. Interesting. I must admit that I have personal reasons for my focus.&quot;

Tapping into a vast database of overheard knowledge or confidently designing complete lies, Emily continued, &quot;Circulatory dysfunction is common in males and females who take extended tours in space. Since the energy crisis tends to mimic the side effects of long-term space travel, our research pulls heavily from observations of Astros who now lack the blood flow necessary for achieving and maintaining a man's 'proper form.'&quot;

&quot;Have you developed methods for testing?&quot;

Surprising herself, Emily felt a cruel delight. &quot;Lucky for you, it's easily diagnosed by a simple physical exam. There are obvious marking spots, but they can be hard to recognize without proper training.&quot;

&quot;Are you accepting new patients?&quot;

&quot;No, unfortunately, we're at full capacity,&quot; Sharon said. &quot;I admire your commitment, though, so I'll do you a favor. I'll examine you right here and now.&quot;

&quot;In an elevator. I can't think of a better place,&quot; he said.

&quot;I need you to lift your shirt and show me your lower back.&quot;

&quot;My shirt?&quot;

&quot;Go ahead, nothing to be embarrassed about,&quot; Emily said. &quot;We see these things all the time. Go ahead. Turn around.&quot;

&quot;Your shirt, please,&quot; Sharon added with alluring dominance.

His eyes darted in every direction as if looking for an escape as he lifted his shirt from his neatly pressed chinos. In the sterile light of the elevator, Emily and Sharon scrutinized his lean frame, honed from countless hours of rigorous training, and the women noted the hint of a life less ordinary. 

The pale skin of his long back revealed a topography of scars and faded bruising—the sort not acquired through clumsy accidents but encounters far more perilous. From his smirk reflected in the elevator door, he was proud of them, like a newlywed of honeymoon photos or a mortician of a well-dressed corpse. Greenbaum held himself with tense ease. As Sharon politely requested him to loosen his belt for a better look, she noticed a brief flash of something—caution perhaps.

&quot;Sure,&quot; he said. The faint sound of his belt unbuckling filled the compact space. Emily's fingers trailed down his spine with professional detachment. Yet she couldn't help but note the tension in his muscles, a body conditioned for swift, decisive action.

&quot;Sometimes the markings are much lower along the spinal column.&quot; And you might want to get this scoliosis looked at.&quot;

&quot;Just a bit lower,&quot; Sharon said, masking her rising curiosity.

Facing away, his movements deliberate, he revealed nothing more than necessary. &quot;Yes, Ma'ams.&quot; The motion revealed more than skin: a slight shift in his stance, a readiness in how he held himself, even while exposed and vulnerable. It was a subtle hint at his mastery—his control—over his every movement. However, as Sharon and Emily locked eyes to note this to one another, he seemed to lose balance like a newbie in a yoga class.

&quot;Do I have them?&quot; he asked with a trace of urgency.

As Sharon peered closer, her fingers tracing the scars leading to and beyond the fabric of his boxers, the doors dinged and began to open. Sharon delicately pulled his unremarkable underwear down his surprisingly remarkable body and whistled as if calling the attention of the physicians in the 60th-floor lounge. 

Emily, embarrassed for him, hid herself next to the control panel. Frozen in shame, Greenbaum tried to pull his shirt over his head and his underwear up, but he fell out of the elevator in the chaos. As the doors slowly closed between the women and Greenbaum, Sharon reached into her handbag, retrieved a lollipop, bent with a hand on her knee, and extended it to him with a devious smile.

&quot;Dum Dum?&quot;

Emily and Sharon's laughter filled the elevator as they ascended the remaining six floors. As they stepped from the elevator into a deserted lobby with three hallways—each a copy of the other—both women grasped to contain their last lingering chuckles.

&quot;Maybe we shouldn't have pushed it so far,&quot; Emily said.

&quot;That creep would have let us take it farther,&quot; Sharon said with a sharp edge of uncaring reality. &quot;Forget him. You did great. Passed the test! This is Vegas, where the unusual is the norm.&quot;

They navigated the hotel's corridors with casual determination. The plush carpet silenced their steps, and the dim lighting fractured and softened the shadows around them. Turning the corner that stole their view of the lobby but offered an eyeshot of their destination felt to them more like being in a carnival funhouse or some haunted hellscape—mundane, repeating, endless.

Room 1122 appeared before them, undistinguished from the others but for the golden numbers signifying they had arrived as intended. It stood quietly, a door just like any other, unaware of its significance to these women or the world. Sharon knocked, and an ordinary sound rang through the quiet halls like a falling tree would through a canyon.

&quot;Just a minute,&quot; a voice from within said without care or haste.

&quot;Room service,&quot; Emily said softly.

&quot;Nope. Go away. I haven't ordered anything.&quot;

Sharon looked Emily up and down. &quot;What happened to the woman I was with a few minutes ago?&quot; she said, lifting an eyebrow. &quot;Tap back into that. If it helps, think of this guy as just another part of the game.&quot;

With slight hesitation, Emily called out again with confidence that felt inauthentic to her but might stop a charging elephant in its path. &quot;Understood, sir,&quot; Emily said. &quot;Happens all the time. Chapter Three

A Thousand Thrills

A small A-frame cabin sat nestled amongst White Fir and Sugar Pine trees at the edge of Sequoia National Park. It was at the end of a dirt forest service road and used to belong to Ulysses Nova, who had used it for hunting. Upon his passing he left it to his favorite nephew Cole, who rarely visited. Cole would check on the cabin between missions to see if the roof had caved in. He often thought about selling it. It was a long drive from his apartment in San Diego, a place he referred to as 'The Hotel Terra Firma,' since he rarely slept there.

After his last mission to Saturn, he could barely stand to be around people. He never imagined that he would ever call the cabin home, or that his roommate would be a six-limbed science project with bad gas.

Cole had just finished his weekly shower and as he entered the bedroom a picture frame caught his eye. It was a group of classmates from the Academy. Corizon, Phillips, Hadley. For some reason it triggered memories farther back into his childhood. A jingle played in his head as he saw the commercial play out in his mind.

'A Thousand Thrills.' He saw kids going down waterslides, racing go-karts. They blissfully stuffed their mouths with cotton candy and funnel cakes. 'A Thousand Thrills' was a theme park about three hours away from his home in Pasadena. He remembered thinking that has to be heaven on earth. I have to experience it.

Cole would plead with his parents every weekend. It's only three hours away. I'll pay for my own ticket and I'll even pay for gas.

At first they offered excuses. We've got a lot going on. Maybe next weekend.

When it became obvious that this wasn't just a phase he was going through, his parents were irritated and blunt. We don't have the money. That would break the bank.

These deflections worked. Cole felt deeply troubled by the idea that his family was so financially fragile, so close to the edge. He began saving money from his paper route and lawn mowing jobs, afraid that they might not have enough for food or electricity. One day Cole's dad came home with a brand new Harley Davidson motorcycle. Cole thought they were broke, yet here was his dad blowing money on a toy.

Cole noticed that in spite of this purchase, the family fared just fine. They weren't evicted. They didn't go hungry. They even kept buying ice cream. Cole felt duped. What else were they lying about?

What Cole didn't understand then, and wouldn't understand for another year, was what that Harley actually meant. His father hadn't bought a toy. His father had bought a last thing. The motorcycle was a man making a list of everything he still wanted to feel before he couldn't feel it anymore — wind, speed, the particular loneliness of a highway at six in the morning. A man who had just been handed a piece of paper in a doctor's office and was trying to decide how much of it to believe.

In the evenings, Cole would hear his parents talking in the kitchen with the door mostly closed. The conversations had a weight to them now that he couldn't name. He would hear his mother's voice strain at the edges. You have to tell them. And his father's voice, quieter, later. Not yet.

Cole spent those months planning his escape instead of listening. As he got closer to his sixteenth birthday he set a goal to buy his own car. He spotted a gray Toyota Tercel parked on the street a couple of blocks away. They were only asking $1,500. That was doable.

He talked to his friend Marcus, and together they hatched a plan to stay in Culver City over the summer. Marcus had an older cousin with his own apartment. They could split rent and share a room. 'A Thousand Thrills' was only five minutes away. They could spend three months not only enjoying the park but getting paid as employees. Other kids at school had friends and relatives who worked there, and they would push Cole's and Marcus' applications to the front of the line. The plan was flawless.

The only family member Cole shared this plan with was Emily. She was a couple of years older and his only sibling. Cole trusted her. She was the most loyal person he knew.

Cole dove into work, eager to get his car and even more eager to get to Culver City. Finally, after over a year, the day came. He finished Mrs. Weber's lawn and ran home with the last twenty dollars he needed. He spread all the cash on his bed and double-counted the piles. He gathered the money, stuffed it into a large zip-lock bag, walked to his neighbor's door, and presented what seemed to Cole a huge sum. He signed the bill of sale.

With the keys in hand, he opened the car and was greeted by the smell of old sun-faded plastic and seat cushions. He could feel the memory of a thousand road trips when he touched the steering wheel. He also discovered something he hadn't planned on: it was a manual. After sheepishly knocking back on the neighbor's door and getting a short lesson in how to release the clutch at just the right pressure, he lurched the car a few blocks home.

His parents were surprised by the car. He wasn't going to tell them about Culver City until the night before. The applications were filled out, the interviews scheduled for the day after school let out. He already had his clothes separated into two piles: what to take, and what to leave. He even had a small pile of stuff he would allow Emily to use for the summer.

A week before his departure, Cole's mom and dad sat him and Emily down on the couch. Cole felt the cushions go cold against his back.

His father did not look like a man who had hidden anything. He looked like a man who was tired of hiding it.

The word was cancer. It had been cancer for eleven months.

Cole's mother held his father's hand and looked at the middle distance between them and the wall. Emily's face, which usually beamed with happiness, was crushed inward and silent tears ran down her cheeks. He broke for her. He could not watch her cry without breaking.

In the following days it became clear what the summer would actually be. His father would need more appointments. His mother would need to work more hours. Emily was barely holding herself together. His dad moved through the house slowly now, as if he had been given permission to stop performing. The Harley sat in the driveway and nobody rode it.

Cole dreaded telling Marcus. In a school stairwell he said it plain: I can't go to Culver.

What? Marcus' face went red. You're the one with the car. This was your idea.

I know. I'm sorry. He searched for the words. My dad is sick. Really sick.

Marcus stared at him for a long moment. So we just throw the whole summer away?

Cole looked at him and understood, in that moment, something he would spend the rest of his life turning over. The look on Marcus' face was not grief. It was arithmetic. He was calculating what he had lost and whether Cole's family emergency was a sufficient explanation. He decided it was not.

School let out. Marcus found another ride to Culver City. When he did, he left Cole a note under the front porch mat. It read: Find yourself a new best friend. I will NEVER forget how you screwed me over. —Marcus.

Cole ended up delivering pizzas. Every time he heard stories about Marcus having the summer of his life at the park, he sat with something complicated — a jealousy he couldn't justify and a steadiness he couldn't explain. He had chosen to stay. He would choose it again.

His father died that November. Cole was seventeen. The hospital corridor where he waited was the quietest room he had ever stood in. Sound stopped at the edges of it. He sat in a plastic chair for six hours and the only thing he could hear was his own blood moving. When the doctor came out to speak to his mother, Cole looked at the closed door and understood for the first time that some rooms you do not get to enter.

After a summer of grief and a fall of worse, Cole promised himself that he would not be trapped in one place again. He started looking into the Space Academy, but when he learned about the high-level classes required, he let himself be let down for exactly one evening. Then Emily knocked on his bedroom door.

Hey. What's wrong? She sat on the bed next to him.

The Academy only accepts straight-A students. I'm not that.

No you're not, she said, and you're going to be. Look at me.

He looked up from the floor. She looked at him with a certainty that he had not earned yet.

You can do anything you want. You just have to work really, really hard. She let out a small laugh. It was the first time he had heard her laugh since the hospital. Come on, poopy pants. I know you're not afraid of hard work.

I'm barely getting Cs, he said.

I'll help you. Every night. I've already aced every class on your schedule for next year. She tilted her head. Or are you going to be a little bitch about it?

Cole wasn't used to hearing his sister curse. It shocked him into laughing. He was convinced that with her help it might just be possible. What other options did he have? Deliver more pizzas?

Every day before and after work, books and papers littered the kitchen table. Emily and Cole filled out diagrams and scribbled in notebooks. Their mother began sneaking in to watch them work. At first she would need a drink of water or check if the oven had been left on. Eventually she became a regular fixture in the study group. It turned out that she had been a real nerd in college.

What were you going to study? Emily asked her one night.

Chemical engineering. She sounded like she was admitting something. I always found it fascinating that small changes could transform a substance. Even shape a society.

Like coffee? Cole offered.

She smiled at him the way she almost never did anymore. I met your father, and I thought I had to stay home and be a good wife. She paused. Don't let anyone tell you how to live your life. Her voice sounded strained in a way that wasn't sadness exactly. More like something resolved.

They got back to work with a greater sense of who she was. By the end of his senior year, Cole had pulled himself to A's and B's.

The day he, Emily, and his mother drove down to San Diego for his Academy entrance exam, the space ships were there waiting. Their presence stirred something in him that he could not account for — something familiar, something that felt like an answer to a question he had not known how to ask. Up there, his only responsibility would be to his crewmates and the mission. No grief. No kitchen table full of bills. No Harley sitting in the rain.

That day, he knew that if the Academy would give him a chance, he would not rest until he was the captain of his own ship.

Captain Nova. He liked the sound of it.

A loud glugging noise from the other room broke the spell.

Oh no. Not again. Cole groaned.


Chapter Four

The morning light filtered through the tall pines, casting dappled light on the forest floor. Cole hefted his old ax over his head and split the last log round. The splitting crack was subdued due to the early spring moisture. Cole's breath clouded in the crisp air. The soft rustle of leaves and the distant call of a mountain jay seemed unbothered by this early morning habit. He paused, straightening to scan the treeline. Warm light burst through a gently swaying thicket of pine needles. 

The low sun cast a hard, angular light as it peeked over the granite peak just east of his cabin. His eyes were intense and watchful. His age, or was it his pain, was increasingly apparent. As he approached the cabin where old wood met new tech, he rubbed his hands together and let out a sudden and glorious howl. Then, just as before, he continued to the comfort of home.

The door creaked open, and Bitboy groaned. The firelight from inside flickered on Bitboy's brown and white fur, catching the streaks of auburn as he scurried over to Cole. His mechanical limbs hummed softly as he reached around Cole's thighs, and the poor monkey sighed in obvious discomfort. One of Bitboy's arms reached out, tugging at Cole's sleeve, guiding him to the warmth.

Cole knelt by the fireplace. Bitboy watched his friend add a freshly cut log to the fire through his delirium. The sap began to boil, and the moisture hissed from the wood until it was dry enough to burn. Bitboy's eyes reflected the dancing flames. The scent of pine mingled with the earthy aroma of the fire, filling the cabin with a comforting fragrance. Cole's fingers brushed against Bitboy's fur. 

The warmth of the fire seeped into his skin, a stark contrast to the morning chill. Outside, the high Sierra Nevadas loomed. Cole softened as he looked at Bitboy. He felt deep gratitude for the unlikely companionship. The morning light, the serene forest, and the warmth of the fire blended into a moment of quiet reflection.

&quot;You little bastard,&quot; Cole said. &quot;What do you have?&quot;

As if dropping a one-gallon glass bottle of ethanol next to an open fire would obliterate his wrongdoing, Bitboy did just that: rolled on his back and forced all six limbs into the air like a dead bug. With a brilliant puff, Cole's expectation of a relaxing day went up in flames, along with the lingering fumes from a well-finished bottle that should have lasted the damned monkey at least a few days. 

Cole blamed the Federation. Their quest to re-engineer life had ended in almost complete failure. Other than Bitboy, no other monsters had made it past a year. It turned out that attempting to craft a weapon with the learning capacity of a human but devoid of moral complexities—a creature entirely expendable—was a terrible idea. Now Cole was the unwilling father of a junky. A junky with forearms containing a network of metal rods, which themselves acted as the unholy foundation for retractable steel blades. 

Bitboy's upgraded form required ethanol not as a vice but as a vital power source for these and other mechanical augmentations. Even with its enhancements, his primal brain lacked the discipline to moderate. Ethanol was akin to ambrosia to him—fueling his strength, sharpening his intellect, and enhancing his agility, making it irresistible. His inability to self-regulate his consumption posed a risk of overindulgence to the point of shutdown.

Meanwhile, the world outside grappled with a crisis of its own. The airwaves carried the manufactured accent belonging only to news anchors.

More on the disappearance of 43-year-old Dr. Stephen Conrad tonight at ten.&quot;

Cole reached for the remote—the vibrant escapism of silence. Bitboy returned from a meandering trip to the kitchen and, with snacks in hand, sat in wonder at the crackling fire and the boiling, spitting sap. Cole cracked open a beer on his way to the medicine cabinet. If selling second-hand Federation tech on the black market had been a rich man's game, Bitboy's thirst and the price of Naloxone-E kept Cole distant from a golden throne. &quot;I need you to walk me through it again. I have the shot, but what's the rest?&quot; Cole asked.

&quot;It's a good thing I'm about there then. Isn't it?&quot; Ricardo replied.

Emily turned red and wide-eyed, which brought out her innocent beauty. Cole, on the other hand, closed the door.

The cargo bay was quieter than space.

He'd laid in the snow at four in the morning when the wind died and the trees didn't even creak. This was quieter than that.

It reached toward him without moving.

He felt it cross his suit the way warmth crosses cloth. There was no sound. There was no — he searched for the word and could not find it — event. There was a before, in which he was a man on the floor of a sealed cargo bay with his hand near a harvester. There was an after, in which he was that same man, and the harvester, and also somehow the bay, and also somehow the thinking part of all of it, the part that noticed.

He knew, distantly, that this should be terrifying. He waited for the terror.

It did not come.

What came was a slow patient attention from somewhere very old. The substance had been waiting a long time, and it was not in a hurry, and it had questions it wanted to ask, and they were not questions Cole knew how to answer in any language he had been taught.

Hello, he wanted to say. He did not say anything.

The bay began to dim.

He understood this in the way one understands things in dreams — instantly, without the connecting steps. Sam was the first thing he registered. Her hand was on his shoulder. She was saying something — Cole, Cole, look at me, are you with me, Cole — and her voice had the particular clipped urgency of a Federation-trained data tech who has decided to be a medic for the next ninety seconds. Behind her, the oxygen line hissed against his helmet seal. Behind that, Ricardo was at the bulkhead, dragging himself across the bay on a tether, his face the color of new bone.

Bitboy was on the override hatch. Bitboy had welded one of his mechanical arms — Cole could see this even through the residue of the vision — to the manual lever, and was using his remaining limbs to hold the substance's spillover back from the venting controls. The chimp was making the small high-pitched sound he made when he was hurt and trying not to show it.

The bay was sealed. The bay was repressurizing. The atmosphere was coming back. The four of them were going to live.

"Cole," Sam said, very close. "Cole, breathe."

He breathed.

"Cole, I need you to tell me you're with me."

"I'm with you."

His voice came out hoarse, like he had been screaming. He didn't think he had been screaming. He thought, distantly, that he had been very quiet.

Sam exhaled hard. She pressed her forehead against his helmet, fast, the way you do when relief comes in faster than you can dignify it. "Don't you dare," she said. "Don't you dare do that to us again."

"I won't," he said.

He meant it. He meant it in a way he had never meant a promise before. The substance was still settling in the room, in him, in her — Gracie, who was a very small new presence at the back of his mind, getting used to her own boundaries — and the part of him that had spent thirty-two years rehearsing the small steady voice that said you don't deserve this was quiet. Not gone. He understood that the voice would never be entirely gone. It was just — the warden was on break. The cell door was open. He was, for the first time he could remember, not the one keeping himself in.

"Where's Bitboy," he said.

Sam helped him sit up. He could not yet stand. The substance had been very gentle, but it had still been the substance, and his body was telling him, in the particular slow way bodies tell you these things, that he had spent a portion of himself.

Bitboy was at the hatch.

Bitboy looked at him.

Bitboy was crying, which Cole had never seen Bitboy do, and the tears were tracking down through the chimp's fur in the small pearl-bright way Cole would later remember without ever being able to describe.

The chimp pulled his welded arm free of the lever — there was a small wet sound that Cole did not allow himself to think about — and crossed the bay on three good limbs and one smoking one, and climbed into Cole's lap, and put his small wet face against Cole's chest plate, and made the high-pitched sound again, and Cole put his hands around the chimp and said, very quietly:

"I know, buddy. I know. It's okay. We're okay. We're okay."

Bitboy chittered something that was not in any of his usual registers.

Cole understood it. He understood it the way you understand things when the substance is still in the room. The chimp was saying — not in words — I knew. I knew you'd come back. I knew her too. I'm going to miss her.

"Yeah," Cole said. "Yeah. Me too."


Ricardo got to them on the tether.

Ricardo did not say anything for a long time. He sat down on the deck plate next to Cole and Sam and Bitboy, and he leaned his big shoulder against Cole's smaller one, and he kept his face turned away while he got his breathing under control. When he spoke, his voice was thick.

"Where the hell did you go."

"I don't know yet," Cole said.

"Were you out?"

"No."

"You weren't responding."

"I wasn't here."

Ricardo nodded. He did not understand. He did not need to. He had been running on Federation grief-protocol for the last hour and he was running on Cole-protocol now and that, for Ricardo, was enough to keep going on.

"Taylor," Ricardo said, after a moment.

"I know."

"He's still on the ship."

"I know."

"What do we do."

Cole sat in the slowly repressurizing bay with his arms around the chimp and Sam's hand on his shoulder and Ricardo's bulk leaning against him, and he listened to the new small voice at the back of his head, and he thought, with a quietness he had never been allowed before:

Whatever I want.

It was not an arrogant thought. It was not the thought of a man who had decided to go full berserker on a betrayer. It was simpler than that. It was the thought of a man who, for the first time in his life, had not delegated the decision-making part of himself to someone else's voice — a Federation chancellor, a dead father, an absent woman, a self he had sentenced thirty-two years ago for crimes he had not committed and could not recall.

He could decide.

He could decide what to do with Taylor.

He could decide what to do with the rest of the mission, with the harvest, with the Federation, with the cabin in Sequoia, with the rest of his life.

The deciding was not going to be easy. The deciding was not going to be light. Sharon was still dead. The crew of the Polaris was still dead. The boy in the Subaru was still grieving his father and would always be grieving his father. The world had not become smaller or simpler.

But he was the one doing it now.

The warden was off the clock.

"We'll figure it out," he said. "All of it. Together."

Ricardo nodded against his shoulder.

Sam squeezed.

Bitboy chittered, very quietly, against his chest.

And in the back of his head, Gracie — Gracie, he allowed himself to think the name now, he tried it on like a coat that almost fit — said softly, Cole. The bay is at full atmosphere. You can take off the helmet.

He took off the helmet.

The recycled air of the cargo bay was the sweetest thing he had ever tasted.


Later, much later — after the bay had been resealed, and the venting had been overridden, and Bitboy's smoking limb had been cut free and replaced with a temporary brace, and Sam had triple-checked the harvester containment, and Ricardo had finally let himself sit down and shake — Cole would walk back to the cockpit alone.

Taylor would be there.

Taylor would be at the helm, where Cody had left him at the end of the last chapter, and Taylor would look up with that small worn-thin smile that Cole now recognized as the smile of a man who had survived too many things by being right about them, and Taylor would say something dry and small.

And Cole, for the first time in his life, would not respond from the warden's voice.

He would respond from his own.

But that was later.

For now, he sat on the floor of the cargo bay with the people who had refused to leave him in it, and he held the chimp who had loved him through everything he had not yet allowed himself to feel, and he listened to the new soft voice in the back of his head adjusting to its own existence, and he thought about his sister, far below, somewhere on a road in Texas, alive, and he thought about a graveside in Sequoia he had not yet been to, and he thought about the long careful work that was waiting for him on the other side of this hour.

He was going to be okay.

He was not okay yet.

But he was going to be.

The substance, he understood — turning the thought over in the new clean room of his mind — had been waiting fifty years for someone to come to it grieving, with nothing left for it to take.

It had finally found him.

And what it had given him in return for the nothing was the only thing it had ever had to give.

Itself.

And himself.

And, in the back of his head, in a voice that was still working out how to be a voice:

Cole?

Yes, Gracie.

The Falcon's on long-range comms. Earth side. Your sister is alive. She wants to talk to you.

He closed his eyes.

He let the tears come.

"Patch her through," he said.
