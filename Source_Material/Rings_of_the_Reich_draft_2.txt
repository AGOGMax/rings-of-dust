Prologue: Seeds of Chaos


The Federation’s secret labs were always a hotbed for experiments that pushed the boundaries of ethics and sanity. Deep within the asteroid belt, on the hidden base designated Facility 42, the most outrageous of these experiments took place. It was here that Heavy Hitler, the bloated and maniacal leader of a rogue faction, first laid eyes on his two greatest creations: the Space Squirrels and the Sexy Sharons.


But before Facility 42 became his stronghold, Heavy Hitler was simply Wilhelm Von Grutzen, the sickly and awkward child of a Federation mechanic and a failed artist. Born on a backwater mining colony, Wilhelm endured constant bullying for his frail frame and peculiar obsession with tinkering. While other children dreamed of becoming pilots or explorers, Wilhelm spent his days building rudimentary robots from salvaged scraps, his dreams centered on creating something—anything—that would make him matter.


By his teenage years, Wilhelm’s intellect had outpaced everyone around him, but his bitterness grew just as quickly. When a Federation-sponsored talent scout arrived on the colony, Wilhelm saw his chance to escape. Accepted into the prestigious Haverick Institute of Advanced Engineering, he left his home planet with a fire in his heart—a determination to rise above the mediocrity that had defined his upbringing.


At the Institute, Wilhelm excelled academically but remained socially isolated. His peers mocked his eccentricities, dubbing him &quot;Grutzen the Gray&quot; for his drab clothing and dour demeanor. A humiliating incident involving an experimental drone that malfunctioned during a demonstration—exploding and covering the audience in sewage—cemented his reputation as an outcast. It was here that his hatred for the Federation began to fester. Despite his brilliance, he saw the system as corrupt, designed to reward the popular and well-connected while discarding geniuses like him.


After graduation, Wilhelm found work as a low-level engineer for the Federation’s sprawling bureaucracy. Frustrated and disillusioned, he began siphoning resources and data from classified projects. His big break came when he uncovered the plans for a highly secretive bioengineering experiment—one that involved enhancing animal intelligence and creating humanoid clones. Seeing its potential, Wilhelm stole the data and fled, narrowly escaping capture.


In the lawless regions of space, Wilhelm reinvented himself. Adopting the moniker &quot;Heavy Hitler,&quot; a name he claimed embodied the weight of his ambitions and the iron fist with which he would rule, he built his reputation as a rogue scientist and tactician. He began gathering a following among outcasts, mercenaries, and disenfranchised scientists who shared his disdain for the Federation. Facility 42 became his sanctuary, a place where his genius could flourish without restraint.


Heavy Hitler’s rise to power was anything but smooth. Rival factions tried to eliminate him, but his cunning and ruthlessness saw him through. He struck deals with smugglers, blackmailed Federation officials, and sold experimental weapons to Alpha Corp, always staying one step ahead of his enemies. Over time, his forces grew, and his vision expanded. No longer content with mere survival, Heavy Hitler aimed to reshape the galaxy, bending it to his will.


It was during these years that he first encountered Sharon Teez. Their paths crossed during a high-stakes negotiation on a neutral asteroid. At the time, Hitler believed Sharon could be swayed to join his cause. Her sharp wit and unwavering confidence captivated him, and for the first time in years, he felt something resembling admiration. Emboldened by her seemingly playful demeanor, he made his intentions clear—both professionally and romantically.


Sharon’s rejection was swift and merciless. &quot;I’d rather align with a malfunctioning toaster,&quot; she said, her words cutting deeper than any blade. Her dismissal, witnessed by his closest advisors, was a blow to his pride he would never forget.


Now, with the power to clone her, Heavy Hitler saw an opportunity to rewrite history. The Sexy Sharons were not merely a weapon; they were his attempt to recreate and control the one person who had defied him.









Chapter One: Facility 42


Hidden deep within the asteroid belt, Facility 42 was more than just a lab—it was a fortress, a thriving microcosm of chaos and control. Encased in layers of reinforced titanium and cloaked by electromagnetic interference fields, the base seemed to hum with the weight of its importance. To Heavy Hitler, it was more than a hideout; it was the crucible in which his legacy would be forged.


The asteroid belt surrounding Facility 42 was both a shield and a sword. The treacherous field served as a natural labyrinth, confusing any ship that dared approach. Hitler had further enhanced the defenses by placing mines disguised as asteroids, each equipped with advanced cloaking technology. Few who entered the belt ever emerged.


The facility itself was a marvel of engineering. Its sprawling layout included multiple wings for research, training, and military operations. The walls were lined with conduits that pulsed with energy, feeding power to the labs and defenses. Above all, the command center stood as the brain of Facility 42, a circular room filled with holographic displays that allowed Hitler to monitor every corner of his domain.


Gathering his followers had been no simple task. Heavy Hitler’s early recruits were mercenaries—men and women who cared little for ideology but saw his growing operation as a means to wealth and survival. Among them was Captain Blitz, a grizzled veteran who had fought in countless skirmishes across the galaxy. Blitz had initially joined out of desperation, fleeing a failed mutiny against Alpha Corp. Over time, his pragmatism and military expertise made him an indispensable part of Facility 42’s hierarchy.


Then came the scientists. Disillusioned former Federation researchers like Dr. Neumann found refuge in Hitler’s vision. Neumann, known for his brilliance and eccentricity, was drawn to the freedom Facility 42 offered—no ethics committees, no bureaucratic oversight. But his clashes with Blitz were legendary. Neumann saw the soldiers as brutes, incapable of appreciating the elegance of science, while Blitz viewed the scientists as reckless liabilities. Hitler often played mediator, his authoritarian charisma keeping the team from splintering.


The Space Squirrels were another source of tension. Enhanced with intelligence and jetpack technology, they were both a marvel and a menace. Their leader, Squeaks, was fiercely loyal to Hitler but had little respect for the humans who dismissed the squirrels as mere tools. Squeaks’ acts of sabotage—often for amusement—fueled resentment among the staff. Yet even Blitz begrudgingly admitted that the squirrels were invaluable for infiltration missions.


Despite their differences, the group remained united by a common enemy: the Federation. Each member, from Blitz to Neumann to Squeaks, had been wronged by the system. For all their disagreements, they shared a singular purpose—to dismantle the establishment that had cast them aside. Hitler’s promises of power and revenge kept them loyal, even as tempers flared and rivalries simmered.


Meanwhile, the mirrored training chamber adjacent to the lab echoed with the sounds of combat. The Sexy Sharons, clad in sleek black bodysuits, practiced their routines with deadly precision. Each clone moved in perfect synchronization, but their vanity often got in the way. &quot;Your form is off,&quot; one clone said, glaring at another. &quot;You’re making us all look bad.&quot;


The criticized clone rolled her eyes. &quot;Oh, please. You’re just jealous because my hair looks better.&quot;


Dr. Neumann, observing from behind a one-way mirror, muttered under his breath. &quot;Perfect soldiers, my foot. More like divas with guns.&quot;


In the command center, Blitz monitored the base’s operations. His fingers danced across the holographic interface as he updated patrol routes and reviewed intercepted transmissions. The air crackled with tension as he prepared to deliver his latest report to Hitler.


&quot;Alpha Corp’s been sniffing around Sector 12 again,&quot; Blitz began. &quot;And we intercepted chatter from the Collective about a shipment of DarkX crystals.&quot;


Heavy Hitler, seated in his reinforced hover-throne, leaned forward. His eyes glinted with a mix of ambition and malice. &quot;Good. We’ll strike before they can act. The crystals will be ours. What of the Falcon crew?&quot;


Blitz hesitated, choosing his words carefully. &quot;They’re on a transport mission, but their route takes them dangerously close to our operations.&quot;


Dr. Neumann scoffed from across the room, adjusting his goggles. &quot;The Falcon crew? You’re wasting your time worrying about a band of misfits. They’re a joke compared to the might of Facility 42.&quot;


Blitz’s tone turned sharper, his frustration evident. &quot;A joke? They’ve outmaneuvered Federation patrols, outsmarted Alpha Corp, and survived skirmishes that would leave most crews in pieces. Underestimating them is the kind of mistake that gets operations compromised.&quot;


&quot;Outmaneuvered by luck, not skill,&quot; Neumann replied with a dismissive wave. &quot;Brute force and bluster only go so far. Science will always triumph.&quot;


Squeaks, perched atop a console, let out a series of chittering laughs. The leader of the Space Squirrels tapped his tiny claws on the console, then activated his translator. &quot;Humans love underdogs. Makes them reckless. Easy to predict. Easy to trap.&quot;


Blitz shot the squirrel a glare. &quot;Predictable doesn’t mean harmless. If they’ve gotten this far, it’s because they’re smarter than you think.&quot;


Squeaks tapped his claws again, bringing up a holographic map of the Falcon crew’s projected trajectory. &quot;Chatty humans. This one talks about needing parts,&quot; he said, motioning to a flickering transmission log. &quot;Broken ship. Weak prey.&quot;


Dr. Neumann snorted. &quot;Or bait. Don’t forget that Federation tactics are far more sophisticated than the bluster of a mercenary ship. If we bite too quickly, we might be playing into their hands.&quot;


Blitz crossed his arms. &quot;So we ignore them? Wait until they stumble onto our doorstep? That’s reckless.&quot;


Hitler’s voice cut through their bickering like a blade. &quot;Enough. Both of you. Whether it’s the Federation, Alpha Corp, or this Falcon crew, they all underestimate us. Facility 42 is untouchable.&quot; He turned to the holographic map, staring intently at the Falcon crew’s trajectory. &quot;We’ll observe for now. Let them get comfortable. If they stray too close, we’ll ensure they regret it.&quot;


Blitz glanced at Hitler. &quot;And Sharon Teez? She’s part of their crew. Their tactician.&quot;


Hitler’s jaw tightened, his gaze growing colder. &quot;Sharon Teez is irrelevant. A relic of poor choices. She’ll regret the day she dismissed me.&quot;


An uneasy silence followed, the tension palpable. Squeaks broke it with another chitter, his mechanical tail flicking. &quot;Humans overthink. Squirrels? We strike. Quick. Efficient.&quot;


Blitz smirked faintly. &quot;And reckless.&quot;


Squeaks tilted his head. &quot;Effective.&quot;


As the room settled, Hitler hovered to the observation window, his attention shifting to the vast expanse of the asteroid field. His quarters, filled with trophies and holograms of past victories, exuded a sense of his grandiose ambition. On one wall, a hologram of Sharon Teez flickered mid-smirk. He stared at it, his expression flickering between resentment and obsession.


Facility 42 was more than a fortress. It was his kingdom, a testament to his genius and defiance against the galaxy that had dismissed him. Every decision, every trap, every ally, and every enemy was part of his plan to dominate.


&quot;Soon,&quot; he murmured, his voice barely audible. &quot;Soon, the galaxy will bow before me.&quot;
