


Chapter 2







Emily and Sharon were about to dive into the kind of weekend that becomes a story you tell in a whisper. Under the gnawing Vegas sun, Emily stared at a graffiti symbol on the wall outside the casino. A single drop of water with a dark circle inside. A circle intertwined with lines, not unlike the sketches she’d seen in Cole's notes: the mark of The Collective. It was a simple tag, easily overlooked by the uninitiated, but to those in the know, it spoke volumes of the underground network's reach and influence. The fresh paint still carried the scent of aerosol on the dry, desert breeze. This unassuming symbol spoke of resistance, of defiance against the oppressive forces of A Corp and their poorly veiled security arm: The Federation. The droplet symbolized the unknown power of each member. Their motto: no drop thinks it’s the flood. Emily and Sharon squinted at the world like it owed them money. 

The sign above the hotel's parking entrance read, &quot;Defying Gravity 2041: Innovations in Combating the Energy Crisis.&quot; This promise of scientific exploration felt out of place in a city that resonated with sin from every rooftop and casino floor. It seemed like a quiet plea for rationality in a land dedicated to the gods of chance. 

&quot;Did you see that banner?&quot; Emily asked before pressing the button for the 66th floor. &quot;Dr. Conrad. Isn't that the guy?&quot;

&quot;That's the one,&quot; Sharon said confidently, sighing, holding Emily's anxiety. &quot;We'll be in and out. Cole wouldn't let you come if this wasn't basically a vacation.&quot;

&quot;But he'd let you?&quot;

&quot;E, your brother is a hell of a man, but I'd love to see him try to stop me,&quot; Sharon said with a wink and a smile.

They stepped into the elevator. Emily soaked in the chill like the first rain after a drought. Sharon was already over it, her mind on how fabulous they looked and the game to come. Sharon demanded they take bright matching bags. The two settled on gold to complement Sharon's olive green dress and long, full red hair. Emily agreed that black suited her and chose a black sheath dress. It felt to her like a costume, and the idea offered a sense of safety. Emily adjusted her gold bag, looking around the elevator. &quot;You know, we owe a lot to the Informants. They're risking everything.&quot;

Sharon nodded, touching up her lipstick in the elevator mirror. &quot;Yeah, and the Operators out there on the ground. Those guys are doing the heavy lifting.&quot;

Emily leaned back, her mind racing. &quot;I just hope our tech team can keep up. If something goes wrong, we're in trouble.&quot;

Sharon gave a small smile. &quot;They've got it covered. Besides, we have more than just tech support. The whole team is solid.&quot;

The elevator stopped at the lobby when a lone figure joined them inside. A man who looked more like he belonged at a convention for army accountants or in the dark corner of a private escort lounge. Unlike Emily and Sharon, who planned to fit in, this man looked like he wanted to stand out. In the oversized elevator, their guest took a stance dead center. Close enough to Emily that she could feel the heat from his skin. Close enough for her to smell the subtle cologne radiating from his navy blue A Corporation polo. The aroma was distinctly chemical, with a greasy undertone and a hint of sulfur.

&quot;Here to defy the energy crisis?&quot; Emily asked.

&quot;Aren't we all?&quot; he said, trying to make it seem his eyes were set on the door before him and not scanning the woman. &quot;Great speakers today. I'm Greenbaum.&quot;

Emily nodded her head in agreement and stepped to the side. &quot;That's what we hear,&quot; she said, gesturing to the woman beside her. Sharon was everything Emily had ever wanted to be: tall, fit, and sure of herself. A goddess by any standards. Like all Nova women, Emily was average in every way and doomed to remain so. Sharon guided a lock of Emily's curly blonde hair behind her ear, whispered, &quot;Ready for a test run?&quot; and shot a mischievous look in the mirrored paneling, causing Emily to blush.

&quot;I do have another reason for attending,&quot; he said, finally allowing his eyes to meet the women.

&quot;Oh, I'm sure it's important A Corp business,&quot; she said with a carefully timed nod at the logo on his polo. &quot;I bet you're one of the guys who make it all possible, like behind the scenes.&quot;

Sharon held her laughter, but Greenbaum cocked his head and smiled with what could only be the moment of validation he's been waiting his whole life for. &quot;I've been researching ways to bulk up and achieve a more robust natural form. My grandfather was Mr. Olympia, but steroids did to his manhood what the energy crisis has been doing to many men.&quot; He paused. Emily and Sharon stared at him.

&quot;Ah,&quot; Sharon said. &quot;I knew the real work was being done here. They should change the name. Should have called it something like…&quot; Sharon bit her lip to tease a thought. &quot;Something like 'Getting it Up.' Then, at least, we'd know we were all here for the same thing.&quot;

Greenbaum and Emily shared a forced laugh while Sharon remained characteristically composed. With her eyes locked on Greenbaum, the silence stretched taut and uneasy until Sharon nudged Emily hard enough to make the poor girl step forward—again too close to Greenbaum.

&quot;I actually specialize in muscle enhancement,&quot; Emily said. &quot;I've been sort of fixated on it since my diagnosis. My work doesn't help me directly,&quot; she paused to make up the next part of the story. &quot;You could say that I help men be men,&quot; she said, shooting a coy smile that disarmed and excited Greenbaum.

&quot;Really?&quot; he asked, completely unprepared.

&quot;If you're lucky enough to survive the energy crisis, your body's blood flow and fluid distribution are significantly altered. This can lead to reduced venous return and potentially impact the vascular aspects of erectile function. It's really a circulatory dysfunction.&quot;

&quot;Circulatory dysfunction,&quot; he repeated without a shred of understanding.

&quot;That's the same conclusion our research has found. Interesting. I must admit that I have personal reasons for my focus.&quot;

Tapping into a vast database of overheard knowledge or confidently designing complete lies, Emily continued, &quot;Circulatory dysfunction is common in males and females who take extended tours in space. Since the energy crisis tends to mimic the side effects of long-term space travel, our research pulls heavily from observations of Astros who now lack the blood flow necessary for achieving and maintaining a man's 'proper form.'&quot;

&quot;Have you developed methods for testing?&quot;

Surprising herself, Emily felt a cruel delight. &quot;Lucky for you, it's easily diagnosed by a simple physical exam. There are obvious marking spots, but they can be hard to recognize without proper training.&quot;

&quot;Are you accepting new patients?&quot;

&quot;No, unfortunately, we're at full capacity,&quot; Sharon said. &quot;I admire your commitment, though, so I'll do you a favor. I'll examine you right here and now.&quot;

&quot;In an elevator. I can't think of a better place,&quot; he said.

&quot;I need you to lift up your shirt and show me your lower back.&quot;

&quot;My shirt?&quot;

&quot;Go ahead, nothing to be embarrassed about,&quot; Emily said. &quot;We see these things all the time. Go ahead. Turn around.&quot;

&quot;Your shirt, please,&quot; Sharon added with alluring dominance.

His eyes darted in every direction as if looking for an escape as he lifted his shirt from his neatly pressed chinos. In the sterile light of the elevator, Emily and Sharon scrutinized his lean frame, honed from what could only be countless hours of rigorous training, and the women noted the hint of a life less ordinary. The pale skin of his long back revealed a topography of scars and faded bruising—the sort not acquired through clumsy accidents but encounters far more perilous. And from his smirk reflected in the elevator door, he was proud of them—like a newlywed of honeymoon photos or a mortician of a well-dressed corpse. Greenbaum held himself with tense ease. As Sharon politely requested him to loosen his belt for a better look, a brief flash of something—caution perhaps.

&quot;Sure,&quot; he said, and the faint sound of his belt unbuckling filled the compact space. Emily's fingers trailed down his spine with professional detachment. Yet she couldn't help but note the tension in his muscles, a body conditioned for swift, decisive action.

&quot;Sometimes the markings are much lower along the spinal column.&quot; And you might want to get this scoliosis looked at.&quot;

&quot;Just a bit lower,&quot; Sharon said, masking her rising curiosity.

Facing away, his movements deliberate, he revealed nothing more than necessary. &quot;Yes, Ma'ams.&quot; The motion revealed more than skin: a slight shift in his stance, a readiness in how he held himself, even while exposed and vulnerable. It was a subtle hint at his mastery—his control—over his every movement. However, as Sharon and Emily locked eyes to note this to one another, he seemed to lose balance like a newbie in a yoga class.

&quot;Do I have them?&quot; he asked with a trace of urgency.

As Sharon peered closer, her fingers tracing the scars leading to and beyond the fabric of his boxers, the doors dinged and began to open. Sharon delicately pulled his unremarkable underwear down his surprisingly remarkable body and whistled as if calling the attention of the physicians in the 60th-floor lounge. Emily, embarrassed for him, hid herself next to the control panel. Frozen in what could only be shame, Greenbaum tried to pull his shirt over his head and his underwear up, but in the chaos, he fell out of the elevator. As the doors slowly closed between the women and Greenbaum, Sharon reached into her handbag, retrieved a lollipop, bent with a hand on her knee, and extended it to him with a devious smile.

&quot;Dum Dum?&quot;

Emily and Sharon's laughter filled the elevator as they ascended the remaining six floors. As they stepped from the elevator to the deserted lobby with three hallways—each a copy of the other—both women grasped to contain their last lingering chuckles.

&quot;Maybe we shouldn't have pushed it so far,&quot; Emily said.

&quot;That creep would have let us take it farther,&quot; Sharon said with a sharp edge of uncaring reality. &quot;Forget him. You did great. Passed the test! This is Vegas, where the unusual is the norm.&quot;

They navigated the hotel's corridors with casual determination. The plush carpet silenced their steps, and the dim lighting fractured and softened the shadows around them. Turning the corner that stole their view of the lobby but offered an eyeshot of their destination felt to them more like being in a carnival funhouse or some haunted hellscape—mundane, repeating, endless.

Room 6644 appeared before them, undistinguished from the others but for the golden numbers signifying they had arrived as intended. It stood quietly, a door just like any other, unaware of its significance to these women or the world. Sharon knocked, and an ordinary sound rang through the quiet halls like a falling tree would through a canyon.

&quot;Just a minute,&quot; a voice from within said without care or haste.

&quot;Room service,&quot; Emily said softly.

&quot;Nope. Go away. I haven't ordered anything.&quot;

Sharon looked Emily up and down. &quot;What happened to the woman I was with a few minutes ago?&quot; she said, lifting an eyebrow. &quot;Tap back into that. If it helps, think of this guy as just another part of the game.&quot;

With slight hesitation, Emily called out again with confidence that felt inauthentic to her but might stop a charging elephant in its path. &quot;Understood, sir,&quot; Emily said. &quot;Happens all the time. We'll check next door—Should have known a man like you wouldn't order coleslaw on his nova burger.&quot;

Pleased, Sharon gave Emily her proudest smile, large enough to hazard the escape of deep secrets.

&quot;My mistake,&quot; the man said. &quot;I'm coming. Don't go.&quot;

Thundering steps charged toward the women from the other side of the door, and the towering man who opened it looked far more like a bear than a renowned thinker. Here was a man who had lost something. His mind, maybe. Whatever it was, it reminded Emily of Cole and how he had shut himself away. A lost man needing a keeper, and this man seemed to have nothing but his thoughts. Like the man, the room was not a mess but chaotic. More like you'd expect to see from an artist than an academic.

&quot;I expected someone else,&quot; he said.

&quot;Luckily, we didn't, Dr. Conrad,&quot; Emily smiled. &quot;Let's make this quick. It looks like you have a lot on your plate.&quot;

&quot;Please, come in,&quot; he stepped to the side, allowing them in without losing his grip on the door, which forced Sharon to duck under his outstretched arm. &quot;Call me Stephen,&quot; he said, peeking his head into the hallway like he was crossing the street. &quot;Any trouble getting here?&quot;

Emily and Sharon enjoyed a silent chuckle, and Sharon said, &quot;Stephen, we can handle ourselves. Where are the crystals?&quot;

&quot;In the van,&quot; he said. &quot;That's the deal. You get the crystals and the van. But something big is coming. A Corp found something. It's all on here. Nothing matters but this.&quot; Stephen raised his massive hand and twisted figurine attached to the keyring to reveal the thumb drive inside. &quot;If you can stay for tonight's opening, I'll give you a small taste of what's on this drive and why it's so important.&quot; His hands were shaking slightly. &quot;Everything you need is on this drive. A Corp found something big. We need to move fast.&quot;

Emily took the drive, her fingers brushing against his. &quot;We'll get this to Cole. He'll know what to do.&quot;

Stephen's eyes were wide with urgency. &quot;Tell him there's a lock for every key. The Collective is counting on you.&quot;

Sharon looked at Emily, her expression serious. &quot;We might need to call in some help from the Navigators. This sounds huge.&quot;

&quot;I don’t know about any of that. The van key is on there too. It's parked on the third level next to several others, just like it. Just hit the clicker to figure out which is for you,&quot; he said. &quot;Be careful with that ship. The information on there will change everything. Everything. I'll keep doing my part. Make sure you tell them I'm doing my part.&quot;

&quot;Who are they?&quot; Emily asked.

&quot;Just get it to Cole. Nothing else matters anymore. The Collective will handle it from there.&quot;

Emily nodded, slipping the drive into her bag. &quot;Agreed. Let's get moving.&quot;

As they left Stephen's room behind, the air outside seemed to snap back into focus. Emily and Sharon, quickened by a shared urgency, found themselves alongside a group of guests. Both unconsciously raced toward the corner that would offer distance from Stephen's palpable anxiety. The hotel's corridors, usually a backdrop to leisure, escapism, and pleasure, now pulsed with energy tinged with anticipation and danger. As they rounded the corner, the elevator doors dinged, and regret itself stepped through the opening doors. Greenbaum walked directly toward them with strength and resolve. Suddenly grateful for the casual bustle of other guests, Emily and Sharon slowed their pace, allowing the group to close the small gap between them. This was not the man they left in the elevator, floundering after their game. No, this Greenbaum moved with purpose, his demeanor hardened. The transformation sent a shiver down Emily's spine. Sharon, as usual, remained steadfast and composed, and if she were feeling strain or fear, Emily could not sense it.

The space between them closed inch by inch, and Emily's mind raced. As Greenbaum drew nearer, his cologne's chemical sulfuric cloud invaded their senses. It's an olfactory assault that marked his territory long before he physically intruded upon it. The scent wrapped around them, a noxious reminder of the man himself—unavoidable, confronting, and laden with implications. Greenbaum's expression only briefly clashed with Emily's, shifting to Sharon and holding her without reprisal. Greenbaum's eyes, sharp and probing, sought something in Sharon's face—perhaps a reaction or acknowledgment of the power dynamics at play. The smirk curling his lips mirrored the one Sharon had given Emily earlier. Without engaging, Emily and Sharon continued on. The memory of their last encounter hung between them. With the cover of their impromptu group, he passed them without an outburst or incident. Emily felt the air rush back to fill the void left by his presence. The encounter left a residue that clung to her thoughts.

As they stepped out of the casino, Emily froze, her eyes widening at the sight before her. The once bare walls were now plastered with dozens of black droplets. They overlapped in places, creating a chaotic, dense pattern that stretched as far as she could see. The fresh graffiti glistened under the dim streetlights, giving the alley an eerie, almost pulsating glow.

&quot;Sharon, look at this,&quot; Emily whispered, her voice tinged with awe and apprehension.

Sharon followed her gaze, taking in the scene with a sharp intake of breath. &quot;Wow. They’ve been busy.&quot;

The droplets covered every available surface—walls, dumpsters, and even the ground. They varied in size, some small and discrete, others large and dominating, but all of them blending into a tapestry of defiance and unity. The overlapping symbols showed the persistence and determination of the Collective, each one adding to the layers of rebellion.

Emily moved closer, her heart pounding as she traced the edge of a droplet with her finger. The paint was still wet, smudging slightly under her touch. &quot;There are so many... It’s like they’re shouting from the shadows.&quot;

&quot;Yeah,&quot; Sharon murmured, her eyes scanning the alley for any movement. &quot;This isn’t just a message. It’s a statement. They’re everywhere, and they’re not hiding anymore.&quot;

Sharon turned to Emily, determination blazing in her eyes. &quot;We’ve got this, E. We just need to get this info to Cole. He’ll figure it out.&quot;

They continued walking, the graffiti symbols a stark reminder of the resistance and the growing unrest. The night felt heavier, the stakes higher as they made their way through the shadowy streets, knowing they were part of something much larger than themselves.

&quot;I have a feeling all your contacts are a bit strange,&quot; Emily said.

&quot;They wouldn't be mixed up with us if they hadn't been through some stuff,&quot; Sharon said. &quot;What can I say? I like my people like I like my toast—slightly burnt.&quot;

&quot;No wonder you're with Cole,&quot; Emily said softly, as though she were speaking of a rescue dog who had finally found a home after years of abuse and abandonment.

&quot;Your brother doesn't burn, E. He is the fire,&quot; Sharon said. &quot;Which reminds me, do you mind driving all night? I'd like to stop at the farmer's market before we get home.&quot;

As they drove, the city lights faded into behind them. The rhythmic hum of the van's engine soothed Emily. Sharon's eyes were fixed on the road ahead, her thoughts a whirlwind of plans and contingencies. Sitting in the passenger seat, Emily stared out the window, her mind replaying the encounter with Stephen and the ominous warning about the Collective, who had become a shadowy presence in their lives since the Saturn disaster. A network of regular people working to undermine A Corp's oppressive grip. Their symbol was becomming a more common sight. Graffiti of the symbol adorned alley walls and abandoned buildings. 

Sharon nodded. &quot;The Collective is everywhere. They're the only reason we have any intel on A Corp. The Informants, Jr. Operators, Sr. Operators, Lead Operators, Jr. Technicians, Sr. Technicians, and Lead Technicians have been particularly active. They've warned us that something big is coming.&quot;

Emily shivered. &quot;Do you think it's related to what Stephen said? That A Corp found something?&quot;

Sharon's grip on the steering wheel tightened. &quot;It has to be. We need to get this information to Cole as soon as possible. The Collective's network is our best chance of staying one step ahead.&quot;

They drove silently for a while, the weight of their mission pressing down on them. As they crossed the state line, Emily thought about the ranks within the Collective. 

&quot;Let's grab some supplies and then head home. We need to be ready for whatever comes next.&quot;

The morning sun began to rise, casting long shadows over the market stalls. Vendors were just setting up, the smell of fresh produce and baked goods filling the air. As they walked through the market, Emily couldn't help but feel a sense of urgency. The miles passed in a blur, the road stretching endlessly before them. Emily's thoughts turned to Cole, hoping he was safe and they could get the information to him in time. The Collective's ranks and the symbols they left behind reminded them that they were not alone in this fight. They finally reached a small farmer's market outside their hometown as dawn approached. Sharon parked the car and turned to Emily.

As they approached their hometown, the familiar sights brought a small measure of comfort. Still, the weight of their mission remained heavy. In the quiet moments before dawn entirely broke, Emily knew she could trust her friend at The Federation.

Sharon turned to Emily. &quot;I can’t wait to get home.&quot;

Emily nodded, taking a deep breath. &quot;Let's hope they're right again. Do you think Cole can make sense of all this?&quot; 

“He’s been through worse, E. He'll figure it out. We just have to trust him.&quot;

Emily nodded, but the unease in her stomach remained. The information they carried was just the beginning. There would be more challenges ahead, more secrets to uncover, and more dangers to face. But as long as they had each other and the support of the Collective, Emily knew they would find a way to overcome. Emily took a deep breath, feeling the weight of their mission settle on her shoulders. She was ready. They were all ready.

They had to be.
