









Rings of the Reich


Prologue: Seeds of Chaos


The Federation’s secret labs were always a hotbed for experiments that pushed the boundaries of ethics and sanity. Deep within the asteroid belt, on the hidden base designated Facility 42, the most outrageous of these experiments took place. It was here that Von Grutzen, the bloated and maniacal leader of Faction 42, first laid eyes on his two greatest creations: the Space Squirrels and the Sexy Sharons.


But before Facility 42 became his stronghold, Von Grutzen was simply Wilhelm, the sickly and awkward child of a Federation mechanic and a failed artist. Born on a backwater mining colony, Wilhelm endured constant bullying for his frail frame and peculiar obsession with tinkering. While other children dreamed of becoming pilots or explorers, Wilhelm spent his days building rudimentary robots from salvaged scraps, his dreams centered on creating something—anything—that would make him matter.


By his teenage years, Wilhelm’s intellect had outpaced everyone around him, but his bitterness grew just as quickly. When a Federation-sponsored talent scout arrived on the colony, Wilhelm saw his chance to escape. Accepted into the prestigious Haverick Institute of Advanced Engineering, he left his home planet with a fire in his heart—a determination to rise above the mediocrity that had defined his upbringing.


At the Institute, Wilhelm excelled academically but remained socially isolated. His peers mocked his eccentricities, dubbing him &quot;Grutzen the Gray&quot; for his drab clothing and dour demeanor. A humiliating incident involving an experimental drone that malfunctioned during a demonstration—exploding and covering the audience in sewage—cemented his reputation as an outcast. It was here that his hatred for the Federation began to fester. Despite his brilliance, he saw the system as corrupt, designed to reward the popular and well-connected while discarding geniuses like him.


After graduation, Wilhelm found work as a low-level engineer for the Federation’s sprawling bureaucracy. Frustrated and disillusioned, he began siphoning resources and data from classified projects. His big break came when he uncovered the plans for a highly secretive bioengineering experiment—one that involved enhancing animal intelligence and creating humanoid clones. Seeing its potential, Wilhelm stole the data and fled, narrowly escaping capture.


In the lawless regions of space, Wilhelm reinvented himself. Adopting the moniker &quot;Heavy Hitler,&quot; a name he claimed embodied the weight of his ambitions and the iron fist with which he would rule, he built his reputation as a rogue scientist and tactician. He began gathering a following among outcasts, mercenaries, and disenfranchised scientists who shared his disdain for the Federation. Facility 42 became his sanctuary, a place where his genius could flourish without restraint.


Von Grutzen’s rise to power was anything but smooth. Rival factions tried to eliminate him, but his cunning and ruthlessness saw him through. He struck deals with smugglers, blackmailed Federation officials, and sold experimental weapons to Alpha Corp, always staying one step ahead of his enemies. Over time, his forces grew, and his vision expanded. No longer content with mere survival, Von Grutzen aimed to reshape the galaxy, bending it to his will through Faction 42.


It was during these years that he first encountered Sharon Teez. Their paths crossed during a high-stakes negotiation on a neutral asteroid. At the time, Von Grutzen believed Sharon could be swayed to join his cause. Her sharp wit and unwavering confidence captivated him, and for the first time in years, he felt something resembling admiration. Emboldened by her seemingly playful demeanor, he made his intentions clear—both professionally and romantically.


Sharon’s rejection was swift and merciless. &quot;I’d rather align with a malfunctioning toaster,&quot; she said, her words cutting deeper than any blade. Her dismissal, witnessed by his closest advisors, was a blow to his pride he would never forget.


Now, with the power to clone her, Von Grutzen saw an opportunity to rewrite history. The Sexy Sharons were not merely a weapon; they were his attempt to recreate and control the one person who had defied him.












Chapter One: Facility 42


Hidden deep within the asteroid belt, Facility 42 was more than just a lab—it was the nerve center of Faction 42, a thriving microcosm of chaos and control. Encased in layers of reinforced titanium and cloaked by electromagnetic interference fields, the base seemed to hum with the weight of its importance. To Von Grutzen, it was more than a hideout; it was the crucible in which his legacy, and Faction 42&apos;s vision, would be forged.


The asteroid belt surrounding Facility 42 was both a shield and a sword. The treacherous field served as a natural labyrinth, confusing any ship that dared approach. Von Grutzen had further enhanced the defenses by placing mines disguised as asteroids, each equipped with advanced cloaking technology. Few who entered the belt ever emerged.


The facility itself was a marvel of engineering. Its sprawling layout included multiple wings for research, training, and military operations. The walls were lined with conduits that pulsed with energy, feeding power to the labs and defenses. Above all, the command center stood as the brain of Facility 42, a circular room filled with holographic displays that allowed Von Grutzen to monitor every corner of his domain.


Gathering his followers had been no simple task. Von Grutzen’s early recruits were mercenaries—men and women who cared little for ideology but saw his growing operation as a means to wealth and survival. Among them was Captain Blitz, a grizzled veteran who had fought in countless skirmishes across the galaxy. Blitz had initially joined out of desperation, fleeing a failed mutiny against Alpha Corp. Over time, his pragmatism and military expertise made him an indispensable part of Facility 42’s hierarchy.


Then came the scientists. Disillusioned former Federation researchers like Dr. Neumann found refuge in Faction 42. Neumann, known for his brilliance and eccentricity, was drawn to the freedom Facility 42 offered—no ethics committees, no bureaucratic oversight. But his clashes with Blitz were legendary. Neumann saw the soldiers as brutes, incapable of appreciating the elegance of science, while Blitz viewed the scientists as reckless liabilities. Von Grutzen often played mediator, his authoritarian charisma keeping the team from splintering.


The Space Squirrels were another source of tension. Enhanced with intelligence and jetpack technology, they were both a marvel and a menace. Their leader, Squeaks, was fiercely loyal to Faction 42 but had little respect for the humans who dismissed the squirrels as mere tools. Squeaks’ acts of sabotage—often for amusement—fueled resentment among the staff. Yet even Blitz begrudgingly admitted that the squirrels were invaluable for infiltration missions.


Despite their differences, the group remained united by a common enemy: the Federation. Each member, from Blitz to Neumann to Squeaks, had been wronged by the system. For all their disagreements, they shared a singular purpose—to dismantle the establishment that had cast them aside. Von Grutzen’s promises of power and revenge kept them loyal, even as tempers flared and rivalries simmered.


Meanwhile, the mirrored training chamber adjacent to the lab echoed with the sounds of combat. The Sexy Sharons, clad in sleek black bodysuits, practiced their routines with deadly precision. Each clone moved in perfect synchronization, but their vanity often got in the way. &quot;Your form is off,&quot; one clone said, glaring at another. &quot;You’re making us all look bad.&quot;


The criticized clone rolled her eyes. &quot;Oh, please. You’re just jealous because my hair looks better.&quot;


Dr. Neumann, observing from behind a one-way mirror, muttered under his breath. &quot;Perfect soldiers, my foot. More like divas with guns.&quot;


In the command center, Blitz monitored the base’s operations. His fingers danced across the holographic interface as he updated patrol routes and reviewed intercepted transmissions. The air crackled with tension as he prepared to deliver his latest report to Von Grutzen.


&quot;Alpha Corp’s been sniffing around Sector 12 again,&quot; Blitz began. &quot;And we intercepted chatter from the Collective about a shipment of DarkX crystals.&quot;


Von Grutzen, seated in his reinforced hover-throne, leaned forward. His eyes glinted with a mix of ambition and malice. &quot;Good. We’ll strike before they can act. The crystals will be ours. What of the Falcon crew?&quot;


Blitz hesitated, choosing his words carefully. &quot;They’re on a transport mission, but their route takes them dangerously close to our operations.&quot;


Dr. Neumann scoffed from across the room, adjusting his goggles. &quot;The Falcon crew? You’re wasting your time worrying about a band of misfits. They’re a joke compared to the might of Faction 42.&quot;


Blitz’s tone turned sharper, his frustration evident. &quot;A joke? They’ve outmaneuvered Federation patrols, outsmarted Alpha Corp, and survived skirmishes that would leave most crews in pieces. Underestimating them is the kind of mistake that gets operations compromised.&quot;


&quot;Outmaneuvered by luck, not skill,&quot; Neumann replied with a dismissive wave. &quot;Brute force and bluster only go so far. Science will always triumph.&quot;


Squeaks, perched atop a console, let out a series of chittering laughs. The leader of the Space Squirrels tapped his tiny claws on the console, then activated his translator. &quot;Humans love underdogs. Makes them reckless. Easy to predict. Easy to trap.&quot;


Blitz shot the squirrel a glare. &quot;Predictable doesn’t mean harmless. If they’ve gotten this far, it’s because they’re smarter than you think.&quot;


Squeaks tapped his claws again, bringing up a holographic map of the Falcon crew’s projected trajectory. &quot;Chatty humans. This one talks about needing parts,&quot; he said, motioning to a flickering transmission log. &quot;Broken ship. Weak prey.&quot;


Dr. Neumann snorted. &quot;Or bait. Don’t forget that Federation tactics are far more sophisticated than the bluster of a mercenary ship. If we bite too quickly, we might be playing into their hands.&quot;


Blitz crossed his arms. &quot;So we ignore them? Wait until they stumble onto our doorstep? That’s reckless.&quot;


Von Grutzen’s voice cut through their bickering like a blade. &quot;Enough. Both of you. Whether it’s the Federation, Alpha Corp, or this Falcon crew, they all underestimate us. Facility 42 is untouchable.&quot; He turned to the holographic map, staring intently at the Falcon crew’s trajectory. &quot;We’ll observe for now. Let them get comfortable. If they stray too close, we’ll ensure they regret it.&quot;


Blitz glanced at Von Grutzen. &quot;And Sharon Teez? She’s part of their crew. Their tactician.&quot;


Von Grutzen’s jaw tightened, his gaze growing colder. &quot;Sharon Teez is irrelevant. A relic of poor choices. She’ll regret the day she dismissed me.&quot;


An uneasy silence followed, the tension palpable. Squeaks broke it with another chitter, his mechanical tail flicking. &quot;Humans overthink. Squirrels? We strike. Quick. Efficient.&quot;


Blitz smirked faintly. &quot;And reckless.&quot;


Squeaks tilted his head. &quot;Effective.&quot;


As the room settled, Von Grutzen hovered to the observation window, his attention shifting to the vast expanse of the asteroid field. His quarters, filled with trophies and holograms of past victories, exuded a sense of his grandiose ambition. On one wall, a hologram of Sharon Teez flickered mid-smirk. He stared at it, his expression flickering between resentment and obsession.


Facility 42 was more than a fortress. It was his kingdom, a testament to his genius and defiance against the galaxy that had dismissed him. Every decision, every trap, every ally, and every enemy was part of his plan to dominate.


&quot;Soon,&quot; he murmured, his voice barely audible. &quot;Soon, the galaxy will bow before me.&quot;











Chapter Two: Seeds of the Reich


The Falcon glided through the black expanse, its matte hull absorbing the faint light of distant stars. Inside the cramped control room, Cole hunched over the console, studying the readings. A soft hum filled the air, punctuated by the occasional chirp of Gracie, the ship’s AI.


“We’re nearing the marker,” Gracie announced, her tone laced with sarcasm. “Another breathtaking pile of rocks awaits.”


Cole sighed. “Just keep us on course. Sharon, what’s the status on those cargo hold repairs?”


Seated cross-legged on the floor, Sharon Teez looked up from a disassembled blaster she was meticulously cleaning. “It’ll hold for now, but the system’s overdue for a complete overhaul. Any chance your grand plan involves funding?”


“That depends,” Cole replied with a smirk. “You think pirates accept IOUs?”


Ricardo Corazon strolled into the control room, wiping his hands on an oil-streaked rag. His tousled hair and relaxed grin contrasted with the tension simmering in the cabin. “Forget IOUs,” he quipped, tossing the rag over his shoulder. “What we really need is a miracle—or another cache of spare parts. Preferably the kind that doesn’t come with laser turrets attached.”


“Fixing miracles is your department,” Cole shot back with a faint grin.


Ricardo clapped him on the shoulder as he leaned over the console. “The department’s running low on inventory. But I got the starboard stabilizer patched up. Won’t hold forever, though. So let’s not get blown up before lunch, yeah?”


Emily, the ship’s youngest crew member, piped up from her station. “We might not need to worry about pirates if Gracie’s right. There’s something off about these readings.”


“Define ‘off,’” Sharon said, standing and moving to Emily’s station.


Emily pointed at the display. “There’s a faint signal—encrypted. It’s not Federation, not Alpha Corp. Something... different.”


Sharon’s eyes narrowed. “Could be scavengers or a decoy. Either way, we stay sharp.”


Ricardo slid into a nearby seat, frowning as he examined the data. “That encryption’s old-school but layered. Whoever sent it knows their way around a signal scrambler. Question is, are they friendly, or are we about to stumble into another trap?”


“Gracie, keep tracking it. And let me know if it changes course,” Cole ordered.


“You got it, fearless leader,” Gracie quipped. “Just don’t blame me if it’s a trap.”


The ship fell silent as the crew settled into their tasks. Sharon holstered her blaster and moved toward the navigation console, her boots clinking lightly against the floor panels. Emily tapped at her station, her brow furrowed as she analyzed the encrypted signal. Cole paced behind them, hands on his hips, his eyes flicking between the console and the main display.


Ricardo leaned back in his chair, tossing a small wrench between his hands. “You know,” he said, “for once, I’d like to find something shiny that doesn’t come with strings attached. Maybe a ship-sized stash of gold dust or a perfectly functional hyperdrive.”


Sharon smirked. “And maybe pigs will start flying.”


“They’d have to build spacesuits first,” Ricardo shot back, earning a chuckle from Emily.





From the engine bay, a metallic clatter echoed through the ship. Moments later, Bitboy Leroy, their six-limbed robotic monkey hybrid, appeared in the doorway. His mismatched eyes—one organic, one a glowing red sensor—blinked rapidly as he dragged a bundle of tangled wires and spare parts behind him.


“Fix now,” Bitboy signed with his upper limbs, his mechanical claws clicking together for emphasis. “Ship weak.”


Ricardo shook his head, a grin tugging at his lips. “Bitboy, you’re the only one working harder than me. You want my job?”


Bitboy chittered softly, signing back: “Humans slow. Machines better. Duct tape helps.”


Sharon raised an eyebrow. “Join the club, Bitboy. We’re all running on fumes.”


Cole let out a short laugh. “Thanks for the insight. How’s the engine holding up?”


Bitboy’s claws whirred as he signed, “Holding. For now.” His mechanical tail twitched, wrapping around a loose cable as he added, “Need more parts. Humans slow.”


“Noted,” Cole said with a wry smile. “Just keep us in one piece, alright?”


Bitboy nodded, disappearing back into the engine bay with a clatter.





Hours later, the Falcon drifted into the orbit of a jagged asteroid. Its surface was pocked with craters and lined with faint, unnatural grooves. The encrypted signal pulsed stronger here, emanating from deep within the asteroid’s shadow.


“This doesn’t feel right,” Emily said, her voice barely above a whisper. She clutched her tablet, scanning the signal’s fluctuations. “It’s too regular. Like someone’s guiding it.”


“Good instincts,” Sharon said, securing her holster. “Cole, what’s the play?”


Cole studied the asteroid, his jaw tight. “We’re not leaving empty-handed. Gracie, prepare a probe. Let’s see what’s down there.”


“Deploying the trusty tin can,” Gracie replied as a small, sleek probe launched from the Falcon’s belly. It zipped toward the asteroid, its cameras relaying a live feed.


On-screen, the probe’s lights illuminated a narrow fissure leading into the asteroid’s core. The walls gleamed faintly with metal—a clear sign of artificial construction.


“Definitely not natural,” Sharon muttered. “Someone’s been here.”


“Or still is,” Emily added, her fingers tightening around the edge of her console.


The probe continued deeper, its path narrowing until it reached a large, hollowed-out chamber. At the center sat a cluster of dark, angular structures, their surfaces etched with unfamiliar symbols. The encrypted signal’s source pulsed from within the largest structure.


“What in the…” Cole began, but he was cut off as the probe’s feed crackled and distorted. Gracie’s voice cut in sharply.


“We’ve got company! Two ships incoming, fast.”





The Chase in the Asteroid Belt


The Falcon surged forward, darting beneath the jagged shadows of the asteroid field as the two enemy ships closed in. The ship’s engines roared under the strain, each maneuver pushing the Falcon’s thrusters to their limit. Gracie’s voice cut through the tension, sharp and urgent.


“They’re not just fast—they’re smart,” she snapped. “They’re predicting our movements, Cole.”


“Then we get unpredictable,” Cole shot back. “Sharon, Ricardo, we need suppressive fire! Emily, give me full power to the thrusters. Gracie, plot the most chaotic course you can through this mess.”


“I’m a fan of chaos,” Gracie quipped, “but don’t blame me if we end up as asteroid paste.”


As the Falcon banked hard, Ricardo leaned into the targeting console, his fingers flying over the controls. “Locking onto the lead ship,” he muttered. “They’re closing too fast.”


“Let’s fix that,” Sharon said, her tone cold and focused. She unleashed a flurry of plasma bursts that streaked through the void, forcing the enemy ship to veer sharply.


The first interceptor retaliated, its lasers blazing in rapid succession. Beams of energy sliced through the asteroid field, one grazing the Falcon’s hull with a deafening screech. Sparks flew from an overhead panel, and Emily’s panicked voice rang out over the comms.


“That hit took our shields down to fifteen percent!” she shouted. “We can’t keep taking hits like that.”


“Then we won’t,” Cole barked. “Ricardo, do you have a shot?”


“Not yet,” Ricardo replied, his jaw clenched as he tracked the enemy’s movements. “They’re weaving between the debris, using the asteroids for cover.”


Sharon’s eyes narrowed. “Two can play that game.” She toggled the Falcon’s targeting system, locking onto a massive asteroid ahead. With a calculated burst from the ship’s cannons, the asteroid shattered into a hail of jagged fragments, scattering directly into the enemy’s path.


The lead interceptor swerved to avoid the debris, but one of the fragments clipped its wing. It spiraled briefly before regaining control, its pilot clearly rattled. The second ship, however, seized the opening and unleashed a barrage of laser fire.





A Narrow Miss


Inside the Falcon, alarms blared as the lasers carved dangerously close to the ship’s engines. Bitboy Leroy scurried into the control room, his mechanical limbs clinking as he signed frantically: “Engine overheating. Stabilizers failing. Immediate fix needed!”


“Fix it fast, Bitboy!” Cole shouted. “We’re not going down here!”


The robotic monkey hybrid let out an irritated chitter, darting back into the engine bay. Sparks flew as he pried open a panel, his claws working with mechanical precision to reroute power and reinforce the thrusters.


Ricardo’s voice broke through the chaos. “I’ve got a shot on the second ship! Teez, cover me.”


Sharon nodded, her focus unshakable. “Firing suppressive bursts. Make it count, Corazon.”


As Sharon unleashed a volley of plasma rounds, Ricardo targeted the second interceptor’s vulnerable undercarriage. With a controlled exhale, he pressed the trigger. The Falcon’s main cannon roared, sending a searing bolt of energy straight into the enemy ship. The shot hit true, tearing through the interceptor’s lower hull.


“Direct hit!” Ricardo shouted as the ship erupted in flames, spiraling into the side of a massive asteroid. The impact sent shockwaves through the debris field, rattling the Falcon.


“One down,” Sharon said, her tone sharp but steady. “Now let’s deal with the other one.”





The Final Battle


The remaining interceptor was relentless, its pilot determined to take the Falcon down. It ducked and weaved through the asteroid field, closing the distance with each pass. The ship’s lasers burned with fierce intensity, forcing the Falcon to twist and roll to avoid the onslaught.


“They’re sticking too close,” Gracie warned. “I can’t shake them!”


“Then let’s use their arrogance against them,” Cole said, his voice calm despite the chaos. “Gracie, decelerate on my mark. Sharon, Ricardo, prepare for a counterattack.”


Ricardo’s hands tightened on the controls. “We’re playing chicken with a fully armed interceptor? Love it. Let’s do this.”


“Ready,” Sharon said, her finger hovering over the trigger.


The Falcon’s engines suddenly dropped to half power, and the ship slowed just enough to throw the interceptor off balance. The enemy ship swerved to avoid a collision, exposing its flank.


“Now!” Cole shouted.


Sharon and Ricardo fired in unison, their shots striking the interceptor’s exposed side. The combined blasts tore through its armor, igniting its fuel cells. The ship exploded in a brilliant flash, the debris scattering harmlessly into the void.





Aftermath


Inside the Falcon, the crew erupted into a mix of cheers and relieved laughter. Ricardo leaned back in his seat, running a hand through his hair. “Two ships, no problem,” he said with a lopsided grin. “Who’s up for a victory toast?”


“We’re not out of the woods yet,” Cole said, though a faint smile tugged at his lips. “Gracie, plot a course away from the debris field. Emily, check for any remaining signals. Sharon, make sure the weapons are ready for round two, just in case.”


Bitboy Leroy reappeared, his claws clicking together as he signed: “Engines stable. Humans owe me fuel. And snacks.”


Sharon let out a low laugh, patting the mechanical monkey on the head. “Nice work, Bitboy. We’ll put your snacks on the tab.”


As the Falcon glided into the safety of the asteroid shadows, Cole glanced around at his crew. They were battered, exhausted, but alive. Whatever lay ahead, he knew they were ready for it.








The Falcon emerged from the chaos, battered but victorious. Ricardo leaned back in his chair, exhaling sharply. “Next time, let’s pick an asteroid with less company.”


Cole grinned faintly. “I’ll see what I can do.”


As the Falcon disappeared into the shadows, Ricardo glanced at Cole. “Whatever’s out here, we’ll face it. Together.”



































Chapter Three: Shadows of the Belt


The Falcon sailed through the asteroid belt, its sleek design gliding effortlessly past the jagged rocks and debris that littered the void. Inside, tension from the recent skirmish lingered in the air, thick and unspoken. The faint hum of the engines was the only sound as the crew processed the events in their own ways.


“How’s the scan coming along?” Cole asked, his voice breaking the uneasy silence.


Emily glanced up from her console, her fingers working furiously to decrypt the intercepted pirate transmission. “Slow. Whatever encryption they’re using isn’t standard. But I’m starting to piece together fragments.”


“Any chance those fragments tell us who hired them?” Sharon asked, leaning against the bulkhead, arms crossed. Her sharp gaze never left Emily.


“Not yet,” Emily admitted. “But there’s something odd about the signal. It’s bouncing off multiple sources. Almost like…” She trailed off, her brow furrowing.


“Like someone’s covering their tracks,” Cole finished for her.


Emily nodded. “Exactly. Whoever sent those pirates didn’t want to be found.”


“That’s comforting,” Gracie chimed in, her tone dripping with sarcasm. “Nothing like a mystery puppet master to spice up our day.”


Cole ignored the AI’s commentary and turned to Sharon. “Weapons status?”


“Fully operational,” Sharon replied. “But if we take another hit like the last one, our shields might not hold. We need to avoid combat until we can reinforce them.”


Cole nodded. “Noted. Emily, keep working on that signal. I want to know what we’re walking into.”


“You got it,” Emily said, her focus already back on the console.


From the crew quarters, Ricardo Corazon strode into the room, a confident smirk playing on his lips as he adjusted his worn leather jacket. “Sounds like we’re living dangerously again,” he remarked, his tone teasing. “What’s the plan, Cole? Keep running and hope the bad guys don’t catch up?”


“Got any better ideas, Corazon?” Cole shot back, his tone light but edged with familiarity.


“Plenty,” Ricardo said, leaning casually against the wall. “But none of them involve sitting here twiddling my thumbs while Emily stares at a screen.”


Emily rolled her eyes but couldn’t suppress a small grin. “Twiddling your thumbs is about all you’re good for.”


Ricardo placed a hand over his heart, mock-wounded. “You wound me, Em.”


Sharon smirked from her post. “Keep it down, lovebirds. Some of us are trying to stay alert.”


Ricardo chuckled, then turned to Cole. “Alright, seriously. If we’re walking into a trap, I’d rather know sooner than later. What’s the status on that signal?”


Emily sighed. “I told you—it’s not simple. This isn’t some amateur encryption. Whoever did this knows what they’re doing.”


“Well, they haven’t met you yet,” Ricardo said with a wink. “You’ll crack it.”





The Derelict Station


As the Falcon ventured deeper into the belt, the asteroids grew denser, their surfaces glinting with traces of ore and ice. The unease among the crew deepened with each passing moment. Gracie broke the silence again.


“Heads up, team. I’m picking up faint power signatures in the area. Could be debris, but…”


“Could be something else,” Sharon finished, moving to the weapons console. “Cole, your call.”


Cole frowned, studying the holographic map projected in front of him. The power signatures were faint, scattered across the field, but their irregular spacing suggested something more deliberate than random wreckage.


“Gracie, take us in slow,” he said. “Let’s not spook anyone—or anything.”


“Creeping forward,” Gracie replied, the Falcon’s engines lowering to a soft purr as the ship eased toward the closest signature.


Minutes passed in tense silence. The ship glided between looming asteroids, their craggy surfaces casting long shadows that seemed to stretch and twist in the dim starlight. Sharon’s fingers hovered near the weapon controls, her eyes scanning every corner of the viewport.


“I don’t like this,” she muttered. “It’s too quiet.”


“Quiet’s better than noisy,” Cole replied, though his tone lacked conviction. “Emily, anything new on the scans?”


“Still faint,” Emily said. “But the signal’s consistent. If it’s debris, it’s active debris.”


“Active debris? That’s a comforting thought,” Gracie quipped. “Next stop, haunted wreckage.”


“Lovely,” Ricardo said, his hand resting lightly on his holster. “Maybe we’ll run into a ghost pirate. Could spice things up.”


As the Falcon rounded a large asteroid, the source of the signal came into view: a derelict mining station embedded in the side of a massive rock. Its structure was battered and covered in frost, but faint lights flickered in its depths, suggesting some systems were still operational.


“Looks abandoned,” Sharon said, narrowing her eyes. “But those lights say otherwise.”


“Scavengers?” Emily suggested. “Or another trap?”


“Only one way to find out,” Cole said. “Gracie, keep scanning for activity. Sharon, prep the boarding gear. Emily, stay on comms and monitor for anything unusual.”


Ricardo stepped forward. “I’m coming too.”


Cole hesitated. “We need you here in case things go sideways.”


“Cole, I’m not sitting around while you and Sharon play space detectives,” Ricardo said firmly. “Besides, you’ll need someone who knows how to sweet-talk a broken console.”


Cole studied him for a moment before nodding. “Fine. But stay sharp.”





Exploring the Station


The Falcon’s boarding hatch extended toward the mining station’s airlock, connecting with a dull, reverberating clang. The sound echoed in the stillness, setting the tone for the tense mission ahead. Sharon, Cole, and Ricardo stood at the ready, weapons drawn, while Emily monitored the connection from the cockpit.


“Airlock’s cycling,” Sharon reported, her voice steady but low. A faint hiss filled the chamber as the old mechanisms groaned to life. “Atmosphere’s thin but breathable.”


“Good enough,” Cole said, stepping forward as the hatch slid open with a reluctant screech. The corridor beyond was dimly lit, its walls coated with frost and grime. Exposed pipes lined the ceiling, dripping condensation that pooled in uneven rivulets along the metal floor.


“Stay close,” Sharon murmured, her weapon raised, eyes scanning every shadow.


The air felt heavy, a mix of stale oxygen and metallic tang clinging to their lungs. Each footstep echoed unnervingly in the confined space, amplifying the sense of isolation. Ricardo’s eyes darted around, his hand hovering near his holstered weapon.


“Charming place,” Ricardo quipped, though his voice was quieter than usual. “Think it comes with a welcome mat?”


“Stay focused,” Sharon replied sharply. Her eyes flicked to a flickering light overhead that cast jittery shadows along the corridor walls. “This place feels wrong.”


Cole knelt by a set of scorched marks streaked across the floor. He ran a gloved hand over the jagged grooves, frowning. “Looks like there was a fight here.”


“A fight or worse,” Sharon said, pointing her weapon at a nearby door. Its surface was dented inward, as though something—or someone—had been trying to break out. A faint trail of what looked like dried blood streaked the wall beside it.


“Comforting,” Ricardo muttered, his voice laced with sarcasm. “Anyone else feel like we’re walking into someone’s bad memory?”


From the Falcon, Gracie’s voice crackled through their earpieces. “Hate to interrupt your sightseeing tour, but I’m picking up faint power signatures ahead. Something’s still alive in there.”


“That makes one of us,” Ricardo muttered under his breath, earning a glare from Sharon.


They moved deeper into the station, the corridor narrowing as the walls grew increasingly cluttered with exposed wiring and shattered panels. The faint hum of electricity coursed through the structure, an intermittent buzz breaking the otherwise oppressive silence.


Sharon stopped abruptly, holding up a fist. The group froze. A faint sound echoed ahead—soft, rhythmic, almost like breathing. Ricardo raised an eyebrow, mouthing silently, “What is that?”


“Could be a ventilation system,” Cole whispered, though he didn’t sound convinced.


Sharon motioned them forward. The group crept toward a branching corridor where a faint light flickered erratically. Ricardo’s pulse quickened as he peered around the corner, his heart sinking at the sight before him.


A humanoid figure slumped against the wall, motionless. Its body was half-buried under debris, with wires snaking out from what remained of its chest. Sparks sputtered faintly from its mechanical limbs. It was a scavenger drone, long deactivated but frozen in a pose that seemed almost defensive.


“Looks like someone took this one apart,” Ricardo murmured, kneeling to inspect the damage. He grimaced as he noticed the jagged tears running through its plating. “And not gently.”


Sharon pulled him back sharply. “Don’t touch it. If it’s rigged to blow, we’re all toast.”


Ricardo stood, dusting his hands off. “Fair point. You always ruin my fun.”

 Their leader…” She glanced nervously at Cole.


“Spit it out,” Cole said, his tone firm.


“Their leader is someone named Von Grutzen,” Emily continued. “He’s a rogue scientist, infamous for experimenting on… well, everything. Bioengineering, robotics, weaponized clones. He’s the reason we’ve been targeted.”


Sharon’s reaction was immediate. Her jaw clenched, and her fists tightened at her sides. “Von Grutzen,” she said, the name dripping with venom. “That arrogant bastard.”


Cole glanced at her, surprised. “Sharon, you know him?”


“Know him?” she echoed bitterly. “We crossed paths during a negotiation gone wrong. He thought he could buy my loyalty—offered me a place in his twisted experiments.”


Emily looked horrified. “What happened?”


Sharon’s gaze turned distant, her voice colder than before. “I turned him down. Publicly. Humiliated him in front of his advisors. Let’s just say, he didn’t take rejection well.”


Ricardo leaned forward, his voice low but steady. “That’s leverage. People like Von Grutzen don’t forget insults like that. It’s not just a grudge—it’s obsession. We can use it.”


Bitboy’s claws clattered as he signed: “Dangerous man. High threat. Must prepare.”


Cole leaned back, exhaling sharply. “Then this isn’t just about the signals. It’s personal.”


“No,” Sharon said grimly. “It’s about control. Von Grutzen doesn’t care about people, just power. And if he’s chasing us this hard, it’s because he sees us as a threat.”


Emily glanced between them, her voice quiet but firm. “I’ll keep digging. If there’s a weakness in their operation, we’ll find it.”





Reflections Around the Table


The crew gathered around the small galley table, the hum of the ship’s life support system their only companion. Sharon had a steaming cup of something that smelled vaguely like coffee, while Emily nursed a nutrient bar. Cole leaned forward, hands clasped together, his expression solemn. Bitboy perched on the floor nearby, his claws busily repairing a broken drone. Ricardo sat back, his fingers drumming softly against the table as he watched the crew.


“We’ve been running for too long,” Cole began. “It’s time we started thinking about what this is really costing us. Not just in resources, but in… everything else.”


Sharon’s eyes narrowed. “You mean Faction 42. Von Grutzen and his goons aren’t just chasing us for sport. They’re after something. And I’d bet my last round of ammo it’s tied to those signals we intercepted.”


Emily hesitated before speaking. “It’s more than that. He has a grudge against you, doesn’t he?”


Sharon set her cup down, her expression unflinching. “He’s the type to hold onto things. I embarrassed him. In his world, that’s unforgivable.”


Ricardo leaned forward, his gaze steady. “And unforgivable mistakes make people reckless. If we can bait him, we might get him to overextend.”


Bitboy paused his work, raising his head. He signed: “Grudge fuels mistakes. Use against him.”


Cole looked at Ricardo, nodding thoughtfully. “Sharon, Ricardo’s right. If this is personal, we need to be ready. He won’t stop until he’s satisfied.”


She met his eyes, determination flashing in her own. “Good. That means he’ll make mistakes. And when he does, we’ll be ready.”








Chapter Seven: A Web of Shadows


The Falcon limped into the neon embrace of Port Vestige, its battered hull bearing the scars of countless encounters. The station’s outer shell glowed faintly, a patchwork of hastily repaired panels and shimmering force fields barely holding the structure together. Inside the cockpit, Cole let out a weary sigh as the ship docked with a heavy clang.


Gracie’s voice filled the cabin, dripping with her usual sarcasm. “Welcome to Port Vestige. Population: Questionable.”


“That’s an understatement,” Sharon muttered, leaning against the bulkhead. Her sharp eyes scanned the viewports as the ship’s systems powered down. “This place hasn’t changed. Still a hotbed for backstabbing and bad deals.”


“Perfect place for us,” Cole replied, his voice heavy with exhaustion. “We need repairs, fuel, and a plan. Let’s not overstay our welcome.”


From the engine bay, the faint sound of scraping metal and muffled clanks echoed through the corridors. Bitboy Leroy, the ship’s resident six-limbed robotic monkey hybrid, appeared in the doorway, his mechanical claws gripping a toolbox. His mismatched eyes blinked rapidly, one organic and the other a glowing red sensor.


“Tools,” Bitboy signed with jerky hand movements, his metallic claws emphasizing the message. “Fix now. Broken.”


Sharon crossed her arms, raising an eyebrow. “Broken doesn’t even begin to cover it, Bitboy. The whole ship’s held together by duct tape and hope.”


Bitboy chittered softly, his claw tapping a rhythmic pattern on the floor. He then signed, “Duct tape is strong. Hope? Weak.”


Emily chuckled from her console. “He’s not wrong.”


Cole pushed off his chair, rubbing his temples. “Alright, here’s the plan. Emily, you’re on parts. See if you can sweet-talk the merchants into a decent deal. Sharon, we need ammo—and don’t scare off the vendors this time. Bitboy, stay on the ship and start patching the critical systems. Gracie, keep an eye on… everything. This station’s crawling with people who’d love to steal a ship like ours.”


Bitboy’s claws clicked together as he signed, “Protect ship. Humans dumb.”


Cole let out a short laugh. “Thanks for the vote of confidence.”





Tensions and Encounters


The corridors of Port Vestige were a chaotic maze of flickering lights, rusting walls, and bustling merchants hawking wares from every corner of the galaxy. Emily moved cautiously, her wide eyes taking in the sights and sounds while keeping a firm grip on her data pad. The merchants’ voices rose and fell like waves, each promising “deals you can’t resist” and “exotic treasures from distant worlds.”


A grizzled merchant with a crooked grin eyed Emily as she approached his stall. “Looking for parts, sweetheart? I’ve got the finest flux regulators this side of the galaxy.”


Emily hesitated, scanning his inventory. “I need stabilizer modules. Do you have any compatible with a Falcon-class ship?”


The merchant’s grin widened, revealing yellowed teeth. “Falcon-class, eh? That’ll cost you extra.”


Before Emily could respond, she felt the hairs on the back of her neck rise. A shadow flitted across her peripheral vision. She turned sharply, but the crowded market offered no clear culprit. Her fingers tightened around her pad as unease settled in.





Sharon and Cole, meanwhile, found themselves in a dimly lit weapons depot, its walls lined with everything from pulse rifles to energy grenades. The proprietor, Captain Rycra, lounged behind the counter, her cybernetic arm resting on the table.


“Well, well,” Rycra drawled, her sharp features breaking into a smirk. “Sharon Teez and her merry band of misfits. What brings you to my corner of hell?”


Sharon’s jaw tightened. “Need to restock. Nothing fancy, just enough to keep us alive.”


Rycra’s gaze flicked to Cole, then back to Sharon. “Alive, huh? Running from someone? Or… something?”


“Does it matter?” Sharon shot back, her tone icy.


Rycra chuckled, leaning forward. “Depends. If you’re crossing the wrong people, it might matter to me.”


Cole interjected, his voice calm but firm. “We’re just passing through. No trouble.”


“Trouble always follows you,” Rycra said, her cybernetic fingers tapping rhythmically. “But, I might have some intel you’d find interesting. Heard of the Faction 42?”


Sharon stiffened, her fingers brushing the grip of her sidearm. “What do you know about them?”


Rycra’s smirk deepened. “Enough to know they’re bad news. And if they’re after you, well… you’ve got bigger problems than ammo shortages.”





A Narrow Escape


Back at the Falcon, Bitboy worked diligently, his claws darting between exposed wires and sparking conduits. His mechanical tail coiled around a stray cable, keeping it in place as he welded a patch onto a damaged panel.


Gracie’s voice crackled over the internal comms. “Hey, Bitboy. We’ve got a potential issue. Emily’s returning with parts, but she’s not alone. Someone’s tailing her.”


Bitboy chittered, dropping his tools. He signed quickly, “Show. Screen.”


A small display lit up, showing Emily weaving through the crowded corridors with a hooded figure in pursuit. Without hesitation, Bitboy activated his jetpack and darted toward the airlock.





Emily’s heart raced as she ducked into a side alley, clutching her parts tightly. The hooded figure loomed closer, their steps quickening. Just as they reached for her, a blur of motion intercepted them. Bitboy slammed into the pursuer with a screech, his claws sparking against the attacker’s energy shield.


The hooded figure stumbled back, revealing a Faction 42 insignia on their chest. They hissed a curse in an alien tongue before retreating into the shadows.


Emily stared at Bitboy, her breath coming in short gasps. “You… saved me.”


Bitboy signed, “Protect humans. Dumb. Weak.” His glowing eye flickered, almost teasingly.


Despite herself, Emily laughed. “Thanks, Bitboy. Let’s get back to the ship.”





The Reveal


Once aboard the Falcon, Emily shared her discovery of the Faction 42’s schematics and the unsettling truth behind their tracking beacon. Sharon’s face darkened as she pieced together the implications.


“Rycra knew,” Sharon said, pacing the room. “She set us up.”


Cole shook his head. “We don’t know that for sure.”


“Don’t we?” Sharon snapped. “The Faction 42’s not going to stop. And if Rycra’s feeding them intel, she’s as dangerous as they are.”


Emily hesitated. “But why? What do they want from us?”


Sharon’s gaze hardened. “They want control. Von Grutzen wants to erase anyone who stands in his way. And we’re next on his list.”


The crew sat in tense silence, the weight of their predicament pressing down on them. Outside, the neon glow of Port Vestige faded as the Falcon slipped into the void, the shadows of the Faction 42 looming ever closer.
