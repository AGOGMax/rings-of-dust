


Chapter 3







The morning light filtered through the tall pines, casting dappled light on the forest floor. Cole hefted his old ax over his head and split the last log round. The splitting crack was subdued due to the early spring moisture. Cole's breath clouded in the crisp air. The soft rustle of leaves and the distant call of a mountain jay seemed unbothered by this early morning habit. He paused, straightening to scan the treeline. Warm light burst through a gently swaying thicket of pine needles. The low sun cast a hard, angular light as it peeked over the granite peak just east of his cabin. His eyes were intense and watchful. His age, or was it his pain, was increasingly apparent. As he approached the cabin where old wood met new tech, he rubbed his hands together and let out a sudden and glorious howl. Then, just as before, he continued to the comfort of home.

The door creaked open, and Bitboy groaned. The firelight from inside flickered on Bitboy's brown and white fur, catching the streaks of auburn as he scurried to Cole. His mechanical limbs hummed softly as he reached around Cole's thighs, and the poor monkey sighed in obvious discomfort. One of Bitboy's arms reached out, tugging at Cole's sleeve, guiding him to the warmth.

Cole knelt by the fireplace. Bitboy watched his friend add a freshly cut log to the fire through his delirium. The sap began to boil, and the moisture hissed from the wood until it was dry enough to burn. Bitboy's eyes reflected the dancing flames. The scent of pine mingled with the earthy aroma of the fire, filling the cabin with a comforting fragrance. Cole's fingers brushed against Bitboy's fur. The warmth of the fire seeped into his skin, a stark contrast to the morning chill. Outside, the high Sierra Nevadas loomed. Cole softened as he looked at Bitboy. He felt deep gratitude for the unlikely companionship. The morning light, the serene forest, and the warmth of the fire blended into a moment of quiet reflection.

&quot;You little bastard,&quot; Cole said. &quot;What do you have?&quot;

As if dropping a one-gallon glass bottle of ethanol next to an open fire would obliterate his wrongdoing, Bitboy did just that, rolled on his back and forced all six limbs into the air like a dead bug. With a brilliant puff, Cole's expectation of a relaxing day went up in flames, along with the lingering fumes from a well-finished bottle that should have lasted the damn monkey at least a few days. Cole blamed the Federation. Their quest to re-engineer life had ended in almost complete failure. Other than Bitboy, no other monsters had made it past a year. It turned out that attempting to craft a weapon with the learning capacity of a human but devoid of moral complexities—a creature entirely expendable—was a terrible idea. Now Cole was the unwilling father of a junky. A junky with forearms containing a network of metal rods, which themselves acted as the unholy foundation for retractable steel blades. Bitboy's upgraded form required ethanol not as a vice but as a vital power source for these and other mechanical augmentations. Even with its enhancements, his primal brain lacked the discipline to moderate. Ethanol to him was akin to ambrosia—fueling his strength, sharpening his intellect, and enhancing his agility, making it irresistible. His inability to self-regulate his consumption posed a risk of overindulgence to the point of shutdown.

Meanwhile, the world outside grappled with a crisis of its own. The airwaves carried the manufactured accent belonging only to news anchors.

&quot;Good morning, California. This is Rachel Vayes for Public Square News. The energy crisis, a situation that has left millions struggling with resource scarcity and power outages, may finally be seeing a solution. During the opening day of Defying Gravity 2041: Innovations in Combating the Energy Crisis, the tech titan A Corp promised a long-term solution. Although initial disappointments stemmed from the keynote speaker's disappearance, A Corp defied the gravity of the situation by announcing that new energy-efficient implants would be available on a subscription basis. More on the disappearance of 43-year-old Dr. Stephen Conrad tonight at ten.&quot;

Cole reached for the remote—the vibrant escapism of silence. Bitboy returned from a meandering trip to the kitchen and, with snacks in hand, sat in wonder at the crackling fire. The crackle. The boiling, spitting sap. Cole cracked open a beer on his way to the medicine cabinet. If selling second-hand Federation tech on the black market had been a rich man's game, Bitboy's thirst and the price of Naloxone-E kept Cole from a golden throne. While walking back to the warmth of the living room, Cole pulled his phone from his pocket and searched for &quot;Heavy Hitter&quot; in his contact list.

&quot;I need you to walk me through it again. I have the shot, but what's the rest?&quot; Cole asked.

&quot;It's a good thing I'm about there then. Isn't it?&quot; Ricardo replied.

&quot;Hell will freeze over. Sharon will marry me. Heck, Emily will get cured—all before you drag your ass out here after seven years,&quot; Cole said.

&quot;I know. I know,&quot; Ricardo replied. &quot;I'm under strict orders to keep this conversation analog. I'll be there in about an hour.&quot;

&quot;Orders? And here I thought you were coming to visit an old friend or, better yet, to pick up your damn pet.&quot;

&quot;I have a 30-rack of natty light,&quot; Ricardo said.

&quot;That's a start,&quot; Cole replied. &quot;But Bitboy and I don't drink anymore. We're watching our figures.&quot;

&quot;Come on, man,&quot; Ricardo insisted. &quot;Just one beer.&quot;

&quot;You're trouble, Hitter,&quot; Cole said. &quot;Just one beer!&quot;

With the Naloxone-E in hand, Cole turned his attention from the phone to Bitboy, who was lost in the dance of the flames and snacks. The glow of the fire cast shadows across Cole's aging face. He knew what came next was a delicate balance between saving Bitboy and pushing him to the brink.

&quot;Alright, buddy,&quot; Cole said. &quot;Just a short prick.&quot;

Bitboy, sensing the shift in Cole's demeanor, turned, his eyes narrowing in confusion and then widening in understanding. An unwelcome whirr came from Bitboy's arms, and the blades peeked out. There was a moment of understanding even a junky monkey could grasp, and Bitboy fell willingly, yet tense, into Cole's arms. With a gentle touch, Cole injected the Naloxone-E. The room held its breath as the solution coursed through Bitboy's veins. His breathing steadied to a rhythm reclaimed from chaos. Cole let out a breath he didn't know he was holding, and relief washed over him as Bitboy's eyes flickered with clarity.

&quot;I'm going to just toss you out in the snow next time,&quot; Cole said. &quot;Do you understand me, Monkey?&quot;

A quiet settled between them, broken only by the occasional pop and hiss from the fire. Bitboy, lucid but weakened, leaned into Cole, and Cole draped an arm around Bitboy. As they sat in the warmth of the living room, Cole's gaze drifted into the fire.

The crisp air filled Ricardo's government-issued SUV with the scent of pine and dust. A nearby stream babbled its way through the valley. He handled the winding country road carelessly like only a pilot could. The unmistakable sound of tires on a dirt road was amplified for miles around the cabin. The sound of a single car was a rarity. Two, like the two approaching the cabin, were reasons for concern.

Gravel crunched under the tires as Ricardo pulled into the driveway. Before him stood a single A-frame cabin. Ricardo chugged the last of a lukewarm energy drink.

Cole cracked the front door and winced at the bright sunlight dazzling across the numerous pines with their dusting of snow. His eyes scanned the environment before settling his sight on his visitor.

&quot;Hey, Nova, I hope you don't mind. I let myself past the gate,&quot; Ricardo said.

Ricardo was the embodiment of the world Cole left behind. A Federation man through and through. With his height and girth, he filled his fatigues just enough to accentuate massive arms and shoulders while leaving room in the midsection to hide his growing gut. Next to his large body, he carried a heavy black briefcase, and he looked like a 4th grader with his lunch pail.

&quot;You didn't get past anything,&quot; Cole said. &quot;I clocked you on the gate's camera and buzzed you in.&quot;

&quot;Always have to be the winner, eh, Nova?&quot;

&quot;Just works out that way, Hitter. My sister and girlfriend will be here soon. Behave.&quot;

Ricardo joined Cole on the wraparound porch and clasped his large hands around Cole's shoulders. The two men looked into each other for only a moment, and the years between them melted away. Cole grunted and patted Ricardo away from their friendly embrace.

&quot;Bitboy is by the fireplace,&quot; Cole said. &quot;Go on and take a look. I'm going to wait for the girls.&quot;

Ricardo pulled a beer from the pouch pocket on his jacket and cracked it open with a crispness that carried off the granite walls around them. Then, from another pocket, he retrieved another and tossed it to Cole before disappearing into the cabin.

The sound of another vehicle crept closer and closer. Cole waved them over when Sharon and Emily finally arrived in a puff of dust. Both women jumped from the van to stretch the long drive from their slender bodies. Cole slowly opened his beer with a hiss instead of a crack and quickly drank a giant's share. He watched Sharon and sent an effortlessly booming whistle through the quiet valley around them.

&quot;Hold your damn horses,&quot; Sharon said. &quot;Some of us give our bodies love from time to time.&quot;

&quot;Put on a few pounds,&quot; Ricardo's voice carried from inside the house, &quot;and I'd take care of that for you.&quot;

Emily turned red and wide-eyed, which brought out her innocent beauty. Cole, on the other hand, closed the door.

&quot;Girls, we have a visitor,&quot; Cole said. &quot;I'd like you to meet Bitboi's deadbeat dad. This disgusting pervert is Heavy Hitter.&quot;

Still outside, the three embraced until Emily squirmed out to allow Cole and Sharon a moment of affection. Cole and Sharon absorbed each other. Cole's hands wrapped around Sharon's lower back, and he lifted her into a longing kiss. With her back turned Emily did her best to pretend she wasn't intruding at that moment.

&quot;How'd she do?&quot; Cole asked in a soft tone that sounded like a secret.

Emily rounded the corner and continued to pretend she wasn't there. Through a lifetime of practice, she had become skilled at feeling invisible.

&quot;Cole, darling,&quot; Sharon looked up into his eyes, &quot;she was incredible. She'll rule the world once she figures out how capable she is.&quot;

&quot;I'm glad to hear it,&quot; Cole said. &quot;But what happened to the guy if it went so well?&quot;

&quot;The guy?&quot; Emily said, popping her head around a nearby corner. &quot;What, uh, what guy?&quot;

&quot;Your contact, E,&quot; Cole said. &quot;You obviously got the tech. But what happened to Dr. Conrad?&quot;

&quot;Cole, hunny,&quot; Sharon said, &quot;you must be more specific. Stephen was a bit of a mess. He gave us this whole doom prediction and this memory stick. Other than that, it mostly went down as expected.&quot;

Sharon handed Cole the van keys, and both women stood silent. Cole held the memory drive, rubbing his thumb across the hull of the figurine. The repetitive motion left a dark silver stain on his skin.

&quot;He was weird, okay,&quot; Emily said, &quot;but nice too. He said to tell you there is a lock for every key,&quot; and she raised her hands in confusion.

&quot;So neither of you know where he went?&quot;

The women looked at each other and shook their heads.

&quot;Okay. Well, he's missing,&quot; Cole said. &quot;Didn't even show for his speech. Thought maybe you had all run off together.&quot;

&quot;How should we know where he went?&quot; Emily said. &quot;Is that something we should know?&quot;

Cole shook his head and opened the door. The smell of bacon and pancakes caressed them inside.

&quot;Who's hungry?&quot; Ricardo said. &quot;Looks like these degenerates have been partying all night…for like years. And from the van and sweatpants, I'd bet you girls could use a meal.&quot;

Bitboy lay in agony next to the dying fire. Ricardo's case sat open on the table next to several accouterments. Sharon added a small jar between the butter and a container of organic maple syrup. Cole set down his empty beer can and reached for the case Ricardo had brought with him. He unpacked the stomach pump from within it and saw Emily holding Bitboy with the care of a mother. Cole wondered where she learned that as he tiptoed toward the two. He kneeled down and nodded to Emily. They transferred Bitboy to Cole's firm embrace, and Bitboy nuzzled into Cole's chest. Emily slid the tip of her finger under Bitboy's chin. She gently pressed up until their eyes met, and Bitboy went limp with safety. Cole gently slid the long tube down the macaque's throat as the poor animal twitched in discomfort layered on pain. The device hummed to life, pulling putrid sludge up with every beat. As it worked, Cole glanced at the empty can.

&quot;Hey, Hitter,&quot; Cole said while leaning over Bitboy, &quot;beer me.&quot;

&quot;You have the grace of a peasant,&quot; Sharon said.

The machine emitted a steady hum, purging the toxins from Bitboy's system. Emily, her face set with determination, carefully removed the tube from Bitboy's mouth. She moved to the sink, the sound of running water mingling with the crackle of the fire. She scrubbed her hands and glanced over her shoulder, noting Ricardo's handiwork. The kitchen was a scene of organized chaos: plates piled high with bacon and a mountain of pancakes, while six empty beer cans stood sentinel on the counter.

&quot;A girl could get used to this,&quot; she said, her smile widening as she caught Ricardo's eye.



Ricardo leaned against the counter with a beer in hand and wearing a lopsided grin. &quot;Shit, sis, this place is stocked like a bomb shelter. So, you know, I could get used to this too.&quot;

Across the room, Cole knelt beside Bitboy, his rough hands surprisingly gentle as he caressed the small creature. Lulled by the fire's warmth and rhythmic crackling, Bitboy's eyes fluttered shut. Cole watched as Bitboy's breathing steadied and deepened. He then carefully laid Bitboy in a plush doggy bed positioned just within the fire's glow. The front door creaked behind, and Sharon stepped in, boasting a devilish grin commanding attention. She moved with deliberate grace, her steps quiet but assured. She placed a hand on Cole's shoulder and drew his gaze away from Bitboy.

&quot;I have a surprise for you,&quot; she said. &quot;Come sit with me and close your eyes.&quot;

Cole raised an eyebrow, but he complied. Sharon took his hand, her touch light but insistent, and led him towards the kitchen table. He settled into the chair, fingers splayed over his eyes, peeking through them. Sharon gently guided his hand away from his face and pressed it to her chest. He could feel the flutter of her heart beneath his palm.

&quot;Okay, open them,&quot; she whispered.

He blinked against the light and saw a dark purple jar nestled beside a stack of steaming pancakes, promising something special inside. Cole's eyes softened, and a smile spread across his face.

&quot;You spoil me,&quot; he said, his voice a mix of affection and awe.

&quot;Only because you deserve it,&quot; Sharon replied, kissing his forehead. The kiss lingered.

As mid-morning light filtered through the kitchen windows, Emily and Ricardo shared a quiet laugh by the sink. The fire crackled, casting a warm glow over the room, while the tall pines stood guard outside. They found a fleeting moment of peace within these walls amidst the aroma of bacon and pancakes. Each of them, in their own way, was home.

&quot;Sharon, I can't believe you. What must I have done in a past life to earn a woman like you?&quot; Cole said, his hand still resting on her heart.

Sharon smiled, squeezing his hand tighter against her chest. &quot;I got this at THE farmer's market. The last blackberry preserves they had!&quot;

&quot;Mmm! Quick. Open it for me,&quot; Ricardo interjected, eager as ever.

Cole opened the jar with a satisfying pop. Sharon dipped her finger into the bittersweet jam and then into Cole's mouth. He melted into his chair, savoring the taste, his lips lingering on her finger as she slowly pulled it away.

&quot;Jackpot,&quot; Ricardo said, a spreading mischievous grin. &quot;I haven't seen you this happy since our first time behind the stick. Let me try that moon goo. Maybe later, we can zip around in whatever plane you have hidden here.&quot;

&quot;I'm not flying,&quot; Cole said, standing abruptly. &quot;Not later. Not ever.&quot;

Ricardo's smile faded. &quot;Then you're not going to like why I'm here,&quot; he said, his tone serious. &quot;Although I don't entirely know myself.&quot;

Sharon fixed her most penetrative glare on Ricardo. &quot;Did you know she's involved with those crackpot collectivists?&quot; Sharon asked.

Ricardo's face fell. &quot;Yes, I knew.&quot;

&quot;And you didn't think to tell me?&quot; Cole asked, his voice rising.

&quot;It's not my place, Cole. She's an adult and can make her own decisions,&quot; Ricardo replied calmly.

Emily, who had been quietly listening, suddenly felt the weight of the conversation pressing down on her. She set her fork down on the table, her eyes watering. She stood up without a word and walked to the kitchen sink, her hands moving automatically as she touched the empty plates, scratching the crumbs off pans. She was unconsciously distracting herself from the hot tears burning her face from the fear of the energy crisis, Cole's sense of duty, and a room full of people who should mind their own business. Emily grasped the sides of her head, let out a silent scream, and returned to the living room—her face red but otherwise composed. She sat on the couch, facing away from her brother. Cole said nothing and continued to pet Bitboy, who had joined him on the sofa. His mind is fixated on the letter, and his thumb drives on the table. Until this moment, his thoughts had been on the monkey, pancakes, and the metric volume of fuel needed to reach Mars—anything other than what these items held for him.

&quot;And burn my apartment down when it self-destructs? No, thank you,&quot; Ricardo said, shaking his head. &quot;I don't know what's in there any more than I know how many lives I've saved via lovemaking.&quot;

In the following pause, Cole scanned their faces, searching for answers and finding only blank stares. Resentment began to simmer in his mind. Emily's gaze wandered around the log cabin, taking in the mounds of dirty laundry and empty cans that littered the floor. Sharon and Ricardo looked back at Cole, their expressions unreadable.

&quot;They can say or do whatever they want. It's been seven years,&quot; Cole said, his frustration boiling over. &quot;Seven years! Do they think they can put a leash on me and run me around like a dog?&quot;

Emily glanced at him, her voice softening. &quot;I don't know if I should feel like I'm in danger or if I should feel satisfied.&quot;

Cole's heart raced. &quot;How do you mean?&quot;

&quot;That's the first real emotion I've seen out of you in months,&quot; Emily said with a smile playing on her lips.

&quot;Tell us you're ready,&quot; Sharon demanded, eyes locking onto Cole's.

Bitboy turned to look at Cole. The metal implements in his arms twined unnaturally around the muscles. Cole's mind reeled, wondering what secrets the envelope held. At the dining table, Ricardo tried to get Bitboy's attention. Still, the monkey ignored him, perhaps because of Ricardo's earlier abandonment or simply because Bitboy had forgotten him. Instead, Bitboy's eyes remained locked on Cole, his mechanical body whirring and tensing in sync with his master's agitation.

Ricardo stood, sighing as he walked from the dining table to the couch, the envelope held out in front of him. He extended the envelope toward Cole, who snatched it from Ricardo's fingers. Cole glanced at Emily and Sharon, then shrugged and extracted crisp white papers from within. Two small captain insignias tumbled out and landed on his lap as he did. His breath caught, and for a moment, he stared at them, the gleaming symbols of authority and recognition. His fingers hovered over the insignias, eyes widening with longing and disbelief. He picked up the insignias, feeling their cold metal against his skin. He had imagined this moment countless times, but this felt like a cruel bribe. His mind flashed back to the lives lost under his command. The insignias dug into his skin. He tossed them on the table, mimicking disinterest, and moved on.

The Federation's emblem in the upper left-hand corner of the letter sent a shudder down his spine. Sharon rested her head on her hand, her arm draped over the back of the sofa, her eyes fixed intently on Cole. Cole's gaze darted across the page, his index finger trembling as it followed each line. He began to read aloud, his voice unsteady, the weight of the words sinking in with every syllable:

From the Office of Federation Chancellor Geoffrey H. DeMint

Dear Mr. Nova,

The Federation stands at a critical juncture, and your unparalleled expertise is urgently required. After extensive deliberation, the Council has concluded that your unique qualifications are essential to our mission. Enclosed is a copy of a legally binding document with your signature dated September 12, 2021, which waives your rights as a United States citizen in exchange for your service in the Stellar Council Forces. Your duty to the Council remains in effect and paramount.

We regret to inform you of the escalating energy shortage crisis, which threatens the very fabric of our society. The severity of this condition is unprecedented, and we seek your immediate involvement with utmost urgency. Your skills and experience make you uniquely positioned to address this dire situation.

You are hereby ordered to prepare for immediate deployment. Report to Federal Space Command Headquarters. Be advised that your movements are under constant surveillance. The Federation relies on your swift and unquestioning compliance. The stakes have never been higher, and the survival of humanity rests on your shoulders.

This is your moment, Mr. Nova. The world is watching. We need the old Cole Nova—the leader, the hero, the man who can turn the tide. Your legacy awaits.

Sincerely,

Geoffrey H. DeMint, Federation Chancellor

Cole crumpled the letter, his face contorted with anger and hurled it into a steel garbage pail. &quot;Bastards! I need more time,&quot; he growled, his voice echoing with frustration. The sudden clatter of dishes from the dining area caught his attention. &quot;Where's Bitboy?&quot;

The macaque perched on the table with a stack of pancakes the size of Lincoln's hat clutched in his tiny hands. He grinned sheepishly, crumbs littering his fur. Cole and Emily shared a laugh bubbling up from their mutual despair. Sharon and Ricardo joined in, their laughter tinged with nervousness. Sharon's mirth quickly turned to rage as she noticed a blue trail of stains leading to Bitboy's many fingers and the now-empty jar of blackberry preserves. The juice had turned his steel claws a dark blue, with a hint of the color marking his lips and tongue.

&quot;You damn dirty ape! Is nothing sacred to you?&quot; she cried, her voice rising in frustration.

Cole turned away from the scene, his smile fading. He strode purposefully to a cluttered desk and jammed the thumb drive into the side of an old laptop. The screen flickered to life. Files exploded across the display in a chaotic frenzy of data. He leaned in, scanning the information with growing unease.

Ricardo walked over and peered over Cole's shoulder. The data scrolling past included information on Cole he had long buried in the recesses of his memory: missions, failures, and personal details he wished to forget. Dirt on Ricardo that could bury him twice over scandalous and damning. Notes on Emily that reduced her fears to a mere footnote in a much larger, darker narrative of corruption and cover-ups. But one encrypted file, tagged &quot;Dark Matter Research,&quot; lured Cole in like a dark siren call. Sharon, her curiosity piqued, leaned closer, her breath steady against the back of his neck, her eyes locked on the screen. The weight of what they might uncover hung heavily in the air.

&quot;Bitboy, play,&quot; Cole commanded, his voice a mixture of resolve and dread. The monkey, driven by some complex programming, scampered to the laptop. His mechanical fingers moved with precise efficiency, tapping away at the keyboard. Bitboy's eyes glowed with a soft light as he interfaced with the encryption, his body humming with a low mechanical whirr. As Bitboy worked, Cole's mind raced. What could be so important that it was hidden behind layers of encryption? The seconds felt like hours as the monkey's limbs moved in a blur of activity. Unable to stay still, Ricardo paced the room, his footsteps adding to the tension. Emily stepped closer, her eyes wide with concern.

Finally, the screen blinked, and the files spilled their secrets, line after line of data unfurling before them. Bitboy, his task complete, returned to his feast of pancakes, the glow in his eyes dimming as he disconnected. Cole's eyes darted over the data, absorbing its implications. Although much of it was unintelligible without careful analysis, the narrative suggested the unthinkable: Cole's reckless dance with dark matter wasn't just a personal folly—it might have birthed the energy crisis. The realization hit him like a gut punch, a cosmic joke with Cole as the punchline. He felt Sharon's hand tighten on his shoulder, her grip a silent affirmation of their shared burden.

Cole scrolled through the documents, his face grimmer with each line he read. Detailed logs chronicled the Federation's secretive experiments with dark matter. Early trials, initially promising, had shown the potential to harness dark matter as a nearly infinite energy source. But the risks had been monumental. One log highlighted a pivotal experiment conducted by Dr. Stephen Conrad. The document described how the experiment had gone awry, destabilizing the global energy grid and triggering catastrophic energy surges.

Emily leaned over Cole's other shoulder, her eyes scanning the text. &quot;That's why the world's in chaos,&quot; she murmured. &quot;They caused it.&quot;

Cole nodded, his jaw set. The logs detailed how the energy crisis was no mere accident but a result of the Federation's relentless pursuit of power, their blind ambition leading to the current disaster. Dr. Conrad's disappearance now made sense—it was a cover-up, an attempt to bury the truth of the failed experiment that had plunged the world into darkness.

As they delved deeper, surveillance reports began to surface. Cole's name appeared frequently. The Federation had been watching him, aware of his expertise and the potential threat he posed. The reports suggested they had planned to leverage his skills, bringing him back into their fold to mitigate the disaster they had created.

Sharon's grip on Cole's shoulder tightened. &quot;They've been playing us all along,&quot; she whispered, her voice thick with anger.

Cole looked at his team and his family and felt a surge of hope. &quot;We can do this,&quot; he said firmly. &quot;Together, we can stop them.&quot;

The firelight cast dancing shadows on the walls, illuminating their determined faces. Cole turned to Bitboy, who had returned to his makeshift bed by the fire, his mechanical limbs finally at rest. Emily's face, now composed, joined them, her eyes reflecting a newfound resolve. Standing by the kitchen counter, Ricardo finished his beer and crushed the can with a loud pop, breaking the silence.

&quot;So, what's the plan, Nova?&quot; Ricardo asked, his voice steady but tinged with the gravity of the situation.

&quot;We need to understand everything in these files,&quot; Cole said. &quot;We have to know what we're dealing with. Then, we figure out how to stop them.&quot;

Emily spoke up, her voice strong and clear. &quot;I've been involved with the Collective. I know people who can help.&quot;

Ricardo looked at her with a mix of surprise and respect. &quot;You really got involved with those collectivists, huh?&quot;

Emily nodded. &quot;They're not all crackpots. Some of them are actually trying to make a difference.&quot;

Cole felt a surge of pride mixed with worry. &quot;We'll need all the help we can get.&quot;

Sharon stood up, her face set with determination. &quot;Let's get to work. We've got a lot to do and not much time to do it.&quot;

As they gathered around the laptop, the fire crackling behind them, the cabin seemed to pulse with a new energy. The quiet refuge had transformed into a war room, and they were ready to face whatever came next. The Federation might have thought they could control Cole Nova. Still, they had underestimated the strength and resolve of those he held dear.

Cole glanced at them, seeing the same fire in their eyes that he felt in his own. &quot;Together,&quot; he said, his voice firm. &quot;We do this together.&quot;

Bitboy, his mechanical fingers poised over the keyboard, began working on the encryption. The room fell silent, and each group member focused on their task. As the files started to reveal their secrets, the scope of the Federation's ambition became clear.

&quot;Look at this,&quot; Emily said, pointing to a series of documents. &quot;They've been experimenting with dark matter for years, using it to create energy-efficient implants and other technologies. But the side effects... they're catastrophic.&quot;

Ricardo added, &quot;They knew the risks and proceeded anyway. The energy crisis was a direct result of their experiments.&quot;

Sharon's face paled as she read through the data. &quot;If we don't stop them, the consequences will be unimaginable.&quot;

Cole looked at his team and his family and felt a surge of hope. &quot;We can do this,&quot; he said firmly. &quot;Together, we can stop them.&quot;

They set their plan into motion, ready to face whatever challenges lay ahead. The Federation might have thought they could control Cole Nova. Still, they had underestimated the strength and resolve of those he held dear. The fight for humanity's future had just begun.
