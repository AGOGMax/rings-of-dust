


Chapter 4





After the fatal extraction of DarkX by Cole tests were done and it was discovered that just as the combination of mineral salts had caused the Vapor to form into a denser plasma like state even more concentrations of salt added to DarkX would harden the substance into a stable crystal state that could be handled and used for up to 15 days.

During these 15 days scientists and engineers tried applying these crystals to every product imaginable from medicines to apparel and electronics. No matter what they tried the crystals (now referred to as Dark Matter in their stable form) would return to the unstable form of DarkX and had to be returned to the harvester containers.

That is until one day when a lab assistant, Max, too lazy to return the crystals to the harvester all the way across the scientific complex that stretched 4 miles across. Instead he placed them into a spent fuel cell on a colleagues desk. For good measure he filled the fuel cell with nitrogen and sealed the cell with negative pressure in the vacuum chamber. He figured that under these conditions a fire or explosion would be unlikely over the weekend.

The following Monday Max arrived early to keep his indiscretion a secret. To his surprise the fuel cell had a slight charge indicated by a faint ember glow from the LED indicator that glowed inside the view glass on its face. Even stranger there was nothing but vapor inside the fuel cell. This should have been shared immediately with his supervisors but he didn’t want to get in trouble for disobeying protocol. Alpha Corps had a culture of harsh disciplinary action and was the last mega corp that offered health insurance and employee preference to the energy supply that was just as unstable as the DarkX he was expecting to find.

Max took the fuel cell and hid it in his locker, the fuel cell continued to charge with this mysterious vapor like a terrarium of fog where you look for the snake or frog at a zoo. On the 90th day the fuel cell was fully charged and the vapor cleared up leaving a small collection of Dark Matter crystals growing from its base. Even stranger, a deposit of dark matter crystals had formed around the lid. Now he knew that this information would be so valuable his supervisors and HR would forgive him of his unsanctioned behavior and probably promote him.

He took his science fair project to the director of his department, who immediately called HR and security. Within minutes Max was stripped of his ID badge and lab coat and escorted to the parking lot. He had made one of the most important discoveries of mankind and was reeling at the thought of how to feed his family.

Alpa Corp realized that the combination of nitrogen, negative pressure and the circuits of the fuel cell made Dark Matter revert to its native vapor state which emitted ever increasing energy. Eventually liquid nitrogen was added to the process which accelerated the forming of the crystals around the lid to appear as soon as 9 days.

Every 9 days a new bloom of little crystals would appear on some of the lids but not all. Over the 90 day “summer” of these blooms only about 5%, or 1 in 20 would get the bonus deposits. The randomness of this anomaly led to the scientist gambling on the serial numbers of the fuel cells holding a workplace “lottery” where losers had to buy junk food out of the vending machines for the winners.

The other phenomenon that they discovered was that the earlier the crystals around the lid formed, the smaller the amount. Every 9 days the deposits almost doubled. On the 90th day when the vapor cleared up each fuel cell would have a little seed of crystals budding in the bottom. The scientists noted that they looked like budding roses and called collecting the crystals “pruning” like they were botanists in a garden.

Every 105 days the crystals inside the fuel cell would bloom and grow. When fuel cells were damaged or faulty and lost their vacuum seal the fuel cell would stop growing in energy. The small amount of dark matter inside the cell if any would be collected and put back into production. With every series of fuel cells there would be about a 3% loss of Dark Matter, the team referred to this loss as tailings like they do in gold mines.

Apha Corp had of course kept all of this information to themselves. After all, what good was having the key to the future if you let others make copies? Share holders were about to become the wealthiest people on earth. The stock was headed to the moon, better yet, Saturn. 








The Federation Headquarters loomed over the city like a fortress, its sleek lines and towering presence a constant reminder of the power within its walls. The reflective glass cast an ominous glow over the sprawling metropolis below. Despite the late hour, the building was alive, and its occupants were driven by the pressing need to solve the deepening energy crisis. Footsteps echoed off the polished floors, and the air was thick with the scent of coffee and ozone, mingling with the faint hum of machinery. The weight of the crisis pressed down on everyone. This invisible burden grew heavier with each passing moment.

In a secure conference room, the atmosphere crackled with tension. The walls, lined with monitors, flickered with live feeds, data streams, and maps. Chancellor Geoffrey H. DeMint presided over the meeting, his presence commanding. He stood at the head of a polished mahogany table, his fingers interlaced, eyes scanning the room. The soft whir of the holographic projectors added a mechanical undercurrent to the silence, a reminder of the technology they were fighting to sustain.

&quot;Let's get started,&quot; DeMint said, his voice slicing through the low murmur of conversation. He leaned forward, the deep lines of his face etched with the weight of the crisis. &quot;The latest energy consumption reports are in, and the situation is worsening.&quot; It was ironic that they were racing time to collect darkX. Saturn being the 6th planet, the number 6 represents time, also chronos is the god of time. 666 represents time, not satan. Time is the destroyer of everything.

As he spoke, the room dimmed, and a holographic display flickered to life, casting an eerie glow that bathed the attendees in a cold light. A world map materialized in mid-air, with pulsating red zones spreading like a virus, indicating severe energy shortages. The silence that followed was palpable as if the very air had been sucked out, leaving a void filled only with the flicker of the hologram. The gravity of the situation etched itself into the furrowed brows and tight lips of those present, their faces illuminated by the stark reminder of their dire predicament.

Dr. Miranda Chen, a lead scientist from Alpha Corp, leaned forward. Alpha Corp had basically taken over everything with private contracts. They held the majority of the ships in the solar system, depots, manufacturing facilities, patents on the technology. DeMint had a front row seat to this unholy take over and was secretly sympathetic to the collective as they were the best chance to stop Alpha Corp’s insatiable appetite. At this point even if they overcame the dire predicament they faced who would be able to afford power with their monopoly.

Miranda’s face, partially obscured by the holographic light. She adjusted her glasses, the reflective surface catching the light and momentarily obscuring her eyes. &quot;Our projections show we'll face widespread blackouts without immediate intervention within six months. The dark matter reserves are depleting faster than anticipated.&quot;

DeMint nodded, his jaw set. The sleepless nights were evident in the dark circles under his eyes, shadows that seemed to deepen with each passing second. &quot;What about the new fuel cells? How soon will they be available to lease?&quot;

Taylor, a Federation Crew Chief  that had been working closely with Alpha Corp on the fuel cells shifted uncomfortably in his seat.

 &quot;The fuel cells require a 90-day stabilization period before deployment. Our current reserves are insufficient for mass production. We need more DarkX from Saturn and additional resources from Mars. The harvesting window is closing, and the next opportunity is uncertain.&quot;

“Sounds like someone is itching for deployment” Demint said wryly.

The holographic display shifted, now showing a timetable of harvesting windows, each marked with critical deadlines. The silence in the room grew heavier, the enormity of their task sinking in like a lead weight. Faces around the table tightened further, eyes darting from one red-marked date to the next, a grim parade of looming failures. The weight of unspoken fears hung in the air, thick and oppressive.

Sam, a seasoned Data Technician with a sharp mind, leaned back in her chair, her eyes narrowing thoughtfully. &quot;We've been exploring alternative sources, including the dark matter accumulations on the exterior of the fuel cells. However, without the proprietary information from A Corp. We are unable to keep the Dark matter crystals stable in our fuel cells for more than 15 days.  Nor have we been able to reproduce the energy production found in Alpha Corp’s fuel cells. The fact that they,” Sam motioned her head toward Dr Chen and the Apha Corp team, “have access to all of the Federations files and data and we have no access to theirs is ridiculous. There is now way we are going to solve this energy crisis without cooperation.” Frustrated, she leaned back in her chair taking a deep breath and biting her tongue before she said something she couldn’t take back.

“It’s been well established that Alpha Corp has every right as a private company to keep their patents and data secure.” Taylor interjected. 

Sam shot him a look of anger, she should’ve known he would align with the corporation.

“They are our partners in tackling the energy crisis, and we are very thankful for their help and resources.” Taylor continued as if he were campaigning on the senate floor of ancient Rome.

“It would be nice for them to share even more of their breakthroughs so that the federation could join them in research and development of fuel cells with more capacity.” Sam was not about to let A Corp’s little bitch defend them in the Federation’s control center.

Dr Chen gave an insincere smile from across the room, “I’ll mention that in the next shareholder’s meeting and see if the board can be more open with our patents.”

Taylor's fingers drummed definitely on the table “I think we can all agree that the answer is more DarkX to increase production of the fuel cells.”

The room fell silent again, the danger darkX imposed was not to be taken lightly. Safety measures had to be taken when handling the harvesters. Even with the processing done off planet at one of Alpha Corps many space station processing plants. There was a risk of not being able to use the dark matter crystals during their 15 day stable period. The Federation would have to ship the crystals back up to A Corps space station to be processed with large amounts of salt, copper and lead before they could be handled in any safe way. Funny how A corp usually only sent their tailings back to the space station and had an almost 97% success rate when applying the crystal to their fuel cells. Sam was sick of failing at their experiments, if A corp would just tell them how they got the crystal back into their vapor state manufacturing could ramp up and the demand for power would be alleviated at least for now.DeMint turned to Sam, who had been closely monitoring security reports.

&quot;Sam, any updates on the security front?&quot; DeMint inquired, his tone measured but urgent.

Sam cleared her throat, caught off guard by the change in topic.

&quot;Yes, Chancellor. We've increased patrols around the dark matter storage facilities, but there have been reports of unauthorized access attempts. We suspect internal sabotage, possibly from elements sympathetic to The Collective. They’re also intercepting shipments of the older fuel cells and unsealing them for the Dark Matter crystal to sell on the black market, our trackers are the only thing keeping them from stealing the fuel cells outright.&quot;

The mention of The Collective sent a ripple of unease through the room. The shadowy group opposing the Federation's dark matter control was a constant threat. Their disruptive actions endangered the delicate balance the Federation aimed to maintain. DeMint's mind flashed back to the recent security breach, the chilling realization that their enemies were closer than ever.

&quot;We need to tighten security further,&quot; DeMint said decisively. He tapped the table for emphasis, the sound sharp and final. &quot;We must accelerate efforts to neutralize The Collective's influence. Dr. Chen and Taylor, keep me updated on the fuel cells and pruning process. We must stabilize our energy supply.&quot;



Taylor charged over to Sam leaving his belongings behind at this table.

“There’s a good chance that you and I will be tapped for this mission, you might want to try a little tact when addressing the hand that feeds us”

“They feed us with one hand while choking us with the other, or in your case pet you with the other. Nice watch, by the way, is that a Versace?”

“You might be able to fool the brass here with your facade, but I see the rebel underneath. You’re just gonna get chewed up by the machine” Taylor hissed.

“Run along, your masters are going” Sam glanced at the Alpha Corp members leaving the room.

“You’re so clueless. You think the real Master is even in this room?” His eyes were menacing and then went cold and emotionless.

Sam was discouraged by the wall of greed she was trying to climb, but she was resilient. She was going to discover the solution to the fuel cell remedy. She remembered the words of Dr. Stephen Conrad, the brilliant scientist whose disappearance had left a void in their research, &quot;Every failure is a step towards success. But some steps are harder to recover from.&quot;

Meanwhile, whispers of conspiracy and fear echoed through the headquarters' corridors. The Federation's control over dark matter and collaboration with A Corp were subjects of hope and suspicion. Some believed the energy crisis was manufactured to consolidate power and control. The shadows seemed to lengthen as these whispers grew.

DeMint stood alone in the conference room, his thoughts tumultuous with determination and doubt. The stakes were higher than ever, and their choices in the coming days would shape humanity's future. He gazed at the holographic display, the red zones glowing ominously. His mind wandered to the confidential reports he'd read earlier, prepared by A Corp  they detailed the potential consequences of failure. Each red zone was a potential spark for unrest.

DeMint's trusted intelligence officer, Vance Black, entered the room with a thin folder in hand. His expression was grave as he approached the Chancellor.

&quot;Chancellor, this just came in from our intelligence division,&quot; Vance said, his voice low but steady. &quot;There's been movement from The Collective. They're mobilizing resources, possibly in response to our latest operations.&quot;

DeMint skimmed the contents, his eyes narrowing as he absorbed the information. The pages contained grainy surveillance photos and intercepted communications. &quot;We need to be ready. Increase security around all dark matter facilities. Did you get that intel on A Corp I requested?&quot;

Vance nodded, his jaw set with determination. &quot;I’ve got a team on it sir, but do you really think that’s the best use of our resources given the escalation of the collective?”

“We can’t let anything go unchecked.”

 Understood, Chancellor. I'll coordinate with security to implement the measures immediately.&quot;

As Vance left, DeMint was once more alone with his thoughts. The pieces were coming together, but the picture was of conflict and chaos. As the shadows lengthened outside, he knew the battle for dark matter—and humanity's future—was far from over. His responsibilities bore down on him, but there was no room for hesitation. The image of a world plunged into darkness loomed in his mind, a nightmare he was determined to prevent.

In the corridors of power and Alpha Corps hidden labs, the accurate scale of the energy crisis was unfolding. Alliances would be tested, secrets revealed, and the balance of power would shift unpredictably. As the night deepened, the Federation Headquarters' lights burned brightly against the encroaching darkness. Each light in the building seemed to represent a glimmer of hope.
